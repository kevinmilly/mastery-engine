## Exercises

**Exercise 1**
A homeowner in a wildfire-prone region is creating a preparedness plan. Classify each of the following actions as either a **Pre-Event Mitigation** measure or an **Immediate Post-Event Response** action.
1. Installing a metal roof with a Class A fire rating.
2. Checking the roof and attic for smoldering embers after the fire front has passed.
3. Sealing attic and crawlspace vents with 1/8-inch metal mesh screening.
4. Shutting off the natural gas supply line at the meter when an evacuation order is issued.
5. Clearing a 100-foot "defensible space" of flammable brush and trees around the house.

**Exercise 2**
A family lives in a region known for severe ice storms that can cause power outages lasting 3-5 days. The household includes two adults, a 6-month-old infant, and an elderly parent who uses a portable oxygen concentrator that requires electricity. Beyond the standard emergency kit items (3 days of water, non-perishable food, flashlights, first-aid kit), identify four *additional*, highly specific items that are critical for this family's kit. For each item, justify its inclusion based on the family's unique circumstances.

**Exercise 3**
You return home after an evacuation to find that your basement has flooded with 8 inches of water due to a prolonged power outage that disabled your sump pump. The storm has passed, and power has just been restored to your house. The basement breaker panel is located high on the wall, well above the water line. List the first four actions you should take, *in the correct order of priority*, to safely begin the recovery process. Provide a brief rationale for the sequence.

**Exercise 4**
A neighbor has prepared for a potential tornado by assembling a "shelter-in-place" kit in their basement. The kit contains a battery-powered weather radio, bottled water, canned goods, a flashlight, and a first-aid kit. They plan to shelter under the basement stairs. Based on the principles of comprehensive disaster planning, identify two significant gaps in their plan related to (1) the building's immediate safety and (2) post-event recovery. Recommend one specific improvement for each gap.

**Exercise 5**
A homeowner is planning a major renovation of a ground-floor bathroom in a house located in a designated flood plain. The project is extensive enough to be classified as a "substantial improvement" under local building codes, triggering a requirement to use flood-resistant materials up to 3 feet above floor level. Integrating concepts from disaster mitigation, long-term material selection, and building code compliance, propose specific material choices for the following three components that would meet these requirements and facilitate easier recovery. Justify each choice.
1. Wall covering (instead of standard paper-faced drywall)
2. Vanity cabinet (instead of standard particleboard/MDF)
3. Flooring (instead of vinyl plank or carpet)

**Exercise 6**
During a severe thunderstorm, a large oak tree limb falls, tearing the home's electrical service mast and cable completely off the side of the house and punching a 2-foot hole in the roof. The main power line is now lying in the yard. The homeowner needs to restore power and repair the damage. Drawing on principles of post-event response, contractor oversight, and electrical systems, create a sequential action plan. The plan must identify which professional (e.g., utility company, electrician, roofer) is needed for each major step and explain *why* this specific sequence is critical for a safe and code-compliant recovery.

## Answer Key

**Answer 1**
1.  **Pre-Event Mitigation:** This is a long-term structural improvement designed to reduce the potential for damage before an event occurs.
2.  **Immediate Post-Event Response:** This is an action taken directly after the disaster has struck to prevent further damage (e.g., a fire reigniting from embers).
3.  **Pre-Event Mitigation:** This is a proactive measure to make the home more resistant to embers entering vulnerable spaces during a fire.
4.  **Immediate Post-Event Response:** Although it happens just before the event hits, this is an immediate action in response to a direct and imminent threat (the approaching fire), not a long-term preparatory measure.
5.  **Pre-Event Mitigation:** This is a fundamental preparedness strategy to reduce a fire's fuel source well in advance of any specific threat.

**Answer 2**
1.  **Item:** A non-electric heat source safe for indoor use, such as a propane or kerosene heater with proper ventilation.
    **Justification:** An infant and an elderly person are more vulnerable to hypothermia. During a multi-day winter power outage, the home's primary heating will be disabled, making a backup heat source essential for survival.
2.  **Item:** A backup power source (e.g., a deep-cycle battery pack, a small inverter generator) capable of running the portable oxygen concentrator for several days.
    **Justification:** The elderly parent's medical device is life-sustaining. The plan must account for keeping it operational when grid power is unavailable.
3.  **Item:** A one-week supply of ready-to-use infant formula and diapers.
    **Justification:** During an extended outage, it may be impossible to sterilize bottles with boiling water or wash cloth diapers. Ready-to-use formula eliminates the need for mixing with potentially unsafe tap water.
4.  **Item:** A non-electric method for preparing food and boiling water, such as a camp stove or outdoor grill (with fuel) to be used safely outside.
    **Justification:** The infant requires warm formula, and the entire family needs a way to heat food. Relying solely on cold canned goods is insufficient, especially in a winter scenario.

**Answer 3**
The correct sequence of actions is:
1.  **Confirm Power is On and Assess Electrical Safety:** Even though the panel is above the water, check that the restored power hasn't created a hazard (e.g., arcing from submerged outlets). Turn off the individual breakers for all basement circuits before entering the water. **Rationale:** Personal safety is the absolute first priority. Electrocution is the most immediate and deadly risk in this scenario.
2.  **Document the Damage:** Before moving or removing anything, thoroughly photograph and video the standing water, the high-water mark on the walls, and any damaged items. **Rationale:** This documentation is crucial for filing a successful insurance claim. Doing it before cleanup ensures the full extent of the damage is recorded.
3.  **Begin Removing Water:** Use a submersible pump or wet/dry vacuum to remove all standing water as quickly as possible. **Rationale:** The faster the bulk water is removed, the less time it has to be absorbed by building materials (drywall, wood framing), which minimizes primary damage and reduces the risk of secondary damage like mold growth.
4.  **Remove Wet Materials and Set Up Drying Equipment:** Move soaked furniture, rugs, and boxes out of the basement. If drywall is wet, cut it away at least 12 inches above the high-water mark. Set up dehumidifiers and high-volume fans. **Rationale:** Porous materials will not dry in place and will quickly grow mold. Aggressive drying of the structure is critical to preventing long-term rot and air quality issues.

**Answer 4**
1.  **Gap 1 (Building Safety):** The plan focuses only on personal safety during the event but neglects to address how the house itself will fare. The primary danger in a tornado is from flying debris and catastrophic pressure changes.
    **Improvement:** The plan should include pre-storm actions to secure potential projectiles. This means securing or bringing inside all patio furniture, grills, trampolines, and other yard items. Additionally, a key mitigation strategy is reinforcing garage doors, which are often a major failure point in high winds.
2.  **Gap 2 (Post-Event Recovery):** The plan has no provisions for what to do after the tornado passes. Recovery involves communication, documentation, and safe re-entry.
    **Improvement:** The kit should be expanded to include a "recovery" component: copies of vital documents (ID, insurance policies, deed) in a waterproof container or stored digitally in the cloud; a list of emergency contacts (insurance agent, contractors); and a USB power bank to charge a cell phone for communication once the storm is over. The plan should also include a post-event checklist for safely assessing the home for gas leaks, downed power lines, and structural instability before exiting the shelter.

**Answer 5**
1.  **Wall Covering:** Cement board (tile backer board) with a skim coat of waterproof plaster and painted with an epoxy paint.
    **Justification:** Unlike drywall, cement board is inorganic and completely inert to water. It will not swell, soften, or grow mold. After a flood, it can be washed, sanitized, and repainted without needing replacement, making it a highly resilient and code-compliant choice.
2.  **Vanity Cabinet:** Cabinets made from PVC (plastic) or marine-grade plywood.
    **Justification:** Standard particleboard or MDF acts like a sponge, rapidly absorbing water and disintegrating. PVC or marine-grade plywood are water-resistant materials that can withstand being submerged. They can be cleaned and reused, saving significant cost and time during recovery.
3.  **Flooring:** Porcelain or ceramic tile installed over a cement board underlayment with a waterproof membrane and epoxy grout.
    **Justification:** This creates a virtually waterproof floor system. The tile itself is impervious to water. This assembly can be thoroughly cleaned and sanitized after a flood, whereas other flooring types like wood, laminate, or carpet would be a complete loss and would trap contaminants. This is a standard solution for flood-resistant construction.

**Answer 6**
The critical sequence is dictated by safety, regulations, and the physical dependencies of the repairs.

1.  **Step 1: Immediate Safety & Contractor Sourcing (Homeowner & Roofer):**
    *   **Action:** Call the utility company immediately to report the downed line and have them de-energize it at the pole if necessary. Cordon off the area. Call your insurance company. Source *both* a licensed electrician and a roofer. Place a temporary tarp over the roof hole to prevent water intrusion.
    *   **Rationale:** Safety is first. A professional roofer is needed to prevent further water damage, and an electrician is needed for the power restoration. Sourcing them in parallel saves time.

2.  **Step 2: Electrical Repair (Licensed Electrician):**
    *   **Action:** The electrician must first repair all *homeowner-owned* equipment. This includes installing a new service mast, weatherhead, meter base, and service entrance conductors connecting the meter to the main electrical panel.
    *   **Rationale:** The utility company is only responsible for the line from the pole to the house (the "service drop"). They are legally prohibited from reconnecting power to a damaged customer-owned mast. This work *must* be done first. The roofer cannot properly seal the roof until the new mast is in place.

3.  **Step 3: Electrical Inspection (Local Building Inspector):**
    *   **Action:** The electrician's work must be inspected and approved by the local code enforcement authority.
    *   **Rationale:** This is a non-negotiable regulatory step. The utility company will require proof of a passed inspection (often a "green tag") before they will proceed with their work.

4.  **Step 4: Power Reconnection (Utility Company):**
    *   **Action:** Once the inspection is passed, the utility company will send a crew to connect their new service drop from the utility pole to the newly installed weatherhead on the house.
    *   **Rationale:** This step can only happen after the homeowner's equipment is certified as safe and code-compliant.

5.  **Step 5: Final Roof Repair (Roofer):**
    *   **Action:** The roofer repairs the structural damage to the roof deck and then installs new underlayment, shingles, and, most importantly, a new vent pipe boot and flashing system, properly sealed around the new electrical mast.
    *   **Rationale:** This is the final step because the permanent roof sealing must be done *around* the newly installed mast. Doing the roof repair first would mean having to cut it open again to install the mast, compromising the repair.
