## Exercises

**Exercise 1**
A homeowner's gas furnace is not producing heat. You are using the simplified diagnostic flowchart below.

1.  Is the thermostat set to HEAT and the temperature setpoint above the current room temperature?
    *   **Yes:** Go to step 2.
    *   **No:** Adjust thermostat settings. Problem solved.
2.  Is the furnace circuit breaker tripped?
    *   **Yes:** Reset breaker. If it trips again, call an electrician.
    *   **No:** Go to step 3.
3.  Is the gas pilot light lit? (for older models)
    *   **Yes:** Go to step 4.
    *   **No:** Follow lighting instructions. If it won't stay lit, the thermocouple may be faulty.
4.  Does the blower motor turn on?
    *   **Yes:** Possible issue with gas valve or ignitor.
    *   **No:** Possible issue with the blower motor or control board.

You arrive and observe the following: the thermostat is set correctly, and you have confirmed the circuit breaker in the main panel is on. The furnace is a modern model without a standing pilot light. When the thermostat calls for heat, you hear a "click" from the furnace, but nothing else happens. Based *only* on the flowchart, what is the most probable area of failure?

**Exercise 2**
A GFCI (Ground Fault Circuit Interrupter) outlet in a bathroom keeps tripping randomly. You are using the process of elimination to diagnose the problem. The known potential causes are:
A. A faulty appliance plugged into the outlet.
B. The GFCI outlet itself is worn out and overly sensitive.
C. There is a "downstream" outlet on the same circuit with a ground fault.
D. Moisture has entered the outlet box.

You unplug everything from the bathroom outlets, wait an hour for any moisture to dry, and press the reset button. The outlet trips again within five minutes, with nothing plugged into it or any other outlet on the circuit. Which potential cause have you definitively isolated as the most likely culprit?

**Exercise 3**
A dishwasher completes its cycle, but the dishes remain dirty and there is no evidence that water ever entered the machine. You confirm the dishwasher has power because the control panel lights up and it makes normal motor noises during the cycle. This suggests the problem is with the water supply system, not the electrical system.

Create the first three logical steps (as question/test pairs) for a diagnostic flowchart to troubleshoot this "no water" problem, starting from the simplest and safest check and moving towards more complex ones.

**Exercise 4**
A homeowner reports that their new high-efficiency washing machine occasionally leaves a puddle of water on the floor, but not after every load. They can't find a pattern related to cycle type or load size. After an inspection, you find no leaks in the supply hoses or the drain hose connection. You suspect an intermittent "suds-lock" condition, where using too much of the wrong type of detergent creates excessive suds that block the machine's ability to drain properly, causing an overflow.

Describe a non-invasive, systematic test the homeowner can perform over the next several laundry loads to confirm or rule out your suds-lock hypothesis.

**Exercise 5**
You are investigating a complaint of low water pressure throughout an entire house. The issue started suddenly yesterday. The home is on a municipal water supply. Your initial thought is a partially closed main shutoff valve or a problem with the city's supply. However, the homeowner also mentions that their tank-style water heater has been making loud "popping" and "banging" noises for the last few months.

How does the information about the noisy water heater potentially change your diagnostic starting point? What is a plausible root cause that connects both the sudden low pressure and the noisy heater, and what would be your first step to verify it?

**Exercise 6**
A homeowner has a persistent, musty smell in their basement that is worse on humid days. They have already confirmed the following:
*   There are no visible leaks from pipes.
*   The sump pump is working correctly.
*   The basement windows and foundation walls show no signs of water intrusion.
*   A dehumidifier is running, but the smell persists.

The problem is elusive because the obvious causes have been ruled out. Outline a systematic diagnostic plan with at least four distinct stages to trace the source of the moisture causing the smell. Your plan should move beyond the obvious checks already performed and consider interconnected systems and less common causes.

## Answer Key

**Answer 1**
Based on the flowchart and the observations, the most probable area of failure is the **blower motor or control board**.

**Reasoning:**
1.  **Step 1 (Thermostat):** Passed. The thermostat is set correctly.
2.  **Step 2 (Circuit Breaker):** Passed. The breaker is not tripped.
3.  **Step 3 (Pilot Light):** Skipped/Not Applicable. It's a modern furnace. We proceed as if this check passed.
4.  **Step 4 (Blower Motor):** The key observation is that after the "click" (the thermostat signaling the furnace to start), the blower motor does *not* turn on. According to the flowchart, if the blower motor fails to start, the likely issue is the motor itself or the control board that is supposed to activate it.

**Answer 2**
The most likely culprit is **B. The GFCI outlet itself is worn out and overly sensitive.**

**Reasoning:**
The process of elimination was used to rule out the other causes:
*   **Ruled Out (A - Faulty Appliance):** The outlet tripped with nothing plugged into it, so an appliance cannot be the cause.
*   **Ruled Out (C - Downstream Fault):** The problem persisted even when nothing was plugged into any other outlets on the circuit, making a downstream fault highly unlikely to be the trigger at that moment.
*   **Ruled Out (D - Moisture):** While not 100% certain, waiting an hour in a dry bathroom makes immediate moisture an unlikely cause. Since the outlet tripped again with no other variables present, the most logical remaining conclusion is that the internal sensing mechanism of the GFCI itself is faulty.

**Answer 3**
Here are the first three logical steps for a diagnostic flowchart:

1.  **Question/Test 1:** Is the hot-water shutoff valve under the sink fully open?
    *   **Action:** Visually inspect the valve. Ensure it is turned fully counter-clockwise. Many dishwasher issues are caused by this valve being accidentally knocked closed.
2.  **Question/Test 2:** Is the water inlet hose kinked or blocked?
    *   **Action:** Look behind the dishwasher to see if the supply hose is bent sharply, restricting flow. This is a common issue after installation or cleaning.
3.  **Question/Test 3:** Is the water inlet valve screen clogged?
    *   **Action:** (Requires shutting off water first). Disconnect the water inlet hose from the dishwasher's inlet valve. Check the small screen inside the valve for sediment or debris that could be blocking water flow.

**Reasoning:**
This sequence follows a systematic flow from easiest/safest to more involved. Step 1 is a simple visual check. Step 2 is also visual but may require moving the appliance slightly. Step 3 is more complex as it requires shutting off the water supply and using tools, so it logically comes after the simpler checks have failed.

**Answer 4**
The systematic test is to manipulate the detergent variable while keeping other factors (load size, cycle type) constant.

**Test Plan:**
1.  **Establish a Baseline:** Have the homeowner run one or two loads exactly as they have been, using their usual detergent and amount. Note if a leak occurs.
2.  **Change the Variable:** Instruct the homeowner to switch to a detergent specifically marked "HE" (High Efficiency). They should use the minimum amount recommended on the packaging for the next 3-4 loads.
3.  **Observe and Conclude:** If the puddles stop appearing after switching to the proper HE detergent and using the correct amount, the suds-lock hypothesis is confirmed. If the problem persists, the cause lies elsewhere, and the hypothesis is ruled out.

**Reasoning:**
This test isolates the suspected variable (detergent type/amount) to determine if it is the root cause. It is non-invasive and requires no tools, making it a perfect example of systematic diagnosis by controlling variables to pinpoint an intermittent problem.

**Answer 5**
The noisy water heater suggests the problem may not be with the external water supply but with a major blockage originating from within the home's plumbing system.

**Plausible Root Cause:** The water heater is failing due to massive sediment buildup. The "popping" noise is water flashing to steam under a layer of sediment at the bottom of the tank. A large piece of this sediment or scale could have broken free and traveled through the hot water pipe, becoming lodged in a central point (like a mixing valve or the main outlet of the heater), causing a sudden and severe pressure drop for the entire house.

**First Diagnostic Step:** Your first step should be to check the hot water pressure specifically. Turn on a hot water tap fully at the sink closest to the water heater. Then turn on only the cold water. If the cold water pressure is significantly higher than the hot, you have confirmed the blockage is in the hot water system, likely originating from the failing water heater. This is a much more targeted first step than inspecting the main city shutoff valve.

**Answer 6**
A systematic plan to find the elusive moisture source should proceed as follows:

1.  **Stage 1: HVAC System Diagnosis:** The AC evaporator coil and drain line are common, hidden sources of moisture.
    *   **Test:** Inspect the indoor air handler unit. Check if the condensate drain pan is cracked or full of water. Ensure the condensate drain line is not clogged by using a wet/dry vac on the exterior outlet to see if it pulls out a blockage. A slow, persistent leak here can feed mold without creating a visible puddle.

2.  **Stage 2: Appliance Leak Check:** Rule out hidden leaks from appliances.
    *   **Test:** Pull out the washing machine and check the floor underneath for signs of slow leaks or past water damage. Check the water heater's pressure relief valve for any signs of weeping or mineral buildup, indicating a slow, intermittent leak.

3.  **Stage 3: Investigate Hidden Plumbing:** A leak inside a wall can go undetected.
    *   **Test:** Use a moisture meter on the drywall behind the basement bathroom or any other plumbing walls. Pay special attention to the area below the kitchen or bathrooms on the floor above. High readings would indicate a leak within the wall cavity.

4.  **Stage 4: Evaluate for Capillary Action / Vapor Barrier Failure:** Moisture can wick up through the concrete slab if the vapor barrier below it has failed.
    *   **Test:** Tape a 1-foot by 1-foot square of plastic sheeting to the concrete floor in the center of the basement. Seal all four edges with duct tape. After 24-48 hours, check for condensation on the underside of the plastic. If present, it confirms moisture is migrating up through the slab from the ground, indicating a more fundamental groundwater or foundation issue.
