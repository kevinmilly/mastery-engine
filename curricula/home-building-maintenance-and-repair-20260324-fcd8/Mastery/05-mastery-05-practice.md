## Exercises

**Exercise 1**
A homeowner is considering two energy efficiency upgrades.
- **Option A: Air Sealing.** Professional air sealing of the building envelope costs $1,200 and is projected to save $250 per year in heating and cooling costs.
- **Option B: Smart Thermostat.** A new smart thermostat costs $250 to purchase and install, and is projected to save $80 per year.

Calculate the Simple Payback Period for each option and determine which one offers a faster return on the initial investment.

**Exercise 2**
A homeowner is replacing a single large window in their living room. They have two choices:
- **Window A:** A high-performance, triple-pane window with a very low U-factor (excellent insulation). It is heavy, requires precise fitting, and the manufacturer strongly recommends professional installation, quoted at $900.
- **Window B:** A standard double-pane, argon-filled window with a good (but not exceptional) U-factor. It is a standard size and the homeowner, who is an experienced DIYer, is confident they can install it themselves over a weekend.

Beyond the initial purchase price, explain three distinct factors from the lesson (ROI, installation complexity, comfort) that the homeowner should weigh against each other to make an informed decision.

**Exercise 3**
A family lives in an old, drafty two-story house in a cold northern climate (e.g., Minnesota). They have a budget of $5,000 for energy upgrades before winter. Their primary complaints are high heating bills and feeling cold drafts in the family room on the first floor. They are considering these three options:
1.  Upgrading all the attic insulation from R-19 to R-49.
2.  Replacing the five original single-pane windows in the first-floor family room with new double-pane windows.
3.  Replacing their 15-year-old, but still functional, natural gas furnace with a new high-efficiency model.

Analyze these options and recommend which one they should prioritize. Justify your recommendation by evaluating the likely ROI, installation complexity, and direct impact on their specific comfort complaints.

**Exercise 4**
A homeowner receives a quote for a new solar panel system. The salesperson presents a simple calculation: "Your average monthly electric bill is $150. Over 20 years, that's $36,000 you'll pay the utility. Our system costs $28,000, so you save $8,000!"

Critique this sales pitch. Identify four critical factors or questions the salesperson's calculation omits that are essential for determining the true Return on Investment for this solar panel system.

**Exercise 5**
A homeowner's 18-year-old electric water heater has a leaking pressure relief valve and is showing signs of rust at the base. A plumber says a temporary repair is possible for $200, but it might only last a year or two. A new, standard electric water heater costs $1,000 installed. A new, high-efficiency heat pump water heater costs $3,000 installed but uses about 60% less electricity. The home's current electricity cost for water heating is estimated at $500 per year.

Integrating concepts from "Balancing Trade-offs: Repair vs. Replace Decisions" and the current lesson on ROI, construct a reasoned argument for choosing one of the three options: repair, replace with standard, or replace with high-efficiency. Your argument must weigh short-term cost, long-term ROI, and the risks associated with the existing unit's age and condition.

**Exercise 6**
A homeowner plans to add R-30 of blown-in insulation to their attic, which currently has a minimal amount of old, compressed insulation. While inspecting the attic to get measurements, they notice two things: the bathroom exhaust fan vents directly into the attic space (not to the outside), and there are no baffles to keep insulation from blocking the soffit vents at the eaves.

Using principles from "Navigating Building Codes & Permits" and the current lesson, develop a revised implementation plan. Explain why simply adding the insulation without addressing these two issues is a major mistake that could negate the project's ROI and potentially damage the house. Your plan should outline the necessary preparatory steps and justify their importance.

## Answer Key

**Answer 1**
The goal is to calculate the Simple Payback Period, which is the time it takes for savings to equal the initial cost. The formula is: `Cost of Upgrade / Annual Savings = Payback Period in Years`.

-   **Option A (Air Sealing):**
    -   Calculation: $1,200 / $250 per year = 4.8 years
-   **Option B (Smart Thermostat):**
    -   Calculation: $250 / $80 per year = 3.125 years

**Conclusion:** The Smart Thermostat (Option B) offers a faster return on investment with a payback period of just over 3 years, compared to nearly 5 years for the air sealing. While air sealing provides greater annual savings, its higher initial cost means it takes longer to recoup the investment.

**Answer 2**
The homeowner must balance the following three factors:

1.  **Return on Investment (ROI):** Window A will provide greater energy savings each year due to its superior U-factor. However, its total installed cost is significantly higher. The homeowner needs to calculate if the incremental energy savings of Window A over Window B will ever pay back the extra $900 in installation cost over the window's lifespan. Window B has a much lower initial cost, leading to a faster, though smaller, ROI.
2.  **Installation Complexity:** This is a major trade-off. Window A requires hiring a professional, which adds cost, scheduling dependencies, and reliance on a third party. Window B leverages the homeowner's existing skills, saving significant money and giving them control over the timeline. However, a poor DIY installation could lead to drafts and water damage, negating the window's efficiency. The homeowner must honestly assess their skill level.
3.  **Comfort:** Window A will likely provide superior comfort. Its triple-pane construction will reduce drafts, make the area near the window feel warmer in winter and cooler in summer, and provide better sound insulation. The decision comes down to whether this increase in day-to-day comfort is worth the significant extra upfront cost and installation hassle compared to the "good enough" performance of Window B.

**Answer 3**
**Recommendation:** Prioritize upgrading the attic insulation.

**Reasoning:**

1.  **Impact on Heat Loss (ROI):** In a cold climate, the most significant heat loss in a home is typically upwards through the attic (since heat rises). Upgrading attic insulation from a low R-value to a high one (R-49) provides one of the highest ROIs of any energy upgrade, directly and substantially reducing heating bills. While new windows help, the total square footage of the ceiling is far greater than that of the five windows, making attic insulation a more impactful starting point. A furnace replacement has a long payback period, and the current one is still functional.
2.  **Addressing Comfort Complaints:** While the drafts are felt on the first floor, a poorly insulated attic contributes to a "cold lid" effect on the entire house, making the heating system work harder and contributing to overall temperature imbalances. While new windows would directly address the drafts in that specific room, the attic insulation addresses a larger systemic issue affecting the whole house's thermal envelope.
3.  **Installation Complexity & Cost:** Blown-in attic insulation is a relatively straightforward project that can often be completed in a single day for under the $5,000 budget. Window replacement is more complex, disruptive to the interior, and can be very expensive, potentially exceeding the budget for five quality windows. A furnace replacement is also a complex, costly professional job. The attic insulation provides the most "bang for the buck" within their stated budget.

**Answer 4**
The salesperson's calculation is an oversimplification that ignores several critical factors needed for a true ROI analysis.

1.  **Net Metering Policies & Electricity Rates:** The pitch assumes a 1-to-1 credit for solar power, which isn't always the case. The homeowner needs to ask: "What is the utility's net metering policy? What rate do they pay for excess power I send to the grid? Does this rate change over time?" Electricity rates also fluctuate, so a simple multiplication is not accurate.
2.  **System Maintenance & Component Lifespan:** The calculation assumes zero costs over 20 years. The homeowner must ask: "What is the warranty on the panels and the inverter? What is the expected lifespan of the inverter, and what is its replacement cost?" Inverters often need replacement within the 20-year lifespan of the panels, which is a significant future expense.
3.  **Actual System Production vs. Consumption:** The pitch implies 100% offset of their bill. The homeowner should ask: "What is the system's estimated annual production in kWh, and how does that compare to my actual annual consumption? How will production vary by season and weather?" This will reveal if the system will truly cover all their needs.
4.  **Time Value of Money & Incentives:** A $28,000 cost today is not equivalent to $36,000 saved over 20 years. A proper analysis would discount future savings to their present value. Furthermore, the pitch ignores any potential tax credits, rebates, or SRECs (Solar Renewable Energy Certificates) that could significantly reduce the *net* upfront cost of the system, which is crucial for the ROI calculation.

**Answer 5**
This problem requires balancing the immediate cost of repair against the long-term value of replacement, considering both standard and high-efficiency options.

-   **Option 1: Repair ($200)**
    -   **Argument:** This is the lowest-cost, immediate solution. If the homeowner has immediate budget constraints, this keeps a critical appliance running.
    -   **Counter-argument/Risk (Repair vs. Replace):** The unit is 18 years old, near the end of its typical lifespan. The repair is temporary, and the rust at the base indicates a high risk of catastrophic failure (a major leak). The $200 could be wasted if the unit fails completely in six months. It also continues the high energy cost of an inefficient unit.

-   **Option 2: Replace with Standard ($1,000)**
    -   **Argument:** This is a reliable, mid-cost option. It permanently solves the leak risk with a new, warrantied unit for a moderate upfront cost. It eliminates the uncertainty of the old unit.
    -   **Counter-argument (ROI):** This option does not provide any energy savings. The homeowner will continue to pay ~$500/year for water heating. It is a safe but financially inefficient long-term choice.

-   **Option 3: Replace with Heat Pump Water Heater ($3,000)**
    -   **Argument:** This option provides the best long-term ROI.
        -   **Annual Savings:** $500/year * 60% savings = $300/year.
        -   **Payback on a premium over a standard unit:** The extra cost is $3,000 - $1,000 = $2,000.
        -   **Simple Payback Period:** $2,000 / $300/year = ~6.7 years.
    -   **Conclusion:** After 6.7 years, the unit will have paid for its extra cost and will continue to generate $300 in savings annually for the rest of its 10-15 year lifespan. Given the old unit's high risk of failure, investing in the high-efficiency model is the most financially sound decision for a homeowner who can afford the upfront cost and plans to stay in the home long-term.

**Recommendation:** Given the age and condition of the old unit, repair is too risky. The Heat Pump Water Heater (Option 3) is the best choice, as it resolves the immediate reliability problem and provides a strong, quantifiable ROI.

**Answer 6**
Simply adding insulation without addressing the underlying issues is a mistake that could lead to moisture damage, poor air quality, and reduced HVAC efficiency, thus destroying the project's ROI.

**Revised Implementation Plan:**

1.  **Step 1: Diagnose and Remediate Core Issues (Pre-Insulation Prep).**
    -   **Action:** Reroute the bathroom exhaust fan ducting to terminate outside the building envelope (e.g., through the roof or a gable-end wall).
    -   **Justification (Codes & Performance):** Venting moist bathroom air into an attic is a building code violation in virtually all jurisdictions. This moisture can lead to mold growth on the underside of the roof sheathing and rot in the roof framing. A moldy, wet attic would be a costly disaster, completely negating any energy savings.
    -   **Action:** Install ventilation baffles (e.g., polystyrene or cardboard) at the eaves, ensuring a clear channel for air to flow from the soffit vents into the attic space.
    -   **Justification (Codes & Performance):** Building codes require attic ventilation to manage heat and moisture. Without baffles, the new blown-in insulation would block the soffit vents, choking off airflow. This can lead to ice dam formation in the winter and extreme heat buildup in the summer, which can damage shingles and increase cooling costs—working directly against the goal of the insulation project.

2.  **Step 2: Air Seal the Attic Floor.**
    -   **Action:** Before insulating, systematically air seal all penetrations between the conditioned living space and the attic. This includes gaps around wiring, plumbing stacks, light fixtures, and the attic access hatch.
    -   **Justification (ROI):** Air leakage (convection) can be a more significant source of energy loss than poor insulation (conduction). Adding a thick blanket of insulation on top of a leaky attic floor is like wearing a winter coat that's unzipped. Air sealing is a low-cost, high-impact step that makes the subsequent insulation perform to its maximum R-value potential.

3.  **Step 3: Install Insulation.**
    -   **Action:** Once the preparatory work is complete, proceed with blowing in the R-30 of insulation to achieve the desired total R-value.
    -   **Justification:** By performing the prerequisite steps, the insulation will now function as intended within a properly ventilated, dry, and sealed building system, ensuring a positive ROI and long-term durability of the home.
