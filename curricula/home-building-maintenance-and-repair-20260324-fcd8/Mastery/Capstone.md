## Capstone: Mastery — home/building maintenance and repair

### The Scenario
You are the owner of a 25-year-old two-story home. For the past several months, you've been dealing with a frustrating and elusive pair of problems. The GFCI outlet in the main upstairs bathroom trips intermittently, usually every few weeks. It seems to happen more frequently within a day or two of heavy rainfall, but not always. Resetting it always works, and all connected appliances have been tested and ruled out as the cause.

Simultaneously, you've noticed a faint, musty odor in the first-floor home office, which is located directly beneath the main bathroom. Recently, a small, palm-sized area of yellow-brown discoloration has appeared on the office ceiling drywall, about four feet from the exterior wall. You’ve done a basic visual inspection in the bathroom—the toilet base is solid, the sink drain isn’t leaking, and the caulking around the tub appears intact. You are concerned the two issues are connected and that a more serious, hidden problem is developing. You intend to live in this house for the long term and want to address this correctly to protect your investment.

### Your Tasks
Your deliverable is a comprehensive **Homeowner's Action & Remediation Plan** that addresses the situation. The plan will be composed of your responses to the following five tasks.

1.  **Systematic Diagnosis Plan:** Develop a systematic diagnostic flowchart to isolate the root cause of both the water intrusion and the intermittent electrical fault. Your flowchart should outline a logical sequence of tests and observations, starting with non-destructive methods, designed to pinpoint the source of the leak and its interaction with the electrical system before any walls or ceilings are opened.

2.  **Repair vs. Replace Decision Matrix:** Assume your diagnosis confirms a slow, intermittent leak from a corroded copper supply line to the shower valve, hidden inside the wall. The leak has saturated a 4x8 foot section of insulation, the office ceiling drywall below, and the wood subfloor. The moisture is wicking along a framing member and dripping onto a non-metallic sheathed cable, causing the upstream GFCI to trip. Create a decision matrix that compares a "Minimal Spot Repair" to a "Comprehensive Remediation." Evaluate both options against at least four critical criteria (e.g., initial cost, long-term resilience, project timeline, likelihood of mold abatement).

3.  **Code & Permit Analysis:** Focusing on the "Comprehensive Remediation" option, which involves opening the wall, replacing a section of plumbing, running a new electrical circuit, and replacing the subfloor, identify two specific project elements that would almost certainly require a municipal building permit. For each element, explain the primary safety-related reason that a permit and subsequent inspection are mandated by most building codes.

4.  **Contractor Scope of Work:** You’ve decided the "Comprehensive Remediation" is beyond your skill set and will hire a general contractor. Draft a concise "Scope of Work" document to provide to three potential bidders. The document must clearly define the project boundaries, list the key demolition and reconstruction tasks, specify that all work must be permitted and inspected, and set expectations for worksite cleanliness and homeowner communication.

5.  **Preventive Maintenance Strategy:** This incident has exposed a gap in your home maintenance routine. Drawing lessons from this specific failure (a hidden, slow plumbing leak), develop three new, specific preventive maintenance tasks to add to your annual home maintenance schedule. For each task, detail the procedure, the recommended frequency (e.g., annually, semi-annually), and the specific type of failure it is designed to proactively detect or prevent.

### What Good Work Looks Like
*   **Demonstrates a logical, evidence-based approach to troubleshooting,** prioritizing non-invasive diagnostics before recommending destructive investigation.
*   **Articulates a clear rationale for major decisions** by weighing multiple competing factors, such as short-term costs versus long-term resilience, risk, and property value.
*   **Shows a strong understanding of the "why" behind building codes and professional oversight,** connecting procedural requirements to tangible safety, legal, and quality outcomes.
*   **Translates lessons learned from a specific failure into a proactive, forward-looking strategy** for preventing future incidents, moving from a reactive to a preventive mindset.
*   **Communicates complex information clearly and professionally,** whether creating a diagnostic plan for personal use or a scope of work document for external contractors.
