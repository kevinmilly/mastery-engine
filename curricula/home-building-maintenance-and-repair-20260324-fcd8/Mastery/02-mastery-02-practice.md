## Exercises

**Exercise 1**
Your kitchen faucet has a persistent drip. A repair kit with new O-rings and seals costs $25 and will take you about an hour to install. A brand new, comparable faucet costs $150 and will take about the same amount of time to install. The current faucet is 12 years old and is showing some cosmetic corrosion. Using the core trade-off factors (cost, effort, longevity, impact), justify which option you would choose and why.

**Exercise 2**
A single burner on your 10-year-old electric stove has stopped working. You've identified the issue as a faulty infinite switch. You have two options:
1.  **Repair:** Replace the switch for $30 in parts. This is a moderately complex repair you've never done before, estimated to take 2 hours.
2.  **Replace:** Purchase a new, similar-quality stove for $700. The other three burners and the oven work perfectly.
Analyze this decision using the repair vs. replace framework, explaining which factor is most influential in this specific case.

**Exercise 3**
Your 18-year-old central air conditioning unit is not cooling. A technician diagnoses the problem as a failed capacitor, a $250 repair (parts and labor). However, the technician notes that the compressor is showing signs of age and the unit uses an older, less efficient refrigerant. The expected remaining lifespan of a unit like this, even after repair, is only 2-4 years. A full system replacement with a modern, high-efficiency unit costs $6,000. How does the concept of "cascading failures" and future operational costs influence your repair vs. replace analysis here?

**Exercise 4**
You own a historic home with original, single-pane wood windows. One window's sash cord has broken, making it difficult to open and close.
- **Repair:** A specialized craftsman can re-rope the sash for $200. This maintains the window's historical character.
- **Replace:** A standard modern vinyl replacement window costs $500 installed. It will be more energy-efficient but will not match the other windows in the house.
Analyze the trade-offs, focusing on how non-financial factors like aesthetic consistency and historical value fit into the "impact" component of your decision-making framework.

**Exercise 5**
A homeowner reports their garbage disposal hums but doesn't spin. This problem is intermittent. First, briefly outline a diagnostic flowchart to isolate the potential cause (e.g., jam, faulty switch, seized motor). Then, for each of the most likely outcomes of your diagnosis, create a corresponding repair vs. replace recommendation, justifying your choice based on the likely cost, effort, and longevity of the solution.

**Exercise 6**
A client's 12-year-old tank-style water heater is leaking slightly from a pressure relief valve. The cost to replace the valve is $150. A new, equivalent water heater costs $800 installed, while a high-efficiency tankless model costs $2,500 installed. The client is environmentally conscious and plans to live in the home for at least another 15 years. They have the budget for any of the three options. Develop a comprehensive recommendation that weighs the immediate problem against the client's long-term financial and personal values.

## Answer Key

**Answer 1**
**Decision:** Replace the faucet.

**Reasoning:**
While the repair is significantly cheaper ($25 vs. $150), the other factors point towards replacement.
-   **Cost:** Repair is cheaper upfront.
-   **Effort:** The time commitment is identical for both options.
-   **Longevity:** At 12 years old and with existing corrosion, the faucet is nearing the end of its typical lifespan. A $25 repair might fix the drip, but another part could fail soon, or the corrosion could worsen. The new faucet offers a full new lifespan.
-   **Impact:** The cosmetic corrosion negatively impacts the kitchen's appearance. A new faucet provides a functional *and* aesthetic upgrade.
In this case, the minimal upfront cost saving of the repair is outweighed by the low expected longevity of the old unit and the positive cosmetic impact of the new one.

**Answer 2**
**Decision:** Repair the stove.

**Reasoning:**
The most influential factor here is the proportionality of the cost and problem.
-   **Cost:** The repair cost ($30) is less than 5% of the replacement cost ($700).
-   **Effort:** The effort is moderate (2 hours), but it's a valuable skill-building opportunity for a homeowner.
-   **Longevity:** The infinite switch is a discrete component. Its failure does not necessarily indicate that the other burners or the oven are about to fail. The repair restores the unit to 100% functionality with a high probability of lasting for several more years.
-   **Impact:** The problem is isolated to one of four burners. Replacing an entire, largely functional appliance for a minor, inexpensive component failure is inefficient and wasteful. The high cost of replacement is not justified by the limited scope of the problem.

**Answer 3**
**Decision:** Replace the A/C unit, if financially feasible.

**Reasoning:**
The concepts of cascading failures and future costs are central to this decision.
-   **Cascading Failures:** The capacitor failure is a symptom of a larger issue: the system's advanced age. The technician's warning about the compressor indicates that this $250 repair is likely the first of several. Spending money on one part when another critical, expensive part (the compressor) is also likely to fail soon is a poor investment. This is a classic "throwing good money after bad" scenario.
-   **Future Operational Costs:** The old unit's inefficiency and use of older refrigerant mean its running costs are higher than a modern unit's. The $6,000 replacement cost is not just for a new piece of equipment; it's also an investment in lower future utility bills and potentially higher reliability. The short remaining lifespan (2-4 years) means the amortization of the $250 repair cost is very high per year, making the long-term investment in a new unit more financially sound.

**Answer 4**
**Decision:** Repair the window.

**Reasoning:**
This decision hinges on the "impact" of non-financial factors.
-   **Financial Cost:** Replacement is more expensive upfront ($500 vs $200), though it offers energy savings that could offset the cost over time.
-   **Impact (Aesthetics & Value):** The primary consideration is the historical and aesthetic integrity of the home. Replacing one window with a modern vinyl unit creates a visual inconsistency that can detract from the property's character and potentially its market value. Preserving the original materials is often a key goal in owning a historic home.
-   **Longevity:** A properly repaired original wood window can last for decades more, so longevity is not a significant advantage for the replacement option in this context.
The framework must expand beyond simple cost-benefit to include value preservation. The negative aesthetic impact and loss of historical character from replacement outweigh the higher cost and lower energy efficiency of the repair.

**Answer 5**
**Diagnostic Flowchart Outline:**
1.  **Check Power:** Is the wall switch on? Is the circuit breaker tripped?
2.  **Attempt Manual Turn:** Turn off power. Use an Allen key on the bottom of the unit to see if the blades turn freely.
    *   **YES (Blades turn):** The issue is likely electrical (switch, wiring, or motor).
    *   **NO (Blades are stuck):** The issue is a physical jam.
3.  **Investigate Jam:** If jammed, look inside (with power off) for foreign objects (e.g., bone, silverware).
4.  **Reset Button:** If no jam is found and blades turn freely, press the reset button on the bottom of the unit and test again.

**Repair vs. Replace Recommendations:**
-   **Outcome 1: Simple Jam (e.g., bottle cap).**
    *   **Recommendation: Repair.**
    *   **Justification:** Cost is $0, effort is low (remove object). The unit is otherwise functional. Replacement is completely unnecessary. Longevity is unaffected.
-   **Outcome 2: Blades turn freely, reset button works.**
    *   **Recommendation: Repair (self-completed).**
    *   **Justification:** The unit's thermal overload was likely tripped. Cost is $0, effort is minimal. This is a designed safety feature, not a failure.
-   **Outcome 3: Seized Motor (blades do not turn, no visible jam).**
    *   **Recommendation: Replace.**
    *   **Justification:** A seized motor is a terminal failure. While technically repairable, the cost of a new motor and the high-effort labor involved would likely approach or exceed the cost of a new disposal unit ($100-$200). Longevity of a repaired motor is questionable. Replacement is more cost-effective and reliable.

**Answer 6**
**Recommendation:** Replace the old water heater with the tankless model.

**Reasoning:** This recommendation synthesizes the immediate need, long-term costs, and the client's stated values.

1.  **Analyze the "Repair" Option ($150):** A 12-year-old tank water heater is already at or beyond its typical 8-12 year lifespan. The leaking valve is a warning sign of systemic age-related failure. Repairing the valve is a short-term fix that ignores the high risk of a catastrophic tank failure (a flood) in the near future. The low upfront cost is deceptive due to the high consequential risk.

2.  **Analyze the "Equivalent Replace" Option ($800):** This is a safe, reliable choice that solves the immediate problem and eliminates the risk of a tank leak for another 8-12 years. However, it does not align with the client's stated values (environmentally conscious) or long-term housing plans. It maintains the energy inefficiency of the old unit.

3.  **Analyze the "Upgrade" Option ($2,500):**
    *   **Cost:** While the highest upfront cost, it must be evaluated over the client's 15+ year timeline. Tankless heaters are ~25-35% more energy-efficient, leading to significant annual savings on utility bills that will offset the initial investment over the life of the unit.
    *   **Longevity:** Tankless heaters have a lifespan of 20+ years, aligning perfectly with the client's plan to stay in the home. The standard tank replacement would likely need to be replaced *again* during that period.
    *   **Impact:** This option directly addresses the client's environmental values by reducing energy consumption. It also provides lifestyle benefits like endless hot water.

**Conclusion:** For this specific client, the tankless model is the superior long-term decision. It aligns their financial investment with their personal values and housing timeline, offers the best longevity, and provides the lowest total cost of ownership when energy savings are factored in over 15+ years. The simple repair is too risky, and the equivalent replacement fails to meet the client's broader goals.
