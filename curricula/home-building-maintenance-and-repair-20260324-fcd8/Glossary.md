# Glossary: home/building maintenance and repair

**Adhesion**
The ability of two different materials to stick together. Technically, it's the molecular attraction between the surfaces of two bodies in contact, causing them to resist separation, often used to describe how glues or epoxies bond materials.

**AFCI (Arc-Fault Circuit Interrupter)**
A special type of electrical safety device that helps prevent fires. It is a circuit breaker designed to detect dangerous electrical arcs (sparks) in wiring that could cause a fire and quickly disconnects power.

**Architectural Symbol**
A standardized drawing or graphic that represents a specific building component or feature on a house plan. These symbols are a universal language used in construction drawings to convey information about materials, fixtures, and structural elements.

**Beam**
A horizontal support piece in a building. It is a structural element designed to carry vertical loads, such as floors or ceilings, and transfer that weight to vertical supports like columns or walls.

**Bid (Project)**
A formal offer from a contractor outlining the cost and scope of work for a specific home improvement or repair project. It serves as a proposal detailing services, materials, and pricing for the work.

**Building Code**
Rules and standards for construction to ensure safety. These are governmental regulations that establish minimum standards for the design, construction, alteration, and maintenance of buildings, primarily to protect public health, safety, and general welfare.

**Building Envelope**
The outer shell of a home that separates the inside from the outside. This includes the roof, walls, windows, and foundation, designed to protect against weather, control heat flow, and manage air and moisture transfer.

**Circuit Breaker**
An automatic safety switch in your electrical panel that turns off power if there's a problem. It protects electrical wiring and appliances by interrupting the flow of electricity when an overload or short circuit occurs, preventing damage or fire.

**Circuit Tracing**
The process of figuring out which wires go where in your electrical system. This involves identifying the path of an electrical circuit from its power source (like a breaker) to the various outlets, switches, and loads it serves.

**Conduction**
Heat moving directly through solid materials. It's the transfer of thermal energy from one particle of matter to another through direct contact, such as heat passing through a solid wall or a hot pan handle.

**Convection**
Heat moving through liquids or gases, like air or water. It is the transfer of heat through the movement of fluids, where warmer, less dense fluid rises and cooler, denser fluid sinks, creating a circulation pattern.

**Current (Electrical)**
The flow of electricity, like water flowing through a pipe. It is the rate of flow of electric charge through a conductor, measured in Amperes (Amps), and is what powers electrical devices.

**Diagnostic Flowchart**
A step-by-step guide, usually visual, used to figure out what's wrong with something. It's a structured methodology using a series of questions and potential actions to systematically identify the root cause of a problem in a system.

**Electrical Circuit**
A complete path that electricity follows to power a device. It's a closed loop through which electric current flows from a power source, through a load (like a light bulb), and back to the source.

**Electrical System**
All the wiring, outlets, and power components that bring electricity to your home. This integrated network distributes electrical power throughout a building, from the service entrance to individual lights and appliances.

**Energy Efficiency**
Using less energy to do the same amount of work or get the same result. It's the practice of reducing the amount of energy required to provide products and services, leading to lower energy consumption and costs.

**Fastener**
A device used to join two or more things together, like a screw or nail. It is a hardware device used to mechanically connect or affix objects, relying on friction or interlocking parts to create a secure joint.

**Flashing**
Thin material, usually metal, installed to stop water from getting into your house at joints or edges. It's an impervious material integrated into the building envelope at vulnerable areas (like roof valleys, window sills) to prevent water penetration and direct moisture away.

**Foundation**
The base of your house that supports the entire structure. It's the lowest load-bearing part of a building, typically below ground level, responsible for transmitting the weight of the structure to the earth.

**GFCI (Ground-Fault Circuit Interrupter)**
A safety outlet or breaker that quickly cuts power if it detects an electrical leak. It protects against electric shock by rapidly shutting off electricity when it senses an imbalance in current flow, indicating a ground fault.

**Gravity Drainage**
The system where wastewater leaves your house by simply flowing downhill. This refers to plumbing systems where wastewater and solids move through sloped pipes, relying solely on the force of gravity to flow towards the sewer or septic system.

**Hazard (Building Maintenance)**
Anything that could potentially cause harm, injury, or damage during home repair tasks. This includes risks like electrical shocks, exposure to dangerous chemicals, or falls from ladders or elevated surfaces.

**HVAC System**
The equipment in your home that controls heating, ventilation, and air conditioning. It's a system responsible for maintaining indoor environmental comfort by managing temperature, humidity, and air quality.

**Inspection (Building)**
A formal check of a construction or repair project by an official. This is a review conducted by a local building official to ensure that work complies with established building codes, safety standards, and approved permits.

**Insulation**
Material used to slow down heat transfer, keeping your home warmer in winter and cooler in summer. It's a material designed to reduce the flow of heat (and often sound) between areas, enhancing thermal comfort and energy efficiency.

**Joist**
Horizontal beams that support floors or ceilings. These are parallel structural timbers or steel beams, typically spaced at regular intervals, that form the framing for floors and ceilings, transferring loads to larger beams or walls.

**Level (Horizontal)**
Perfectly flat or straight across, without any slope up or down. A term indicating that a surface or line is perfectly parallel to the horizontal plane, typically verified using a spirit level or laser level.

**Load (Electrical)**
Any device or appliance that uses electricity. It is any component connected to an electrical circuit that consumes electrical energy to perform work, produce heat, or emit light.

**Load Balancing (Electrical)**
Distributing the electrical devices in your home evenly among your circuits. This practice involves dividing electrical loads as equally as possible across the different phases or circuits within an electrical panel to prevent overloads and ensure stable power delivery.

**Lumber**
Wood that has been cut and shaped into boards or beams for building. It is processed wood material used in construction, typically sold as planks, boards, or structural timbers.

**Main Shut-off Valve**
The primary handle or lever that completely turns off the water or gas supply to your entire home. It's the principal valve that controls the flow of a utility (water or gas) into a building, allowing for full disconnection.

**Mechanical Connection**
Joining materials together using physical fasteners or interlocking parts. This method relies on devices like screws, bolts, or nails, or on the physical interlocking of components, to create a strong and durable joint.

**Mitigation (Disaster)**
Actions taken to reduce the impact or likelihood of a disaster. This involves strategies and measures implemented before an emergency to lessen its potential severity, damage, or disruption to a home.

**Open Circuit**
A break in an electrical path, preventing electricity from flowing. It's a condition where an electrical circuit is incomplete, typically due to a disconnected wire, a faulty switch, or a blown fuse, stopping the flow of current.

**P-trap**
A curved section of pipe under a sink or drain that holds water. This water creates a seal to prevent unpleasant sewer gases from entering your home while allowing wastewater to pass through.

**Permit (Building)**
Official permission from your local government to start a construction or renovation project. It's an authorization issued by a municipal or county authority that allows a property owner or contractor to proceed with a project, ensuring compliance with local codes.

**Personal Protective Equipment (PPE)**
Special gear worn to protect yourself from harm during work. This includes items like safety glasses, gloves, dust masks, or hard hats, designed to protect an individual from health and safety hazards.

**Plumb (Vertical)**
Perfectly straight up and down, without any lean. A term indicating that a surface or line is perfectly perpendicular to the horizontal plane, often checked with a plumb bob or a level.

**Plumbing System**
All the pipes, fixtures, and appliances that bring water into your home and take wastewater away. This network includes supply lines for fresh water and drainage lines for wastewater, along with all connected fixtures.

**Plumbing Vent**
A pipe that allows air into your drainage system. It's a crucial part of the drainage system that equalizes air pressure, preventing water from being siphoned out of traps and allowing sewer gases to safely escape through the roof.

**Preventive Maintenance**
Regular, planned care for your home and its systems to stop problems before they start. It involves routine inspections, cleaning, and upkeep performed on equipment or systems to prevent breakdowns, extend lifespan, and avoid costly reactive repairs.

**Radiation**
Heat moving through space as invisible waves, like the warmth from the sun or a campfire. It's the transfer of heat through electromagnetic waves, which does not require direct contact or a medium for transfer.

**Refrigeration Cycle**
The process a refrigerator uses to make things cold by moving heat. It's a thermodynamic process involving compression, condensation, expansion, and evaporation of a refrigerant to transfer heat from a cooler space to a warmer one.

**Remediation (Water Damage)**
The process of cleaning up, repairing, and restoring an area after it has been damaged by water. This includes drying out affected areas, removing damaged materials, preventing mold growth, and rebuilding.

**Resistance (Electrical)**
How much a material opposes the flow of electricity, which generates heat. It's the opposition to the flow of electric current in a circuit, measured in Ohms (Ω), converting electrical energy into heat.

**ROI (Return on Investment)**
A way to measure the financial benefit of an expense or upgrade. It's a performance metric that evaluates the efficiency of an investment by comparing the gain or loss from an investment relative to its cost.

**Roof Truss**
A pre-built framework of connected timbers, usually triangular, that supports your roof. It's a prefabricated structural component, engineered to efficiently distribute the weight and loads of a roof across its members to the walls.

**Scope of Work**
A detailed description of everything that will be done in a project. It defines the tasks, deliverables, and responsibilities, clearly outlining the boundaries and expectations for a specific construction or repair job.

**Short Circuit**
When electricity takes an unintended, easy path and creates a sudden surge, often causing a spark or tripping a breaker. It's an abnormal connection between two points in an electrical circuit that should be at different voltages, resulting in excessively high current flow.

**Siphoning (in plumbing)**
When water is sucked out of a drain trap due to negative pressure, allowing sewer gas to enter. This unintentional emptying of a P-trap's water seal can occur if there's inadequate venting in the plumbing drainage system.

**Structural Integrity**
The overall strength and stability of a building. It refers to the ability of a structure and its components to withstand applied loads, forces, and stresses without failing, breaking, or deforming excessively.

**Structural System**
The main framework of your house that holds it up. This includes the foundation, load-bearing walls, columns, beams, and roof framing, which together provide stability and support the entire weight of the building.

**Stud (Wall)**
A vertical wooden or metal beam that forms the frame of a wall. These are key framing members that extend from the bottom to the top plate of a wall, supporting its structure and carrying loads from floors and the roof.

**Vapor Barrier**
A material designed to stop moisture vapor from passing through walls or ceilings. It's a thin sheet or coating that restricts the passage of water vapor through building assemblies, preventing condensation within walls and insulation.

**Vent Stack**
The main vertical pipe in your plumbing system that goes through the roof. It provides fresh air to the drainage system, allowing sewer gases to escape and helping ensure proper wastewater flow.

**Voltage**
The "push" or force of electricity, similar to water pressure in a pipe. It's the electrical potential difference between two points, driving electric current through a circuit and measured in Volts (V).

**Waste Line**
The pipes that carry dirty water and sewage away from your sinks, toilets, and other fixtures. These are the drainage pipes that transport wastewater and solid waste from plumbing fixtures to the main sewer line or septic system.

**Water Intrusion**
When unwanted water gets into your house, often through leaks in the roof, walls, or foundation. It refers to the entry of water into a building structure, which can lead to damage, mold growth, and compromised structural integrity.

**Water Pressure**
The force at which water flows out of your faucets. It's the force per unit area exerted by water, typically measured in pounds per square inch (PSI), which determines the strength and volume of water delivery from fixtures.
