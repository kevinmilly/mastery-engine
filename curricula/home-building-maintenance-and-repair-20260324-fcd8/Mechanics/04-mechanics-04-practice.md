## Exercises

**Exercise 1**
A homeowner reports that their living room, which has a large, single-pane window, feels much colder than the rest of the house in winter, even when the thermostat is set to 70°F (21°C). Identify the three modes of heat transfer responsible for this heat loss through the window and describe how each one contributes to the problem.

**Exercise 2**
A homeowner's furnace is running, and the air coming out of the vents in most of the house is warm. However, one bedroom remains cold. Upon inspection, you find a large bookcase has been pushed directly against the supply vent in that room. Explain, using the principle of convection, why blocking this vent prevents the room from heating up effectively.

**Exercise 3**
Two identical houses are built next to each other in a hot, sunny climate. House A has a dark-shingled roof and small, clogged soffit and ridge vents. House B has a light-colored metal roof and large, clear soffit and ridge vents. Which house's air conditioning system will have to work harder to maintain a cool indoor temperature? Analyze the situation using the principles of radiation and convection to justify your answer.

**Exercise 4**
In a two-story home during the summer, the air conditioning is running, but the upstairs is consistently too warm while the downstairs is uncomfortably cold. The HVAC system has been checked and is functioning correctly. Using your knowledge of air movement and heat transfer, explain the physical principle that is most likely causing this temperature imbalance and suggest a change to the system's operation (not equipment replacement) that could help mitigate it.

**Exercise 5**
A customer calls because the circuit breaker for their outdoor air conditioning unit trips repeatedly on hot afternoons. You observe that the condenser coils on the unit are heavily caked with dirt and cottonwood seeds. Connecting what you know about heat transfer with your knowledge of electrical loads, explain the chain of events that leads from the dirty coils to the tripped breaker.

**Exercise 6**
A homeowner is planning a renovation to create an open-concept living area by removing a central wall on the first floor of their two-story house. This wall is not load-bearing, but it contains a large return air grille and the main return air duct that serves the entire first floor. As a maintenance technician, what two distinct problems—one related to HVAC performance and one related to occupant comfort—would you advise the homeowner this change will likely cause? Explain your reasoning for each.

## Answer Key

**Answer 1**
The three modes of heat transfer all contribute to the heat loss through the window:
1.  **Conduction:** The indoor air heats the interior surface of the glass. This thermal energy is then conducted directly through the solid glass pane to the colder outer surface. Single-pane glass is a poor insulator and a relatively good conductor, so this process happens quickly.
2.  **Convection:** Warm air inside the room circulates and comes into contact with the cold interior surface of the glass. The air transfers its heat to the glass, cools, becomes denser, and sinks. This creates a continuous convective loop, constantly bringing more warm air to the window to lose its heat. A similar process happens on the outside, where wind carries heat away from the outer surface of the glass.
3.  **Radiation:** Warm objects in the room, including people, furniture, and walls, radiate thermal energy. The glass is transparent to some of this infrared radiation, allowing it to pass directly outside. The interior surface of the glass itself also radiates heat to colder objects outside.

**Answer 2**
The furnace heats the room primarily through **forced convection**. The system's blower fan forces heated air through the ductwork and out the supply vent into the room. This warm air then circulates, mixing with the cooler air already present and transferring heat until the entire volume of air reaches the desired temperature.

By blocking the supply vent with a bookcase, the homeowner has stopped this process. The warm air cannot enter the room to displace or mix with the cold air. The room is therefore isolated from the HVAC system's convective heating loop, and it will only warm up very slowly through conduction from adjacent rooms, which is a much less effective method of heat transfer for this purpose.

**Answer 3**
The air conditioning system in **House A** will have to work significantly harder.

Here's the analysis based on heat transfer principles:
1.  **Radiation:** The dark-shingled roof of House A will absorb a much larger amount of solar radiation compared to the light-colored metal roof of House B, which will reflect more of it. This absorbed radiation heats the roof materials to a very high temperature. The hot roofing then radiates this heat downward into the attic space.
2.  **Convection:** The hot air that builds up in the attic needs to be vented out and replaced with cooler, ambient air from outside. In House A, the small, clogged vents severely restrict this natural convective airflow. The attic becomes a super-heated, stagnant pocket of air. In House B, the large, clear vents allow for robust airflow, enabling convection to continuously remove the hot air generated by solar radiation, keeping the attic temperature much closer to the outside air temperature.

The combination of high radiative heat gain and poor convective heat removal in House A's attic creates an extremely hot buffer zone above the living space. This intense heat then conducts through the ceiling insulation and into the conditioned rooms, creating a much larger cooling load for the AC system.

**Answer 4**
The principle causing the temperature imbalance is **natural convection**, also known as the "stack effect."
*   **Explanation:** Cool, conditioned air supplied by the HVAC system is denser than warm air. It naturally sinks and pools on the lower level of the house. Conversely, warm air (from solar gain through windows, heat from occupants and appliances, etc.) is less dense and naturally rises. In a two-story house, this creates a cycle where the cool air settles downstairs and the warm air accumulates upstairs, overwhelming the cooling being supplied to the second floor.
*   **Suggested Change:** A simple operational change is to run the HVAC system's fan continuously (switching the thermostat's fan setting from "Auto" to "On"). This forces constant air circulation throughout the entire house, even when the AC compressor isn't running. The fan will mix the air, pulling the cold air from downstairs and circulating it upstairs, and vice-versa, which helps to break up the thermal stratification and create a more even temperature between the floors.

**Answer 5**
This problem connects a failure in heat transfer directly to an electrical overload. Here is the chain of events:
1.  **Reduced Heat Transfer:** The outdoor condenser unit's job is to release heat from the refrigerant into the outside air. This is a form of convection. The dirt and seeds caked on the coils act as an insulator, preventing air from flowing freely across the fins and drastically reducing the system's ability to transfer heat.
2.  **Increased Refrigerant Pressure & Temperature:** Because the heat cannot be effectively released, the refrigerant in the coils remains hot. This causes the pressure within the system to rise far above its normal operating level.
3.  **Compressor Overload:** The compressor is the pump that circulates the refrigerant. Pumping against this extremely high pressure requires the compressor motor to work much harder than it was designed to.
4.  **Increased Electrical Load:** A motor working harder draws more electrical current (amps). This increased current draw exceeds the rating of the circuit breaker.
5.  **Tripped Breaker:** The circuit breaker is a safety device designed to interrupt the circuit when it detects excessive current. It trips to protect the compressor motor and the house wiring from overheating and damage.

**Answer 6**
Removing the wall with the central return air duct would cause two significant problems:

1.  **HVAC Performance Problem:** The return air duct is critical for proper airflow circulation. It draws air from the conditioned space back to the air handler to be re-heated or re-cooled. Removing the main return for the first floor would effectively "starve" the system of air. This reduces the total airflow across the furnace's heat exchanger or the AC's evaporator coil. Reduced airflow can cause an AC coil to freeze into a block of ice or a furnace heat exchanger to overheat and crack, leading to major equipment failure. The system will run inefficiently and struggle to heat or cool the house.

2.  **Occupant Comfort Problem:** A centrally located return helps create a proper circulation pattern in a large space. It pulls air from across the rooms towards the center, promoting mixing and preventing stagnant, uncomfortable pockets of hot or cold air. By removing it, the air circulation pattern will be destroyed. The supply vents will blow conditioned air into the new, large room, but without a clear path back to the system, the air will stratify. This will likely result in some areas being drafty and cold while other areas far from the supply vents remain stuffy and hot (or cold in winter), leading to a significant decrease in overall comfort.
