## Capstone: Mechanics — home/building maintenance and repair

### The Scenario
Your friend, Alex, has just purchased their first home—a 1970s bungalow—and is feeling a bit overwhelmed. The house was sold "as-is," and after living in it for a week, Alex has discovered several issues and has asked you, as someone who understands how houses work, to provide an initial assessment. They aren't asking you to fix anything, but rather to help them understand what’s going on, what the potential connections are between the problems, and what they should worry about first.

Here is the list of issues Alex has described to you:
- **Plumbing:** When the upstairs shower is used, the kitchen sink on the floor below makes a loud gurgling sound. The shower itself also drains very slowly, with water pooling around the drain for several minutes after the faucet is turned off.
- **Electrical:** There is an old, secondary refrigerator in the finished basement that is plugged into a two-prong (ungrounded) outlet. The basement has a persistent musty smell, and the concrete floor near the refrigerator often feels damp. This refrigerator has tripped its circuit breaker twice in the past week.
- **Structural/Envelope:** The door to the main bedroom on the second floor sticks badly at the top, making it difficult to close. Looking closely, you see a faint, brownish water stain on the ceiling right next to the top of the door frame. The wooden window frame in that same bedroom, located on the wall adjacent to the sticking door, feels soft and spongy to the touch at the sill.

Alex wants you to compile your thoughts into an "Initial Assessment & Prioritization Report" to help them make sense of the situation and plan their next steps with contractors.

### Your Tasks
1.  **Plumbing System Analysis:** Analyze the interconnected plumbing symptoms (gurgling kitchen sink, slow-draining upstairs shower). Based on the principles of drainage, vents, and traps, explain the most probable mechanical cause for *both* of these issues occurring simultaneously. Create a simple diagram of the likely Drain-Waste-Vent (DWV) line configuration to illustrate your explanation.

2.  **Basement Risk Evaluation:** Evaluate the electrical situation in the basement involving the old refrigerator, the two-prong outlet, and the tripping breaker in a damp environment. Identify three distinct types of hazards present, and for each hazard, explain the underlying principle (related to the circuit, the appliance, and/or the environment) that makes it a safety concern.

3.  **Root Cause Diagnostic Plan:** The sticking door, ceiling stain, and rotting window frame are likely related. Propose a step-by-step diagnostic plan to determine the root cause of these interconnected issues. For each step, state what you would look for and how the finding would confirm or rule out a specific failure mechanism related to the building envelope or structural support system.

4.  **Repair Prioritization:** Based on your analyses, create a prioritized list of the three main problem areas you've identified (Plumbing, Electrical, and Structural/Envelope). Justify your ranking by explaining the potential consequences of leaving each issue unaddressed, considering immediate safety, the potential for further damage, and cost escalation over time.

5.  **Durable Repair Strategy:** Assume your diagnostic plan from Task 3 confirms that water intrusion at the bedroom window is the primary cause of the damage. Recommend a strategy for installing a replacement window. Justify your choice of one type of mechanical fastener (e.g., screws, nails) and one type of sealant/flashing material by explaining how they address the specific forces and environmental exposures (moisture, material expansion, UV degradation) relevant to that location in the building envelope.

### What Good Work Looks Like
*   Demonstrates the ability to connect seemingly separate symptoms (e.g., a gurgle and a slow drain) to a single, underlying system-level cause, citing the relevant physical principles.
*   Effectively distinguishes between a component failure (what is broken) and the resulting safety hazard (why it is dangerous), using correct terminology for electrical and structural principles.
*   Constructs a logical and efficient diagnostic sequence, where each step is designed to isolate variables and test a specific hypothesis about the interaction between different building systems.
*   Provides clear, well-reasoned justifications for prioritizing repairs, weighing factors like immediate safety risks, the potential for cascading damage, and the principles of material degradation over time.
*   Applies theoretical knowledge of materials science and mechanical fastening to a practical application, justifying choices based on an analysis of environmental loads and long-term durability.
