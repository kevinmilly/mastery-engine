## Exercises

**Exercise 1**
A homeowner living in a three-story townhome complains that the shower on the third floor has noticeably weaker flow than the utility sink in the basement. All the water pipes in the home are the same diameter. Based on the principles of water pressure, explain this difference.

**Exercise 2**
After running the bathroom sink for about 30 seconds, you hear a "glug-glug" or gurgling sound coming from the drain as the last bit of water empties. What plumbing principle is failing to function correctly, and what does this sound indicate about the drain system?

**Exercise 3**
A homeowner replaces their standard kitchen faucet with a new, water-efficient model. Immediately after, they complain of "low pressure" at that sink, even though all other faucets in the house work perfectly. Explain why this is likely a flow rate issue, not a pressure issue, and identify the component responsible.

**Exercise 4**
Whenever the upstairs washing machine drains, the water level in the nearby toilet bowl drops significantly, sometimes making a gurgling sound. The toilet and washing machine are in separate rooms but are close to each other. Using the principles of a Drain-Waste-Vent (DWV) system, explain the most probable cause of this interaction.

**Exercise 5**
A homeowner is planning to add a utility sink in a backyard workshop. The workshop's floor is 2 feet *higher* than the point where the house's main sewer line exits the foundation. Using the principles of drainage, identify the primary challenge this elevation difference creates and propose a type of system that would solve it, explaining why it's necessary.

**Exercise 6**
A house on a municipal water supply suddenly experiences very low water pressure at *every* fixture, both hot and cold. The homeowner confirms with their next-door neighbor that the neighbor's pressure is normal. Propose a logical, three-step diagnostic sequence to identify the source of the problem. For each step, state what you would check and why it's in that order.

---

## Answer Key

**Answer 1**
The difference in flow is caused by "head loss" or the effect of gravity on water pressure. Water pressure supplied to the house has to push the water vertically to reach the upper floors. For every foot of elevation gained, some pressure is lost. Therefore, the faucet at the lowest point (the basement sink) will have the highest pressure, while the fixture at the highest point (the third-floor shower) will have the lowest effective pressure, resulting in weaker flow.

**Answer 2**
The principle failing is proper venting. The gurgling sound indicates that the drain's vent is partially or fully blocked. A vent's purpose is to allow air to enter the pipe behind the draining water, preventing a vacuum. When the vent is blocked, the rushing water creates a vacuum, which then sucks air from the easiest place it can: through the sink's P-trap. The "glug-glug" sound is the sound of air being pulled through the water seal in the trap.

**Answer 3**
This is a flow rate issue, not a pressure issue. Because all other faucets are fine, the home's overall water pressure is not the problem. The responsible component is the aerator on the new, water-efficient faucet. Aerators work by restricting the volume of water (the flow rate) and mixing it with air. This uses less water but can feel like "low pressure" to the user. The static pressure in the pipe is unchanged, but the amount of water exiting the faucet per minute has been intentionally reduced by design.

**Answer 4**
The most probable cause is a blocked or inadequate vent serving that section of the plumbing. A washing machine dumps a large volume of water into the drainpipe at once, creating a powerful negative pressure (suction) behind it. A properly functioning vent would allow air to rush in and neutralize this vacuum. Since the vent is blocked, the vacuum pulls air from the next closest source, which is the toilet's P-trap. This suction siphons water out of the toilet bowl, causing the level to drop and the gurgling sound.

**Answer 5**
The primary challenge is that a standard, gravity-fed drainage system will not work. Drains rely on a downward slope for gravity to carry waste away to the sewer line. Since the workshop sink is higher than the sewer connection point, waste cannot flow "uphill" on its own.
The necessary solution is a sewage ejector pump (or "lift station"). This system uses a basin to collect the wastewater from the sink, and when the basin fills to a certain level, a pump automatically turns on, creating pressure to force the waste up and over to the main sewer line.

**Answer 6**
A logical three-step sequence would proceed from the easiest and most common issues to the more complex, moving from the point of entry inward.

1.  **Step 1: Check the Main Water Shutoff Valve.** I would first locate the main shutoff valve inside the house (usually near the water meter) and confirm that it is 100% open. A partially closed valve is a major restriction and the simplest explanation for a sudden, whole-house pressure drop.
2.  **Step 2: Inspect the Pressure Reducing Valve (PRV).** If the main valve is fully open, the next likely culprit is a failed PRV. This device, usually located right after the main valve, can fail and get stuck in a nearly-closed position. This would reduce pressure to the entire house. Since the neighbor's pressure is fine, the problem is isolated to this property, making the PRV a strong suspect.
3.  **Step 3: Suspect a Clog in the Main Supply Line.** If both the main valve and PRV are fine, the final possibility on the property is a major obstruction in the underground supply line running between the municipal connection at the street and the house. While less common to occur "suddenly," this is the last component that could affect the entire house before the water lines branch out.
