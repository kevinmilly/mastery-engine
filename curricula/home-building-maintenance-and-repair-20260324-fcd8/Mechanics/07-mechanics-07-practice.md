## Exercises

**Exercise 1**
A refrigerator's compressor is running constantly, but the inside is not getting cold. Based on the principle of heat exchange in the refrigeration cycle, what is one of the first physical things you should check on the exterior of the unit, and why would this cause the described problem?

**Exercise 2**
A homeowner reports that their hot water is only lukewarm, not hot. If they have a standard dual-element electric water heater, what is a likely component failure? Conversely, if they have a gas water heater with a working pilot light, what is a likely control-related cause for the same symptom? Explain your reasoning for each case.

**Exercise 3**
You are troubleshooting a refrigerator that is warm inside and completely silent (no compressor hum). You turn the temperature dial from 'off' to the coldest setting and hear a distinct "click," but the compressor does not start. What does this click likely indicate about the state of the thermostat, and what does the compressor's failure to start suggest about the location of the problem in the system?

**Exercise 4**
A client reports a loud popping or rumbling noise coming from their water heater tank whenever it is actively heating. The water temperature at the tap is acceptably hot. What physical process inside the tank is the most likely cause of this sound, and how does it create the noise?

**Exercise 5**
You encounter a refrigerator where the interior light turns on when the door is opened, but the compressor and condenser fan never run, and the unit is warm. Drawing on your knowledge of electrical circuits, why does the working light effectively rule out a problem with the main power cord or the wall outlet? Given this, what part of the refrigerator's internal cooling circuit should you investigate first?

**Exercise 6**
A homeowner with a gas water heater located in their garage reports that their pilot light repeatedly goes out, but only on very windy days. They can relight it, but it won't stay lit until the wind dies down. The thermocouple was replaced last year and is functioning correctly. Integrating your knowledge of water heater operation with the principles of air movement and the building envelope, propose a plausible external cause for this issue and explain the mechanism by which it could extinguish the pilot light.

## Answer Key

**Answer 1**
You should check the condenser coils, which are typically on the back or bottom of the refrigerator.

**Reasoning:** The refrigeration cycle works by moving heat from inside the refrigerator to the outside. The condenser coils are where the compressed, hot refrigerant gas releases its heat to the surrounding air. If these coils are covered in a thick layer of dust, pet hair, or are otherwise obstructed, they cannot dissipate heat effectively. The system will run continuously trying to reach the set temperature, but because it cannot get rid of the heat it has extracted, the overall cooling performance will be poor or nonexistent.

**Answer 2**
**Electric Water Heater:** The most likely failure is the upper heating element. In a dual-element system, the upper element heats the top portion of the tank first. Only after the top is hot does power switch to the lower element to heat the rest. If the upper element fails, only the lower element will ever turn on, resulting in a tank that is only partially heated from the bottom up, producing lukewarm water at the tap.

**Gas Water Heater:** A likely cause is a miscalibrated or failing thermostat/gas control valve. The pilot light is on, indicating there is gas flow and the safety system is operational. However, if the thermostat isn't correctly sensing the water temperature or is unable to properly open the main gas valve, it won't call for the main burner to ignite and heat the water to the set temperature, leaving it lukewarm.

**Answer 3**
The "click" indicates that the thermostat is likely working. It is sensing that the internal temperature is above its set point and is closing the switch to send power to the compressor circuit.

The problem, therefore, is likely "downstream" from the thermostat. The failure to start suggests an issue with the components that directly run the compressor, such as the start relay, the overload protector, or the compressor motor itself. The thermostat has done its job of calling for cooling, but the mechanical/electrical system responsible for starting the motor has failed.

**Answer 4**
The most likely cause is sediment (mineral scale) buildup at the bottom of the tank.

**Reasoning:** Over time, minerals from the water supply settle at the bottom of the tank, creating a hard layer of scale over the bottom surface, which is directly above the gas burner. When the burner fires, it heats this layer of sediment. Water trapped beneath the scale is superheated into steam bubbles. These bubbles then violently erupt through the sediment layer, creating the "popping" or "rumbling" sound as they collapse. This process is inefficient but can still produce hot water, explaining why the symptom is noise rather than a lack of heat.

**Answer 5**
The working light rules out the power cord and outlet because the light and the cooling system share the same main power source. If there were no power coming into the refrigerator at all, nothing would work.

**Reasoning:** Inside an appliance like a refrigerator, power is often split into different circuits. The light is on a simple circuit controlled by the door switch. The cooling system (compressor, fans) is on a separate, more complex circuit controlled by the thermostat and defrost timer. Since the light has power, we know the main electrical pathway is intact. The fault must be within the dedicated cooling circuit. The first place to investigate would be the cold control thermostat, as it is the primary switch that sends power to the rest of the cooling components.

**Answer 6**
A plausible external cause is negative air pressure in the garage combined with a poor flue draft.

**Reasoning:** A gas water heater needs a consistent supply of combustion air and must be able to safely vent exhaust gases up its flue. On windy days, air moving over the roof can create a low-pressure zone, which can cause "downdrafting" — pushing exhaust gases and outside air back down the flue pipe. Furthermore, if the garage is well-sealed, other exhaust fans in the house (like a kitchen or bathroom fan) can create negative pressure inside, pulling air from any available opening, including the water heater's flue. This downdraft can be strong enough to blow out the small, sensitive pilot light flame, even if the thermocouple is working perfectly. The problem is not with the heater's components, but with the air pressure dynamics affecting its ability to draft correctly.
