## Exercises

**Exercise 1**
A homeowner notices a distinct sewer-gas smell coming from the drain of a guest shower that has not been used for several months. The drain is not clogged and appears clean from the outside. What is the most likely mechanical failure in the drainage system causing this odor, and what simple action will likely resolve it immediately?

**Exercise 2**
During a renovation, a new horizontal drain line for a washing machine is installed. The pipe runs 8 feet from the machine to the main stack. Due to a measurement error, the pipe is installed with a "negative" or "reverse" pitch, meaning the end connecting to the stack is slightly higher than the end connected to the machine's drain. What will be the immediate and long-term consequences of this installation flaw?

**Exercise 3**
A family lives in a two-story house. Whenever they drain a full bathtub on the second floor, they hear loud gurgling noises from the first-floor powder room sink, which is located directly below the bathroom. The sink itself drains normally when used. What is the most probable cause of this cross-fixture symptom, and why does draining a large volume of water like a tub trigger it?

**Exercise 4**
A homeowner is experiencing a slow-draining kitchen sink. They suspect a clog in the P-trap, so they disassemble and thoroughly clean it, finding only a small amount of debris. After reassembly, the sink still drains just as slowly. What does this result indicate about the location of the actual blockage, and why was the P-trap not the source of the problem?

**Exercise 5**
Following a week of heavy, windy rain, a homeowner reports that all the drains in their house have suddenly started draining slowly and making gurgling sounds, and there is a faint sewer odor. This is a new problem affecting all fixtures simultaneously. Integrating your knowledge of the drainage system's components, which part of the system is most likely obstructed, and how could the weather have caused this specific obstruction?

**Exercise 6**
A homeowner wants to add a utility sink in their basement, 15 feet away from the main waste stack. The concrete floor makes it impossible to install a traditional gravity-fed drain with the proper 1/4-inch-per-foot pitch without extensive trenching. The homeowner proposes running the drainpipe with a very shallow pitch (almost level) to avoid breaking up the concrete. Based on your knowledge of drainage mechanics and potential water damage (related to the building envelope), evaluate the risk of this proposal and explain why an alternative solution, such as a macerating or up-flush toilet system with an integrated pump, would be a mechanically sounder choice.

---

## Answer Key

**Answer 1**
The most likely cause is that the water in the P-trap has evaporated over time. The P-trap is designed to hold a plug of water that acts as a seal, physically blocking sewer gases from rising up through the drain. In a rarely used fixture, this water seal can evaporate completely, leaving an open path for odors.

The immediate remedy is to run water into the shower drain for a minute or two. This will refill the trap, re-establish the water seal, and block the sewer gas.

**Answer 2**
The reverse pitch will cause both immediate and long-term problems.
*   **Immediate Consequence:** Water will not drain completely from the pipe. A pool of stagnant water will remain in the horizontal pipe after the washing machine finishes its cycle because gravity cannot pull it "uphill" to the stack.
*   **Long-Term Consequence:** Solid waste, such as lint and detergent residue, will settle out of the stagnant water and collect at the low point of the pipe. Over time, this will build up and create a serious clog that is difficult to clear. This will also lead to persistent odors from the decomposing, trapped waste.

**Answer 3**
The most probable cause is a partial or fully blocked vent stack. The vent stack's purpose is to allow air to enter the drainage system to replace the volume of water flowing out. When a large volume of water (from the tub) rushes down the main stack, it creates a powerful vacuum behind it. A functioning vent would supply air to neutralize this vacuum. Since the vent is blocked, the system desperately tries to pull air from elsewhere. The gurgling sound is the vacuum siphoning air through the water in the powder room sink's P-trap, which is the path of least resistance. This can also pull enough water out of the trap to compromise its seal.

**Answer 4**
The result indicates that the clog is located further downstream in the waste line, *after* the P-trap. The P-trap is only designed to block sewer gas and catch small, heavy objects (like rings); it is not the common location for large, built-up clogs from grease or food waste. Since the slow draining persists even with a clean trap, the restriction must be in the section of pipe between the trap and the main vertical stack. The water is successfully passing through the trap but is then backing up from the downstream blockage.

**Answer 5**
The problem is almost certainly a blocked main vent stack opening on the roof. The fact that all drains are affected simultaneously points to a system-wide issue, not individual clogs. During a windy, rainy storm, debris like leaves, twigs, or even an animal's nest can be blown into the vent pipe's opening, creating a blockage. This obstruction prevents air from entering the system, leading to the same symptoms as a clog: slow drains, gurgling, and negative pressure that can siphon water from P-traps, allowing sewer gas to enter the home.

**Answer 6**
The homeowner's proposal to use a nearly level drainpipe is extremely risky.
*   **Risk Evaluation:** A shallow pitch is insufficient for gravity to effectively move solid waste along with the water. The water will drain (very slowly), but solids will be left behind, inevitably leading to a severe, recurring clog in the middle of the 15-foot run. A clog inside a pipe under a concrete slab is a major and expensive repair. Furthermore, any backup could lead to wastewater seeping into the foundation, creating moisture problems related to the building envelope.

*   **Mechanically Sounder Alternative:** An up-flush or macerating system is a better choice because it does not rely on gravity for horizontal transport. The system uses a grinder/macerator to liquefy solid waste and a pump to actively eject the slurry under pressure through a smaller-diameter pipe to the main stack. This overcomes the lack of pitch and ensures waste is effectively removed, preventing the clogs and potential damage inherent in the homeowner's proposal.
