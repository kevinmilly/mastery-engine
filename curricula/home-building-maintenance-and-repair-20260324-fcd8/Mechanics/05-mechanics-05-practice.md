## Exercises

**Exercise 1**
On a cold winter morning, a homeowner notices a significant amount of condensation, and even some frost, on the interior surface of their double-pane aluminum-framed windows. The home's interior humidity is at a normal level. What specific failure of the building envelope does this indicate, and which two barrier functions are compromised?

**Exercise 2**
A contractor is replacing a section of vinyl siding. After removing the old siding, they find the wood sheathing is damp and shows signs of early rot, even though the siding itself has no cracks or holes. The damage is concentrated around the bottom corners of a window located above the damaged area. What component of the water-resistive barrier system was likely installed incorrectly or is missing?

**Exercise 3**
A homeowner is renovating their attic in a cold climate. They plan to add a thick layer of fiberglass batt insulation between the ceiling joists. To save money, they are considering using an un-faced insulation (no paper or foil backing) and not adding any separate membrane. Explain the specific risk this approach creates for the attic's roof structure during the winter.

**Exercise 4**
A house has a vented attic. During a winter inspection, an energy auditor notes that the insulation near the eaves (the edges of the roof) is dirty and discolored, and there are signs of ice dams on the roof edge outside. The insulation in the middle of the attic is clean. What is likely causing this specific pattern of discoloration, and how does it relate to the home's air and thermal barriers?

**Exercise 5**
A homeowner decides to install a new bathroom exhaust fan and vents it through a soffit (the underside of the roof overhang). Considering the principles of *Heat Transfer & Air Movement* and the building envelope, explain how this seemingly logical venting choice can lead to moisture problems in the attic and degradation of the roof structure.

**Exercise 6**
A new, tightly-sealed house is built with a high-efficiency furnace located in the basement. The furnace combustion air is supplied by a pipe running directly outside. However, the home's gas-fired water heater is an older, "atmospherically vented" model that draws its combustion air from the basement itself. Based on your knowledge of the building envelope and *The Electrical Circuit*, what dangerous situation can occur if the homeowner runs the clothes dryer and the kitchen exhaust fan at the same time?

---

## Answer Key

**Answer 1**
The frost indicates a failure in the **thermal barrier**. The aluminum window frame is acting as a **thermal bridge**, conducting heat from the inside of the house to the cold outside air much more efficiently than the insulated glass or walls.

The two compromised barrier functions are:
1.  **Thermal Barrier:** The window assembly is not effectively stopping heat transfer.
2.  **Vapor Barrier (at that specific surface):** As the warm, moist interior air comes into contact with the cold window surface (which is below the dew point), the water vapor in the air condenses into liquid water or frost. While the home's main vapor barrier might be intact, the cold surface of the window creates a localized failure point for moisture management.

**Answer 2**
The component that was likely installed incorrectly or is missing is the **window flashing**. Flashing is a thin, impervious material used to direct water away from openings and joints. It should be integrated with the house wrap (the main water-resistive barrier) in a shingle-like fashion, where upper layers overlap lower layers. The damage at the corners suggests that water entering around the window frame was not properly directed over the top of the siding below, allowing it to get behind the siding and saturate the sheathing.

**Answer 3**
The risk is **condensation within the attic**. Warm, moist air from the living space below will move through the ceiling and the permeable fiberglass insulation. When this water vapor hits the cold underside of the roof deck in the winter, it will condense into liquid water or frost. This moisture can cause the roof sheathing to rot, encourage mold growth, and reduce the effectiveness of the insulation. A **vapor barrier** (like the paper/foil facing on insulation or a separate polyethylene sheet) should be installed on the warm side (the ceiling side) of the insulation to prevent interior water vapor from entering the cold attic assembly.

**Answer 4**
The discoloration is caused by **air leakage** from the heated part of the house into the attic. The dirty appearance comes from the insulation acting as a filter, trapping dust and particulates from the air that is passing through it. This indicates that the **air barrier** at the top of the walls (the top plate) and at the ceiling-to-wall transition is incomplete.

This air leak also compromises the **thermal barrier**. The warm air escaping into the attic heats the underside of the roof deck. This heat melts snow on the roof, which then runs down and refreezes at the colder eaves, forming ice dams.

**Answer 5**
This installation creates a moisture loop. The soffits are designed to be air *intakes* for attic ventilation.
1.  **Air Movement:** When the fan pushes warm, moist bathroom air out into the soffit, the natural attic ventilation (driven by convection or wind) will immediately draw that same humid air back into the attic through the soffit vents.
2.  **Heat Transfer & Condensation:** During colder months, this warm, moist air will come into contact with the cold surfaces inside the attic, such as the roof sheathing, trusses, and insulation. The moisture will condense, leading to rot, mold, and reduced insulation performance.
Proper venting requires exhausting the air directly to the exterior through the roof or a gable wall, away from any attic intake vents.

**Answer 6**
The dangerous situation is the **backdrafting of the water heater**.
1.  **Building Envelope:** The house is tightly sealed, meaning there are few natural paths for makeup air to enter when interior air is exhausted.
2.  **Air Movement:** The clothes dryer and kitchen exhaust fan are powerful appliances that push a large volume of air out of the house. Running them simultaneously creates significant **negative pressure** inside the home.
3.  **System Interaction:** The atmospherically vented water heater relies on natural buoyancy (hot air rising) to send its exhaust gases (including dangerous carbon monoxide) up its flue pipe and outside. When the house is under strong negative pressure, the path of least resistance for makeup air is *down* the water heater's flue. This reverse flow will pull the toxic exhaust gases back into the basement instead of venting them safely outdoors, creating a serious health and safety hazard. The furnace is unaffected because it has a dedicated outdoor air supply.
