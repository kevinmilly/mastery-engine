## Exercises

**Exercise 1**
A homeowner hangs a very heavy antique chandelier (200 lbs) from the ceiling in the middle of their living room. They attached it directly to the drywall, but it held for a few days before the mounting failed. They now plan to re-hang it by screwing a large hook directly into the nearest wooden ceiling joist. From a structural perspective, explain why this is a better plan and identify the path the chandelier's weight will now follow to the ground.

**Exercise 2**
You are looking at plans for a simple, single-story cabin. The roof trusses are designed to span the full width of the cabin, resting on the two parallel exterior side walls. A potential buyer wants to add a wall down the center of the cabin, parallel to the side walls, to divide the main space into two rooms. Is this new wall likely to be considered "load-bearing"? Explain your reasoning.

**Exercise 3**
During a home inspection, you notice a significant "bounce" or deflection in the floor of a second-story bedroom. In the basement directly below, you see that a previous owner cut a large, 6-inch-wide rectangular hole through the center of a major wooden support beam to run HVAC ductwork. How did this modification lead to the "bouncy" floor one level above?

**Exercise 4**
A homeowner wants to install a large, heavy hot tub on their outdoor wooden deck. The deck is supported by several 4x4 vertical posts resting on concrete footings. They notice the ground has slightly eroded around one of the footings, causing the post on that corner to tilt slightly. Why is it structurally critical to fix this seemingly minor tilt before adding the weight of the hot tub?

**Exercise 5**
A homeowner is renovating their kitchen and wants to remove a 6-foot section of a wall to create a pass-through to the dining room. The wall is load-bearing. Their contractor says they must install a "header" above the new opening. The homeowner asks if they can just use a pipe from a previous plumbing project, since it's strong steel. Using your knowledge of how loads are distributed, explain why a round pipe is an inappropriate choice for a header compared to a properly sized wooden or steel I-beam.

**Exercise 6**
You are investigating the cause of a leak in a home with an attic. You find evidence of water intrusion near a vent pipe that penetrates the roof. You also notice that the roof sheathing around the pipe is dark and soft, and the top chord of a nearby roof truss feels damp and spongy. Drawing on concepts from this lesson (Structural Loads) and the prior lesson (Water Pressure & Flow), explain the cascading risk. Why is this situation more dangerous than just a simple roof leak?

---

## Answer Key

**Answer 1**
The first attempt failed because drywall and its fasteners are only meant to hold their own weight and light loads; they are not part of the structural load path. Attaching the hook to a ceiling joist is the correct approach.

*Reasoning:* The ceiling joist is a structural member designed to carry loads. The weight of the chandelier (a "live load") will be transferred from the hook to the joist. The joist transfers this load to the walls it rests on (typically load-bearing walls). The walls, via their studs, transfer the load down to the foundation, which then distributes the weight into the ground. This provides a continuous and secure path for the weight.

**Answer 2**
No, this new interior wall is not likely to be load-bearing.

*Reasoning:* The problem states that the roof trusses are designed to span the *full width* of the cabin, resting on the exterior walls. This means the roof's weight is already being transferred directly to the foundation via the two outer side walls. A central wall added later simply divides the space and bears only its own weight; it does not support the roof or ceiling structure above it.

**Answer 3**
The modification directly compromised the primary support for the floor above, leading to the "bouncy" feeling.

*Reasoning:* A beam's strength comes from its total height. The top of the beam resists compression, and the bottom resists tension. Cutting a large hole in the center of the beam removed material from both the tension and compression zones, severely weakening its ability to resist bending under the load of the floor joists, subfloor, furniture, and people above. This weakness allows the beam to flex or "deflect" more than it should, which is experienced as a bounce in the floor.

**Answer 4**
It is critical to fix the tilt because vertical posts are designed to transfer loads directly downwards (compressive loads), and a tilt introduces dangerous lateral and bending forces.

*Reasoning:* When the post is perfectly vertical, the weight from the deck is a compressive force that travels straight down the post into the footing. When the post is tilted, the load is no longer aligned with the post's central axis. This creates a bending force (leverage) that the post was not designed to handle. Adding the immense, concentrated weight of a hot tub to this compromised support could cause the post to buckle and fail, leading to a partial or total collapse of the deck.

**Answer 5**
A round pipe is inappropriate because it lacks the necessary shape to effectively resist the vertical load that a header must support.

*Reasoning:* A header acts as a beam to transfer the vertical load from the roof and floors above, around the opening, and down to the studs on either side. A properly sized wooden header or a steel I-beam is tall and flat. This height gives it immense resistance to bending under a vertical load. A round pipe, by contrast, would easily deform or roll under the same load. The flat top surface of a proper header provides a stable platform for the joists or studs above it, while the flat bottom provides a stable surface for the top of the new opening. A pipe provides neither.

**Answer 6**
This is more dangerous than a simple leak because the water has begun to compromise a critical structural element of the roof system.

*Reasoning:* The initial problem is a failure in the waterproofing system (part of the "Water" topic). However, the chronic moisture has now caused the top chord of the roof truss to rot. The top chord of a truss is under immense compression from the roof's weight. Rotted, spongy wood cannot handle compressive forces. This compromised truss is at risk of buckling, which would cause a section of the roof to sag or even collapse. This transforms a simple maintenance issue (a leak) into a major structural failure risk, as the load from the roof can no longer be safely transferred to the exterior walls.
