## Exercises

**Exercise 1**
A standard interior door handle uses a simple lever mechanism. When you push down on the handle (the lever), it operates a small internal bolt called a latch. The pivot point (fulcrum) is inside the handle assembly. The force you apply is at the end of the handle, and the latch that is moved is very close to the fulcrum. Based on this arrangement, explain what class of lever a door handle is and why this design is effective for its purpose.

**Exercise 2**
You are examining a simple sash window that is counterweighted. A single rope is tied to the top of the window sash, runs up and over a pulley fixed to the top of the window frame, and then down to a heavy cast iron weight hidden inside the frame. If the window sash itself weighs 15 lbs, what is the ideal weight for the counterweight to make the window feel "weightless" to lift? Explain the role of the pulley in this system.

**Exercise 3**
A homeowner's stand mixer has stopped working correctly. When they turn it on, the motor makes a whirring sound, but the mixing attachments don't spin. After opening the housing, you find a small nylon "worm gear" on the motor shaft that meshes with a larger metal gear connected to the attachments. The nylon gear's threads are completely stripped and smooth. The larger metal gear appears undamaged. Why would the manufacturer deliberately use a softer material like nylon for the smaller gear that is subjected to the motor's direct, high-speed force?

**Exercise 4**
You are tasked with installing a small manual dumbwaiter to lift items from a basement to the kitchen, a 10-foot vertical distance. You are considering two designs:
- **Design A:** A direct-lift system where a single rope is attached to the dumbwaiter car and runs up to a crank handle with a simple axle.
- **Design B:** A block-and-tackle system with two pulleys on the car and two fixed pulleys at the top, creating four supporting rope segments.
From a mechanical perspective, compare the effort required to lift a 40 lb load and the amount of rope you would need to pull for each design to lift the car the full 10 feet.

**Exercise 5**
A client's metal fence gate is sagging and has become difficult to close. The gate is 5 feet wide and is supported by two hinges on a steel post. Upon inspection, you see that the weld holding the top hinge to the post has cracked. The bottom hinge appears intact but the metal around it is slightly bent. Using the principles of levers and integrating the concept of material degradation, explain why the top hinge failed under tension while the bottom hinge is experiencing compression, leading to the gate's sag.

**Exercise 6**
A manually operated skylight, positioned on a sloped roof, is opened and closed by a hand crank connected to a long pole. The crank mechanism uses a small bevel gear that turns a larger gear, which is attached to a threaded rod. As the rod turns, it pushes a hinged arm that opens the skylight. The skylight has become very stiff to operate, especially when closing it against the wind. The homeowner is concerned they will break the crank. Integrating your knowledge of mechanical linkages, heat transfer, and the building envelope, propose two distinct possible causes for the increased stiffness. For each cause, explain which part of the mechanical system is under the most stress and why.

## Answer Key

**Answer 1**
This is a Class 2 lever.
- **Reasoning:** The fulcrum is the pivot point inside the door. The load is the latch mechanism being moved, which is located between the fulcrum and the effort. The effort is the force applied by your hand at the end of the handle. In a Class 2 lever, the load is between the fulcrum and the effort. This arrangement provides a mechanical advantage, allowing a small amount of force applied far from the pivot to move the latch, which may be under pressure from the door seal or misalignment, with much greater force.

**Answer 2**
The ideal weight for the counterweight is 15 lbs.
- **Reasoning:** The pulley in this system is a single fixed pulley. A single fixed pulley does not provide any mechanical advantage; its purpose is to change the direction of the force. The force required to lift the 15 lb sash is still 15 lbs. The counterweight simply provides an equal and opposite force, balancing the system. This makes the "net weight" of the sash zero, so the user only has to overcome friction to move it up or down.

**Answer 3**
The nylon gear is designed as a "sacrificial" part.
- **Reasoning:** The worm gear on the motor shaft is the driving gear. If the mixing attachments get jammed (e.g., by a very thick dough or a dropped spoon), the motor will continue to try to turn. This creates an immense amount of force on the gear train. By making the small, easily replaceable motor gear out of a softer material, it will be the first component to fail under extreme stress. The stripped nylon gear protects the more expensive and difficult-to-replace motor and the larger metal gears from catastrophic damage. It's a built-in mechanical fuse.

**Answer 4**
- **Design A (Direct-lift):**
    - **Effort:** You would need to apply 40 lbs of force (ignoring friction) to lift the 40 lb load. There is no mechanical advantage.
    - **Rope Pulled:** To lift the car 10 feet, you would need to pull exactly 10 feet of rope.
- **Design B (Block-and-tackle):**
    - **Effort:** With four supporting rope segments, the mechanical advantage is approximately 4. The effort required would be 40 lbs / 4 = 10 lbs.
    - **Rope Pulled:** To achieve the mechanical advantage, you must pull more rope. To lift the car 10 feet, you would need to pull 4 times the distance: 10 feet * 4 = 40 feet of rope.
- **Comparison Summary:** Design A is faster but requires maximum effort. Design B is much easier to lift but requires pulling a significantly longer length of rope.

**Answer 5**
The gate acts as a long lever arm, with its weight (the load) concentrated at its center of gravity.
- **Reasoning:** The hinge pins act as fulcrums. The gate's weight pulls straight down. This force creates a rotational pull, or torque, around the bottom hinge.
    - **Top Hinge (Tension):** To resist this rotation, the top hinge is constantly being pulled away from the post. This places the hinge and its fasteners (the weld) under tension. Over time, cycles of stress and environmental factors (like rust, a form of material degradation) can cause a tension failure like a cracked weld.
    - **Bottom Hinge (Compression):** Simultaneously, the bottom hinge is being pushed into the post, carrying the vertical weight of the gate and resisting the rotational force. This places the hinge and the post material around it under compression. The visible bending is evidence of this compressive stress.

**Answer 6**
Here are two distinct possible causes:

1.  **Cause: Debris in the Gearbox/Linkage.**
    - **Explanation:** The skylight is part of the building envelope, exposed to dust, leaves, insects, or grit. This debris can work its way into the bevel gear assembly or the hinge points of the arm. This increases friction dramatically. When closing against the wind (an external load), the added friction requires a much higher force from the crank.
    - **Stress Point:** The most stressed part would be the teeth of the small bevel gear on the crank. This is where the force is concentrated to overcome both the wind and the friction. Its small size makes it vulnerable to stripping under high torque.

2.  **Cause: Seal Adhesion or Thermal Expansion.**
    - **Explanation:** The skylight has a weatherproof seal (part of the building envelope). Over time, heat (from solar gain) and UV exposure can cause this rubber or silicone seal to become sticky or brittle. When the skylight is closed, it can adhere to the frame. The initial force needed to "break" this seal when opening would be very high. Similarly, thermal expansion could cause the skylight frame to bind against the roof opening, increasing friction on all moving parts.
    - **Stress Point:** The most stressed part in this case would be the threaded rod and the linkage arm it pushes. These components must transmit the entire force required to overcome the adhesion or binding friction. The threads on the rod or the pivot pin of the arm could be damaged or bent from the excessive compressive/tensile force.
