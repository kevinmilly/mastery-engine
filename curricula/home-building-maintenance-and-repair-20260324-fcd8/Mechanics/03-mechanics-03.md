# Structural Loads & Supports: Foundations, Walls, & Roof Trusses

## The Hook
This lesson will show you how to see the invisible skeleton of your house, allowing you to understand why some walls are absolutely essential and others are just simple room dividers.

Imagine carrying a heavy box on your head. The weight of the box travels through your neck, down your spine, through your legs, and into the ground through your feet. A house manages weight in the exact same way. The entire structure is a carefully designed path to guide weight—from the roof to the soil—safely and without breaking.

## Why It Matters
The most dangerous—and expensive—mistake in any home renovation is removing a wall without understanding its job. Someone decides they want an open-concept kitchen and living room. They see a wall, assume it's just drywall and wood, and tear it out.

Weeks or months later, they notice the ceiling above is sagging. Doors upstairs no longer close properly. Cracks appear in the drywall. They have accidentally removed a critical "bone" from the house's skeleton. The load that the wall was carrying—the weight of the second floor, furniture, and people—had nowhere to go. The structure, under immense stress, began to bend and fail. Understanding the load path isn't just academic; it’s the fundamental knowledge that separates a safe modification from a potential structural collapse.

## The Ladder
A house is constantly under pressure from two types of forces, or **loads**.
*   **Dead Load:** The fixed weight of the structure itself—the wood, drywall, shingles, and everything permanently attached.
*   **Live Load:** The temporary or variable weight—snow on the roof, furniture, people walking around, and even strong wind pressure.

The entire frame of a house is a system designed to create a continuous **load path**, channeling the combined force of these loads down to the ground. Let's trace this path from top to bottom.

1.  **The Roof:** The roof collects the initial load—its own weight plus rain or snow. The structure of the roof, often made of triangular **roof trusses** or angled beams called **rafters**, is designed to direct this weight downward and outward onto the walls below. The triangle is an incredibly strong shape, which is why it's used so extensively.

2.  **The Walls:** The roof trusses rest on top of the main walls. The walls act like your spine, carrying the load vertically downwards. While a wall looks like a solid surface, the real work is done by the vertical wooden planks inside it called **studs**. The drywall is just a covering, like skin. Most exterior walls are load-bearing. Some interior walls are too, acting like columns in the middle of the house to support the structure above.

3.  **The Floors:** The studs in the walls don't just stop at the floor. They transfer their load *through* the floor system. A floor is built from a series of parallel beams called **joists**. These joists carry the load of the floor itself (furniture, people) plus any load being passed down from the walls above. The ends of the joists rest on walls or on larger, thicker beams.

4.  **The Beams:** Sometimes, you can't have a wall running down the middle of a room (like in an open basement). In this case, a large, strong **beam** (often made of steel or laminated wood) is used to carry the load from the joists above. This beam is then supported by posts or columns, which continue the load path down.

5.  **The Foundation:** All of these paths—from the walls, beams, and posts—eventually meet at the **foundation**. This is the concrete or block base of the house. The foundation's job is to collect the entire, accumulated weight of the building.

6.  **The Footing and the Ground:** The foundation walls don't just sit on the dirt. They sit on a wider base of poured concrete called the **footing**. The footing is like the sole of your shoe; it spreads the immense, concentrated weight of the house over a larger area of soil. This prevents the house from sinking.

The entire system, from truss to footing, is one interconnected chain. If you remove a link, the chain breaks.

## Worked Reality
A homeowner wants to remove a 12-foot section of a wall between their living room and kitchen to create a more open space. Before grabbing a sledgehammer, they do some investigation.

First, they go down to their unfinished basement. They look up at the ceiling, which is the underside of the first floor. They can clearly see the floor joists—a series of 2x10 boards running from the front of the house to the back, spaced 16 inches apart.

Next, they locate the wall they want to remove on the floor above. They see that this wall runs **perpendicular** (at a 90-degree angle) to the direction of the joists. This is a major red flag. A wall running perpendicular to joists is often supporting them mid-span, preventing them from sagging. A wall running parallel to the joists, sitting right between two of them, is typically just a non-load-bearing divider.

To confirm, they use a stud finder and a small drill bit to make a few test holes in the ceiling next to the wall. They find that the joists from the second floor run in the same direction and end directly on top of the wall they want to remove.

**Conclusion:** The wall is absolutely load-bearing. It's supporting the middle of the first-floor joists *and* the second floor above.

Instead of just demolishing it, they now know the correct procedure. They must create a new path for the load. This involves temporarily supporting the ceiling on both sides of the wall, removing the old studs, and installing a massive beam, called a **header**, in the opening. This header will now catch the load from the joists and transfer it to reinforced posts at either end of the new opening. Those posts will then carry the load down into the foundation. The load path is maintained, and the renovation is safe.

## Friction Point
The most common misunderstanding is thinking: **"Only exterior walls are structurally important."**

This is tempting because exterior walls are obviously holding up the roof and protecting the house from the elements. They look and feel more substantial. Interior walls often seem like simple, thin partitions put there to create rooms. Since many interior walls *are* non-load-bearing, it's easy to mistakenly assume they all are.

The correct mental model is: **A wall's job is defined by the structure above and below it, not its location in the house.**

Think of a wide bridge. The supports at either riverbank (the abutments) are essential, like the exterior walls of a house. But if the bridge is long, it also needs piers in the middle of the river to hold up the center. A load-bearing interior wall is like one of those piers. It might be located in the center of the house, but it's critical for supporting the span of the floors and roof. Removing it is like knocking out a bridge pier—the structure will sag in the middle. A non-load-bearing partition wall is more like a lane divider painted on the road; it organizes traffic but holds up nothing.
