## Exercises

**Exercise 1**
You need to assemble a flat-pack bookshelf. The instructions require you to join several wooden panels together using the provided screws. Which power tool from the lesson is the most appropriate for this task, and what is one key setting you should check on the tool to avoid damaging the wood?

**Exercise 2**
You are preparing to use a circular saw to make a straight cut across a piece of plywood. The plywood is resting on two sawhorses. Describe the two most important actions you must take to secure your workpiece *before* starting the saw.

**Exercise 3**
While drilling a hole into a dense piece of oak, the drill bit stops spinning, but you can still hear the drill's motor running and making a high-pitched whining sound. What is the immediate, safest action to take, and what is the most likely cause of this problem?

**Exercise 4**
You are helping a friend clear out their shed and find a corded jigsaw. The blade is rusty and the plastic housing near the air vents is slightly melted. Your friend suggests plugging it in just to see if it still works. Explain the specific safety risks associated with testing this tool in its current condition.

**Exercise 5**
You plan to spend an afternoon in your garage building a small wooden planter box, a project that will require cutting wood with a power saw and drilling pilot holes. Drawing on your knowledge of PPE and site safety, list three essential safety preparations you must make *in your workspace* before you plug in any tools.

**Exercise 6**
A homeowner wants to remove a small, non-load-bearing interior wall to combine two rooms. They plan to use a powerful reciprocating saw to cut through the drywall and the wooden studs. Based on your understanding of the home as a system, identify two different potential hidden hazards they might encounter within the wall and explain why using the saw without checking first is extremely dangerous.

---

## Answer Key

**Answer 1**
**Tool:** A cordless drill with a screwdriver bit attachment is the best choice.
**Reasoning:** A drill is designed to create rotational force. When used with a screwdriver bit, it can drive screws much faster and with less effort than a manual screwdriver. The key setting to check is the clutch (or torque setting). You should set it to a low number initially. This will stop the drill from spinning once a certain level of resistance is met, preventing you from driving the screw too deep and stripping the hole or cracking the wood.

**Answer 2**
**Actions:**
1.  **Clamp the workpiece:** The plywood must be firmly clamped to both sawhorses. Using C-clamps or quick-grip clamps will prevent the wood from vibrating, shifting, or pinching the saw blade during the cut.
2.  **Ensure a clear path:** You must check that the saw's path is clear on both sides of the cut line and underneath. The section of wood being cut off (the offcut) needs a clear space to fall so it does not jam the blade or kick back. Your power cord must also be positioned so it cannot be caught or cut.

**Answer 3**
**Immediate Action:** Immediately release the trigger to stop the motor. Do not try to force the drill to turn.
**Likely Cause:** The drill bit is bound up in the wood. This happens when the torque required to turn the bit is greater than what the drill can provide at its current setting. The motor continues to try, creating the whining sound. The cause is often a combination of using too much pressure, having the drill on too high a speed/too low a torque setting for a hardwood, or a dull bit.

**Answer 4**
**Safety Risks:**
1.  **Electrical Hazard:** The melted housing near the vents suggests the tool has previously overheated, which may have damaged the internal wiring. Plugging it in could create a short circuit, posing a risk of electric shock or fire.
2.  **Mechanical Failure:** A rusty blade is a weak blade. If the tool starts, the force and vibration could cause the rusty blade to snap, sending sharp metal fragments flying. The rust also indicates the tool was stored improperly, and other internal mechanical parts could be seized or weakened.

**Answer 5**
**Workspace Preparations:**
1.  **Clear the Area:** Ensure the floor is free of clutter, tripping hazards (like extension cords), and any flammable materials (like sawdust piles or oily rags). A clean, organized space is critical for safe movement.
2.  **Ensure Proper Lighting:** Good visibility is essential for making accurate cuts and seeing potential hazards. Set up task lighting if the general garage lighting is insufficient.
3.  **Check Ventilation:** Cutting and sanding create fine dust particles that are harmful to inhale. You should open a garage door or window and, if possible, use a fan to ensure air is circulating away from your breathing zone.
(Note: Wearing the correct PPE, like safety glasses and a dust mask, is also essential but is a personal preparation, not a workspace one.)

**Answer 6**
**Potential Hidden Hazards:**
1.  **Electrical Wiring:** Interior walls are commonly used to run electrical cables to outlets, switches, and light fixtures. A reciprocating saw will cut through live wiring instantly, which can cause severe electric shock, electrocution, or a fire.
2.  **Plumbing Pipes:** Walls, especially those near kitchens or bathrooms, often contain water supply lines (copper or PEX) or drain pipes (PVC/ABS). Cutting into a supply line would cause major flooding, while cutting a drain pipe could lead to water damage and release of sewer gas.
**Reasoning for Danger:** A reciprocating saw is a powerful and aggressive tool that cuts quickly through common building materials. It is not a tool for exploration. Using it without first shutting off the power and water and then visually inspecting the inside of the wall (by cutting a small exploratory hole with a hand tool) is reckless because you cannot see these hidden systems before the blade has already caused catastrophic damage.
