## Exercises

**Exercise 1**
You need to attach a 1/2-inch thick sheet of drywall to a wooden wall stud. Which fastener is the better choice: a drywall screw or a framing nail? Explain your reasoning.

**Exercise 2**
After a minor leak under a bathroom sink, you notice a section of the wall is soft, swollen, and the paint is bubbling. A nearby wooden baseboard feels solid but is slightly discolored. Based on this evidence, which material (the wall or the baseboard) has likely sustained more significant structural damage, and why?

**Exercise 3**
Your neighbor is building a simple, free-standing bookshelf for their living room. They plan to build the entire structure—frame and shelves—out of drywall because it's cheap and has a smooth, paintable surface. Identify the primary flaw in their material selection and explain what is likely to happen if they build it.

**Exercise 4**
You find a small hole in your garage wall, about the size of a fist. You can see pink insulation inside. To patch it, you'll need to cut a clean square around the hole and fit a new piece of material. What is this wall material most likely to be, and what property makes it unsuitable for hanging a heavy tool rack directly from it?

**Exercise 5**
You need to hang a heavy mirror (30 lbs) on an interior wall. You have a stud finder (from a previous lesson on tools) and a box of 2-inch wood screws. Your stud finder indicates there is a solid wooden stud directly behind the drywall where you want to hang the mirror. Describe the most secure installation plan and explain why it works, referencing the properties of at least two materials involved.

**Exercise 6**
A friend built a small outdoor planter box using untreated pine lumber (like a standard 2x4) and drywall screws. After one rainy season, the box is starting to fall apart at the corners, and some of the wood is beginning to feel soft and rot. Identify the two main material-related mistakes that led to this rapid failure and suggest better alternatives.

---

## Answer Key

**Answer 1**
A drywall screw is the better choice.

*Reasoning:* Drywall is a relatively soft, brittle material. The threads of a screw provide superior pull-out (tensile) strength compared to a nail. This ensures the drywall sheet is held tightly against the stud and prevents it from popping off over time. A nail could easily pull through the soft gypsum core of the drywall.

**Answer 2**
The wall material (drywall) has sustained more significant structural damage.

*Reasoning:* Drywall is made of gypsum plaster pressed between two sheets of paper. It has very low tolerance for moisture and loses its structural integrity quickly when wet, becoming soft and crumbly. Wood is also susceptible to water damage (rot) over time, but it is far more resilient to short-term exposure and will not lose its fundamental strength as rapidly as drywall will. The discoloration on the wood indicates exposure, but the fact that it remains solid means it's in much better condition than the soft, swollen drywall.

**Answer 3**
The primary flaw is using a non-structural material (drywall) to build a load-bearing structure (a bookshelf).

*Reasoning:* Drywall has very little strength and is designed only as a surface covering for walls and ceilings. If used for a bookshelf, the frame would not be able to support its own weight, let alone the weight of books. The structure would likely collapse as soon as any weight was placed on the shelves. The fasteners would easily tear through the soft gypsum material.

**Answer 4**
The wall material is most likely drywall.

*Reasoning:* Drywall is the standard material for interior walls that are not made of solid materials like brick or concrete. Its key property that makes it unsuitable for directly hanging heavy objects is its low pull-out strength and brittle nature. A screw or nail driven only into the drywall panel cannot support significant weight because the soft gypsum core will crumble and give way under the load.

**Answer 5**
The most secure plan is to drive the 2-inch wood screws through the drywall and directly into the wooden stud behind it.

*Reasoning:* This plan utilizes the strengths of both materials correctly. The drywall acts only as a cosmetic surface. The real work of supporting the mirror's weight is done by the wooden stud, which is a strong structural component of the wall frame. The wood screws are ideal because their threads will bite firmly into the wood stud, providing excellent pull-out strength needed to resist the downward pull of the heavy mirror. This is far superior to using a drywall anchor, which only distributes the weight across the weak drywall panel itself.

**Answer 6**
The two main mistakes were using untreated lumber and using drywall screws for an outdoor application.

*Reasoning:*
1.  **Untreated Lumber:** Standard pine lumber is not resistant to moisture. When used outdoors and exposed to rain and soil, it will quickly absorb water, leading to fungal growth, rot, and structural decay. A better choice would be a pressure-treated lumber or a naturally rot-resistant wood like cedar or redwood.
2.  **Drywall Screws:** These screws are designed for interior use. They typically have a thin black phosphate coating that is not sufficient to prevent rust. When exposed to moisture, they will corrode, lose their strength, and eventually fail, causing the planter box to fall apart at the joints. A better choice would be exterior-rated fasteners, such as galvanized or stainless steel deck screws, which are designed to resist corrosion.
