## Exercises

**Exercise 1**
You flick a light switch to turn on a table lamp, but it doesn't light up. The lamp is plugged into a wall outlet that you know was working earlier today. Using the observation-deduction model, what are the two most likely and simple causes? What single, safe action would you take first to test the most probable cause?

**Exercise 2**
A homeowner tells you the garbage disposal in their kitchen sink has stopped working. When they flip the switch, they hear absolutely nothing – no hum, no buzz, no sound at all. List three specific diagnostic questions you would ask to gather more information before touching the appliance.

**Exercise 3**
You enter a basement and notice a musty smell and a dark, discolored patch at the bottom of a drywall panel. The patch is dry to the touch. The homeowner mentions that their gutters were clogged and overflowing during a heavy rainstorm last week, but they have since been cleaned. What is your primary deduction about the *root cause* of the stain, and why is the observation that it's currently "dry" a key piece of information?

**Exercise 4**
A toilet continues to run for a long time after being flushed, making a constant refilling sound. You take the lid off the tank and observe that the water level never reaches the top of the overflow tube, causing the fill valve to run continuously. You don't see any water on the floor around the toilet. Based on this direct observation, what is the most logical deduction about where the water is going?

**Exercise 5**
While using a drill to hang a picture, the drill suddenly loses power. You also notice that a nearby radio, which is plugged into the same outlet, has also turned off. The lights in the rest of the room are still on. Integrating your knowledge of basic electrical systems and troubleshooting logic, what is the most likely immediate cause of this problem? What is your very first step to investigate and resolve it safely?

**Exercise 6**
A homeowner is concerned about a bedroom door that has recently become very difficult to close; it sticks at the top corner. You observe that it is a humid summer day, and the air conditioner is not running. You also look at the door hinges and see they are secure, with no loose screws. Synthesizing your knowledge of common materials and troubleshooting logic, propose two different potential root causes for this problem. What follow-up observation or action could help you differentiate between these two possibilities?

---

## Answer Key

**Answer 1**
*   **Two Likely Causes:**
    1.  The light bulb has burned out.
    2.  The lamp has been accidentally unplugged from the wall outlet.
*   **First Action & Reasoning:** The simplest and most common point of failure is the bulb. The first action would be to unplug the lamp and check the bulb. You could test it in another known working lamp or replace it with a new bulb. This is a quick, safe test that addresses the most probable cause before investigating more complex issues like the lamp's cord or the switch itself.

**Answer 2**
1.  **"Did you happen to press the small reset button (usually red or black) on the bottom of the disposal unit?"** This question checks if the unit's internal overload protector has been tripped, which is a very common and easily fixable cause.
2.  **"Have you checked your circuit breaker panel to see if the breaker for the disposal has been tripped?"** The complete lack of sound suggests a total power loss, which could be due to a tripped breaker.
3.  **"What, if anything, was put down the disposal right before it stopped working?"** This question helps determine if a hard object (like a bone or silverware) caused a jam that triggered the overload, or if fibrous materials (like celery or potato peels) overworked the motor.

**Answer 3**
*   **Primary Deduction:** The root cause of the stain was most likely water intrusion from the outside, caused by the overflowing gutters during the recent heavy rain. The water likely seeped down the foundation wall and was absorbed by the bottom of the drywall.
*   **Reasoning for "Dry":** The fact that the stain is now dry indicates that the problem was likely a one-time event (or an intermittent one related to rain) that has since stopped. This strongly supports the connection to the now-repaired gutter issue. If the patch were still wet, you would suspect an active, ongoing leak, such as from a pipe.

**Answer 4**
*   **Deduction:** The water is constantly leaking from the tank into the toilet bowl.
*   **Reasoning:** You have observed that the water level inside the tank is not rising, yet the fill valve is continuously adding more water. Since there is no water on the floor, the only other place for it to go is down into the bowl. This is almost always caused by a faulty flapper or flush valve seal at the bottom of the tank, which is failing to create a proper seal and allowing water to leak past it.

**Answer 5**
*   **Likely Cause:** You have tripped a circuit breaker (or blown a fuse, in an older home).
*   **First Step & Reasoning:** The first step is to go to the main electrical panel. The key observation is that multiple devices (drill and radio) in the same area lost power simultaneously, while lights in the same room (likely on a different circuit) remained on. This isolates the problem to a single circuit. A power drill draws a significant amount of current on startup and can easily overload a circuit, causing the breaker to trip as a safety measure. Before doing anything else, you must locate the panel and find the tripped breaker to reset it.

**Answer 6**
*   **Potential Cause 1 (Material-Based):** The door has swollen due to high humidity. Wood is a hygroscopic material, meaning it absorbs moisture from the air. On humid days, a wooden door can expand slightly, causing it to stick in its frame, especially if the initial clearance was tight.
*   **Potential Cause 2 (Structural-Based):** The house has settled, causing the door frame to shift out of square. This is less common for a sudden change but is a possible root cause for a door that has progressively worsened.
*   **Differentiating Action/Observation:** To differentiate, you could wait for a few dry, less humid days (or run a dehumidifier in the house) and see if the door's operation improves. If it returns to normal, the cause was humidity (Cause 1). If the door continues to stick regardless of the weather, it suggests a more permanent alignment issue with the frame, pointing toward settlement (Cause 2). You could also use a level or a square on the door frame to check if it is still plumb and square.
