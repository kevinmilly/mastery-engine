## Exercises

**Exercise 1**
Your tape measure has markings for 1/16 of an inch. You measure a piece of wood and the edge lands on the third small mark after the 5 ½ inch line. What is the exact measurement?

**Exercise 2**
You are hanging a picture frame and place a spirit level on top. The bubble is sitting hard against the left side of the two center lines. To make the frame level, do you need to raise the left side or the right side? Why?

**Exercise 3**
You need to take a precise inside measurement of a window frame for a new pane of glass. However, the metal hook on the end of your tape measure is bent and loose. How can you still get an accurate measurement using this tape measure? Describe the technique and a sample calculation.

**Exercise 4**
You are tasked with installing a chair rail that will run around the entire perimeter of a 15-foot by 20-foot room. You need to ensure the top of the rail is perfectly level all the way around. You have a 2-foot spirit level and a laser level. Which tool is better suited for this job, and what problems might you encounter if you chose the less suitable tool?

**Exercise 5**
You need to cut three identical shelves from a single 8-foot-long piece of 1x10 lumber. Each finished shelf must be exactly 30 ¾ inches long. Considering the material lost by the saw blade (the "kerf"), which is ⅛ inch wide, describe the sequence of your first two measurements and marks to ensure all three pieces are the correct length.

**Exercise 6**
You are building a small deck frame. You've assembled the outer frame (the rim joists) and need to ensure it is perfectly square before installing the inner joists. You don't have a large framing square. Using only a tape measure, how can you verify the frame is square? Describe the measurement technique you would use.

## Answer Key

**Answer 1**
The exact measurement is 5 11/16 inches.

**Reasoning:**
The 5 ½ inch mark is equivalent to 5 8/16 inches. Each small mark represents 1/16 of an inch. Counting three small marks past the 5 8/16 mark gives you:
5 8/16 + 1/16 + 1/16 + 1/16 = 5 11/16 inches.

**Answer 2**
You need to raise the right side.

**Reasoning:**
A spirit level's bubble always moves towards the higher side due to gravity. If the bubble is on the left side, it means the left side is higher than the right. To make the frame level, you must raise the lower side (the right side) until the bubble is centered between the lines.

**Answer 3**
Use the technique of "burning an inch" (or more).

**Reasoning:**
A loose or damaged hook can introduce inaccuracy. To bypass this, start your measurement from a clear, whole number on the tape, such as the 1-inch mark.
1.  Align the 1-inch mark with the inside edge of one side of the window frame.
2.  Read the measurement at the other inside edge.
3.  Subtract the "burned" inch from your final reading to get the true dimension.

**Sample Calculation:**
If you align the 1-inch mark and the final reading is 35 ¼ inches, the actual measurement is:
35 ¼" - 1" = 34 ¼".

**Answer 4**
The laser level is better suited for this job.

**Reasoning:**
A laser level can project a perfectly level line across all four walls simultaneously from a single setup point. This creates a continuous, reliable reference for the entire room, ensuring the chair rail is at a consistent height.

Using a 2-foot spirit level would be slow and prone to cumulative error. You would have to "leapfrog" the level across the wall, making a mark, moving the level, aligning it with the mark, and repeating. Each time you move the level, there is a small chance of introducing an error, and these small errors can add up over the 20-foot length of a wall, resulting in a rail that is visibly sloped by the end.

**Answer 5**
1.  Measure from the factory-cut end of the board and make a mark at 30 ¾ inches. This is the cut line for your first shelf.
2.  After making the first cut, *do not* measure from the same end again. Measure from your newly cut edge and make your next mark at 30 ¾ inches. This marks the second shelf.

**Reasoning:**
This method accounts for the material removed by the saw blade (the ⅛" kerf) with each cut. If you were to measure and mark all your cut lines from the original end (at 30 ¾", 61 ½", etc.), your second and third pieces would be too short. Specifically, the second piece would be short by the width of the first cut (⅛"), and the third piece would be short by the width of two cuts (¼"). Measuring each new piece from the previous fresh cut ensures every piece is exactly the required length.

**Answer 6**
Use the 3-4-5 method, which is an application of the Pythagorean theorem (a² + b² = c²) to verify a 90-degree angle.

**Reasoning:**
1.  Pick one corner of the deck frame to test.
2.  Measure 3 feet along one rim joist from the corner and make a clear mark.
3.  Measure 4 feet along the connecting rim joist from the same corner and make a clear mark.
4.  Now, measure the diagonal distance between those two marks.
    -   If the distance is exactly 5 feet, the corner is a perfect 90-degree square.
    -   If the distance is less than 5 feet, the angle is less than 90 degrees.
    -   If the distance is more than 5 feet, the angle is greater than 90 degrees.
5.  Repeat for all four corners to ensure the entire frame is square before proceeding. (For larger frames, you can use multiples of 3-4-5, such as 6-8-10, for greater accuracy.)
