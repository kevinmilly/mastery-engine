## Exercises

**Exercise 1**
A homeowner notices that several shingles have been blown off their roof after a major windstorm, and they can see the underlying wood sheathing in a few spots. A problem with which major home subsystem is this?

**Exercise 2**
When you turn on the faucet in the upstairs bathroom, you hear a loud banging sound coming from inside the walls. Which home subsystem is the source of this noise?

**Exercise 3**
In the middle of a hot summer, the air conditioning unit outside the house is running constantly, but the air blowing from the vents inside feels cool, not cold. The house never seems to reach the temperature set on the thermostat. Identify two different subsystems that could be the primary cause of this issue.

**Exercise 4**
A homeowner decides to install a new, heavy granite countertop in their kitchen. A month later, they notice that the cabinet doors below the new countertop no longer close properly and there is a new, thin crack in the floor tile just in front of the cabinets. Which two subsystems are interacting to cause this problem?

**Exercise 5**
You are helping a friend move into an older home. When you plug a vacuum cleaner into a wall outlet in the garage, the overhead lights in the garage go out and the vacuum immediately stops working. Based on your knowledge of home systems and the "Safety First" principles, which subsystem is clearly malfunctioning, and what is the first step you should take before attempting any further investigation?

**Exercise 6**
During a winter freeze, you notice a significant amount of frost has formed on the inside of the windows in one specific bedroom. The wall below the window feels damp to the touch, and you see the early stages of dark mold spots forming on the drywall. Identify the three interconnected subsystems at play here and explain how their interaction is creating this complex problem.

---

## Answer Key

**Answer 1**
This is a problem with the **building envelope**. The roof is a critical component of the envelope, which is the system that separates the controlled interior environment from the uncontrolled exterior. The missing shingles compromise the envelope's ability to protect the home from weather.

**Answer 2**
The noise is coming from the **plumbing** system. The banging sound, often called "water hammer," occurs when the flow of water is suddenly stopped, causing shockwaves to travel through the pipes. This indicates an issue within the plumbing, not the walls themselves (the structural system).

**Answer 3**
The two most likely subsystems are:
1.  **HVAC System:** The air conditioner itself could be malfunctioning (e.g., low on refrigerant, dirty coils), preventing it from cooling the air effectively.
2.  **Building Envelope:** The house may have poor insulation, leaky windows, or unsealed gaps that allow hot outside air to infiltrate and cool conditioned air to escape. This forces the HVAC system to work harder without being able to keep up.

**Answer 4**
The two interacting subsystems are the **structural** system and the **electrical** system.
*   **Reasoning:** The flickering lights when a large appliance starts indicate a sudden, high demand for power. The **HVAC** system (the A/C unit) is placing a heavy load on the **electrical** system. This suggests the circuit may be overloaded, the wiring may be undersized for the load, or there could be a loose connection somewhere in the circuit.

**Answer 5**
*   **Malfunctioning Subsystem:** The **electrical** system is malfunctioning. Plugging in a high-draw appliance (the vacuum) caused the circuit to overload and trip the breaker (or blow a fuse), cutting power to that circuit.
*   **First Step:** Based on "Safety First," the first step is to locate the main electrical panel and ensure the breaker for that circuit is turned off before you unplug the vacuum or attempt to inspect the outlet. This prevents any risk of electric shock.

**Answer 6**
The three interconnected subsystems are the **building envelope**, the **HVAC system**, and the **structural system**.
*   **Interaction Explained:**
    1.  The **building envelope** is failing at the window, which is either poorly sealed or not insulated well. This allows cold outside air to make the interior surface of the window very cold.
    2.  The **HVAC system** is maintaining a warm, humid interior environment. When this warm, moist air comes into contact with the cold window surface, the moisture condenses into water (or frost, if it's cold enough).
    3.  This condensation runs down the window and is being absorbed by the drywall and wood framing of the wall, which are components of the **structural system**. This moisture absorption leads to physical damage and promotes mold growth.
