## Exercises

**Exercise 1**
Imagine you are looking at a house plan for an unfurnished room. The only symbols inside the room's boundary are for a toilet, a single-basin sink, and a shower/tub combination. Based on these architectural symbols, what is the intended function of this room?

**Exercise 2**
A plan snippet shows a bedroom door. The symbol for the door is a straight line for the door itself and a quarter-circle arc showing its path. The arc swings *into* the bedroom, ending near the adjacent wall. If you were hired to move a large, heavy dresser into this room, what is the first practical step you would need to take inside the room after opening the door, and why?

**Exercise 3**
You are tasked with upgrading a ceiling light fixture in a living room. The house plan shows the light fixture symbol in the center of the ceiling. A thin, dashed line connects the light symbol to a switch symbol (`S`) on the wall next to the main doorway. What does this dashed line tell you about the relationship between the switch and the light?

**Exercise 4**
A homeowner wants to mount a new, heavy flat-screen TV on the wall separating the family room from the kitchen. Looking at the plan, you see two duplex outlet symbols (a circle with two parallel lines) on that specific wall in the family room. What potential, unseen hazard should you check for before drilling any mounting holes?

**Exercise 5**
You are using a house plan with a listed scale of 1/4" = 1'-0" to estimate materials for a small renovation. You need to install a new vinyl baseboard along one continuous wall in a den. Using a ruler, you measure the length of this wall on the paper plan, and it is exactly 4.5 inches. What is the actual length of the wall in feet, and which tool from your prior 'Essential Hand Tools' lesson is the most appropriate for marking your cut on the new baseboard?

**Exercise 6**
A work order says there is a slow drip from the faucet of the main bathroom sink. The house plan shows the location of the sink fixture, but it does not show the specific plumbing lines or local shut-off valves. Drawing on your knowledge of 'Utility Shut-offs' and 'The Home as a System', what is the *first* practical action you should take to stop the water flow before you begin disassembling the faucet, and why is this preferable to immediately shutting off water to the entire house?

---

## Answer Key

**Answer 1**
The room is a full bathroom. The symbols for a toilet, sink, and shower/tub are standard representations for plumbing fixtures that define the function of such a space, even without any furniture or labels.

**Answer 2**
The first step would be to clear the area where the door swings open. Because the plan shows the door swings *into* the room, the dresser cannot be placed in the door's swing path. Clearing that space first ensures you have enough room to fully open the door and maneuver the furniture without it getting blocked or damaging the wall/door.

**Answer 3**
The dashed line indicates a switched connection. It tells you that the switch on the wall is electrically connected to, and therefore controls, the ceiling light fixture. This allows you to know which switch to test or de-energize before working on that specific light.

**Answer 4**
The outlet symbols indicate the presence of electrical wiring inside that wall. Before drilling, you must check for the location of these wires to avoid the severe hazards of electric shock or causing a short. You would need to use a non-contact voltage tester or a stud finder with an AC detection feature to locate the wiring paths.

**Answer 5**
**Calculation:**
- The scale is 1/4 inch on the plan equals 1 foot in reality.
- This means every full inch on the plan represents 4 feet (since 1 inch = 4 x 1/4 inch).
- The wall measures 4.5 inches on the plan.
- Actual length = 4.5 inches * 4 feet/inch = 18 feet.
The actual length of the wall is 18 feet.

**Tool Selection:**
From the 'Essential Hand Tools' lesson, a combination square or a speed square would be the most appropriate tool for marking a straight, 90-degree cut line on the baseboard before cutting. A tape measure would be used to find the 18-foot mark.

**Answer 6**
The first action is to look for local shut-off valves, typically located in the cabinet directly beneath the sink. These are known as "angle stops" or "fixture shut-offs."

This is preferable because it isolates the problem to a single fixture. Based on 'The Home as a System', shutting off only the bathroom sink allows the rest of the house's water system (kitchen, other bathrooms, laundry) to remain fully operational. Shutting off water to the entire house is a much larger disruption and should be reserved for situations where the local shut-off fails or the repair involves the main supply lines.
