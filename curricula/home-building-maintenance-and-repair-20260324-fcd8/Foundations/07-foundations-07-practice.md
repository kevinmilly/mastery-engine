## Exercises

**Exercise 1**
A connection on the flexible supply line under your bathroom sink has started spraying water. You have a bucket and some towels. Of the two shut-off valves you know about—the main house shut-off in the basement and the small valve directly behind the toilet—which is the more appropriate one to use first? Justify your choice.

**Exercise 2**
You are preparing to install a new ceiling fan in a bedroom. You have located the home's main electrical panel in the garage. The panel has a large, single "Main" breaker at the top and two columns of smaller, numbered breakers below. Describe the specific steps you would take at the panel to ensure the work area is safe, while minimizing disruption to the rest of the house.

**Exercise 3**
You are at a friend's house, and they want to replace their old, leaking water heater. You locate the water heater in a utility closet. There is a single lever-style shut-off valve on the cold water pipe going *into* the top of the tank. If you close this valve, is it now safe to disconnect both the hot and cold water lines from the old heater? Explain why or why not.

**Exercise 4**
You are cooking and notice that the flame on your gas stove is weak and yellow instead of strong and blue. You don't smell gas, but you suspect a problem with the gas supply. You know where the main gas meter and shut-off valve are located outside. Is this a situation where you should shut off the main gas supply yourself? Explain the reasoning behind your decision.

**Exercise 5**
You plan to demolish a small, non-load-bearing interior wall between a kitchen and dining room. The wall contains a light switch and a standard electrical outlet. Based on what you have learned about utility shut-offs and site safety, list the sequence of utility-related actions you must perform *before* you start swinging a sledgehammer. For each action, briefly explain its purpose.

**Exercise 6**
During a heavy rainstorm, you hear a loud pop from your basement, and several lights go out. You go to investigate and find water seeping in through a foundation wall and flowing across the floor directly towards your main electrical panel. You are wearing rubber-soled boots. Should your first action be to wade through the shallow water to shut off the main breaker? Explain the critical risks in this scenario and justify the single safest immediate action.

---

## Answer Key

**Answer 1**
The more appropriate valve to use is the small one directly behind the toilet (the fixture shut-off).

**Reasoning:** This is a localized leak. Using the fixture shut-off will stop the water flow to the toilet specifically, allowing you to make the repair while keeping the water on for the rest of the house (sinks, showers, etc.). Shutting off the main house valve would work, but it is unnecessarily disruptive for such a minor, contained problem. The principle is to use the most local shut-off available for the problem at hand.

**Answer 2**
To safely de-energize the bedroom circuit while minimizing disruption, you should shut off only the individual circuit breaker for that room.

**Reasoning:**
1.  **Identify the Breaker:** First, you would need to identify which of the smaller breakers controls the bedroom circuit. If the panel is not labeled, you would need to test them by turning on the bedroom light and then flipping breakers one by one until it goes out.
2.  **Shut off the Individual Breaker:** Once identified, you would flip that specific breaker to the "OFF" position.
3.  **Verify Power is Off:** Use a voltage tester at the fixture's wiring to confirm that the power is truly off before beginning work.
This approach is correct because it isolates the work area, which is the primary safety goal, without needlessly cutting power to the refrigerator, router, and other essential appliances in the home. The main breaker is for emergencies or when the entire system needs to be de-energized.

**Answer 3**
No, it is not yet safe to disconnect both lines.

**Reasoning:** The shut-off valve on the cold water inlet only stops *new* cold water from entering the tank. The tank itself is still full of hot water under pressure. Furthermore, this valve does not stop hot water from potentially flowing backward out of the house's hot water pipes (a phenomenon called backflow) and out of the hot water connection you are trying to disconnect. To work safely, you would need to shut off the main water supply to the house and then drain the tank.

**Answer 4**
No, this is not a situation that calls for shutting off the main gas supply yourself.

**Reasoning:** The main gas shut-off is intended for emergencies only, specifically when you can smell gas and suspect a leak that poses an immediate fire or explosion hazard. A poorly performing appliance does not constitute that kind of emergency. The correct action is to stop using the appliance and call the gas utility company or a qualified appliance technician to diagnose the problem. The rule is: only shut the gas off if you smell gas and suspect a leak. A professional must always be the one to turn it back on.

**Answer 5**
The correct sequence of utility-related actions is:
1.  **Shut off the specific electrical circuit(s):** Go to the main panel and turn off the breaker(s) for the outlet and light switch in that wall. This is to prevent electric shock when you encounter the wiring during demolition.
2.  **Verify power is off:** Use a non-contact voltage tester to confirm there is no power at the switch or outlet. This is a critical safety verification step.
3.  **Shut off the main water supply:** Turn off the main water shut-off valve for the house. The purpose is precautionary. Since you are opening a wall in a kitchen, there is a risk of hitting a hidden water supply or drain line you were not aware of. Shutting off the water prevents a major flood if you accidentally break a pipe.

**Answer 6**
No, you absolutely should not wade through the water to shut off the main breaker.

**Reasoning:**
*   **Critical Risk:** Water is an excellent conductor of electricity. The moment you step into the water, which is in contact with a potentially compromised and energized electrical panel, your body could become the path for electricity to travel to the ground, resulting in severe electrocution. The rubber soles of your boots do not offer guaranteed protection in this situation.
*   **Safest Immediate Action:** The single safest immediate action is to **evacuate the area immediately and call 911** from a safe location. The fire department and the electric utility company have the training and equipment to handle this extremely hazardous "water-plus-electricity" emergency. This situation is beyond the scope of a DIY repair; the priority is personal safety, which means recognizing when *not* to approach the shut-offs.
