## The Hook
After this lesson, you will be able to describe the background scenery of a memory, transforming a simple list of past events into a vivid story.

Think of the Spanish past tenses as a film director's tools. The preterite tense, which you already know, is the **action camera**. It captures specific, completed events: the hero *jumped* (`saltó`), the car *exploded* (`explotó`), the credits *rolled* (`rodaron`). The imperfect tense is the **scenery camera**. It sets the scene and describes the ongoing context: the sun *was shining* (`brillaba`), the crowd *was cheering* (`gritaba`), the mood *was* tense (`era`). A great story needs both.

## Why It Matters
Without the imperfect tense, you can’t tell a compelling story in Spanish. You can only report a series of facts.

Imagine trying to tell a friend about a memorable concert using only the preterite. You could say: “I *arrived* at 8. The band *played* 15 songs. I *left* at 11.” It sounds like a police report. It’s dry and lacks any sense of atmosphere.

The moment you try to describe the scene—"The lights *were* dim, the music *was* loud, and everyone *was dancing* and *singing*”—you hit a wall. You need the imperfect to paint the picture, to describe how things were, what used to happen, or what was going on in the background when a specific event occurred. Skipping this tense means you can state what happened, but you can never explain *what it was like*.

## The Ladder
The imperfect tense describes past actions or states that were ongoing, habitual, or incomplete from the narrator's perspective. It provides the backdrop for the main events.

#### Step 1: Forming Regular Imperfect Verbs

The good news is that forming regular imperfect verbs is simpler and more consistent than the preterite. There are only two sets of endings to learn.

**For -AR verbs (like `hablar`, to speak):**
Remove the `-ar` and add these endings.

| Pronoun | Ending | Example: `hablar` |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| yo | `-aba` | `hablaba` (I was speaking) |
| tú | `-abas` | `hablabas` (you were speaking) |
| él/ella/ud. | `-aba` | `hablaba` (he/she/you were speaking) |
| nosotros | `-ábamos` | `hablábamos` (we were speaking) |
| vosotros | `-abais` | `hablabais` (you all were speaking) |
| ellos/ellas/uds. | `-aban` | `hablaban` (they/you all were speaking) |

Notice the `yo` and `él/ella/usted` forms are identical (`hablaba`). You'll rely on context to know who is doing the action.

**For -ER and -IR verbs (like `comer`, to eat, and `vivir`, to live):**
This is even easier: -ER and -IR verbs share the exact same set of endings. Just remove the infinitive ending and add these.

| Pronoun | Ending | Example: `comer` | Example: `vivir` |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| yo | `-ía` | `comía` (I was eating) | `vivía` (I was living) |
| tú | `-ías` | `comías` (you were eating) | `vivías` (you were living) |
| él/ella/ud. | `-ía` | `comía` (he/she was eating) | `vivía` (he/she was living) |
| nosotros | `-íamos`| `comíamos` (we were eating) | `vivíamos` (we were living) |
| vosotros | `-íais` | `comíais` (you all were eating) | `vivíais` (you all were living) |
| ellos/ellas/uds. | `-ían` | `comían` (they were eating) | `vivían` (they were living) |

The key here is the accent on the `í` in every single form. It ensures the stress falls in the right place.

#### Step 2: The Three (and Only Three) Irregular Verbs

Unlike the preterite with its many irregulars, the imperfect tense only has three. Memorize these, and you've mastered all imperfect conjugations.

**1. `ir` (to go)**
You don't use the regular `-ía` endings. Instead, it becomes:
`iba`, `ibas`, `iba`, `íbamos`, `ibais`, `iban`
*Example: `Cuando era niño, iba al parque.` (When I was a child, I used to go to the park.)*

**2. `ser` (to be)**
This is essential for descriptions. It becomes:
`era`, `eras`, `era`, `éramos`, `erais`, `eran`
*Example: `La casa era azul.` (The house was blue.)*

**3. `ver` (to see)**
This one is only slightly irregular. It keeps its `ve-` stem before adding the regular `-ía` endings.
`veía`, `veías`, `veía`, `veíamos`, `veíais`, `veían`
*Example: `Yo veía mi programa favorito todos los martes.` (I used to watch my favorite show every Tuesday.)*

#### The Implication

You now have a complete toolkit for past-tense narration. The preterite (`llegó` - he arrived) provides the plot points. The imperfect (`llovía` - it was raining) provides the color, context, and setting. A skilled speaker weaves them together to tell a full story.

## Worked Reality
Let’s walk through a simple memory that shows how these two tenses work together. Imagine you're telling a friend about the time you met your partner.

**Sentence 1: "It was a cold December night, and it was snowing."**
This is pure background and description. You're setting the scene. Both verbs will be in the imperfect.
- *to be*: `ser` -> `Era una noche fría de diciembre...`
- *to snow*: `nevar` -> `...y nevaba.`
**Full sentence: `Era una noche fría de diciembre y nevaba.`**

**Sentence 2: "I was waiting for the bus, and I was listening to music."**
This describes ongoing actions—what you *were doing* without a specified endpoint. Both are imperfect.
- *to wait*: `esperar` -> `Yo esperaba el autobús...`
- *to listen*: `escuchar` -> `...y escuchaba música.`
**Full sentence: `Yo esperaba el autobús y escuchaba música.`**

**Sentence 3: "Suddenly, a woman asked me for the time."**
This is the plot point! A new, specific, completed action interrupted the background scene. This verb must be in the preterite.
- *to ask*: `preguntar` -> `...una mujer me preguntó la hora.`
**Full sentence: `De repente, una mujer me preguntó la hora.`**

Putting it all together, you get a story, not a list:
*`Era una noche fría de diciembre y nevaba. Yo esperaba el autobús y escuchaba música. De repente, una mujer me preguntó la hora.`*

The imperfect (`era`, `nevaba`, `esperaba`, `escuchaba`) paints the picture of the cold, snowy night and your ongoing actions. The preterite (`preguntó`) delivers the key event that moved the story forward.

## Friction Point
The most common friction point is thinking the choice between preterite and imperfect is about the **duration of the action**.

**The wrong mental model:** "The preterite is for short actions, and the imperfect is for long actions."

**Why it's tempting:** This is a common shortcut used to simplify the concept. "I sneezed" feels short (preterite), and "I was sleeping" feels long (imperfect). It works sometimes, but it breaks down quickly and leads to major errors. For example, "The Roman Empire existed for centuries" uses the preterite (`existió`) because we are viewing its entire existence as one completed block of time.

**The correct mental model:** The choice is about **perspective**, not a stopwatch. Are you viewing the action as a completed, self-contained event, or as part of the ongoing background scenery?

-   **Preterite:** You are narrating a finished event. It had a beginning and an end in your story.
    -   `Leí el libro.` (I read the book.) -> I finished it. It's a completed accomplishment.
-   **Imperfect:** You are describing a scene or an action that was in progress. Its completion is not the point.
    -   `Leía el libro.` (I was reading the book.) -> This describes what I was doing when something else happened, or what my general state was. Whether I finished it is unknown and irrelevant to the statement.

Think of it this way: The preterite puts an event in a frame and hangs it on the wall. The imperfect describes the color of the wall it’s hanging on.
