## Exercises

**Exercise 1**
Combine the following two sentences into a single, fluid sentence using the most appropriate relative pronoun (`que` or `quien`). Explain your choice.

*   *El artista recibió un premio.*
*   *Hablé con el artista ayer.*

**Exercise 2**
Both sentences below are grammatically correct, but one is stylistically preferable in a formal or literary context due to its clarity. Identify the preferred sentence and explain what makes it clearer or more formal than the other.

*   **Sentence A:** La conferencia, sobre la que leíste en el periódico de la mañana, fue cancelada.
*   **Sentence B:** La conferencia, sobre la cual leíste en el periódico de la mañana, fue cancelada.

**Exercise 3**
A fellow student has written the following sentence, but it contains a common error related to relative pronouns. Identify the error, explain the rule that was broken, and rewrite the sentence correctly.

*Incorrect Sentence:* "Los problemas que te preocupan tienen soluciones."

**Exercise 4**
Explain the difference in meaning between the following two sentences. Specifically, describe what each sentence implies about the speaker's siblings.

*   **Sentence 1:** Mi hermano, que vive en Madrid, es médico.
*   **Sentence 2:** Mi hermano que vive en Madrid es médico.

**Exercise 5**
Translate and combine the following ideas into a single, complex Spanish sentence. Your sentence must correctly use a relative pronoun and choose the appropriate past tense (preterite or imperfect) for the verb in the relative clause. Justify your choice of both the pronoun and the verb tense.

*Idea to convey:* The car was having problems. I sold that car last week.

**Exercise 6**
Imagine you are describing the type of boss you hope to find for your next job. You are expressing a wish or a requirement for a hypothetical person. Combine the following ideas into one sentence that uses a relative pronoun and the correct verb mood (indicative or subjunctive). Explain your reasoning for the verb mood you chose.

*   *I am looking for a boss.*
*   *This boss must understand the importance of work-life balance.*

## Answer Key

**Answer 1**
**Correct Sentence:** El artista, con **quien** hablé ayer, recibió un premio.

**Reasoning:**
When a relative pronoun refers to a person and follows a preposition (in this case, "con"), the pronoun `quien` (or `quienes` for plural) must be used. The pronoun `que` is incorrect in this specific structure ("con que hablé"). The commas are used because "con quien hablé ayer" is a non-restrictive clause, adding extra information about a specific, already identified artist.

**Answer 2**
**Preferred Sentence:** Sentence B: La conferencia, sobre **la cual** leíste en el periódico de la mañana, fue cancelada.

**Reasoning:**
While both `que` (preceded by an article like `la que`) and `el/la cual` can be used after prepositions, `el/la cual` is often preferred for a few reasons that apply here:
1.  **Formality:** `El/la cual` is generally considered more formal and literary than `el/la que`.
2.  **Clarity:** It is especially useful after multi-syllable prepositions (like "sobre") or when the antecedent is far from the relative clause. In this sentence, it adds a touch of formality and clearly links back to "La conferencia," avoiding any potential ambiguity.

**Answer 3**
**Error:** The sentence is grammatically **correct** as written. The exercise presents a "trick" scenario to test deep understanding of restrictive clauses.

**Reasoning:**
The sentence "Los problemas que te preocupan tienen soluciones" uses `que` correctly. Here, `que te preocupan` is a restrictive clause. It is essential information that specifies *which* problems have solutions (only the ones that worry you). In restrictive clauses that refer to either people or things, `que` is the correct and most common relative pronoun. There is no preposition preceding the pronoun, so `quien` would be incorrect. The student might mistakenly think `el cual` or another form is "better," but for a simple, restrictive clause like this, `que` is perfectly appropriate and standard. This tests the student's confidence in applying the most fundamental rule without overcomplicating it.

**Answer 4**
**Difference in Meaning:** The presence or absence of commas changes the clause from non-restrictive to restrictive, which fundamentally alters the information conveyed about the speaker's family.

*   **Sentence 1 (with commas):** *Mi hermano, que vive en Madrid, es médico.*
    The clause "que vive en Madrid" is non-restrictive (explicativa). It simply adds extra information about the brother. This sentence structure implies that the speaker has **only one brother**.

*   **Sentence 2 (without commas):** *Mi hermano que vive en Madrid es médico.*
    The clause "que vive en Madrid" is restrictive (especificativa). It is essential information used to specify *which* brother is being discussed. This sentence implies that the speaker has **more than one brother**, and they are distinguishing the one in Madrid from others who live elsewhere.

**Answer 5**
**Correct Sentence:** El coche **que tenía** problemas **lo vendí** la semana pasada. (or *Vendí el coche que tenía problemas la semana pasada.*)

**Reasoning:**
1.  **Relative Pronoun:** `que` is used because it refers to a thing (el coche) and is the subject of the relative clause.
2.  **Verb Tense:**
    *   `tenía` (imperfect): The verb in the relative clause is in the imperfect tense because "having problems" was an ongoing state or background condition of the car. It describes what the car *was like*.
    *   `vendí` (preterite): The main verb of the sentence is in the preterite because selling the car was a specific, completed action that occurred "la semana pasada."

**Answer 6**
**Correct Sentence:** Busco un jefe **que entienda** la importancia del equilibrio entre la vida laboral y personal.

**Reasoning:**
1.  **Relative Pronoun:** `que` is used because it connects the main clause to the subordinate clause and refers to the antecedent (un jefe).
2.  **Verb Mood (Subjunctive):** The verb `entender` must be in the present subjunctive (`entienda`). This is because the main clause "Busco un jefe" expresses a search for an antecedent that is unknown, hypothetical, or may not even exist. The speaker is not referring to a specific boss they already know, but rather describing the characteristics of a desired, non-specific person. This uncertainty or non-existence of the antecedent is a classic trigger for the subjunctive mood in adjective clauses. If the speaker knew the boss, they would use the indicative: "Tengo un jefe que *entiende*..."
