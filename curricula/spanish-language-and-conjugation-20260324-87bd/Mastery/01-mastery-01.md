## The Hook
After this lesson, you will be able to state precisely what someone *did* at a specific point in the past.

Think of the Spanish preterite tense as the timestamp on a digital photograph. When you look at a photo's data, it tells you the exact date and time the picture was taken: "June 10th, 2:15 PM." It captures a single, completed moment. The preterite does the same for actions. It marks an event as finished, boxed up, and placed at a specific point on a timeline.

## Why It Matters
The preterite is your primary tool for telling a story. Without it, you can't narrate a sequence of events. If a friend asks you, "¿Qué hiciste el fin de semana?" ("What did you do this weekend?"), and you don't know the preterite, you are stuck. You might be able to say "I walk in the park" (present tense), but you can't say "I walk*ed* in the park."

The friction of not knowing this is immediate and severe. You cannot answer the simplest questions about your day, your trip, or what happened in a movie. You can describe what *is*, but you cannot recount what *was*. Learning the preterite is the first major step from just describing your surroundings to telling your own story.

## The Ladder
The Spanish preterite is used for actions that were completed in the past. Think of them as individual points on a timeline. The action started, it finished, and it's done.

To use the preterite, we change the ending of the verb. This is called **conjugation**. In Spanish, regular verbs fall into three families, identified by the last two letters of their non-conjugated form (the infinitive): **-ar**, **-er**, and **-ir**.

The process is a simple, two-step formula:
1.  Remove the **-ar**, **-er**, or **-ir** ending from the infinitive verb to find its **stem**.
2.  Add the correct preterite ending to the stem based on who performed the action.

**1. Regular -AR Verbs**

Let's use the verb `comprar` (to buy). The stem is `compr-`.

Here are the endings for **-ar** verbs in the preterite:

| Pronoun | Ending | Example: `comprar` | Translation |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| yo (I) | **-é** | Yo compr**é** | I bought |
| tú (you, informal) | **-aste** | Tú compr**aste** | You bought |
| él/ella/usted (he/she/you formal) | **-ó** | Él compr**ó** | He bought |
| nosotros (we) | **-amos** | Nosotros compr**amos** | We bought |
| vosotros (you all, Spain) | **-asteis** | Vosotros compr**asteis** | You all bought |
| ellos/ellas/ustedes (they/you all)| **-aron** | Ellas compr**aron**| They bought |

Notice the written accents on the `yo` and `él/ella/usted` forms. These are not optional. They change the pronunciation and the meaning entirely.

**2. Regular -ER and -IR Verbs**

Here's the good news: regular **-er** and **-ir** verbs use the exact same set of endings in the preterite. You learn one pattern and get two families of verbs for free.

Let's use `vender` (to sell) and `abrir` (to open). The stems are `vend-` and `abr-`.

Here are the endings for **-er** and **-ir** verbs in the preterite:

| Pronoun | Ending | Example: `vender` | Example: `abrir` | Translation |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| yo (I) | **-í** | Yo vend**í** | Yo abr**í** | I sold / I opened |
| tú (you, informal) | **-iste** | Tú vend**iste** | Tú abr**iste** | You sold / You opened |
| él/ella/usted (he/she/you form.) | **-ió** | Ella vend**ió** | Ella abr**ió** | She sold / She opened |
| nosotros (we) | **-imos** | Nosotros vend**imos**| Nosotros abr**imos**| We sold / We opened |
| vosotros (you all, Spain) | **-isteis** | Vosotros vend**isteis**| Vosotros abr**isteis**| You all sold / You all opened |
| ellos/ellas/ustedes (they/you all)| **-ieron** | Ellos vend**ieron**| Ellos abr**ieron** | They sold / They opened |

Again, the accents on `yo` and `él/ella/usted` are crucial.

By learning these two sets of endings (-ar, and the shared -er/-ir set), you can now conjugate hundreds of regular Spanish verbs to describe specific, completed actions in the past.

## Worked Reality
Imagine a friend is asking you about your business trip to Madrid last month. You need to explain what happened on the first day.

Your friend asks: “¿Y qué pasó el primer día? ¿Visitaste a los clientes?” (So what happened the first day? Did you visit the clients?)

You reply, constructing the story with the preterite:
“Sí. El lunes, **llegué** al hotel por la mañana. **Dejé** mis maletas y **corrí** a la primera reunión. Después, el equipo y yo **comimos** en un restaurante cerca de la oficina. Por la tarde, **recibimos** buenas noticias sobre el contrato y **decidimos** celebrar.”

Let's break that down:
*   **llegué** - "I arrived." From `llegar` (-ar verb), `yo` form. (Note: `llegar` has a small spelling change in the `yo` form to preserve the sound, which you'll learn about later, but the ending is the standard `-é`.)
*   **Dejé** - "I left (behind)." From `dejar` (-ar verb), `yo` form. You took the stem `dej-` and added `-é`.
*   **corrí** - "I ran." From `correr` (-er verb), `yo` form. You took the stem `corr-` and added `-í`.
*   **comimos** - "we ate." From `comer` (-er verb), `nosotros` form. Stem `com-` plus ending `-imos`.
*   **recibimos** - "we received." From `recibir` (-ir verb), `nosotros` form. Stem `recib-` plus ending `-imos`.
*   **decidimos** - "we decided." From `decidir` (-ir verb), `nosotros` form. Stem `decid-` plus ending `-imos`.

Each verb is a timestamped event. You arrived. You left your bags. You ran. You ate. You received news. You decided. It’s a clear, sequential story of completed actions, all made possible by the preterite.

## Friction Point
The most common and most critical mistake is mixing up the `yo` (I) and the `él/ella/usted` (he/she/you) forms.

The wrong mental model is: "The little accent mark is just a minor detail; `hable` and `habló` are basically the same." This is tempting because in English, the verb doesn't change between "I" and "he" in the past tense ("I talked," "he talked").

The correct mental model is: **The accent is not a detail; it is the entire signal that changes the actor.** It carries as much meaning as the first letter of the word.

Consider the verb `hablar` (to speak):
*   `habl**é**` (I spoke). The stress is on the last syllable: ha-BLÉ.
*   `habl**ó**` (He/She/You spoke). The stress is also on the last syllable: ha-BLÓ.

If you are telling a story about what you did and you say, "Ayer, *habló* con mi jefe," you have accidentally said, "Yesterday, *he spoke* with my boss." You have written yourself out of your own story. The meaning is completely different.

Always double-check your accents for `yo` and `él/ella/usted`. Saying them aloud helps. `Habl**é**` ends in a sharp "ay" sound. `Habl**ó**` ends in a round "oh" sound. They are as different as "I did" and "he did."
