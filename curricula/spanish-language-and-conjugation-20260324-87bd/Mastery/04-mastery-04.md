## The Hook
Mastering this topic will allow you to tell a compelling story in Spanish, making the difference between a confusing list of facts and a vivid, engaging narrative.

Imagine you're building a scene with LEGO bricks. The **imperfect** tense is the large, green baseplate you start with; it's the landscape, the setting. It’s the steady, unchanging foundation: "The ground *was* green, the sky *was* blue." The **preterite** tense is every individual brick you snap onto that baseplate to make something happen. "I *placed* a red brick. Then I *added* a window. The wall *fell* over." The preterite builds the action, step by step, on the foundation set by the imperfect.

## Why It Matters
The distinction between preterite and imperfect is not a minor grammatical detail; it fundamentally changes the meaning of what you're saying. If you misuse them, you can’t tell a clear story. Your listener won't be able to distinguish between the background scenery and the main plot points.

Imagine you are a witness telling a police officer what you saw.
- If you say, `El coche se acercaba`, you're using the imperfect. You're describing an ongoing scene: "The car *was approaching*." It sets the stage but doesn't state what happened next.
- If you say, `El coche se acercó`, you're using the preterite. You're stating a completed event: "The car *approached* (and then stopped)." This is a specific plot point.

Mixing these up could mean the difference between describing a general situation ("cars were often on that street") and stating a key fact of the incident ("that specific car pulled up"). Getting this wrong creates a confusing narrative where the listener can't tell what the main events were versus what the background context was.

## The Ladder
In the previous lessons, you learned *how* to form the preterite and imperfect tenses. Now, we focus on the decision of *when* to use each. This choice isn't about objective reality; it's about what you, the speaker, want to emphasize.

**1. The Core Function: Plot vs. Description**

Think of yourself as a narrator. Your job is to communicate two different kinds of information:
-   **Plot:** The main events that happen one after another to move the story forward.
-   **Description:** The background details that set the scene, describe people, or explain the context.

Spanish has a dedicated tense for each of these narrative jobs.

-   **Preterite is for Plot.** It reports completed, single-event actions. It answers the question, "What happened next?" These are the bricks you add to the baseplate. They are distinct events with a clear end.
    -   `Llegué a la fiesta.` (I *arrived* at the party.) - A single, completed action.
    -   `Comimos pastel.` (We *ate* cake.) - Another completed action.
    -   `Él se fue.` (He *left*.) - A third completed action.

-   **Imperfect is for Description.** It describes ongoing conditions, habitual actions, or what *was happening* in the background. It answers the question, "What was it like?" or "What was going on?" This is your LEGO baseplate.
    -   `La música sonaba.` (The music *was playing*.) - An ongoing background condition.
    -   `Mucha gente bailaba.` (Many people *were dancing*.) - Describing the general scene.
    -   `Yo estaba feliz.` (I *was* happy.) - Describing an internal state or feeling.

**2. How They Work Together**

The real power comes when you combine them in the same story, or even the same sentence. The imperfect sets the stage, and the preterite interrupts it with a key event.

Study this classic structure: `Imperfect action` **cuando** `Preterite action.`
-   `Yo leía un libro cuando sonó el teléfono.`
-   **`leía` (imperfect):** This is what *was happening*. It's the background scene. I was in the middle of reading; the action is not presented as completed.
-   **`sonó` (preterite):** This is the event that *happened*. It interrupted the background scene. The phone ringing is a specific, single, completed event.
-   **Translation:** "I *was reading* a book when the phone *rang*."

If you were to use the preterite for both (`Yo leí un libro cuando sonó el teléfono`), it would mean "I *read* a book (from start to finish) when the phone *rang*." It sounds like a sequence: maybe the phone ringing was your signal to start and finish a book, which is nonsensical. The tense choice creates the meaning.

**3. Two Special Cases for the Imperfect**

Besides setting the scene, the imperfect has two other common descriptive jobs:

-   **Habitual Actions ("used to"):** To talk about things that happened repeatedly or habitually in the past.
    -   `Cuando era niño, jugaba al fútbol todos los días.` (When I was a child, I *used to play* soccer every day.)
    -   `jugaba` is imperfect because it wasn't a single event; it was a repeated, habitual routine.

-   **Descriptions of People, Places, and Things:** To tell us what something or someone *was like*.
    -   `La casa era grande y tenía ventanas azules.` (The house *was* big and *had* blue windows.)
    -   `era` and `tenía` are imperfect because they are describing the state or characteristics of the house, not an action that it performed.

The preterite reports what happened. The imperfect describes what was going on, what used to happen, or what things were like.

## Worked Reality
Let's analyze a simple story to see the tenses in action. You're telling a friend about your morning.

"This morning I was walking to the cafe. It was a beautiful day; the sun was shining and it was warm. Suddenly, I saw my professor. He was talking on his phone. I said hello, and he smiled."

Let's break down the verb choices for a Spanish translation.

1.  **`caminaba`** (was walking)
    -   *Spanish:* `Esta mañana, yo caminaba al café.`
    -   *Tense:* Imperfect.
    -   *Why:* This is the background action. It's the scene-setter for the story. You were *in the middle* of walking when the main events occurred. This is the LEGO baseplate.

2.  **`era`** (was), **`brillaba`** (was shining), **`hacía calor`** (was warm)
    -   *Spanish:* `Era un día bonito; el sol brillaba y hacía calor.`
    -   *Tense:* Imperfect.
    -   *Why:* This is pure description. You're painting a picture of the setting—what the weather and day *were like*. No action is moving the plot forward.

3.  **`vi`** (saw)
    -   *Spanish:* `De repente, vi a mi profesor.`
    -   *Tense:* Preterite.
    -   *Why:* This is the first key event. It's a specific, completed action that interrupts the background scene of you walking on a nice day. *Snap*. A new brick is placed on the baseplate.

4.  **`hablaba`** (was talking)
    -   *Spanish:* `Él hablaba por teléfono.`
    -   *Tense:* Imperfect.
    -   *Why:* This describes what the professor was doing when you saw him. It's a description of his ongoing state, not a new plot point in your story. You are describing the scene of him on the phone.

5.  **`dije`** (said), **`sonrió`** (smiled)
    -   *Spanish:* `Le dije "hola", y él sonrió.`
    -   *Tense:* Preterite.
    -   *Why:* These are two more plot points that happened in sequence. First, you *said* hello (completed action). Second, he *smiled* (completed action). *Snap. Snap.* Two more bricks are added, concluding the short story.

The story flows logically because the tenses are doing their specific jobs: imperfect sets the scene, and preterite moves the plot.

## Friction Point
The most common misunderstanding is believing that the duration of an action determines the tense.

-   **The Wrong Mental Model:** "Short actions are preterite; long actions are imperfect."
-   **Why It's Tempting:** This often seems to work. `Saltó` (he jumped) feels short and is preterite. `Dormía` (he was sleeping) feels long and is imperfect. It's an easy but flawed shortcut.
-   **The Correct Mental Model:** The choice of tense is not about how long an action took in minutes or hours. It's about how the speaker is framing it: as a single, completed block of time, or as an ongoing, unfinished background scene.

Consider the verb `vivir` (to live).
-   `Viví en Madrid durante cinco años.` (I lived in Madrid for five years.)
    -   We use the **preterite** (`viví`) even though five years is a very long time. Why? Because the speaker is presenting that five-year period as a single, completed chapter of their life. It has a clear beginning and a clear end. It's a finished block.
-   `Cuando vivía en Madrid, iba al Museo del Prado todos los meses.` (When I lived in Madrid, I used to go to the Prado Museum every month.)
    -   Here, we use the **imperfect** (`vivía`) to set the scene. "Living in Madrid" is the background context for the habitual action that follows (`iba`, also imperfect). We are not focused on the beginning or end of living there, but on what life *was like* during that time.

The key is not duration, but **completion** and **narrative function**. Is the action a self-contained event in your story (preterite), or is it the backdrop against which the story happens (imperfect)?
