## The Hook
After this lesson, you'll be able to say "I am 30 years old," "I am hungry," and "I have a question" in Spanish, using the same powerful verb for all three.

Think of the verb `tener` as a Swiss Army knife. Its main blade is for "to have" in the sense of possession, like having a key or a book. But it also has other specific, built-in tools for special jobs, like expressing hunger, thirst, or age. You wouldn't use the knife's corkscrew to cut a rope, and you wouldn't use the blade to open a bottle. In the same way, Spanish uses the different functions of `tener` for very specific situations, some of which might surprise you.

## Why It Matters
If you don't master `tener` and its special expressions, you will constantly make a very specific, common error: translating "I am..." directly from English. For example, to say "I'm hot," a beginner might try `Estoy caliente`. This is a classic mistake. While technically a sentence, in many contexts it means something closer to "I'm sexually aroused," which is probably not what you're trying to communicate when you want someone to open a window.

The correct way is `Tengo calor`. Learning the `tener` expressions isn't just about vocabulary; it's about avoiding embarrassing and confusing mistakes. These phrases are fundamental to everyday conversation, and using them correctly is a major step toward sounding natural and being understood as you intend.

## The Ladder
`Tener` means "to have," but it's an irregular verb. It doesn't follow the standard pattern for `-ER` verbs you've learned. It actually combines two different types of irregularities.

**1. The Conjugation: A "Yo-Go" Verb with a Stem Change**

Let's look at the present-tense conjugation of `tener`. Notice the changes in the stem and the unique `yo` form.

| Subject Pronoun | Verb Form | Translation | Irregularity |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| yo | **tengo** | I have | The 'g' is added (a "yo-go" verb) |
| tú | **tienes** | you (informal) have | Stem changes from `e` -> `ie` |
| él/ella/usted | **tiene** | he/she/you (formal) have | Stem changes from `e` -> `ie` |
| nosotros/nosotras | tenemos | we have | Regular (no change) |
| vosotros/vosotras | tenéis | you all (informal) have | Regular (no change) |
| ellos/ellas/ustedes | **tienen** | they/you all have | Stem changes from `e` -> `ie` |

You've already seen stem-changing verbs that follow the "boot" pattern, where `nosotros` and `vosotros` are left out. `Tener` does this, too (`tienes`, `tiene`, `tienen`). The new piece is the `yo` form. `Tengo` doesn't have a stem change, but it gets a `g` inserted. This "yo-go" pattern appears in a handful of other very important verbs, so learning it now is crucial.

**2. The First Use: Possession**

The most straightforward use of `tener` is to talk about things you possess. This works exactly like "to have" in English.

*   *Tengo un coche rojo.* (I have a red car.)
*   *¿Tienes la hora?* (Do you have the time?)
*   *Mis padres tienen una casa en el campo.* (My parents have a house in the countryside.)

**3. The Second Use: Idiomatic Expressions**

This is where the other tools of our Swiss Army knife come in. Spanish uses `tener` with a noun to describe many states of being or feelings that English expresses with "to be" plus an adjective.

Think of it literally as "to have a feeling." You don't *are* hunger, you *have* hunger.

Here are the most essential `tener` expressions:

| Spanish Expression | Literal Meaning | Common English Translation |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **tener hambre** | to have hunger | to be hungry |
| **tener sed** | to have thirst | to be thirsty |
| **tener frío** | to have cold | to be cold |
| **tener calor** | to have heat | to be hot |
| **tener sueño** | to have sleepiness | to be sleepy |
| **tener prisa** | to have haste | to be in a hurry |
| **tener ___ años** | to have ___ years | to be ___ years old |
| **tener miedo** | to have fear | to be scared |
| **tener razón** | to have reason | to be right |

When you use these, you conjugate `tener` to match the person, but the second word (the noun) stays the same.

*   Example: To say "She is sleepy."
    1.  Subject is "She" (`ella`).
    2.  The `ella` form of `tener` is `tiene`.
    3.  The expression for "to be sleepy" is `tener sueño`.
    4.  Combine them: *Ella tiene sueño.*

## Worked Reality
Imagine you're catching up with a friend, David, who just moved for a new job.

You ask him about his new life:
**You:** *¿Qué tal tu apartamento nuevo?* (How's your new apartment?)
**David:** *Es pequeño, pero **tengo** todo lo que necesito. Y **tiene** una vista increíble de la ciudad.* (It's small, but I have everything I need. And it has an incredible view of the city.)

Here, David uses `tengo` for what he possesses and `tiene` to describe a characteristic the apartment possesses.

He continues, talking about his demanding job:
**David:** *El trabajo es intenso. Casi todas las noches, llego a casa y **tengo** mucho **sueño**. A veces no ceno porque no **tengo hambre**, solo quiero dormir.* (The job is intense. Almost every night, I get home and I'm very sleepy. Sometimes I don't eat dinner because I'm not hungry, I just want to sleep.)

In this part, he's not talking about possessing objects. He's using `tener sueño` and `tener hambre` to describe his physical state. He "has sleepiness" and "doesn't have hunger." Notice that he uses the `yo` form, `tengo`, because he is talking about himself.

You respond with some advice:
**You:** *Cuidado, David. **Tienes que** descansar. ¿Cuántos años **tienes**? ¿Veinticinco? No eres invencible.* (Be careful, David. You have to rest. How old are you? Twenty-five? You're not invincible.)

Here, you use `tienes` to ask his age, literally asking "How many years do you have?" You also see a new structure, `tener que`, which means "to have to do something"—another essential use of our multi-tool verb.

## Friction Point
The most common friction point is trying to use the logic of `ser` (permanent) vs. `estar` (temporary) for the `tener` expressions.

**The wrong mental model:** "Hunger is a temporary feeling, so I should use `estar`. `Estoy hambre` must be correct."

**Why it's tempting:** This thinking is perfectly logical based on what you know about temporary states. You learned that `estoy cansado` means "I am tired," so it feels consistent to say `estoy hambre` for "I am hungry."

**The correct mental model:** You must treat these specific expressions as a fixed set of rules that operate outside the normal `ser`/`estar` system. For hunger, thirst, cold, heat, age, and fear, Spanish has decided that these are not states you *are*, but rather conditions you *have*. Think of them as nouns you possess temporarily: you possess "hunger," you possess "heat," you possess "twenty-five years." This is a fundamental conceptual difference between the languages. Memorize this list as a special category, and you will avoid the problem entirely.
