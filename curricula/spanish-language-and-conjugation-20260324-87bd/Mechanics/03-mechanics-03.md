## The Hook
With this lesson, you will be able to correctly describe actions like living, writing, and opening, completing your foundation for using any regular Spanish verb in the present tense.

Think of Spanish verbs as belonging to three different families of locks: -AR, -ER, and -IR. The verb's stem (like `habl-` or `com-`) is the master key blank that works for one whole family. The ending you add is like the specific cut made on that key, allowing it to turn the lock for a specific person (`yo`, `tú`, `nosotros`, etc.). You've learned the cutting patterns for -AR and -ER locks. Now, you'll learn the pattern for the -IR family, which is surprisingly similar to the -ERs.

## Why It Matters
Not knowing this final pattern means you can't talk about some of life's most basic activities. Imagine you're in an office in Madrid trying to explain a simple workflow. You want to say, "We receive the emails, we write the reports, and then we open the new files." Two of those three essential verbs—`escribir` (to write) and `abrir` (to open)—are -IR verbs.

If you mistakenly use an -ER pattern, you might say "*escribemos*" instead of "*escribimos*." A native speaker will understand you, but the error is jarring. It's like saying "we writed the reports." It instantly signals a lack of fundamental knowledge and can force your listener to mentally correct your grammar instead of focusing on your message, creating a small but constant barrier to smooth communication. Mastering -IR verbs closes this gap.

## The Ladder
You already have the core concept down from -AR and -ER verbs: find the stem, add the right ending for the subject. The good news is that -IR verbs follow this exact same process. Even better, they are nearly identical twins to -ER verbs.

Let's use the verb `vivir` (to live) as our guide.

**Step 1: Find the Stem**
Just like before, you find the stem by removing the infinitive ending. The infinitive is the "to ___" form of the verb.

*   Infinitive: `vivir`
*   Remove the `-ir`: `viv-`
*   The stem is `viv-`. This is the base you will build upon.

**Step 2: Add the Subject-Specific Ending**
Now, you attach the correct ending to the stem `viv-` based on who is doing the living. Here is the complete set of endings for regular -IR verbs in the present tense:

| Subject | Ending | Example: `vivir` (to live) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| `yo` (I) | `-o` | `Yo vivo` (I live) |
| `tú` (you, informal) | `-es` | `Tú vives` (You live) |
| `él / ella / usted` (he / she / you, formal) | `-e` | `Él vive` (He lives) |
| `nosotros / nosotras` (we) | `-imos` | `Nosotros vivimos` (We live) |
| `vosotros / vosotras` (you all, informal) | `-ís` | `Vosotros vivís` (You all live) |
| `ellos / ellas / ustedes` (they / you all, formal) | `-en` | `Ellos viven` (They live) |

**The Critical Pattern to Notice**
Look closely at this list and compare it to the -ER verb endings you already know (like for `comer`: `como`, `comes`, `come`, `comemos`, `coméis`, `comen`).

Do you see it? Four of the six endings are *exactly the same* as -ER verbs!
*   `yo`: `-o` (same)
*   `tú`: `-es` (same)
*   `él/ella/usted`: `-e` (same)
*   `ellos/ellas/ustedes`: `-en` (same)

The only places where -IR verbs differ from -ER verbs are in the `nosotros` and `vosotros` forms.
*   For `nosotros`, -ER verbs use `-emos` (`comemos`), but -IR verbs use **`-imos`** (`vivimos`).
*   For `vosotros`, -ER verbs use `-éis` (`coméis`), but -IR verbs use **`-ís`** (`vivís`).

This is the key takeaway: if you know how to conjugate an -ER verb, you already know most of the pattern for an -IR verb. You just need to remember to switch the vowel from 'e' to 'i' for "we" and "you all."

## Worked Reality
You're helping your new roommate, Javier, move into an apartment in a Spanish-speaking city. You need to communicate about dividing up tasks. The key verbs are `abrir` (to open) and `decidir` (to decide).

First, you need to open some boxes. You want to ask Javier, "Do you open this box?"
1.  **Verb:** `abrir` (to open)
2.  **Subject:** `tú` (Javier, informal)
3.  **Find the stem:** `abrir` -> `abr-`
4.  **Find the `tú` ending for -IR verbs:** It's `-es`.
5.  **Combine:** `abr-` + `-es` = `abres`.
6.  **You ask:** "¿Abres esta caja?"

Javier agrees and starts opening it. Meanwhile, your other two roommates, Maria and Carlos, arrive. You need to tell them what's happening. You want to say, "Javier and I are deciding where the furniture goes."
1.  **Verb:** `decidir` (to decide)
2.  **Subject:** "Javier and I," which is `nosotros`.
3.  **Find the stem:** `decidir` -> `decid-`
4.  **Find the `nosotros` ending for -IR verbs:** This is one of the special ones. It's `-imos`.
5.  **Combine:** `decid-` + `-imos` = `decidimos`.
6.  **You say:** "Javier y yo decidimos dónde van los muebles."

Finally, you see that Maria and Carlos are just standing there. You want to prompt them to help by saying, "You all (plural, formal) are living here too, right?"
1.  **Verb:** `vivir` (to live)
2.  **Subject:** `ustedes` (Maria and Carlos, formal/polite)
3.  **Find the stem:** `vivir` -> `viv-`
4.  **Find the `ustedes` ending for -IR verbs:** It's `-en`.
5.  **Combine:** `viv-` + `-en` = `viven`.
6.  **You say:** "Ustedes viven aquí también, ¿no?"

In this short interaction, you’ve naturally used three different subjects and three different -IR verbs, correctly applying the patterns for `tú`, `nosotros`, and `ustedes`.

## Friction Point
The most common mistake is assuming that since -ER and -IR verbs are so similar, they must be identical.

**The Wrong Mental Model:** "-ER and -IR verbs are conjugated exactly the same way."

**Why It's Tempting:** This is tempting because it's true 66% of the time! The endings for `yo`, `tú`, `él/ella/usted`, and `ellos/ellas/ustedes` are identical. The mind loves a simple pattern, and it's easy to over-generalize and assume the last two forms (`nosotros` and `vosotros`) follow suit. This leads learners to say "*vivemos*" instead of `vivimos`, or "*abremos*" instead of `abrimos`.

**The Correct Mental Model:** Think of -ER and -IR verbs as fraternal twins who dress almost alike. They wear the same hat (`-o`), shirt (`-es`, `-e`), and pants (`-en`). But they always wear different shoes. The `nosotros` and `vosotros` forms are the shoes.
*   The -ER twin wears 'e' shoes: `com**e**mos`, `com**é**is`.
*   The -IR twin wears 'i' shoes: `viv**i**mos`, `viv**í**s`.

Always check the shoes. When you see a `nosotros` or `vosotros` subject, pause and ask: is this an -ER verb or an -IR verb? That conscious check will prevent the most frequent error.
