## The Hook
After this lesson, you will be able to turn a simple statement into a direct, friendly command, like telling a friend "Listen to this song" instead of just "You listen to this song."

Think of a verb's present tense forms like the different gears in a manual car. First gear (`yo`), second gear (`tú`), third gear (`él/ella`) all describe movement that is happening. An affirmative command is like shifting into a special "action" gear. It doesn't just describe what's happening; it makes something happen. You're not reporting on the action, you're initiating it.

## Why It Matters
This isn't just a grammar rule; it's the core of giving instructions, advice, and making direct requests in everyday life. Without commands, you're stuck describing or asking questions when what you really need is action.

Imagine you're trying to help a friend parallel park in a tight spot. Saying, "You turn the wheel" (`Giras el volante`) is a description. It's slow and indirect. In the heat of the moment, you need to say, "**Turn** the wheel!" (`¡Gira el volante!`). Not knowing how to form this command creates a frustrating gap between what you want to say and what you can actually say, leading to confusion when clarity and speed are essential.

## The Ladder
Let's build the mechanism for giving these direct instructions to a friend (the `tú` form).

**Step 1: The Basic Rule for Regular Verbs**

Here’s the surprising shortcut: for the vast majority of Spanish verbs, the affirmative `tú` command is identical to the `él/ella/usted` form in the present tense.

You already know how to conjugate for `él/ella`. You've been practicing it since you learned present tense verbs. That work is about to pay off.

Let's see it in action:
*   For the verb **`hablar`** (to speak):
    *   He speaks -> `Él **habla**.`
    *   The command "Speak!" is -> `¡**Habla**!`
*   For the verb **`comer`** (to eat):
    *   She eats -> `Ella **come**.`
    *   The command "Eat!" is -> `¡**Come**!`
*   For the verb **`abrir`** (to open):
    *   You (formal) open -> `Usted **abre**.`
    *   The command "Open!" is -> `¡**Abre**!`

You simply find the third-person singular (he/she/you-formal) form, and you have your command.

**Step 2: The Irregular Verbs**

Just like in English ("Go!" not "Goes!"), Spanish has a small group of extremely common verbs with unique, short command forms. You can't guess these; you just have to learn them. Fortunately, there are only eight main ones.

| Verb | Meaning | Command Form |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| `venir` | to come | `**ven**` |
| `decir` | to say/tell | `**di**` |
| `salir` | to leave | `**sal**` |
| `hacer` | to do/make | `**haz**` |
| `tener` | to have | `**ten**` |
| `ir` | to go | `**ve**` |
| `poner` | to put | `**pon**` |
| `ser` | to be | `**sé**` |

A classic mnemonic to remember these is: **"Vin Diesel has ten weapons, eh?"** (ven, di, sal, haz, ten, ve, pon, sé).

**Step 3: Attaching Pronouns**

This step connects directly to what you've learned about object pronouns. When you give an affirmative command and want to include a pronoun (like *me, te, lo, la, le*), you do something new: you attach it directly to the end of the command.

Let's take the command `ayuda` ("Help!").
*   To say "Help **me**!": `Ayuda` + `me` -> `¡**Ayúdame**!`
*   To say "Do **it**!" (the homework, *la tarea*, is `la`): `Haz` + `la` -> `¡**Hazla**!`
*   To say "Tell **me**!": `Di` + `me` -> `¡**Dime**!`

Notice the accent on `Ayúdame`. Here's the rule of thumb: If the original command word has two or more syllables, and you add a pronoun, you'll need to add an accent to the vowel that was originally stressed.
*   `A-**YU**-da.` -> `a-**YÚ**-da-me.`
This keeps the emphasis in the right place so the word sounds natural. For one-syllable commands like `haz` or `di`, you don't need the accent when adding one pronoun (`Hazla`, `Dime`).

## Worked Reality
You and your friend are collaborating on a presentation. Your friend is controlling the laptop while you're looking at a printed draft with your notes.

You see a slide that needs a change. "Okay, on the third slide, find the chart," you say. "I need you to change the title."

Here’s your internal monologue for forming that command:

1.  **The Verb:** "to change" is `cambiar`.
2.  **The Command Form:** This is a regular -AR verb. I need the `él/ella` form of `cambiar`. That's `cambia`. So the command is `Cambia`.
3.  **The Object:** What needs to be changed? "the title" (`el título`). The direct object pronoun for `el título` is `lo`.
4.  **Putting It Together:** I need to attach the pronoun `lo` to the end of the command `cambia`.
    *   `Cambia` + `lo` = `Cámbialo`.
5.  **Check the Stress:** The original command is `cam-**BIA**`. It's two syllables. When I add `lo`, it becomes `cam-**BIA**-lo`. The stress wants to shift. To keep it on the "a" of `bia`, I need an accent mark. `Cámbialo`.

You say it out loud:
"**`Cámbialo`**, por favor. Pon 'Resultados del Segundo Trimestre'." (Change it, please. Put 'Second Quarter Results'.)

Your friend immediately understands and makes the change. You used a command to give a clear, direct, and efficient instruction.

## Friction Point
The most common mistake is thinking the `tú` command should use the `tú` verb form.

**The Wrong Mental Model:** "If I'm talking to *tú*, I use the verb form that ends in -s, like `hablas` or `comes`."

**Why It's Tempting:** It's logical! The `tú` form is for "you," so it feels like it should be the command form. Using `hablas` to mean "speak" seems to make sense.

**The Correct Mental Model:** The `tú` form with the `-s` (`hablas`) is for *describing or asking*, not *commanding*. The command form *borrows* from a different part of the verb chart—the `él/ella` form—to signal that it's an instruction.

*   `Tú comes la manzana.` = You eat the apple. (A statement of fact).
*   `¡Come la manzana!` = Eat the apple! (An order).

That final "-s" is the critical difference between a description and a directive. Removing it is what shifts the verb from a statement gear into an action gear.
