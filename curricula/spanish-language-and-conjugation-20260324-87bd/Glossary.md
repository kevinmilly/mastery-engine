# Glossary: spanish language and conjugation

**Adjective**
A word that describes a noun, telling you more about its qualities. In Spanish, adjectives typically change their endings to match the gender (masculine/feminine) and number (singular/plural) of the noun they modify. For example, "rojo" (red) in "un coche rojo" (a red car).

**Agreement (Grammatical)**
The way words in a sentence change their forms to match each other in characteristics like gender, number, or person. In Spanish, this is crucial for adjectives to match nouns, and for verbs to match their subjects. For instance, "las casas rojas" (the red houses) shows agreement in gender and number.

**Article (Grammatical)**
A small word placed before a noun to indicate if it's specific or general. In Spanish, articles must agree in gender and number with the noun they introduce, such as 'el' (the, masculine singular) or 'una' (a/an, feminine singular).

**Clause**
A group of words that contains a subject and a verb, forming a complete thought or part of a larger sentence. In Spanish, clauses can be independent (stand alone) or dependent (rely on another clause for full meaning), often connected by words like relative pronouns.

**Command (Imperative)**
A verb form used to give an order, instruction, or make a request directly. In Spanish, commands vary depending on the formality and number of people being addressed (e.g., "Habla!" - Speak! for 'tú').

**Conditional Tense**
A verb tense used to talk about hypothetical situations, polite requests, or what "would" or "could" happen. In Spanish, it often translates to "would + verb" in English, such as "hablaría" (I would speak).

**Conjugation**
The process of changing a verb's ending to show who is performing the action (the subject) and when the action occurs (the tense). For example, the infinitive "hablar" (to speak) conjugates to "yo hablo" (I speak) and "tú hablas" (you speak).

**Consonant**
A speech sound produced by obstructing airflow in the vocal tract. In Spanish, consonants generally have clear and consistent pronunciations, distinct from English, which aids in accurate reading and speaking.

**Definite Article**
A word equivalent to "the" in English, used before a noun to indicate a specific item or person. Spanish definite articles (el, la, los, las) must agree with the gender and number of the noun they modify.

**Direct Object**
The noun or pronoun that directly receives the action of the verb in a sentence. It answers the question "what?" or "whom?" after the verb. For example, in "Leo un libro" (I read a book), "un libro" is the direct object.

**Direct Object Pronoun**
A small word that replaces a direct object, avoiding repetition and streamlining sentences. In Spanish, these pronouns (e.g., me, te, lo, la) are typically placed before the conjugated verb or attached to an infinitive or gerund.

**Estar**
One of the two primary Spanish verbs meaning "to be," used to express temporary conditions, location, feelings, or actions in progress. Its usage contrasts with "ser," which is for more permanent characteristics, and mastering this distinction is fundamental.

**Feminine (Grammatical Gender)**
A grammatical category assigned to nouns, articles, and adjectives in Spanish, often ending in -a. This gender does not necessarily relate to biological sex but governs agreement with other words in the sentence. For example, "la mesa" (the table) is feminine.

**Future Simple Tense**
A verb tense used to express actions that will happen at a later time, predictions, or conjectures about the present. In Spanish, it's typically formed by adding specific endings to the infinitive form of the verb, such as "hablaré" (I will speak).

**Gender (Grammatical)**
A system in Spanish where nouns are classified as either masculine or feminine, regardless of whether they refer to animate or inanimate objects. This classification dictates the form of accompanying articles, adjectives, and some pronouns.

**Gerund**
The form of a verb that, in English, ends in "-ing" (e.g., "speaking"), used to describe an action in progress. In Spanish, gerunds end in "-ando" for -AR verbs and "-iendo" for -ER/-IR verbs, and are used with "estar" to form the present progressive tense.

**Idiomatic Expression**
A phrase or group of words whose meaning cannot be understood from the literal meanings of its individual words. Spanish has many such expressions, like "tener hambre" (to be hungry), which literally means "to have hunger."

**Imperfect Tense**
A past verb tense used to describe habitual actions, ongoing events, or provide background descriptions in the past. Unlike the preterite, it emphasizes the duration or repeated nature of an action without a clear endpoint, such as "hablaba" (I used to speak/I was speaking).

**Indefinite Article**
A word equivalent to "a/an" or "some" in English, used before a noun to refer to a non-specific item or quantity. Spanish indefinite articles (un, una, unos, unas) must agree with the gender and number of the noun.

**Indirect Object**
The noun or pronoun that indirectly receives the action of the verb, indicating to whom or for whom the action is performed. It often precedes a direct object. For example, in "Le doy un libro a María" (I give a book to María), "a María" is the indirect object.

**Indirect Object Pronoun**
A small word that replaces an indirect object, simplifying sentences and preventing repetition. In Spanish, these pronouns (e.g., me, te, le, les) are usually placed before the conjugated verb, similar to direct object pronouns.

**Infinitive**
The basic, unconjugated form of a verb, typically preceded by "to" in English (e.g., "to speak"). In Spanish, infinitives end in -ar, -er, or -ir (e.g., "hablar," "comer," "vivir").

**Informal (Tú)**
The familiar way of addressing a single person, used among friends, family, and peers. This contrasts with the formal "usted," which is used to show respect or address strangers, and it dictates specific verb conjugations and command forms.

**Irregular Verb**
A verb that does not follow the standard conjugation patterns of regular verbs, and therefore must be memorized. Common irregular verbs in Spanish include "ser," "estar," "ir," and "tener," which have unique forms in various tenses.

**Masculine (Grammatical Gender)**
A grammatical category assigned to nouns, articles, and adjectives in Spanish, often ending in -o. This gender does not necessarily correspond to biological sex but is crucial for grammatical agreement. For example, "el libro" (the book) is masculine.

**Mood (Verb)**
A grammatical category of verbs that expresses the speaker's attitude or intention regarding the action—whether it's a fact (indicative), a command (imperative), or a wish/doubt/emotion (subjunctive).

**Noun**
A word that names a person, place, thing, or idea. In Spanish, all nouns have a grammatical gender (masculine or feminine) and can be singular or plural, which affects the forms of articles and adjectives used with them.

**Number (Grammatical)**
The grammatical characteristic of nouns, articles, adjectives, and verbs that indicates whether they are singular (referring to one) or plural (referring to more than one). Words must agree in number within a sentence.

**Object (Grammar)**
A noun or pronoun that receives the action of a verb or is governed by a preposition. Objects can be direct (receiving the action directly) or indirect (receiving the action indirectly, to/for whom).

**Personal Pronoun**
A word that replaces a noun, referring to a specific person or thing without naming them directly. In Spanish, these include subject pronouns (yo, tú), direct object pronouns (lo, la), and indirect object pronouns (le, les).

**Plural (Grammatical Number)**
The form of a noun, adjective, or verb that indicates there is more than one of something. In Spanish, regular nouns and adjectives form the plural by adding -s or -es to the singular form, depending on their ending.

**Preposition**
A word that connects a noun or pronoun to another word in a sentence, showing a relationship of time, place, direction, or manner. Common Spanish prepositions include "a" (to/at), "de" (of/from), "en" (in/on/at), "con" (with), "para" (for/to), and "por" (for/by/through).

**Present Progressive**
A verb tense used to describe an action that is currently happening or in progress. It is formed with the present tense of "estar" followed by the gerund (e.g., "Estoy hablando" - I am speaking).

**Present Tense**
A verb tense used to describe actions happening now, habitual actions, or general truths. In Spanish, it is one of the most frequently used tenses and has distinct conjugations for regular and irregular verbs.

**Preterite Tense**
A past verb tense used to describe completed actions that occurred at a specific point in the past. It emphasizes the beginning or end of an action, treating it as a single, finished event, such as "hablé" (I spoke). Often contrasted with the imperfect tense.

**Pronoun**
A word that substitutes for a noun, avoiding repetition and making sentences more concise. Spanish has various types of pronouns, including personal, direct object, indirect object, and relative pronouns, each serving a specific grammatical function.

**Pronunciation**
The way a word or language is spoken, including the sounds of vowels, consonants, and the rhythm and stress patterns. Mastering Spanish pronunciation is key for both speaking clearly and understanding spoken Spanish.

**Regular Verb**
A verb that follows predictable and consistent patterns for its conjugations in different tenses. In Spanish, regular verbs fall into three categories based on their infinitive endings: -AR, -ER, and -IR.

**Relative Pronoun**
A word used to connect a dependent clause to a noun or pronoun in the main clause, providing additional information. Common Spanish relative pronouns include "que" (that/which/who) and "quien" (who/whom).

**Ser**
One of the two primary Spanish verbs meaning "to be," used to express permanent characteristics, identity, origin, time, and possession. It is fundamental to Spanish and is crucial to distinguish from "estar," which conveys temporary states.

**Singular (Grammatical Number)**
The form of a noun, adjective, or verb that indicates there is only one of something. In Spanish, singular forms are the base from which plural forms are often derived.

**Stem (Verb)**
The base part of a verb that carries its core meaning, remaining after the infinitive ending (-ar, -er, -ir) has been removed. In some verbs, this stem can change when conjugated, leading to "stem-changing verbs."

**Stem-Changing Verb**
A type of irregular verb in Spanish where the vowel in the verb stem changes in certain conjugations of the present tense (e.g., 'e' changes to 'ie' or 'i', 'o' changes to 'ue'). These changes do not occur in all forms (e.g., not usually in nosotros/vosotros).

**Subject (Grammar)**
The noun or pronoun that performs the action of the verb or is described by the verb in a sentence. In Spanish, the subject pronoun is often omitted because the verb ending clearly indicates who the subject is.

**Subjunctive Mood**
A verb mood used to express non-factual statements such as wishes, emotions, doubt, uncertainty, recommendations, or impersonal expressions. It contrasts with the indicative mood, which expresses facts and certainties.

**Tense (Verb)**
A grammatical category of verbs that indicates when an action takes place (e.g., present, past, future). Spanish has multiple tenses, allowing for precise expression of timing and completion of actions.

**Usted**
The formal way of addressing a single person, used to show respect, address strangers, or in professional settings. It takes the same verb conjugations as 'él' and 'ella' (he/she) and implies a respectful distance.

**Verb**
A word that describes an action, an occurrence, or a state of being. Verbs are central to every sentence and are conjugated in Spanish to agree with the subject and indicate the time of the action.

**Vosotros**
The informal plural form of "you" used specifically in Spain when addressing a group of friends, family, or peers. It has its own unique set of verb conjugations that are not typically used in Latin America.

**Vowel**
A speech sound produced with an open vocal tract, allowing air to flow freely. Spanish vowels (a, e, i, o, u) each have a single, consistent sound, making pronunciation more predictable than in English.
