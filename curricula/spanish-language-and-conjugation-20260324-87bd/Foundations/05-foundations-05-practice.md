## Answer Key

**Answer 1**
This exercise tests the direct application of matching articles to nouns based on gender and number.

1.  **el** libro (libro is masculine and singular)
2.  **las** casas (casas is feminine and plural)
3.  **el** profesor (profesor is masculine and singular)
4.  **la** lección (nouns ending in -ción are typically feminine; this one is singular)
5.  **los** hombres (hombres is masculine and plural)
6.  **la** luz (luz is feminine and singular)

**Answer 2**
This exercise checks the same agreement rule but requires working backward from the article to the noun.

1.  **universidad**: The article `la` is feminine and singular, so the singular noun `universidad` is correct.
2.  **problema**: The article `el` is masculine and singular. Even though `problema` ends in -a, it's a common exception and is a masculine noun. The singular form is correct.
3.  **mujeres**: The article `las` is feminine and plural, so the plural noun `mujeres` is correct.
4.  **lápices**: The article `los` is masculine and plural. The plural of `lápiz` is `lápices`, so this is the correct choice.

**Answer 3**
This exercise tests understanding of a common exception to the definite article rule.

The article `el` is used instead of `la` because `agua` is a feminine singular noun that begins with a stressed "a-" sound. Using `la` would cause the two "a" sounds to blend together (`la-agua`), which is difficult to pronounce clearly. The switch to `el` (`el agua`) separates the sounds.

This rule does **not** change the gender of `agua`. It is still a feminine noun. If you describe it with an adjective, the adjective must be feminine: "El agua está frí**a**," not "frío." The rule only applies to the singular definite article. In the plural, it returns to normal: "**las** aguas."

**Answer 4**
This exercise assesses if you can apply the exception rule from Exercise 3 to new words by analyzing their pronunciation.

1.  **el** hacha: `Hacha` is a feminine singular noun that starts with a stressed "a-" sound (the 'h' is silent). Therefore, it uses `el` to avoid the "la-acha" sound blending.
2.  **el** área: `Área` is a feminine singular noun that starts with a stressed "a-" sound. It follows the same rule and uses `el`.
3.  **la** alarma: Although `alarma` is a feminine singular noun that starts with "a-", the stress is on the second syllable (`a-LAR-ma`). Since the first "a-" is unstressed, the exception does not apply, and the standard feminine article `la` is used.
4.  **el** alma: `Alma` is a feminine singular noun that starts with a stressed "a-" sound, so it uses `el`.

**Answer 5**

1.  **el**: `profesor` is a masculine, singular noun.
2.  **la**: `clase` is a feminine, singular noun.
3.  **los**: `estudiantes` is a masculine, plural noun. (Note: `estudiantes` can refer to a group of mixed gender or all males).

The complete text is: "Hola, soy Ana. Soy una estudiante. En mi clase, (1) **el** profesor es muy simpático. (2) **la** clase es pequeña, pero (3) **los** estudiantes son muy trabajadores. A mí me gustan (4) **los** días con mucho sol."

**Answer 6**
This exercise tests a higher-level synthesis skill: applying definite articles to general or abstract concepts, a key difference between Spanish and English.

The corrected sentences are:
1.  "Me gusta **la** música."
2.  "**La** paciencia es una virtud."

**Explanation of the rule:**
In Spanish, the definite article (`el`, `la`, `los`, `las`) is used when talking about a noun as a general concept or category, or when referring to "all" of something. In English, we often omit the article in these cases.

-   In "Me gusta la música," you aren't talking about a specific piece of music but "music in general." Therefore, the feminine singular article `la` is required.
-   In "La paciencia es una virtud," you are talking about the abstract concept of "patience." In Spanish, this requires the definite article `la`. Your classmate's sentences were incorrect because they applied the English rule (no article for general concepts) to Spanish.
