## The Hook
After this lesson, you will be able to begin and end a simple conversation in Spanish, asking someone for their name and giving your own.

Think of greetings and introductions as the standard set of keys to a house. The keys themselves are simple, but they unlock the entire space inside—the conversation. No matter how complex the house is, the key to the front door usually works in a very simple, predictable way. These phrases are your keys.

## Why It Matters
Knowing these phrases is about more than being polite; it’s about opening the door to practice. If you walk into a shop in Madrid and mumble an English "Hello?", the shopkeeper will almost certainly switch to English for you. Your opportunity to have a real Spanish interaction is over before it starts.

The real-world friction isn't just a moment of awkwardness. It's the moment your conversation partner decides, consciously or not, that speaking Spanish with you is going to be too much work. By using the correct, basic greeting, you signal, "I'm trying to meet you in your language." This simple act keeps the door open for you to use and learn more Spanish. Without it, that door often slams shut.

## The Ladder
In the last lesson, you learned that Spanish vowels have one pure, unchanging sound. Keep that in mind as you pronounce these phrases. Listen for the clean `A`, `E`, `I`, `O`, `U` sounds.

### Step 1: Hellos and Goodbyes

The most basic, all-purpose greeting is `Hola`. It works anytime, anywhere.

*   `Hola` (OH-lah) - Hello

For more specific times of day, you can use these:

*   `Buenos días` (BWAY-nohs DEE-ahs) - Good morning (used until noon)
*   `Buenas tardes` (BWAY-nahs TAR-dehs) - Good afternoon (used from noon until evening)
*   `Buenas noches` (BWAY-nahs NO-chehs) - Good evening / Good night (used in the evening, both for greeting and for leaving)

When you leave, you have a few options:

*   `Adiós` (ah-dee-OHS) - Goodbye (can sound a bit final, like you don't expect to see them again soon)
*   `Hasta luego` (AHS-tah loo-EH-go) - See you later (very common and neutral)
*   `Chao` (chow) - Bye (very common and informal, borrowed from Italian)

### Step 2: Checking In - "How are you?"

After `Hola`, it's natural to ask how someone is. Spanish has two main ways to do this, depending on your relationship with the person. This is our first look at a concept called **formality**.

*   **Informal:** Used with friends, family, people your age. Think of it as the T-shirt and jeans of Spanish.
*   **Formal:** Used with people you don't know, older people, or in professional settings (like with a boss or a store clerk). This is the "business casual" of Spanish.

To ask "How are you?" informally, you say:
*   `¿Cómo estás?` (KOH-moh es-TAHS)

To ask formally, you drop the final 's':
*   `¿Cómo está?` (KOH-moh es-TAH)

Notice the only difference is the 's'. That single letter signals your perceived social relationship.

To reply, you can say:
*   `Bien, gracias.` (bee-EN, GRAH-see-ahs) - Good, thank you.
*   `Muy bien.` (mwee bee-EN) - Very good.

And to be polite, you return the question:
*   `¿Y tú?` (ee TOO) - And you? (informal)
*   `¿Y usted?` (ee oos-TED) - And you? (formal)

You use `tú` if they used `estás` with you. You use `usted` if they used `está` with you. Just mirror what you hear.

### Step 3: Introductions - "What's your name?"

This is where Spanish structure differs most from English. We don't ask what a name *is*, we ask how someone *calls themselves*.

Informal question:
*   `¿Cómo te llamas?` (KOH-moh teh YAH-mahs) - Literally: "How do you call yourself?"

Formal question:
*   `¿Cómo se llama usted?` (KOH-moh seh YAH-mah oos-TED) - "How do you call yourself?" (formal)

The most common way to answer is by using the same verb, `llamar` (to call):
*   `Me llamo...` (meh YAH-moh...) - "I call myself..."
    *   Example: `Me llamo David.`

Finally, to say "Nice to meet you":
*   `Mucho gusto.` (MOO-choh GOOS-toh)

The typical reply is:
*   `Igualmente.` (ee-gwahl-MEN-tay) - Likewise.

## Worked Reality
Imagine you're at a language exchange event in Bogotá. You see someone your age standing alone and decide to introduce yourself.

You walk over and start.
**You:** `Hola, buenas tardes.`
(It's 4 PM, so `buenas tardes` is appropriate. You've successfully opened the door.)

**Her:** `Hola, buenas tardes. ¿Cómo estás?`
(She smiles and uses the informal `estás`. This is a good sign; she sees you as a peer.)

**You:** `Muy bien, gracias. ¿Y tú?`
(You correctly answer and mirror her informal `tú`.)

**Her:** `Bien, también. Me llamo Carolina.`
(She answers, "Good, too," and offers her name.)

**You:** `Me llamo John. Mucho gusto.`
(You give your name and say it's nice to meet her.)

**Her:** `Igualmente, John.`
(Likewise, John.)

In this short exchange, you've used all the key pieces. You navigated the informal context, asked and answered questions, and established a friendly, basic connection, all in Spanish. You didn't just exchange data; you followed a cultural script that made the interaction feel natural and respectful.

## Friction Point
The most common misunderstanding for English speakers is mixing up `¿Cómo estás?` (How are you?) and `¿Cómo te llamas?` (What is your name?).

The wrong mental model is trying to translate word-for-word from English. You think, "What is your name?" so you look for the Spanish word for "what" (`qué`) and try to form a sentence. This leads to confusion when you hear a question that starts with `¿Cómo...?` ("How...?"). You know `¿Cómo estás?` means "How are you?", so you might think `¿Cómo te llamas?` is another "how are you" variation.

The correct mental model is to stop translating directly and embrace the Spanish way of thinking. Your name isn't a static *thing* you possess (like in "What *is* your name?"). Instead, it's tied to the action of being called.

*   `llamar` = the verb "to call"
*   `Me llamo` = "I call myself"
*   `¿Cómo te llamas?` = "How do you call yourself?"

Once you see `llamas` as an action word related to "calling," it becomes impossible to confuse with `estás`, which is a state-of-being word. Think of it this way: one question asks about your *condition* (`estás`), the other asks about your *identification* (`llamas`).
