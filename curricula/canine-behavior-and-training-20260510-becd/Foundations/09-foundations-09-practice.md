## Exercises

**Exercise 1**
A trainer gives a dog a treat every time it sits on cue. The dog begins sitting faster and more reliably over the following week. A second trainer uses a sharp verbal "no!" whenever the dog jumps on guests. The dog jumps less frequently over time. Identify the type of consequence each trainer is using and explain what makes each one that type — without using the words "add" or "remove."

**Exercise 2**
A dog pulls toward a squirrel on leash. The owner stops walking completely and stands still until the leash loosens, then resumes walking. Over several weeks, the dog pulls less. Is this reinforcement or punishment? Which variant? Justify your classification based on what happened to the dog's future behavior and what consequence was applied.

**Exercise 3**
A dog barks at the mail carrier every day. The owner, wanting to stop the barking, runs to the dog and gives it a chew toy to distract it. After two months, the barking is louder and starts earlier in the mail carrier's approach. Explain what went wrong from a reinforcement/punishment framework perspective. What was the owner intending to do, and what were they actually doing?

**Exercise 4**
Two dogs are in separate training programs. Dog A learns to stay in a down position because staying earns a food reward. Dog B learns to stay in a down position because getting up results in a leash correction. Both dogs reliably hold a down-stay. From a reinforcement/punishment standpoint, are these outcomes equivalent? Explain why the mechanism matters even when the observable result looks the same.

**Exercise 5**
A fearful dog (known to display whale eye, tucked tail, and flattened ears in stressful contexts) is being trained to accept grooming. The trainer holds the dog still when it tries to move away, proceeding with brushing until the dog stops struggling, then releases the dog. The trainer believes this is "teaching the dog that grooming isn't scary." Using reinforcement/punishment concepts alongside what you know about fear and stress states, evaluate what is most likely being learned and what behavioral outcome should be expected over time.

**Exercise 6**
A dog has a strong predatory drive and frequently chases bicycles. A trainer proposes two plans: Plan A — every time the dog orients toward a bicycle without lunging, it receives a high-value food reward. Plan B — every time the dog lunges toward a bicycle, it receives a leash pop. The owner asks: "Which plan actually changes behavior, and which one just suppresses it?" Construct an answer grounded in reinforcement/punishment theory and the dog's motivational and arousal states. Identify which plan carries a higher welfare risk and why.

---

## Answer Key

**Answer 1**
The first trainer is using positive reinforcement: the dog's behavior (sitting) increases in frequency and reliability because something was delivered following the behavior (a treat). The consequence strengthened the behavior. The second trainer is using positive punishment: the dog's behavior (jumping) decreases because something was delivered following the behavior (a verbal reprimand). The consequence weakened the behavior. What makes each one its type is the direction of the behavioral change — strengthened means reinforcement, weakened means punishment — and the fact that a stimulus was presented in both cases, which is what makes both "positive" in the technical sense.

**Answer 2**
This is negative reinforcement. The dog's pulling behavior decreases over time (reinforcement effect — the behavior of walking politely is strengthened, or alternatively, pulling is weakened, so this could also be framed as punishment — the key is to look at what behavior is being targeted). More precisely: the owner removes the opportunity to move forward (a desired outcome for the dog) when the dog pulls, and restores it when the leash loosens. The dog learns that a loose leash makes forward movement resume. The leash tension being relieved upon correct behavior is the removal of an aversive, which is negative reinforcement of loose-leash walking. The stopping itself functions as mild positive punishment of pulling. Either framing is valid, but the mechanism is the contingent removal/restoration of forward motion as a consequence.

**Answer 3**
The owner intended to apply punishment — to stop the barking by interrupting it with something incompatible (chewing). Instead, they accidentally applied positive reinforcement. From the dog's perspective, barking at the mail carrier reliably produced the owner's attention and a high-value chew toy. The behavior increased in intensity and began earlier, which is exactly what reinforcement produces: behavior that works gets stronger and more anticipatory. The owner's intent was irrelevant to the outcome — what mattered was the contingency the dog experienced. The chew toy, delivered immediately after barking, reinforced the barking regardless of what the owner was trying to communicate.

**Answer 4**
The mechanisms are not equivalent, even if the surface behavior looks identical. Dog A's stay is maintained by the presence of something the dog wants (food), which means the behavior has intrinsic momentum — the dog is an active participant seeking the reward. Dog B's stay is maintained by the absence of something the dog wants to avoid (the correction), which means the behavior persists because moving causes discomfort, not because staying is rewarding. The practical differences: Dog B's behavior is more likely to deteriorate when the threat of correction is absent, and the dog may associate the training context with aversion, which can compound into stress or avoidance. The mechanism matters because it shapes the dog's emotional state during training, the generalizability of the behavior, and the long-term welfare implications — all of which are invisible in a snapshot of the behavior itself.

**Answer 5**
What is most likely being learned is not that grooming is safe — it is that struggling does not work, and that the grooming context predicts restraint and discomfort. This is negative reinforcement of stillness (the dog stops struggling → the restraint pressure eases → stillness is reinforced), but the aversive stimulus causing the struggling is the grooming process itself. The dog is not learning a positive association with brushing; it is learning to suppress escape behavior. Given what we know about fear states — that a dog showing whale eye, tucked tail, and flat ears is already in a stressed arousal state — continued exposure to an aversive stimulus while preventing escape is a form of flooding. This is likely to maintain or worsen the fear response over time, even if overt struggling decreases. The expected outcome is a dog that becomes more sensitized to grooming triggers, shows more anticipatory stress signals earlier in the sequence, or develops learned helplessness — appearing calm but remaining in a chronic stress state.

**Answer 6**
Both plans can reduce the visible lunging behavior, but through different mechanisms with different implications. Plan A uses positive reinforcement to build an incompatible behavior (orienting calmly) that competes with the chase response. Because it works with the dog's motivational system rather than against it, the dog is learning what to do — and the behavior of calm orientation is being strengthened. This addresses the behavioral pattern at the level of learning a new response. Plan B uses positive punishment to suppress the lunge after it begins. Punishment targets a behavior that has already occurred; it does not teach an alternative. Given the dog's strong predatory drive and the high arousal state triggered by moving bicycles, the leash pop may be insufficient to outcompete the drive, or may suppress the visible lunge while the underlying arousal remains — meaning the behavior could erupt more intensely in a lower-inhibition moment. Plan B carries a higher welfare risk for two reasons: first, it relies on aversive stimulation in an already high-arousal state, which can cause the dog to associate pain or discomfort with the presence of bicycles, potentially escalating reactivity rather than reducing it; second, suppressed behavior without an alternative leaves the dog without a coping strategy. Plan A changes behavior; Plan B risks only hiding it.
