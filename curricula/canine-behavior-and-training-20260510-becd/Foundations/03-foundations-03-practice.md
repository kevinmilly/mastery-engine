## Exercises

**Exercise 1**
A dog approaches a stranger with its tail held low and wagging slowly, ears flattened back, and body slightly crouched. A child nearby says, "The dog is wagging its tail — it must be happy and want to play!" Is the child's interpretation likely correct? Explain what these signals together are more likely communicating.

**Exercise 2**
Two dogs meet at a dog park. Dog A sniffs Dog B's rear end for several seconds. Dog B stands still and allows it, then turns to sniff Dog A in return. A nearby owner pulls Dog A away after a few seconds, saying, "That's rude — I don't want my dog doing that." What is the owner misunderstanding about what is happening between the dogs, and what is the likely consequence of repeatedly interrupting this behavior?

**Exercise 3**
A dog that has been alone in the house for eight hours greets its owner at the door by barking repeatedly, jumping up, and urinating a small amount on the floor. The owner assumes the dog is poorly trained and punishes it for the urination. Identify each behavior, explain what it most likely signals, and describe why the owner's response is likely to make the situation worse rather than better.

**Exercise 4**
You are observing a dog at a shelter. When a staff member enters the kennel, the dog yawns repeatedly, turns its head away, and licks its lips — but does not growl or bark. A volunteer concludes the dog is calm and relaxed because it isn't being aggressive. What is the volunteer missing, and what would be a more accurate reading of the dog's communication?

**Exercise 5**
A newly adopted dog consistently ignores commands it reliably performed at the shelter. Its new owner notices it spends long periods sniffing specific corners of the yard, frequently pauses on walks to smell patches of grass, and seems "distracted." Drawing on both what you know about canine communication signals and the dog's sensory world, explain why this behavior is better understood as information-gathering than disobedience — and describe one practical implication for how the owner should approach early training sessions.

**Exercise 6**
A dog rescue organization places dogs in foster homes and asks fosters to report whether dogs seem "comfortable or stressed" within the first 48 hours. One foster reports that her dog seems perfectly comfortable: it hasn't barked or growled once. A second foster reports concern because his dog keeps yawning, moving away when approached, and showing what he describes as "a whale eye" (showing the whites of its eyes). Which foster's assessment is more reliable, and what does this scenario reveal about the relationship between the absence of obvious signals and actual emotional state? Use specific signal categories to support your answer.

---

## Answer Key

**Answer 1**
The child's interpretation is likely incorrect. While tail wagging is often associated with positive emotion, the meaning of a wag depends heavily on the full body posture and context. A tail held low with a slow wag, combined with flattened ears and a crouched body, signals appeasement or anxiety — not playful excitement. The dog is communicating that it feels uncertain or submissive, possibly fearful. These are calming or deferent signals, not an invitation to play. Misreading them as happiness could lead the child to approach too quickly, increasing the dog's stress and potentially triggering a fear-based reaction.

**Answer 2**
The owner is misreading a normal and socially important olfactory communication behavior as a social impropriety based on human norms. Dogs gather detailed information — identity, sex, reproductive status, health, emotional state, and more — through scent signals concentrated around the anal region. Mutual rear-end sniffing is a structured greeting ritual that allows both dogs to assess each other before deciding how to proceed socially. Repeatedly interrupting this exchange prevents the dogs from completing their natural assessment process. This can create social tension between the dogs, make future greetings more fraught or reactive, and deprive both animals of information they rely on to feel secure in social interactions.

**Answer 3**
Each behavior should be read as a communication signal rather than a training failure. The repeated barking is a vocalization expressing high arousal and likely excitement or anxiety after prolonged isolation. The jumping is a contact-seeking behavior — dogs greet face-to-face, and jumping is an attempt to reach the owner's face as they would another dog's muzzle. The small urination is most likely submissive or excitement urination, an involuntary physiological response tied to emotional overwhelm, not a failure of house-training. Punishing the dog for these signals is problematic on two levels: first, the dog cannot connect a punishment to an involuntary physical response, so the urination will not decrease; second, punishment adds a negative emotional experience to an already high-arousal moment, which can increase anxiety over time and potentially suppress early warning signals the owner would actually want to see, making the dog's emotional state harder to read in the future.

**Answer 4**
The volunteer is conflating the absence of overt aggression with calmness, which is a significant misreading. Yawning out of context, lip licking, and head-turning away are recognized appeasement and stress-displacement signals — the dog is actively communicating discomfort and is attempting to de-escalate or signal non-threat. These are often called "calming signals" and represent an early tier of stress communication. A dog displaying these signals is not relaxed; it is working to manage a situation it finds threatening. The volunteer is only watching for the most obvious end-of-spectrum signals (growling, snapping) and missing the subtler but meaningful communication happening earlier in the stress response. Failing to recognize these signals and continuing to press the dog could push it past its threshold toward more overt defensive behavior — which would then be described as "coming out of nowhere."

**Answer 5**
From the lesson on canine communication, sniffing is a primary channel through which dogs receive and process olfactory signals — including information left by other animals about territory, presence, identity, and emotional state. What looks like distraction is actually the dog actively reading its new environment's "scent landscape," which is rich with information it has never encountered before. From the prior lesson on the canine sensory world, we know dogs have a vastly more sensitive and information-dense olfactory system than humans, meaning this environment is genuinely complex and cognitively engaging in a way that has no human equivalent. The dog is not ignoring the owner — it is overwhelmed by novel input and prioritizing the most biologically urgent task: understanding where it is and what has been there.

The practical implication is that the owner should not expect reliable command performance in the new environment during the adjustment period, and should design early training sessions in low-distraction indoor spaces where the olfactory environment is more familiar. Rushing to enforce commands in a scent-rich outdoor space is likely to produce frustration on both sides. Allowing structured sniff time as a reward during walks can also make training sessions feel less oppositional.

**Answer 6**
The second foster's assessment is more reliable, even though his dog has shown no aggression. He is correctly identifying a cluster of stress signals — yawning out of context, avoidance movement, and whale eye (visible sclera, indicating the dog is turning its head away while keeping the stimulus in view, a sign of tension) — that collectively indicate the dog is in a state of anxiety. These are visual communication signals in the appeasement and stress category. The first foster's dog may genuinely be comfortable, but the absence of barking or growling only tells us the dog has not reached the threshold for overt defensive vocalization; it says nothing about the dog's actual emotional state.

This scenario illustrates a critical principle: communication signals exist on a continuum, and dogs often signal distress well before they reach the behaviors most humans recognize as concerning. A dog that has learned its early signals are ignored or ineffective may suppress them over time and appear "calm" until it reaches a breaking point — a pattern sometimes described as a dog that "snapped without warning," when in fact the warnings were present but unrecognized. Reliable welfare assessment requires watching for the full range of visual signals, not only the most extreme ones.
