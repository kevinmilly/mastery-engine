## Capstone: Foundations — Canine Behavior and Training

### The Scenario

You are a behavior consultant who has been called in by a rescue organization to assess a 4-year-old male Belgian Malinois named Koda. Koda was surrendered after his previous owner — an amateur who acquired him as a puppy from a working-dog breeding line — found him "unmanageable." Shelter staff report that Koda is difficult to handle during feeding time, becomes extremely agitated when unfamiliar people enter the kennel area, and has been snapping at volunteers. One staff member says he "acts aggressive for no reason." Another believes he is "dominant" and needs a "firmer hand."

During your initial observation through the kennel door, you note the following: Koda is pacing repeatedly in the same path along the back wall. When a volunteer walks past, he freezes, his weight shifts slightly forward, his ears rotate toward the sound, his tail rises and stiffens, and he emits a low, continuous vocalization. When the volunteer stops to look at him, Koda suddenly lunges forward, barking in rapid bursts, then immediately retreats to the back of the kennel and resumes pacing. He is not offered any enrichment, his feeding schedule is irregular, and the kennel is located directly adjacent to a high-traffic area where staff talk loudly, carts roll by, and dogs bark constantly throughout the day.

The rescue director wants a written behavioral report that will guide how staff interact with Koda going forward, inform any remediation plan, and be shared with a prospective adopter — a semi-rural family with prior dog experience — who has already expressed interest.

### Your Tasks

1. **Sensory Environment Analysis** — Describe what Koda is likely experiencing in his current kennel environment from a canine sensory perspective. Identify at least three specific features of his housing situation that would be qualitatively different from what a human observer would perceive, and explain why those differences matter for interpreting his behavior.

2. **Signal Decoding** — Break down the behavioral sequence observed during the volunteer's approach — the freeze, weight shift, ear rotation, tail position, vocalization, lunge, and retreat — into its component communication signals. Explain what each signal indicates about Koda's internal state and how the sequence as a whole should be read, rather than treating the lunge as an isolated event.

3. **Welfare Assessment** — Evaluate Koda's current living conditions against the fundamental principles of canine welfare. Identify which specific needs are unmet or inadequately met, and explain how those deficits are likely contributing to the behavioral profile you observed.

4. **Reframing the Interpretation** — The shelter staff have offered two competing explanations for Koda's behavior: that he is "aggressive for no reason" and that he is "dominant." Using what you know about stress and arousal states, Koda's breed background and drives, and the concept of antecedents and consequences, construct a more accurate behavioral explanation for what is driving his reactions. Your explanation should account for the specific history and environmental context, not offer a generic rebuttal.

5. **Remediation Recommendations** — Propose a structured, humane plan for the rescue organization that addresses Koda's immediate welfare, begins to reduce his stress response to human approach, and prepares him for assessment as an adoption candidate. Your plan must draw explicitly on learning theory principles — specifically the distinction between reinforcement and punishment — and justify each recommendation in behavioral terms.

### What Good Work Looks Like

- Strong responses demonstrate that sensory analysis goes beyond listing dog facts — the learner connects specific environmental features (noise frequency, irregular schedules, proximity of other dogs) to Koda's specific observed behaviors, not to dogs in general
- Signal decoding is sequential and contextual, not a glossary — a strong response treats the behavioral sequence as a coherent communicative act and explains how early signals escalated because they were not recognized or responded to
- The welfare analysis is specific to Koda's profile: a high-drive working-line dog has different baseline requirements than an average companion dog, and strong responses reflect this rather than applying a generic checklist
- The reframing task is answered with behavioral precision — strong responses replace vague labels like "dominance" or "aggression" with a mechanism: what antecedents are triggering what responses, maintained by what consequences, given what arousal state
- The remediation plan is internally coherent: each recommendation connects to an identified problem, and the learning theory rationale is applied correctly — not invoked by name only
