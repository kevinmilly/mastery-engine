## Exercises

**Exercise 1**
A dog at a veterinary clinic holds its tail low and tucked, yawns repeatedly, and turns its head away when the vet reaches toward it. A second dog in the same waiting room has its tail held high and stiff, is scanning the room rapidly, and vocalizes with short, sharp barks. Identify which emotional arousal state each dog is most likely experiencing, and name two specific signals that support your identification for each dog.

**Exercise 2**
A trainer notices that a dog being introduced to a new environment is panting heavily, though the room is air-conditioned and the dog has not been exercising. The owner says, "He always pants like that — he's just excited to be somewhere new." What is the problem with accepting the owner's interpretation at face value, and what additional behavioral signals would help you distinguish excitement from fear-based stress?

**Exercise 3**
A dog is brought to an agility class and immediately begins performing behaviors rapidly — completing obstacles quickly, barking between obstacles, and missing contact zones it normally hits reliably. Its ears are forward, its movement is fast, and it is slow to respond to its handler's cues. Is this dog performing well? What arousal state does this pattern suggest, and why does high arousal impair performance even when a dog appears energetic and engaged?

**Exercise 4**
Two dogs display the following responses to a sudden loud noise: Dog A freezes instantly, lowers its body, and refuses to move for several minutes before slowly retreating toward the exit. Dog B startles, orients toward the sound briefly, then returns to sniffing the ground within 30 seconds. Explain what differentiates these two responses in terms of arousal state and recovery, and what this difference implies for how a trainer should work with each dog.

**Exercise 5**
A rescue dog that was neglected in its first year shows a pattern of reacting fearfully to strangers in a way that has escalated over six months into lunging and snapping. The owner reports that the dog was never punished — it simply "got worse on its own." Using learning theory vocabulary and your understanding of fear and arousal states, construct an explanation for how this escalation could occur without deliberate reinforcement of the aggressive behavior.

**Exercise 6**
You are assessing a dog for a rehabilitation program. The dog was previously trained using aversive methods and now shows shutdown behavior during training sessions: it moves slowly, avoids eye contact, offers no voluntary behaviors, and lies down frequently even when cued to stand. A colleague argues this dog is "calm and relaxed" because it is not barking or jumping. Construct a counter-argument using body language knowledge, arousal state concepts, and welfare principles. Then outline what the first phase of a humane rehabilitation plan would need to address and why.

## Answer Key

**Answer 1**
Dog 1 is most likely experiencing fear or anxiety. Supporting signals: (1) tail tucked low — a classic indicator of fearful submission and threat-reduction posturing; (2) repeated yawning and head-turning away — both are appeasement and displacement signals dogs use to communicate discomfort and deflect potential conflict. Dog 2 is most likely in a state of over-arousal or alert excitement, possibly with some threat-assessment component. Supporting signals: (1) tail held high and stiff — indicates heightened arousal and confident alertness rather than fear; (2) rapid environmental scanning and short sharp barks — reflect a nervous system in high activation, orienting toward stimuli rather than withdrawing from them.

**Answer 2**
The problem is that panting is a physiological stress response driven by the autonomic nervous system — it occurs in both excitement and fear states and cannot be attributed to one without corroborating behavioral evidence. Accepting the owner's framing risks misreading stress as enthusiasm, which could lead to pushing the dog into situations it cannot cope with. To distinguish the states, look for: body posture (loose and forward-leaning suggests excitement; lowered, tucked, or crouched suggests fear); facial tension (soft eyes and relaxed mouth vs. whale eye, tight commissures, or a furrowed brow); movement quality (free and investigative vs. inhibited or erratic); and whether the dog is orienting toward or away from stimuli in the environment.

**Answer 3**
The dog is not performing well despite appearing active. The pattern — impulsive obstacle completion, vocalizing, missed contacts, and slow response to handler cues — indicates over-arousal. High arousal narrows cognitive bandwidth: the dog's sympathetic nervous system is highly activated, which prioritizes fast motor output over precise, attended behavior. Learning and fine motor control both degrade under excessive arousal because the dog is operating more from instinctive drive than thoughtful response. The handler's cues become harder to process because the dog's attention is fragmented. This illustrates the inverted-U relationship between arousal and performance: a moderate arousal level supports focus and motivation, but beyond that threshold, performance deteriorates even though the dog appears energetic.

**Answer 4**
Dog A shows a fear response with slow recovery — the freeze, body lowering, and sustained reluctance to move indicate the dog's nervous system has moved into a high-stress or shutdown state. The extended time before retreating suggests the arousal did not de-escalate quickly and the dog may have a lower threshold for fear responses or a history of unresolved fear. Dog B shows a normal startle response followed by rapid recovery — the brief orientation and return to calm behavior within 30 seconds indicates good emotional resilience and a nervous system that can self-regulate after a sudden stressor. For training: Dog A requires careful threshold management — working at distances or intensities well below the point of reaction — and counter-conditioning to build positive associations. Dog B's resilience means it can tolerate more varied environments and recover from mistakes more easily, though its triggers and thresholds should still be understood.

**Answer 5**
The escalation can be explained through the interaction of sensitization, reinforcement of avoidance, and the dog's unmet need to communicate distress. Initially, the dog used low-level fear signals (body lowering, head turns, lip licks) around strangers. If these signals were missed or ignored — as is common with people unfamiliar with canine communication — the dog learned that subtle signals do not create distance from the threat. Over repeated exposures without relief, the nervous system sensitizes: the fear response activates faster and more intensely with each encounter. The dog then escalates to behaviors that do reliably create distance — lunging and snapping. These are negatively reinforced (the stranger retreats or the situation ends), which strengthens them without any deliberate human reinforcement. The behavior did not appear from nowhere; it was shaped by the dog's own learning history and a failure of early intervention.

**Answer 6**
Counter-argument: The colleague is conflating absence of high-energy output with relaxation. Shutdown behavior — slow movement, avoidance of eye contact, absence of voluntary behavior, frequent lying down — is a recognized stress response, not a calm baseline. It reflects learned helplessness, a state in which the dog has learned that its behavior does not reliably influence outcomes and has stopped attempting to engage. Body language indicators (averted gaze, low body posture, lack of offered behaviors) are consistent with a suppressed fear state, not contentment. From a welfare standpoint, a dog that does not interact is not thriving — positive welfare requires the ability to express natural behaviors and experience some degree of agency. The first phase of rehabilitation must: (1) rebuild the dog's sense of agency by introducing a predictable, low-pressure environment where the dog can make simple choices and receive consistent positive outcomes; (2) avoid any demands that trigger shutdown by working entirely below the dog's stress threshold; and (3) use reinforcement-based methods to re-establish the association between offering behavior and good outcomes — essentially teaching the dog that its actions matter again before any formal skill training begins.
