## Exercises

**Exercise 1**
A family adopts a two-year-old Labrador Retriever. Within the first week, the dog begins chewing furniture, barking excessively, and stealing food from counters. The family assumes the dog has a "behavior problem" and considers returning him. Based on basic needs and welfare principles, what is the most likely explanation for this behavior, and what should the family address first?

**Exercise 2**
A dog owner works from home and keeps her Border Collie in the house all day. The dog gets a 15-minute walk in the morning and another in the evening. The dog is well-fed, healthy, and has access to water. Despite this, the dog constantly nudges her arm, barks at nothing, and rearranges furniture cushions. Which welfare need is most likely unmet, and why does meeting it matter for this particular breed?

**Exercise 3**
Two dogs live in the same household. Dog A spends four hours a day in a yard with no toys, no human interaction, and no structured activity. Dog B spends four hours a day in a smaller indoor space but has puzzle feeders, brief training sessions, and window access to watch the street. Which dog is more likely to develop behavioral problems, and what does this comparison reveal about how we should prioritize the components of a dog's environment?

**Exercise 4**
A rescue organization places dogs in foster homes while they await adoption. Some fosters report that newly placed dogs seem "shut down" — they barely move, ignore food, and show little interest in their surroundings. Other fosters report dogs that are hyperactive, destructive, or difficult to settle. Using welfare principles, explain why both patterns might actually reflect the same underlying problem rather than two different ones.

**Exercise 5**
You are consulting for a family whose three-year-old mixed-breed dog has begun growling at their young child during mealtimes. The dog has never done this before. The family recently moved to a smaller apartment, reduced the dog's walks due to a busy schedule, and started feeding the dog in a shared living space because there is no longer a separate room. Drawing on both this lesson and your knowledge of canine communication signals and stress indicators, construct a welfare-based explanation for why this behavior has emerged, and outline the priority order in which the family should address contributing factors.

**Exercise 6**
A professional dog trainer argues that behavioral problems in pet dogs are almost always a training failure — the owner simply hasn't taught the dog what to do. A welfare advocate argues that most behavioral problems are a needs failure — the dog's physical or mental requirements aren't being met. Using what you know about basic needs, canine communication, and behavior, evaluate both positions. Under what conditions is each view most accurate, and what would a complete, integrated framework look like?

---

## Answer Key

**Answer 1**
The behavior is almost certainly not a trained problem — it is a needs problem triggered by a major transition. Moving to a new home disrupts everything the dog knew: his routine, his environment, and his sense of safety. Chewing, barking, and counter-surfing are all normal outlets for stress, excess energy, and under-stimulation, not signs of a "bad" dog.

The family should first assess whether his baseline needs are being met: sufficient physical exercise for a high-energy breed, mental stimulation, predictable daily routines, and a safe, consistent space. Labradors have substantial energy and activity requirements. A dog whose needs are unmet in an unfamiliar environment will find his own outlets. Returning the dog without addressing these factors would likely produce the same result in the next home. The first intervention should be structured exercise, enrichment, and routine — not punishment or rehoming.

**Answer 2**
The unmet need is mental stimulation. The dog's physical needs (food, water, shelter, and moderate exercise) appear to be covered, but Border Collies were selectively bred for sustained, complex cognitive work — herding requires problem-solving, responsiveness to cues, and constant decision-making. A breed with this heritage experiences idle time not as rest but as deprivation.

Nudging, barking at nothing, and object manipulation are displacement behaviors — the dog is generating his own stimulation because none is being provided. This matters beyond simple nuisance: chronic under-stimulation can progress to compulsive behaviors, anxiety, and heightened reactivity. Meeting this need means adding structured mental work — training sessions, scent games, puzzle feeders, or tasks — not just more physical exercise, which alone won't satisfy a cognitively driven dog.

**Answer 3**
Dog B is significantly less likely to develop behavioral problems, even though the physical space is smaller and the time constraint is the same. This comparison reveals that the quality and variety of environmental engagement matter more than raw space or unsupervised time outdoors.

Dog A has access to physical space but no cognitive engagement, social interaction, or purpose. An empty yard does not meet mental stimulation or social bonding needs. Dog B's environment is smaller but functionally richer — puzzle feeders address foraging instincts, training sessions provide mental challenge and human connection, and window access offers sensory engagement.

The implication for prioritization is that a stimulating, interactive, smaller environment outperforms a large, barren one. Physical space is a welfare factor, but it is not a substitute for enrichment, structure, and relationship.

**Answer 4**
Both behavioral patterns — shutdown and hyperactivity — can reflect the same underlying problem: a nervous system responding to an environment that feels unsafe or uncontrollable. Welfare deprivation produces stress, and stress does not express itself uniformly across individuals or situations.

Shutdown behavior (freezing, appetite loss, disengagement) is a suppression response — the dog has withdrawn because the environment feels threatening or overwhelming and active coping has failed or been learned to be useless. Hyperactivity and destructiveness are activation responses — the dog is attempting to self-regulate or escape the same experience of stress through movement and action.

The welfare principle at work is that safety and predictability are foundational needs. Without them, no other need can be adequately met. Both foster groups are likely seeing the same deficit — an absence of perceived safety — expressed through different temperamental or experiential filters.

**Answer 5**
This behavior has not emerged from nowhere — it is the product of multiple simultaneous welfare stressors that have accumulated below the threshold of visible reaction until a specific trigger (proximity during feeding) pushed the dog over.

**Welfare-based explanation:** The dog has lost three things at once: exercise quantity (reduced walks), environmental space (smaller apartment, no separate room), and resource security (eating in a shared, busier space). Reduced exercise increases baseline stress and arousal. Loss of a predictable feeding location removes a safety anchor — from the dog's perspective, the child's proximity during eating in an unfamiliar, open space may represent an unpredictable threat to a resource. The growl is a communication signal — the dog is warning, not attacking, which means communication channels are still functioning but are being reached more quickly than before.

**Priority order:**
1. **Immediate safety management** — restructure feeding so the dog eats in a calm, low-traffic location, not because the growl is "wrong" but because the current setup is predictably triggering it. Remove the stressor while changes are made.
2. **Restore exercise levels** — even partial restoration will reduce baseline arousal and lower the dog's general stress threshold.
3. **Enrich the environment** — in a smaller space, mental stimulation becomes more, not less, important. Add enrichment to compensate for reduced physical outlet.
4. **Rebuild predictability and safe spaces** — identify and designate areas where the dog can disengage undisturbed, especially from the child.
5. **Consult a professional** — resource guarding that targets a child warrants assessment by a qualified trainer or behaviorist, even if the root cause is welfare-based.

**Answer 6**
Both positions contain truth but are incomplete in isolation.

**The training view is most accurate when:** a dog's needs are substantially met but the dog has never been taught alternative behaviors, impulse control, or how to navigate specific situations. A dog who jumps on guests despite adequate exercise and enrichment may simply need to learn an incompatible behavior (sit for greeting). In these cases, behavior is a skill deficit, and training directly addresses it.

**The welfare view is most accurate when:** the dog's environment is chronically under-stimulating, unpredictable, or stressful. Training a dog who is in sustained need-deprivation is like teaching someone to concentrate while they are hungry and sleep-deprived — the system is not available for learning in the way we are asking of it. Many "stubborn" or "untrainable" dogs are simply overwhelmed.

**The integrated framework** recognizes that needs and training are interdependent, not competing. Unmet needs raise baseline stress and lower the threshold for problematic behavior, making training harder and less durable. Training without addressing needs treats symptoms. But needs-meeting without guidance leaves a dog with energy and no behavioral structure to channel it. A complete framework asks first: *Is this dog's welfare baseline sufficient for learning?* Then: *What does this dog need to learn to navigate its environment successfully?* Canine communication knowledge adds a third layer — a dog's signals tell us in real time whether the current welfare and training context is working or is pushing the animal past its capacity to cope.
