## Exercises

**Exercise 1**
A trainer is working with a Labrador Retriever who ignores kibble treats during training sessions but becomes intensely focused when a tennis ball is present. What does this tell the trainer about how to structure reward delivery in this dog's training program? Give a specific example of how they could use this information in a recall exercise.

**Exercise 2**
Two dogs live in the same household. Dog A will work through distractions and repeat behaviors many times to earn a food treat. Dog B loses interest in food quickly but will perform enthusiastically if the session ends with a short game of tug. Explain why using identical reward strategies for both dogs would likely produce different results, and identify what the trainer should adjust for each.

**Exercise 3**
A Border Collie in a basic obedience class keeps breaking her "stay" to chase leaves blowing across the training field. Her owner tries to redirect her with cheese, but the dog shows no interest and continues to fixate on the leaves. Analyze what is happening in terms of competing motivations, and explain why the food reward is failing in this moment.

**Exercise 4**
A rescue dog with an unknown history is brought to a shelter trainer for assessment. The dog ignores food, toys, and praise entirely during the first session. A colleague suggests the dog "just isn't food motivated" and recommends skipping treat-based training. Do you agree with this interpretation? What alternative explanations should the trainer consider before drawing conclusions about this dog's motivational profile?

**Exercise 5**
A trainer is preparing a 12-week program for a young Belgian Malinois whose owner wants reliable off-leash obedience in a busy urban environment. The dog shows high predatory drive (chasing squirrels, fixating on cyclists), moderate food motivation, and very high play/tug drive. Using your understanding of motivation, drives, and learning theory, outline the key decisions the trainer must make about reward selection and sequencing. What risks arise if predatory drive is ignored rather than incorporated into the training plan?

**Exercise 6**
A pet dog named Buster has been successfully trained to sit, down, and come using food rewards at home. His owner reports that in the park, all training "falls apart" — Buster runs to greet other dogs, ignores cues, and won't take treats. The owner concludes that Buster is being "stubborn" and "dominant." Drawing on motivation and drives, canine communication, and welfare principles, construct an alternative explanation for Buster's behavior and recommend a concrete training approach that addresses the root cause rather than the symptoms.

---

## Answer Key

**Answer 1**
The dog's indifference to kibble but intense focus on the tennis ball reveals that play/prey-related rewards — not food — are this dog's primary motivator. Effective training must use whatever the dog finds most reinforcing, not whatever is most convenient for the handler. For a recall exercise, the trainer could have the owner call the dog from a short distance and, the instant the dog arrives and touches the owner's hand, immediately produce the tennis ball and throw it for a brief retrieve. The ball functions as the reinforcer, making the act of returning to the owner highly rewarding. Over many repetitions, the recall itself becomes associated with the onset of play, building a strong and reliable response.

**Answer 2**
Motivation is individual — a reward only functions as a reinforcer if the dog actually values it in that moment. Using kibble for Dog B would likely produce weak, declining responses because food does not hold sufficient value for that dog; the reinforcer isn't reinforcing. Dog A should receive food rewards on a schedule that maintains engagement (varied timing and quantity to sustain interest). Dog B should receive food sparingly or not at all as the primary reward; instead, tug should be used as the terminal reinforcer at the end of a correct behavior chain, or broken into very short training bursts where each successful rep earns a brief game. Matching the reward to the individual dog's motivational hierarchy is what makes training efficient.

**Answer 3**
Predatory drive — triggered by the moving leaves — is generating a very high level of arousal and motivational urgency in the Border Collie. Predatory drive is a deeply ingrained, self-reinforcing behavioral system rooted in the dog's ancestry; it does not require an external reward because the chase itself is intrinsically motivating. The cheese fails because it cannot compete with the arousal level the dog is already experiencing. From a learning theory perspective, the value of any reinforcer is context-dependent: a moderately valued food reward cannot outbid a high-intensity internal drive state. The trainer needs to either reduce the intensity of the trigger (increase distance from the leaves, work in lower-distraction conditions first) or find a way to redirect the predatory impulse into a controlled outlet before food rewards can regain influence.

**Answer 4**
Disagreement is warranted. Concluding that a dog "isn't food motivated" after a single session in a shelter environment overlooks several critical alternative explanations. First, a new or stressful environment elevates cortisol, which suppresses appetite and exploratory behavior — a dog in a fear or shutdown state will not eat even if it is hungry. This connects directly to welfare and stress indicator knowledge: food refusal is a recognized stress signal, not a fixed personality trait. Second, the dog may simply need time to acclimate before its normal motivational systems become accessible. Third, the dog's history is unknown — it may have experienced aversive handling that has generalized to wariness of humans offering things. The trainer should first assess the dog's stress indicators, allow decompression time, try higher-value food in a quieter setting, and also test for social and play motivation before concluding that food-based training won't work.

**Answer 5**
Key decisions the trainer must make:
- **Reward hierarchy**: Tug should be the primary terminal reinforcer for high-effort behaviors (recalls, distance downs) because it aligns with the dog's highest drive. Food can be used for precision shaping and duration behaviors where interrupting flow with a tug game would be impractical.
- **Drive as a training resource**: The Malinois's play drive should be deliberately aroused before demanding high-effort behaviors, and used to reward them — not suppressed. Trying to train this dog in a flat, low-arousal state will produce poor results.
- **Sequencing and proofing**: Training must be systematically proofed against predatory triggers (cyclists, squirrels) by initially working below the dog's threshold and gradually raising criteria — a principle grounded in learning theory (stimulus control and generalization).
- **Risk of ignoring predatory drive**: If the drive is not channeled, the dog has a persistent, self-reinforcing behavior system that competes directly with trained behaviors. In an urban environment, an unmanaged predatory response creates safety risks (dog bolting into traffic chasing a cyclist) and will reliably override trained cues in high-arousal moments. The drive does not disappear because it is ignored; it simply remains uncontrolled.

**Answer 6**
The "stubborn and dominant" interpretation misreads motivation and communication. A more accurate explanation: Buster's training was conditioned in a low-distraction home environment, meaning his trained behaviors have not been generalized to high-arousal contexts. In the park, social drive (motivation to interact with other dogs) is extremely high — likely higher than food motivation in that moment, especially if the park environment also raises his overall arousal. Food refusal in this context is not defiance; it is a predictable outcome when a low-value reward competes against a high-value internal motivator. His behavior also likely reflects normal canine communication drives — approaching other dogs is a social signal behavior system, not a dominance display.

**Recommended approach:**
1. Begin training in the park at a distance where Buster can see other dogs but his arousal stays manageable enough for food to work — this is threshold management from stress/welfare principles.
2. Reinforce check-ins and cued behaviors heavily at this distance before gradually decreasing distance to other dogs.
3. Identify whether access to greet other dogs can itself be used as a reinforcer (ask for a sit, then release with "go say hi") — this uses social motivation as the reward rather than fighting against it.
4. Build a history of reinforcement in park contexts so that the trained behaviors generalize, and the owner becomes a source of good outcomes even in high-distraction environments. The goal is not to suppress Buster's social drive but to make the owner's cues relevant within it.
