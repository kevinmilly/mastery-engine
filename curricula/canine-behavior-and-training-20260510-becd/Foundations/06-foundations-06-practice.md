## Exercises

**Exercise 1**
A trainer says "Sit," the dog sits, and the trainer immediately gives a treat. Identify the antecedent, behavior, and consequence in this sequence. Then explain why the order of these three elements matters — what would change if the consequence came before the behavior?

**Exercise 2**
A dog is walking past a fire hydrant. The smell of another dog's urine causes the dog to stop and sniff intensely. Identify the stimulus and the response in this interaction. Is this an example of a learned association or an unlearned one? Justify your answer.

**Exercise 3**
Every evening, a dog owner picks up the leash from a hook by the door, and the dog immediately begins spinning and whining with excitement — even before the owner says a word or reaches for the door handle. Using learning theory vocabulary, explain how the leash came to produce this reaction. What specific type of relationship between events does this illustrate?

**Exercise 4**
Two trainers disagree. Trainer A says: "The dog barked because she saw a squirrel." Trainer B says: "The dog barked because she's anxious." Analyze both explanations using antecedent–behavior–consequence language. Which explanation is more useful for a trainer trying to change the barking, and why?

**Exercise 5**
A shelter dog consistently growls when a staff member reaches toward her food bowl. The staff decide to simply avoid reaching into her bowl as a solution. Using learning theory vocabulary, explain what is happening from the dog's perspective — including the roles of behavior, consequence, and association. Then, drawing on what you know about canine welfare and stress indicators from prior lessons, evaluate whether avoiding the bowl is a satisfactory long-term solution.

**Exercise 6**
You are observing a dog who has lived in a noisy urban environment since puppyhood. Despite loud truck sounds nearby, the dog shows no stress response — ears remain neutral, body is loose, and she continues sniffing the ground calmly. A second dog, recently adopted from a quiet rural shelter, startles and freezes at the same sound. Both dogs have the same basic sensory capability for hearing. Using learning theory vocabulary and your knowledge of canine sensory processing and stress indicators, construct an explanation for why these two dogs respond so differently to the identical stimulus.

## Answer Key

**Answer 1**
The antecedent is the verbal cue "Sit" — the event that occurs before the behavior and sets the occasion for it. The behavior is the dog sitting — the observable, measurable action. The consequence is receiving the treat — the event that follows the behavior and will influence whether it is repeated.

Order matters because consequences only strengthen or weaken behaviors they immediately follow. If the treat were delivered before the dog sat, it would not function as a consequence of sitting at all — it would simply be a free event in the environment. The dog would have no way to connect the treat to the act of sitting, because sitting had not yet occurred. The A–B–C sequence has directional logic: the antecedent signals what to do, the behavior occurs, and then the consequence tells the dog whether that behavior was worth repeating. Reversing the order breaks that informational chain.

**Answer 2**
The stimulus is the smell of the other dog's urine on the hydrant — the environmental event that acts on the dog's senses. The response is the dog stopping and sniffing intensely — the observable reaction to that stimulus.

This is most likely an unlearned response. Dogs are born with the sensory architecture and behavioral tendency to investigate scent-based social information — this is part of their evolved olfactory and social biology, not something acquired through individual experience with that specific hydrant. The dog did not need prior training or repeated exposure to learn to find urine scent compelling; the tendency comes pre-wired. This contrasts with a learned association, which requires some history of pairing between events across the dog's individual lifetime.

**Answer 3**
Initially, the leash was a neutral stimulus — it had no particular meaning to the dog. Over time, however, the leash was consistently followed by the walk: a highly rewarding experience involving movement, outdoor smells, and exploration. Through repeated pairing, the dog formed an association between the leash (stimulus) and the walk (outcome). The leash stopped being neutral and became a reliable predictor of something good.

Now, the leash alone is enough to trigger the excitement response — spinning and whining — because the dog has learned that leash predicts walk. This illustrates associative learning: a relationship formed between two events that occur together reliably, such that one comes to elicit a reaction originally produced only by the other. The leash is functioning as a conditioned stimulus. Importantly, no one taught the dog to get excited about the leash directly; the association formed through experience.

**Answer 4**
Trainer A's explanation is antecedent-focused: the squirrel (antecedent/stimulus) triggered the bark (behavior). This is descriptive of what immediately preceded the behavior and is observable.

Trainer B's explanation invokes an internal state — anxiety — as the cause. While internal states are real, "anxiety" is not directly observable; it must be inferred. In A–B–C terms, Trainer B is pointing to a background condition that may influence how the dog processes antecedents, but anxiety itself is not a discrete antecedent event.

For a trainer trying to change the barking, Trainer A's framing is more immediately actionable: it identifies a specific, controllable antecedent (squirrel visibility) that can be manipulated — distance, exposure, environment management, or building a different response to that stimulus. Trainer B's framing is harder to act on directly because you cannot remove anxiety by removing a single environmental event.

That said, the most complete analysis would recognize that both may be true simultaneously: the squirrel is the triggering antecedent, but the dog's anxious baseline may mean the threshold for that trigger is lower. Trainer B's concern is not wrong — it's just incomplete as a standalone training plan.

**Answer 5**
From the dog's perspective, reaching toward the food bowl is the antecedent — a stimulus that has come to predict something the dog perceives as threatening (perhaps past experiences of food being taken, or discomfort with hands near the face). The behavior is growling — a communicative response aimed at increasing distance from the threat. The consequence, when staff back away, is that the threat disappears. This means the growling was effective; from the dog's experience, growling caused the aversive stimulus to go away, which strengthens the behavior.

An association has formed: hand approaching bowl → threat → growl → threat retreats. The dog has learned that growling works.

Avoiding the bowl prevents the immediate conflict, but it is not a satisfactory long-term solution for several reasons. First, it does not change the underlying association — the dog still perceives approach as threatening, and the conditioned emotional response remains intact. Second, the behavior is likely to generalize: if the association is not addressed, the dog may begin growling in other resource-related or approach situations. Third, from a welfare standpoint, a dog who is regularly in a state that necessitates defensive growling is experiencing chronic stress — prior lessons identified growling and stiffening as stress indicators, not just disobedience. Suppressing or avoiding the trigger without addressing the root association leaves the dog's welfare unresolved and may cause escalation over time if avoidance is eventually impossible.

**Answer 6**
Both dogs have equivalent sensory hardware — their auditory systems detect the truck sound with similar fidelity. The difference lies in what each dog has learned to associate with that sound.

The urban dog has been exposed to loud vehicle sounds repeatedly since early in life, in contexts where those sounds were not paired with any negative consequence — the noise occurred, nothing harmful followed, and life continued normally. Through this history, an association formed (or rather, failed to form) between the sound and threat. The sound became a familiar, non-predictive stimulus. This is sometimes called habituation or the absence of a conditioned fear response: the dog's nervous system has learned that this stimulus is not a reliable signal of anything worth reacting to. Behaviorally, the loose body, neutral ears, and continued sniffing confirm an absence of stress arousal.

The rural dog has no such history with urban sounds. For her, the truck noise is a novel, sudden, high-intensity stimulus — and novel stimuli, particularly those that are loud or unexpected, tend to trigger an orienting or startle response as a default. Without prior associations that predict safety, the dog has no learned basis for dismissing the sound. She freezes — a stress indicator consistent with prior lessons — because her system is assessing an unknown stimulus with no contextual information to indicate it is harmless.

The key concept is that identical stimuli produce different responses when the two organisms have different associative histories with those stimuli. The stimulus is the same; the meaning each dog has learned to assign to it is not.
