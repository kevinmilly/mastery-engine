## Exercises

**Exercise 1**
A dog repeatedly circles a spot on the living room carpet before lying down, even though the carpet is already flat and comfortable. A new dog owner finds this puzzling and assumes the dog is being "weird." Using what you know about canine evolutionary history, explain why this behavior makes sense — and why calling it "weird" misframes the situation.

**Exercise 2**
Two dogs meet at a dog park. One approaches in a low, curved arc with its body slightly lowered; the other stands tall and approaches in a straight line. Without knowing either dog's breed or training history, what does domestication history suggest about what each posture likely signals, and why would these signals have been preserved through the domestication process?

**Exercise 3**
A family adopts a two-year-old Siberian Husky. Despite living entirely indoors, the dog howls frequently, attempts to dig under the fence, and seems restless unless given hours of daily exercise. The family wonders if the dog is "badly behaved" or "broken." How would an understanding of canine ancestry and domestication reframe this interpretation — and what practical implication follows for the family?

**Exercise 4**
Wolves live in family-based social units with fluid, context-dependent roles. Dogs, shaped by thousands of years of domestication, live with humans rather than conspecifics. Given this shift in social environment, why might a dog that was never properly socialized with other dogs still function well socially with humans — and what does this suggest about what domestication actually selected for?

**Exercise 5**
A trainer argues: "Dogs are basically wolves, so you need to establish dominance over them the way an alpha wolf dominates its pack." Using your understanding of canine evolutionary history and domestication, identify the specific flaws in this reasoning. Construct a more accurate alternative framework for understanding the human-dog relationship.

**Exercise 6**
Consider two dogs: one is a livestock guardian breed selectively bred for thousands of years to work independently and make autonomous decisions, and the other is a herding breed selectively bred to work in close coordination with a human handler. Both dogs join a basic obedience class. Predict how each dog might respond differently to standard reward-based training, and explain those predicted differences in terms of domestication and selective breeding pressures — not breed stereotypes or anecdote.

## Answer Key

**Answer 1**
The behavior — circling before lying down — traces back to pre-domestication survival needs. Wild canids living in grasslands or brush would circle to trample vegetation, check for hidden threats like insects or snakes, and create a more comfortable, secure resting spot. The dog is not being "weird"; it is executing a behavioral pattern that was adaptive for its ancestors and has been retained through domestication because it caused no harm and was never selected against. Calling it "weird" misframes the behavior by judging it through a human lens rather than an evolutionary one. The correct framing is that this is a *normal, ancestrally-rooted behavior* occurring in a context where its original function no longer applies — not a malfunction or quirk.

**Answer 2**
The curved, lowered approach signals non-threat and social deference — a dog communicating that it does not intend conflict and is open to interaction. The straight, upright approach signals confidence or potential challenge. These signals are preserved from wolf social communication, where managing intragroup tension without constant physical conflict was essential to group survival. Domestication did not eliminate these signals because they remained functional: dogs still need to negotiate social encounters, both with other dogs and, in modified form, with humans. Domestication may have actually amplified dogs' ability to read and produce social signals, since individuals that communicated effectively with humans and other animals were more likely to thrive in human environments.

**Answer 3**
The dog is not badly behaved or broken. Siberian Huskies were selectively bred over centuries by the Chukchi people for long-distance sled pulling in harsh Arctic conditions — a role requiring enormous endurance, strong independent drive, and vocalization for communication across distances. These traits are deeply embedded in the breed's behavioral profile as a result of that selective history. An indoor, low-exercise lifestyle does not erase those drives; it simply gives them no outlet, which produces the restlessness, howling, and escape attempts the family observes. The practical implication is that the family must match the dog's environment and daily activity to its ancestral behavioral needs — not attempt to suppress those needs or attribute them to bad character. Adequate high-intensity exercise and mental engagement are not optional extras for this dog; they are requirements.

**Answer 4**
Domestication appears to have selected not just for tolerance of humans but for a *generalized capacity for cross-species social bonding and communication* — dogs that could read human cues, respond to human emotional states, and integrate into a human-led social group had strong survival advantages over those that could not. This is a different selection pressure than what shaped wolf social behavior, which was calibrated for bonding with conspecifics in a family unit. A dog with limited dog-dog socialization may struggle to read canine-specific signals, but its core social wiring — attachment, responsiveness to social cues, ability to function within a group hierarchy — is calibrated toward humans. This suggests domestication selected for *flexibility in social attachment* and *human-specific attunement*, not merely a scaled-down version of wolf pack behavior.

**Answer 5**
The argument contains at least two significant flaws rooted in evolutionary misunderstanding. First, it misrepresents wolf social structure: research on wild wolf populations shows that wolves live in family units where "dominance" is more accurately described as parental leadership than competitive rank-enforcement. The rigid alpha/beta hierarchy model was based on studies of unrelated captive wolves — an artificial situation. Second, and more fundamentally, dogs are not wolves. Thousands of years of domestication have meaningfully diverged dog behavior, cognition, and social orientation from their wolf ancestors. Dogs have been specifically shaped to look to humans for guidance and cooperation, not to engage in dominance competition with them. A more accurate framework: the human-dog relationship is best understood as a *cooperative social bond* in which the human functions as a provider of structure, resources, and guidance — more analogous to a trusted leader within a family unit than a dominance-enforcing competitor. Training approaches that leverage this cooperative orientation (clear communication, reward, consistency) align with how dogs are actually wired; dominance-based approaches risk damaging the trust that domestication built in.

**Answer 6**
The livestock guardian breed was selected for *low human-direction dependency* — it needed to assess situations and act without handler input while protecting a flock. This creates a dog that may be less intrinsically motivated to orient toward the handler, less responsive to handler-initiated cues as inherently rewarding, and more likely to make independent decisions during training. Standard reward-based obedience, which relies heavily on the dog finding handler interaction and approval reinforcing, may progress more slowly because that handler-focus was not what this dog's lineage was optimized for. The herding breed, by contrast, was selected for *precise, real-time responsiveness to human signals* — its entire working role depended on reading and reacting to a handler's commands across a field. This dog is likely to find handler engagement intrinsically motivating, may acquire cued behaviors quickly, and may actively seek instruction. The difference is not about intelligence or willingness in a moral sense — it reflects different *selective pressures* that shaped what each dog finds reinforcing and how each dog is wired to relate to human direction. Effective training for the guardian breed may require building handler value deliberately and finding what that individual dog finds genuinely motivating beyond social approval.
