# Glossary: canine behavior and training

**Aggression**
Behavior intended to cause harm or threat, often manifested as growling, snarling, snapping, or biting. In canine behavior, it is typically a defensive response stemming from fear, anxiety, resource guarding, or pain, rather than malicious intent.

**Anxiety**
An emotional state characterized by worry, apprehension, and nervousness, often in anticipation of a potential threat or undesirable outcome. In dogs, anxiety can lead to a range of stress behaviors and is distinct from fear, which is a response to an immediate threat.

**Antecedent**
The event or situation that immediately precedes a behavior. In learning theory, it's the "what happens right before" that sets the stage for a dog's action.

**Arousal**
A state of physiological and psychological activation, ranging from very calm to highly excited or stressed. In dogs, high arousal can sometimes lead to difficulty in processing information and controlling impulses.

**Association**
The mental link formed between two or more stimuli, or between a behavior and a consequence. This foundational concept explains how dogs learn to connect events or actions, such as a leash with a walk.

**Aversive Techniques**
Training methods that involve the use of unpleasant or punishing stimuli to suppress unwanted behaviors. Examples include the use of shock collars, prong collars, or physical corrections, which can carry ethical concerns and potential adverse effects on a dog's welfare.

**Behavior**
Any observable action or reaction of an organism. In the context of canine behavior, this includes everything from sitting on cue to barking at the doorbell.

**Behavior Chain**
A sequence of individual behaviors linked together, where the completion of one behavior serves as the cue for the next, and the final behavior in the sequence is reinforced. This allows for teaching complex multi-step tasks to dogs.

**Behavior Modification**
The systematic application of learning theory principles to change undesirable behaviors and teach desired ones. This field focuses on understanding the antecedents and consequences of behavior to achieve lasting change.

**Body Postures**
Specific positions of a dog's body that convey information about their emotional state and intentions. Interpreting these subtle cues is essential for understanding canine communication.

**Classical Conditioning**
A type of learning where an animal learns to associate two stimuli, resulting in an involuntary, automatic response to a previously neutral stimulus. Also known as Pavlovian conditioning, it explains how dogs form emotional responses like fear or excitement to certain triggers.

**Consequence**
The event or situation that immediately follows a behavior. In learning theory, consequences are crucial because they either increase or decrease the future likelihood of that behavior occurring again.

**Counter-Conditioning**
A technique used to change a dog's emotional response to a stimulus from negative to positive. This is achieved by pairing the feared or disliked stimulus with something the dog enjoys, such as high-value treats.

**Cues**
Signals (verbal, visual, or physical) that tell a dog a specific behavior is requested. Unlike commands, which imply forced compliance, cues invite a dog to perform a learned behavior.

**Desensitization**
The gradual and systematic exposure of a dog to a feared or disliked stimulus at levels below which it elicits a negative response. The goal is to reduce the emotional reaction over time by making the stimulus less impactful.

**Distress**
A state of suffering, anxiety, or pain, often indicated by specific behaviors or physiological signs. In canine behavior, it’s a critical indicator that a dog’s welfare may be compromised.

**Domestication**
The process by which wild animals are adapted to live with humans over generations, involving genetic changes and changes in behavior. This process has profoundly shaped the modern dog's social structures and innate tendencies.

**Drives**
Internal motivations or urges that compel an animal to act in certain ways (e.g., predatory drive, social drive, play drive). Identifying a dog's primary drives is essential for designing effective training and enrichment strategies.

**Environmental Reinforcers**
Naturally occurring rewards in the environment that motivate a dog's behavior. These can include sniffing new smells, chasing squirrels, exploring, or greeting other dogs, often more powerful than artificial rewards.

**Extinction**
The process of reducing a previously reinforced behavior by removing the reinforcement that maintains it. For example, ignoring a dog's attention-seeking bark can lead to the barking decreasing over time.

**Extinction Burst**
A temporary increase in the frequency, intensity, or duration of a behavior when extinction is first implemented. This often occurs because the dog tries harder to get the previously available reinforcement.

**Fading Lures**
The gradual removal of a lure as a dog begins to perform the desired behavior independently. This ensures the dog performs the behavior in response to the cue, rather than relying on the physical guidance of the lure.

**Facial Expressions**
Muscle movements in a dog's face that convey their emotional states and intentions. Observing details like eye contact, ear position, and mouth tension provides insight into a dog's feelings.

**Fear**
An emotional and physiological response to a perceived threat, whether real or imagined. In dogs, fear can manifest as avoidance, freezing, or defensive aggression.

**Fixed Interval (FI) Schedule**
An intermittent reinforcement schedule where reinforcement is delivered for the first response after a specific, predictable amount of time has passed. This often leads to a "scallop" pattern of behavior, with responses increasing as the time for reinforcement approaches.

**Fixed Ratio (FR) Schedule**
An intermittent reinforcement schedule where reinforcement is delivered after a specific, predictable number of responses. This typically results in a high rate of response, followed by a pause after reinforcement.

**Generalization**
The ability of a dog to perform a learned behavior reliably in different contexts (environments, people, distractions). It means the dog truly understands the behavior, rather than associating it with a specific setting.

**Intermittent Reinforcement**
Providing reinforcement only some of the time a behavior is performed, rather than every time. This schedule strengthens the persistence and durability of behaviors, making them more resistant to extinction.

**Involuntary Emotional Associations**
Automatic links formed in a dog's mind between stimuli that evoke emotional responses without conscious control. These are the basis of classical conditioning and explain many reactive behaviors.

**Jackpotting**
Delivering a significantly larger or higher-value reward than usual for an exceptionally good or unexpected behavior, or a breakthrough in learning. This dramatically increases motivation and reinforces the desired action.

**Learning Theory**
A scientific framework for understanding how animals (and humans) acquire, modify, and maintain behaviors through experience. It provides the foundational principles for all effective dog training.

**Leash Reactivity**
A dog's overreaction (e.g., barking, lunging, growling) to specific triggers (like other dogs, people, or vehicles) while on a leash. This behavior is often rooted in fear, frustration, or a desire to create distance.

**Lure**
Using a treat or toy to physically guide a dog into performing a desired behavior. Lures are a common initial step in teaching new behaviors, but should be faded quickly.

**Mental Stimulation**
Activities that engage a dog's cognitive abilities, such as puzzle toys, scent work, or training new tricks. Providing adequate mental stimulation is crucial for preventing boredom and related behavioral problems.

**Motivators**
Anything that causes an individual to want to behave in a particular way. In dog training, understanding a dog's primary motivators (e.g., food, toys, praise, play) is key to designing effective reward systems.

**Negative Punishment (P-)**
Removing something desirable to decrease the future likelihood of a behavior. For example, if a dog jumps on you for attention and you turn away, you are removing the desired attention. Often confused with being "bad," "negative" simply means subtracting.

**Negative Reinforcement (R-)**
Removing something aversive to increase the future likelihood of a behavior. For example, a dog might learn to sit to make the handler stop pushing down on their rear. Often confused with being "bad," "negative" simply means subtracting.

**Olfactory Signals**
Chemical signals (scents) used by dogs for communication. Dogs possess a superior sense of smell and rely heavily on these signals to gather information about their environment and other canines.

**Operant Conditioning**
A type of learning where an animal learns to associate a voluntary behavior with its consequences, leading to an increase or decrease in that behavior. This is the core framework for teaching and modifying purposeful behaviors in dogs.

**Over-Excitement**
A state of excessive physiological and emotional arousal, often leading to uncontrolled or frantic behavior. While often appearing joyful, over-excitement can hinder a dog's ability to respond to cues and manage their impulses.

**Positive Punishment (P+)**
Adding something aversive to decrease the future likelihood of a behavior. For example, giving a leash correction to stop a dog from pulling. Often confused with being "good," "positive" simply means adding.

**Positive Reinforcement (P+)**
Adding something desirable to increase the future likelihood of a behavior. For example, giving a treat to a dog after it sits on cue. Often confused with being "good," "positive" simply means adding.

**Premack Principle**
The concept that a high-probability behavior (something a dog is likely to do) can be used to reinforce a low-probability behavior (something a dog is less likely to do). For example, "first sit, then you can go outside and play."

**Proofing**
The process of practicing a learned behavior in various environments, with different distractions, and with different handlers to ensure reliability. This helps a dog perform the behavior consistently in real-world situations.

**Punishment**
A consequence that weakens or decreases the future likelihood of a behavior. In learning theory, the term is defined by its effect on behavior, not by the intention or harshness of the action.

**Reactive Behaviors**
Behaviors (often fear-based or aggressive) displayed by a dog in response to a specific trigger, usually due to a learned association. These responses are typically involuntary and driven by emotion.

**Reinforcement**
A consequence that strengthens or increases the future likelihood of a behavior. This is a fundamental principle in dog training, encouraging desired actions through positive outcomes.

**Resource Guarding**
A dog's defensive behavior (e.g., growling, snapping, staring) to protect valuable items (like food, toys, beds, or even people) from perceived threats. It stems from an underlying insecurity about losing a valued possession.

**Response**
A specific reaction or behavior emitted by an organism in reaction to a stimulus. In learning theory, it's the dog's action following an antecedent.

**Reward Value**
The desirability or importance of a reinforcer to a dog. Higher reward value means a dog is more motivated to perform a behavior to earn that specific reward.

**Separation Anxiety**
A severe distress response exhibited by dogs when left alone or separated from their attachment figures, often manifesting as destructive behavior, inappropriate urination/defecation, or excessive vocalization. It is a complex anxiety disorder requiring careful intervention.

**Shaping**
A training technique where successive approximations of a desired behavior are reinforced until the full behavior is achieved. This is indispensable for teaching behaviors a dog doesn't naturally offer, like closing a door.

**Stimulus (plural: stimuli)**
Anything that causes a specific reaction or response from an organism. This could be an external event like a sound, or an internal sensation like hunger.

**Stress Indicators**
Specific behaviors or physical signs that indicate a dog is experiencing stress or discomfort. These can be subtle, like lip licking or yawning, or more overt, like panting or trembling.

**Successive Approximations**
Behaviors that are increasingly similar to the final desired behavior. In shaping, these are the small steps a dog takes toward the target behavior, each reinforced to build momentum.

**Variable Interval (VI) Schedule**
An intermittent reinforcement schedule where reinforcement is delivered for the first response after an unpredictable amount of time has passed, averaging out to a certain interval. This schedule produces a steady, moderate rate of response.

**Variable Ratio (VR) Schedule**
An intermittent reinforcement schedule where reinforcement is delivered after an unpredictable number of responses, averaging out to a certain ratio. This schedule creates high and steady rates of response, making the behavior very persistent.

**Variable Reinforcement**
A schedule of reinforcement where rewards are given unpredictably, rather than every single time a behavior is performed. This maintains a dog's motivation and makes behaviors more resilient.

**Veterinary Behaviorist**
A veterinarian who has specialized training and is board-certified in the field of animal behavior. They are qualified to diagnose and treat complex behavioral disorders that may have underlying medical or neurological components.

**Visual Cues**
Observable body postures, facial expressions, and tail movements dogs use to communicate their intentions and emotional states. Understanding these is vital for interpreting canine social interactions.

**Vocalizations**
Sounds made by dogs for communication, including barks, growls, whimpers, howls, and yelps. Each vocalization can convey different messages depending on context and accompanying body language.

**Welfare**
The state of an animal's physical and mental health and well-being. Ensuring a dog's welfare encompasses meeting their basic needs, providing appropriate mental stimulation, and minimizing stress and fear.
