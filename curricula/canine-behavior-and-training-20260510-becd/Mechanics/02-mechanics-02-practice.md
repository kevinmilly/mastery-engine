## Exercises

**Exercise 1**
A trainer wants a dog to sit before receiving its food bowl. Every time the dog sits, the bowl is placed down. Every time the dog jumps up, the trainer turns away and waits. Identify which quadrant is operating in each scenario and explain what outcome each is designed to produce.

**Exercise 2**
A dog barks at the front door whenever someone knocks. The owner yells "No!" each time this happens. Two weeks later, the barking has increased. Using the four-quadrant framework, explain what went wrong with the owner's reasoning, and identify which quadrant their intervention actually belonged to — regardless of their intent.

**Exercise 3**
A service dog in training must learn to walk calmly past food on the floor without sniffing or veering. Design a training plan using exactly two different quadrants — one to increase the desired behavior and one to decrease the undesired behavior. Justify why you chose those two quadrants over the other two, and identify any risk in your chosen approach.

**Exercise 4**
A dog repeatedly pulls toward other dogs on leash. A trainer uses a prong collar that causes mild discomfort when the dog pulls, and the discomfort stops the moment the leash goes slack. A second trainer uses a different method: whenever the dog pulls, the walk immediately stops and reverses direction, removing the dog's ability to approach other dogs. Classify each method by quadrant and explain which is more likely to produce reliable behavior change without side effects. Defend your answer.

**Exercise 5**
You are working with a rescue dog that has a history of fear-based reactions (lunging and barking) toward strangers. A colleague suggests using positive punishment to suppress the lunging quickly. Drawing on both operant conditioning principles and what you know from classical conditioning, explain why this approach carries a specific risk beyond simple suppression — and what alternative quadrant-based strategy would address the root cause rather than the symptom.

**Exercise 6**
A dog has learned to ring a bell by the door to go outside. The owners, finding this convenient, respond every time. Over six months, the dog begins ringing the bell up to 20 times a day, often with no apparent need to go out. Using the four quadrants, explain the behavioral history that produced this outcome. Then propose a modification plan that uses at least two quadrants to bring the behavior back to a functional frequency — without eliminating it entirely.

---

## Answer Key

**Answer 1**
Placing the food bowl when the dog sits is **positive reinforcement** (R+): a desirable stimulus (food) is added following the behavior (sitting), increasing the likelihood that sitting will occur again before meals. Turning away when the dog jumps is **negative punishment** (P−): a desired stimulus (the owner's attention and the bowl) is removed following the behavior (jumping), decreasing the likelihood of jumping in the future. Both quadrants are being used in tandem — R+ builds the wanted behavior, P− reduces the competing one.

**Answer 2**
The owner assumed yelling would suppress barking, treating it as positive punishment (adding an aversive to decrease behavior). But barking increased — which means the intervention functioned as **positive reinforcement** (R+). The dog experienced the owner's attention as a rewarding stimulus added after barking, making barking more likely. Intent does not determine quadrant — outcome does. The key lesson: any consequence that increases a behavior is reinforcement, regardless of what the trainer believed they were delivering.

**Answer 3**
A reasonable plan: use **positive reinforcement** (R+) to increase calm walking past food — reward the dog with a high-value treat the moment it passes without sniffing. Use **negative punishment** (P−) to decrease veering — the moment the dog starts to veer toward the food, immediately remove forward progress (stop or redirect away), taking away the opportunity to approach. These two quadrants are preferred over positive punishment (which risks fear or suppression of exploratory behavior needed for a working dog) and negative reinforcement (which requires manufacturing an aversive state, complicating the emotional picture). Risk: if the food reward is less valuable than the food on the floor, the R+ contingency will lose to the environmental reinforcer.

**Answer 4**
The prong collar method is **negative reinforcement** (R−): an aversive (discomfort) is present and is removed when the dog produces the desired behavior (loose leash), increasing loose-leash walking. The reverse-direction method is **negative punishment** (P−): a desired outcome (approaching other dogs) is removed when the dog pulls, decreasing pulling. The P− approach is more likely to produce reliable, side-effect-free behavior change. R− using physical discomfort risks conditioning a fear or pain association with the presence of other dogs — a classically conditioned side effect layered on top of the operant procedure. P− removes access to the reinforcer (other dogs) without introducing pain, keeping the dog's emotional state neutral and the learning clean. It also teaches the dog that *it* controls access to other dogs through leash manners, building a clearer contingency.

**Answer 5**
The specific risk of using positive punishment (P+) on a fear-reactive dog is not just that it suppresses the behavior — it is that punishment delivered in the presence of strangers will, through **classical conditioning**, become associated with strangers themselves. The dog already has a conditioned fear response (stranger → anxiety → lunge). Adding punishment in that context pairs strangers with an additional aversive, deepening the negative emotional association and potentially intensifying the underlying fear even as the overt behavior is suppressed. This produces a dog that is outwardly quieter but internally more reactive — a dangerous combination. The better approach uses **counter-conditioning** (classical) combined with **positive reinforcement** (R+): expose the dog to strangers at a sub-threshold distance, pair their presence with high-value food, and reinforce calm orienting behavior toward the handler. This addresses the conditioned fear state, not just its behavioral expression.

**Answer 6**
The behavioral history: bell-ringing was initially shaped and maintained by **positive reinforcement** (R+) — owners added the reward of going outside every time the dog rang the bell. Over time, "going outside" itself became a powerful reinforcer (sniffing, exploring, social contact with the environment). The 100% reinforcement schedule meant the behavior was never placed under any extinction pressure, so it increased in frequency as the dog discovered it could access outdoor reinforcers on demand. The modification plan: (1) Apply **extinction** selectively — do not respond to bell rings that occur within a defined interval after the last outing (e.g., less than 90 minutes). This uses the removal of R+ to reduce non-functional rings without eliminating the behavior entirely. (2) Use **positive reinforcement** (R+) to strengthen rings that occur at appropriate intervals — respond promptly and enthusiastically to those, reinforcing the functional behavior on a variable schedule that increases its durability. This two-quadrant approach recalibrates the behavior to a functional frequency while preserving the communication system the dog has learned.
