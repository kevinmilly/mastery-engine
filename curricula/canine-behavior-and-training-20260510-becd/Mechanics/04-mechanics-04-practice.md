## Exercises

**Exercise 1**
A trainer wants to teach a dog to retrieve a dumbbell. The dog currently shows no interest in picking up objects. The trainer decides to start by clicking and treating whenever the dog sniffs the dumbbell. Is this a reasonable first step in a shaping plan? Explain why or why not, and describe what the trainer should look for before moving to the next approximation.

**Exercise 2**
You are shaping a dog to push a ball with its nose. The dog is reliably nudging the ball 2 inches. You raise your criteria and now require 6 inches — but the dog stops offering the behavior entirely and stands looking at you. What shaping error likely occurred, and what should you do next?

**Exercise 3**
A trainer is shaping a dog to weave between poles. She identifies these approximations in order: (1) walk past one pole, (2) curve body around one pole, (3) complete one full weave entry, (4) weave through three poles, (5) weave through six poles at speed. Midway through training, the dog regresses and can only reliably complete step 2. What does this regression tell you, and how should the trainer respond without undermining the progress already made?

**Exercise 4**
Two trainers are shaping the same behavior — a dog lying flat on its side ("play dead"). Trainer A marks and rewards any downward head movement toward the floor. Trainer B waits silently until the dog spontaneously rolls completely onto its side, then marks. After 10 sessions, Trainer A's dog is progressing steadily; Trainer B's dog has been marked only twice. Analyze the difference in their approaches and explain which principle each trainer is applying (or failing to apply).

**Exercise 5**
A dog has been conditioned through classical conditioning to associate a clicker with food (prior topic). Now the trainer uses that clicker to shape a novel behavior: spinning in a circle. Explain how the two learning processes work together in this scenario, and describe what would happen to the shaping process if the classical association between clicker and food were to weaken — for example, if the trainer frequently clicked without following with food.

**Exercise 6**
A trainer is working with a rescue dog that is fearful and rarely offers spontaneous behavior — it mostly freezes or hides. Standard shaping relies on the animal offering approximations voluntarily. Given what you know about shaping, positive reinforcement, and classical conditioning, design a modified approach that could begin building the conditions necessary for shaping to work. Justify each step in terms of the underlying behavioral principles.

---

## Answer Key

**Answer 1**
Yes, this is a reasonable first step. Shaping works by reinforcing successive approximations — behaviors that are closer and closer to the final goal. Since the dog shows no interest in the dumbbell, sniffing it is the closest available behavior to the end goal of picking it up. Marking and rewarding sniffing creates a behavioral history with the object and increases the probability the dog will interact with it further. Before moving to the next approximation (e.g., mouthing or touching with teeth), the trainer should look for the behavior to be offered consistently and confidently — typically several times per session without hesitation — indicating the dog understands that interacting with the dumbbell is what produces reinforcement.

**Answer 2**
The likely error is raising criteria too steeply — jumping from 2 inches to 6 inches was too large a step for the dog at this stage. When the dog stops offering behavior entirely, it signals that the new criterion is outside the dog's current "success zone," leading to extinction of the behavior. The correct response is to temporarily lower criteria back to 2 inches (or even slightly less), let the dog succeed and rebuild momentum, then raise criteria in smaller increments — perhaps to 3 inches, then 4 — before attempting 6 inches again. This keeps the reinforcement rate high enough to maintain the dog's motivation and behavioral output.

**Answer 3**
Regression to an earlier step usually signals that criteria were raised too quickly, that training sessions have been too long or too infrequent, or that something in the environment is affecting the dog's performance (stress, distraction, fatigue). It does not mean the earlier learning is lost — the dog can still perform step 2, which confirms that foundation is solid. The trainer should not start over from step 1. Instead, she should return to step 2, reinforce it a few times to rebuild confidence and fluency, then progress more gradually — possibly adding an intermediate step between 2 and 3 — before attempting step 3 again. Treating regression as a training data point rather than failure is key.

**Answer 4**
Trainer A is correctly applying the core principle of shaping: reinforcing successive approximations. By marking any downward head movement, she keeps the reinforcement rate high, which sustains the dog's motivation and provides clear information about what is earning reward. The dog can build toward the final behavior incrementally. Trainer B is misapplying shaping by waiting for the complete final behavior before marking — this is closer to capturing (waiting for a fully formed natural behavior) than shaping. Because the full behavior rarely occurs spontaneously, the reinforcement rate drops to near zero, and the dog has no gradient of feedback to follow. The result is a frustrated or disengaged dog. Effective shaping requires meeting the dog where it currently is, not where you want it to end up.

**Answer 5**
The two processes work together in series. Classical conditioning established the clicker as a conditioned reinforcer: through repeated pairing with food, the click acquired the ability to produce the same anticipatory response as food itself. This makes the click meaningful and immediate — it can "mark" the exact moment of a correct approximation even before food is delivered. Shaping then uses that conditioned reinforcer to selectively strengthen successive approximations of the spin. Without the classical association, the click would be a neutral sound with no reinforcing value, and marking approximations would have no effect on behavior. If the trainer frequently clicked without delivering food, the classical association would undergo extinction — the click would lose its conditioned reinforcer status through unpaired presentations. The dog would gradually stop responding to the click as meaningful feedback, and the shaping plan would stall because the marker no longer functions as a reinforcer.

**Answer 6**
A fearful dog that freezes or hides is not in a behavioral state that supports shaping — shaping requires the animal to voluntarily offer behavior, which requires a baseline of safety and engagement. A modified approach would proceed in phases:

**Phase 1 — Classical conditioning to the environment and trainer.** Before asking for any behavior, pair the trainer's presence (and later the sound of the clicker) with high-value food delivered without any behavioral requirement. This begins to shift the dog's emotional response from fear to neutral or mild anticipation. The dog learns: trainer appearing → good things happen, unconditionally.

**Phase 2 — Reinforce any voluntary movement.** Once the dog is no longer freezing and begins to make small voluntary movements (shifting weight, turning head, taking a step), mark and reward these — not for being "correct," but for being offered. This uses positive reinforcement to increase the overall rate of behavioral output, which is the raw material shaping requires.

**Phase 3 — Begin shaping proper.** Once the dog is actively offering behavior and has a positive association with the marker, the trainer can begin selecting specific approximations toward a target behavior.

Each step is justified: Phase 1 uses classical conditioning to change the emotional valence of the context. Phase 2 uses positive reinforcement to increase behavioral variability. Phase 3 applies shaping only once the preconditions — emotional safety and active behavioral output — are in place. Attempting shaping before Phase 1 and 2 are complete would fail because a frozen or avoidant dog produces no approximations to reinforce.
