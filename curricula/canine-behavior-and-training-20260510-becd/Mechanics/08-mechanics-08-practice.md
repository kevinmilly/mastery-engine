## Exercises

**Exercise 1**
A dog barks and lunges at skateboards. You begin a counter-conditioning protocol by presenting a skateboard at 50 feet and immediately feeding high-value treats. After several sessions, the dog no longer reacts at 50 feet, so you move to 40 feet. At 40 feet, the dog begins barking again. What should you do next, and why?

**Exercise 2**
You are working with a dog that is mildly uncomfortable around strangers. You design a desensitization plan where a stranger stands across the street while you feed the dog cheese. The dog eats eagerly and has loose body language. A week later, a stranger walks directly up and pets the dog without warning — the dog snaps. What went wrong in the protocol, and what does this tell you about the relationship between desensitization and real-world exposure?

**Exercise 3**
A trainer is counter-conditioning a dog's fear of thunderstorms by playing a recording of thunder at low volume while feeding chicken. After several weeks, the dog is calm during the recordings but still panics during real storms. Identify at least two reasons why the protocol may be failing to generalize, and explain what you would do differently.

**Exercise 4**
Two dogs both have a fear of nail trims. Dog A has a mild startle response when the clippers appear. Dog B freezes, pants, and tries to escape when the clippers are brought into the same room. A trainer applies the same starting protocol to both dogs: touching clippers to paw while feeding treats. Analyze why this protocol is likely appropriate for one dog but a mistake for the other, and describe how you would adjust for the problematic case.

**Exercise 5**
A client's dog has begun growling at children after being stepped on by a toddler six months ago. The owner has been scolding the dog every time it growls, hoping to suppress the behavior. You are now brought in to help. Drawing on counter-conditioning, desensitization, and your knowledge of extinction and the four quadrants, explain what problems the owner's approach has created and outline a corrected intervention plan.

**Exercise 6**
You are designing a structured training plan (as covered in prior topics) for a reactive dog that lunges at other dogs on leash. The owner has limited time — only two 10-minute sessions per day — and lives in a dense urban neighborhood where controlled exposure is difficult. Using the principles of counter-conditioning, desensitization, shaping, and generalization, describe how you would structure the first four weeks of the plan, including how you would handle the environmental constraints and how you would measure progress.

---

## Answer Key

**Answer 1**
Move back to a distance where the dog was last successful — likely 50 feet or slightly beyond — before attempting 40 feet again. The dog's barking at 40 feet signals that the threshold was crossed too quickly; the stimulus is still above the dog's tolerance at that distance. In counter-conditioning and desensitization, progress must follow the dog's actual emotional state, not a predetermined schedule. Continuing at 40 feet risks sensitization (making the fear worse) and undermines the positive emotional association being built. The correct move is to reduce intensity, re-establish calm, and decrease distance more gradually in future sessions.

**Answer 2**
The protocol was progressing correctly in controlled conditions, but the real-world exposure violated the foundational rule of desensitization: the stimulus must remain below the dog's threshold throughout training. A stranger approaching and touching the dog directly is a drastically higher-intensity version of the stimulus than "stranger across the street." The dog's snap is not a failure of the training — it is a predictable outcome of flooding-level exposure before the dog was ready. This illustrates that desensitization builds tolerance to a specific intensity level, not immunity to all versions of the stimulus. The owner (or anyone interacting with the dog) must be coached to manage the environment and prevent above-threshold exposures between sessions, or progress is undermined.

**Answer 3**
Two likely reasons the protocol is failing to generalize: First, recorded thunder lacks the full sensory profile of a real storm — barometric pressure changes, lightning flashes, static electricity, and smell are absent from recordings. The dog has been conditioned to tolerate a stimulus that is meaningfully different from the real one. Second, the intensity ceiling of the recording may be far below what a real storm produces, so the dog was never actually prepared for high-intensity versions of the sound. Adjustments: use a multi-sensory approach where possible (e.g., pairing sessions with low-pressure weather, dimming lights, using fans), gradually increase recording volume to near-real levels, and consider working during actual mild storms at a distance (e.g., inside a car, or indoors during a light rain) to expose the dog to the real stimulus at manageable intensity. Generalization of desensitization requires exposure across varied contexts, not just a single artificial one.

**Answer 4**
The protocol is likely appropriate for Dog A, whose mild startle suggests the clippers entering the room and touching the paw is still within or near threshold — food can compete with the mild discomfort and begin building a positive association. For Dog B, bringing clippers into the same room already triggers a stress response (freezing, panting, escape attempts), which means the clippers touching the paw is far above threshold. Presenting food at this intensity will likely fail because a dog in a fear state cannot eat or form positive associations effectively — the emotional response overrides the conditioning. The adjusted protocol for Dog B should begin at a much lower intensity: perhaps the clippers in a closed bag across the room, or even just the sound of the clipper case opening in another room. Only once the dog is fully relaxed at each stage should intensity increase. Shaping the dog's tolerance in tiny increments (as covered in the shaping topic) is essential here.

**Answer 5**
The owner's scolding represents positive punishment — adding an aversive to suppress growling. This creates two serious problems. First, growling is a warning signal; suppressing it does not change the dog's underlying emotional state (fear/negative association with children), it only removes the warning. This produces a dog that may bite without warning, which is more dangerous. Second, pairing children with an aversive (the scolding) can worsen the negative association through classical conditioning — the dog now associates children with both the original pain and the punishment. The corrected plan: (1) Stop all punishment immediately to avoid further negative conditioning. (2) Begin a counter-conditioning protocol: at a distance where the dog is aware of children but not reactive, pair their presence with high-value treats. The child's appearance predicts good things. (3) Desensitize gradually — increase proximity only as the dog shows consistent calm and relaxed body language. (4) Maintain the growl as a warning signal; instead of suppressing it, change the emotional state so the growl is no longer needed. (5) Manage the environment strictly to prevent above-threshold exposure while the protocol is underway.

**Answer 6**
The core challenge is that real-world exposure is hard to control and sessions are short, so the plan must prioritize threshold management and structured sub-threshold exposure. **Week 1:** Establish a baseline by identifying the dog's current threshold distance — the distance at which the dog notices another dog but does not lunge (may be 30–50+ feet in a dense area). Use the 10-minute sessions for counter-conditioning at this distance: dog sees another dog → high-value food appears, food stops when dog disappears. Use controlled setups (e.g., a parked car as a barrier, a friend's calm dog at a distance) rather than random street encounters. Teach the owner to read early stress signals (stiffening, hard stare) to interrupt before threshold is crossed. **Week 2:** Begin shaping a default behavior (e.g., look-at-me or U-turn) as an alternative response to dog sightings, using the shaping protocol from prior topics. Practice this behavior without other dogs present until fluent. **Week 3:** Integrate the cued behavior into counter-conditioning sessions — dog sees dog at distance, owner cues the look-at-me, dog gets jackpot. Gradually vary distance, direction of approach, and dog size/breed to begin generalization. **Week 4:** Increase environmental variability — different streets, times of day, types of dogs — while keeping intensity below threshold. Measure progress by tracking the dog's threshold distance (is it shrinking?), the dog's ability to eat treats near other dogs (eating = below threshold), and the fluency of the alternative behavior under distraction. Urban density is managed by using timing (early morning, less foot traffic), physical barriers, and pre-planned routes rather than waiting for spontaneous encounters.
