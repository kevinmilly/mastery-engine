## The Hook

After this lesson, you will be able to look at a dog's persistent unwanted behavior and identify exactly what is feeding it — and then deliberately cut off that fuel source in a way that reduces the behavior permanently rather than accidentally strengthening it.

Think of extinction like unplugging a vending machine. If a button used to reliably dispense a snack and you press it today and nothing comes out, your first instinct isn't to shrug and walk away — it's to press harder, faster, maybe try the button next to it. The machine looks broken. You haven't learned yet that the machine is off for good. That gap between "stopped working" and "I've accepted it won't work" is the entire challenge of extinction in dog training.

---

## Why It Matters

Most trainers eventually hit a wall that looks like this: they've read about ignoring bad behavior, they've tried it, and the behavior got *worse*. So they conclude that ignoring doesn't work, or that their dog is stubborn, or that this approach isn't right for their dog. They abandon the method at the exact moment it was starting to work.

This isn't a failure of the method — it's a failure to recognize a completely predictable phase called an **extinction burst**. Without understanding extinction as a mechanism, trainers misread the burst as evidence that ignoring reinforces the behavior, which is the opposite of what's happening. They end up accidentally making the behavior more persistent through intermittent reinforcement, which (as you'll see) is the most powerful way to cement a habit.

Understanding extinction is what lets you hold the line when holding is hardest.

## The Ladder

**Start with the basic question: why does a behavior exist at all?**

From your work with operant conditioning, you know that behaviors that produce consequences tend to repeat. A dog jumps on you, you make eye contact and push them down — to the dog, that's attention, which is a consequence they wanted. The jumping was reinforced. It will happen again.

This is the foundation of extinction: **behaviors exist because something is maintaining them.** Remove that something, and the behavior loses its reason to occur. Over time, it fades.

**What extinction actually means**

Extinction is not punishment. It doesn't add anything aversive. It doesn't remove something the dog has. It simply stops delivering the reinforcer that the behavior has been producing.

More precisely: **extinction is the process of reducing the frequency of a behavior by withholding the reinforcement that has been maintaining it.**

If a dog barks at you until you look up from your phone, attention is the reinforcer. Extinction means: no eye contact, no talking, no getting up, no sighing loudly — nothing that could register as a payoff. If the dog has been getting even *occasional* looks, those occasional looks have been maintaining the behavior. Extinction requires complete and consistent removal.

**The mechanism underneath**

Here's what's happening neurologically, building on what you learned about classical conditioning: the dog's brain has linked the behavior to an outcome. Barking → attention. That link was built through repetition. Extinction works by repeatedly running the behavior without the outcome, which gradually weakens the association until the brain no longer predicts that the behavior leads to anything.

This takes time because the brain doesn't conclude "this never works" after one failure. It concludes "this worked before — maybe try again." Which brings us to the most important concept in this lesson.

**The extinction burst**

When a previously reinforced behavior stops producing results, behavior doesn't decrease smoothly from the start. Instead, it *escalates* first. The dog tries harder: louder barking, jumping higher, nudging more insistently. This is the **extinction burst** — a temporary and predictable increase in the behavior before it begins to decline.

The extinction burst isn't random. It's the behavioral equivalent of pressing the vending machine button harder. The dog is doing what worked before, with more intensity, because the consequence has become unpredictable. This is actually evidence that extinction is working — the behavior is being tested before it's abandoned.

The critical implication: **if you give in during the burst, you have just reinforced the most extreme version of the behavior.** You have taught the dog that the key to getting what they want is to escalate until you cave. The behavior will return stronger, louder, and more persistent than before. This is how mild nuisances become serious problems.

**What extinction can and cannot address**

Extinction works when the reinforcer maintaining the behavior is something you control and can withhold. Attention-seeking barking, demand pawing, whining at the dinner table — these are good candidates because the payoff (your response) is in your hands.

But some behaviors are **self-reinforcing**: chasing squirrels, chewing on something satisfying, digging in cool dirt. The reinforcer is built into the act itself — the dog doesn't need you to deliver it. You cannot run extinction on self-reinforcing behaviors without removing access to the situation entirely, because you can't withhold a consequence you don't control.

**The role of reinforcement schedule in how hard extinction is**

You've learned that reinforcement schedules matter. Here's why they matter for extinction specifically: behaviors that were reinforced on a **variable ratio schedule** — rewarded unpredictably, sometimes after one try, sometimes after ten — are dramatically more resistant to extinction than behaviors that were reinforced every single time.

This is why the "sometimes ignoring, sometimes giving in" pattern is so damaging. You haven't been ignoring the behavior — you've been putting it on a variable ratio schedule. The dog's brain has learned: *keep going, because you never know when the payoff will come.* That's the schedule that produces the most persistent behavior and the slowest extinction.

## Worked Reality

Consider Margot, who has a two-year-old Labrador named Scout. Scout learned early that barking at the back door would get someone to let him out. That was the intended behavior — fine.

But Scout generalized. Now he barks at Margot whenever he wants anything: dinner earlier than scheduled, a toy from the shelf, her attention when she's on a call. The barking is persistent and sharp.

Margot decides to implement extinction for the demand barking. She stops responding to barks that aren't for outside access. No eye contact. No "no." No moving toward the shelf. She turns slightly away and waits for silence.

For two days, Scout barks more. He adds in pawing. He tries barking from different positions in the room. It's noticeably worse than before Margot started. She's frustrated and doubting herself.

What's happening: this is the extinction burst. Scout's behavior escalated because barking no longer reliably produced results, and his brain is running through all the variations that ever worked before. Margot is witnessing the burst — which means the process is working.

On day three, Margot makes a mistake. Scout has been barking for four minutes and her phone call is suffering. She looks at him and says "not now." Scout stops barking immediately.

She just did two things: she reinforced four minutes of escalating barking with attention, and she accidentally taught Scout that four minutes is the threshold. The next session, Scout goes to five minutes without much prompting — because last time, longer worked.

If Margot had held through the burst until a pause in barking — even a two-second one — and then calmly acknowledged Scout at that moment, she would have reinforced silence and moved the process forward. Extinction requires not just withholding reinforcement during the behavior, but catching and reinforcing the *absence* of the behavior to give the dog something to move toward.

## Friction Point

**The wrong mental model:** "If the behavior is getting worse, my approach is making it worse."

This is the most tempting mistake in extinction work. When the burst hits and behavior escalates, it *looks* like your intervention is backfiring. The natural conclusion is that something has gone wrong and you should change strategies.

But this model confuses a symptom of the process with evidence that the process is failing. The burst is not caused by ignoring — it's caused by the *history of reinforcement* the dog already has. You're watching that history get tested. The burst would occur regardless of what you do from this point forward; the question is only whether you outlast it or reinforce the peak of it.

**The correct model:** an extinction burst is a predictable, time-limited feature of extinction, not feedback that your approach needs to change. The only thing that should change your approach is evidence that you've misidentified the reinforcer — for example, if the behavior is self-reinforcing and ignoring genuinely has no effect. If the behavior is escalating dramatically and specifically when you withhold your response, that's confirmation you've identified the right reinforcer and extinction is active.

The test: is the behavior escalating *toward you specifically when you're not responding*? That's a burst. Is it escalating randomly without reference to your presence or attention? Then you may be working on the wrong variable.
