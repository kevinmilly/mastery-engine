## The Hook

After this lesson, you will be able to design a training plan for any behavior your dog has never offered — not by waiting for luck or forcing the dog into position, but by systematically building toward it one step at a time.

Think of shaping like navigating with a GPS that can only say "warmer" or "colder." You don't hand the destination to the traveler — you give real-time feedback as they move through the terrain, rewarding each step that gets them closer until they arrive somewhere they've never been before.

---

## Why It Matters

Positive reinforcement works cleanly when a dog is already doing something you want to capture and repeat. But most behaviors worth teaching — a reliable recall, a dog that stays calm on a vet table, a retrieval to hand — aren't things a dog spontaneously does. They're behaviors the dog has never offered, or has only offered in fragments.

Without shaping, trainers hit a specific wall: they wait for the "full behavior" before rewarding, and the dog never produces it, so nothing gets reinforced and nothing improves. Or they physically force the dog into position (luring, pushing, molding) and then find the dog only performs when the food or hand is present — because the dog learned to follow a prompt, not to perform a behavior.

This is the competence friction you'll hit without this lesson: a dog that seems to understand something in one context, then completely falls apart when conditions change slightly. Shaping builds behaviors that belong to the dog, not to the lure.

## The Ladder

**Start with what reinforcement actually requires**

You already know from previous work that positive reinforcement increases behavior by adding something the dog wants. The timing window is tight — about one to two seconds — and the dog must want the reward for it to work.

Now here's the implication that shaping exploits: *you don't have to wait for the finished behavior before reinforcing*. You can reinforce any behavior that moves in the right direction. And by moving the target in small steps, you can guide a dog through a sequence of approximations until you arrive at something complex.

**What "successive approximations" means**

An approximation is any behavior that resembles — even loosely — the behavior you ultimately want. A *successive* approximation is one that's slightly closer to the goal than the last one you rewarded.

Say you want your dog to rest her chin on a mat. On day one, she's never done this. But she might glance toward the mat. That's an approximation — barely, but it's movement in the right direction. You click and treat. Now she's orienting toward the mat more. You wait for her to take a step toward it. Click, treat. Step by step — approach the mat, sniff the mat, lower her head near the mat, touch the mat with her chin, hold it for a second — you're building.

This process of reinforcing successive approximations until the full behavior is achieved is called **shaping**.

**The mechanism underneath**

What makes this work isn't magic — it's the same operant conditioning you've already seen. The dog is doing things, consequences follow, and the frequency of behaviors shifts. What shaping adds is *deliberate criteria management*: you, the trainer, decide in advance what counts as "close enough to reinforce" at each stage, and you systematically raise the bar.

The key tool that makes shaping precise is the **marker** — usually a clicker or a short word like "yes." The marker bridges the timing gap between the behavior and the reward. When the dog's chin touches the mat, you click immediately. That click tells the dog: "That exact thing you just did is what earned this." Without a marker, you're always a step behind, and the dog's mental model of what earned the treat becomes fuzzy.

**What "raising criteria" means in practice**

Criteria are the rules you're currently using to decide whether to click. "Any movement toward the mat" is a criterion. So is "chin must touch mat for one second." You start with wide, easy criteria. As the dog consistently meets them, you narrow or increase them.

The core discipline of shaping is this: only raise criteria one dimension at a time. Duration, distance, distraction, precision — these can all be criteria, but you don't raise two of them simultaneously. If you've just extended the chin hold to three seconds, don't also start asking for it across the room. You'll lose the behavior.

There's also a practical rule experienced trainers use: if the dog fails three times in a row, your criteria are too high. Drop back to the last level where the dog was succeeding, and build more gradually.

**The trainer's role**

In shaping, the trainer does not lure, push, or place the dog. The dog offers behavior; the trainer evaluates it against current criteria and either marks and rewards or waits. This makes the dog an active problem-solver rather than a prompt-follower. Dogs trained this way tend to show what trainers call "offered behavior" — they try things, they think, they engage. Dogs that have only been lured often go still and wait when the lure disappears.

## Worked Reality

A trainer wants to teach her border collie, Mack, to retrieve a dumbbell — specifically to pick it up and carry it to her hand. Mack has never done this. She starts shaping.

**Session 1 — Contact criterion:** She places the dumbbell on the floor. Her criteria: any nose contact with the dumbbell earns a click and treat. Mack sniffs it almost immediately. Click, treat. Sniffs again. Click, treat. Within five minutes, Mack is deliberately bumping the dumbbell with his nose because that's what's been paying off.

**Session 2 — Mouth criterion:** She raises criteria. Nose contact no longer earns a click. Now she waits for Mack to mouth the dumbbell — any tooth contact. Mack tries the nose, nothing happens. He escalates, mouths the end of the dumbbell. Click, treat. Mack figures it out quickly.

**Session 3 — Duration criterion:** Now she wants Mack to hold it, not just touch it. She starts clicking for holds of one second, then two, then five.

**Session 4 — Carry criterion:** She introduces movement. She clicks when Mack picks it up and she takes a step back — if Mack follows with the dumbbell, click and treat. She's now building a carry.

Notice what didn't happen: she never put the dumbbell in Mack's mouth. She never lured him with food near the object. At each stage, Mack chose the behavior because it was the one paying off. When the dumbbell criterion appeared, Mack was already engaged and problem-solving rather than waiting for a cue. The final retrieve belongs to him.

Also notice her discipline: when Mack started dropping the dumbbell too quickly in session 3, she dropped back to two-second holds for several repetitions before trying five again. She didn't push through the failure.

## Friction Point

**The wrong mental model:** Shaping means waiting patiently for the final behavior and then rewarding it.

This is tempting because "reinforcing what you want" sounds like it means waiting for the exact thing you want before clicking. Trainers who think this way end up standing in silence while their dog wanders, sniffs the ground, and loses interest — and nothing gets trained.

**Why it's wrong:** Shaping is not passive observation. It's active management of criteria. You're not waiting for the destination; you're rewarding each step of the journey. The word "successive" in "successive approximations" is the key: each approximation must be slightly better than the last, and you must be actively deciding what "better" means right now, in this session, for this dog.

The correct mental model: before each session, you know your current criterion (what counts as close enough to click today). You observe the dog's behavior against that criterion — not against the final goal. When the dog consistently meets the current criterion, you raise it. You are always working at the edge of the dog's capability, not waiting for a leap to the finish line.

The confusion often comes from the word "shaping" itself, which sounds like something you do to clay — passive molding. It's better understood as a feedback loop you're running in real time, where you're the one moving the target.
