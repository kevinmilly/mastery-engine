## Exercises

**Exercise 1**
You are planning a session to teach a dog to sit on cue. List three specific, measurable goals you would set for a single 10-minute session, and explain why each goal is appropriately scoped for one session rather than multiple sessions.

**Exercise 2**
A trainer wants to teach "place" (go to a mat and stay). Break this behavior into at least four discrete sub-steps suitable for sequential training, ordering them from foundational to final. For each step, state what the dog must successfully do before moving to the next.

**Exercise 3**
A dog has learned to sit reliably in the kitchen but fails the same cue in the backyard, at the park, and at a friend's house. Identify two environmental variables that likely explain this breakdown, and describe how you would restructure the training plan to address them systematically.

**Exercise 4**
A trainer tracks session data and notices the dog's success rate on "down" has plateaued at 60% for three consecutive sessions. Propose two distinct plan adjustments the trainer should consider, explaining the reasoning behind each based on what a plateau at that accuracy level likely indicates.

**Exercise 5**
You are designing a training plan for a rescue dog that has a history of leash reactivity. The owner wants the dog to walk calmly past other dogs within six weeks. Using concepts from structured plan design, classical conditioning, and shaping, outline the key phases of your plan. Explain how you would sequence goals across the six weeks and what data you would collect to make go/no-go decisions between phases.

**Exercise 6**
A trainer working with an 8-month-old Labrador has successfully shaped a reliable "recall" in low-distraction environments using positive reinforcement. During the proofing phase, they introduce high-distraction environments too quickly and the behavior collapses. The owner, frustrated, begins calling the dog multiple times when it doesn't respond. Analyze what has gone wrong at the plan-design level and at the execution level, and redesign the proofing progression to prevent both failure modes. Include how prior learning about extinction and generalization should inform your redesign.

## Answer Key

**Answer 1**
Three appropriately scoped goals might be:
1. *The dog sits within 3 seconds of the verbal cue, 8 out of 10 trials, with the handler standing directly in front.* — This is measurable (timing, success rate, handler position) and realistic for a single session with a dog already learning the behavior.
2. *The handler delivers a reinforcer within 1 second of the dog's sit, consistently across all trials.* — This targets handler mechanics, which can be meaningfully improved in one session and directly affects learning speed.
3. *The dog remains in the sit position for 2 seconds before the reinforcer is delivered, for 7 out of 10 reps.* — Duration is a discreet variable that can be incrementally built; 2 seconds is a small, achievable target for a single session.

These are appropriately scoped because each isolates one variable (rate of response, handler timing, or duration), making success measurable and failure diagnosable. Goals that combine duration + distance + distraction would be too broad for a single session.

**Answer 2**
Sub-steps for "place":
1. *Dog approaches and sniffs the mat* — reinforced simply for orienting toward the mat; this builds mat value before any behavior is required.
2. *Dog places two or more paws on the mat* — criterion increases to physical contact; handler reinforces any mat engagement.
3. *Dog stands or sits with all four paws on the mat* — full body on mat is now required; the dog must demonstrate voluntary mat-seeking.
4. *Dog lies down on the mat and holds for a count of 3 seconds* — a specific position (down) and minimal duration are added; this is the finished behavior in prototype form.
5. *Dog performs the above from increasing distances from the mat (send)* — the cue transitions from the handler pointing to a verbal/gestural send.

Each step functions as a prerequisite because the dog must have a reinforcement history with the mat before offering deliberate behavior on it, and it must hold position before distance is introduced.

**Answer 3**
Two environmental variables:
1. *Distraction level* — The kitchen is a familiar, low-stimulation environment. The backyard, park, and friend's house introduce novel smells, sounds, animals, people, and movement. The dog's sit behavior was never trained or reinforced in the presence of these stimuli, so the cue has not generalized to them.
2. *Handler's own behavior/context* — Handlers often stand differently, use different body language, or feel less composed outdoors. The dog may have learned to respond to subtle postural cues that are absent or altered in novel locations.

Restructured plan: Apply systematic generalization by introducing one new environment at a time, starting with the least distracting (e.g., quiet backyard at a low-traffic time), returning to a high rate of reinforcement, and gradually raising criteria. This mirrors the proofing process: change only one variable per session (location, distraction type, distance from handler) before combining variables. Track success rate per location and only advance when the dog hits the target criterion (e.g., 80% over two sessions) in the current environment.

**Answer 4**
A 60% plateau across three sessions suggests the current criterion is slightly beyond the dog's reliable performance but not so far that the dog is failing completely. Two adjustments:

1. *Reduce the criterion and rebuild* — If the dog was asked for "down" at a specific duration or from a standing position, temporarily reduce the difficulty (e.g., reward faster, from a sit, or with a shorter hold). Rebuilding success rate above 80% before re-raising criteria prevents rehearsal of failure and re-establishes a strong reinforcement history.

2. *Examine the training environment and reinforcer value* — A plateau can result from handler inconsistency (variable cue timing, late marking) or a drop in reinforcer motivation (satiation, boring treat). Switching to a higher-value reinforcer or analyzing video of the handler's mechanics may reveal an error in delivery rather than a problem with the dog's understanding.

Choosing between them requires data: if accuracy was higher in earlier sessions, look for a difficulty jump; if it was always around 60%, look at mechanics and motivation.

**Answer 5**
Key phases across six weeks:

**Phase 1 (Weeks 1–2): Threshold identification and classical conditioning.** Establish the dog's current threshold distance from other dogs where no reactivity occurs. At or below threshold, pair the sight of a dog with high-value food (classical conditioning: other dog → good things). Goal: dog orients to handler when another dog appears at threshold distance. Data to collect: threshold distance in feet, latency of reorientation to handler, body language indicators (ears, tail, muscle tension).

**Phase 2 (Weeks 3–4): Shaping calm behavior under mild exposure.** Begin reinforcing loose-leash walking at threshold distance. Gradually decrease distance to other dogs across sessions as the dog demonstrates consistent calm reorientation. Use a shaping plan with defined criteria increments (e.g., reduce distance by 5 feet only after 80% calm trials at current distance). Data: distance to trigger, step count of calm walking, any reactive episodes.

**Phase 3 (Weeks 5–6): Generalization and real-world proofing.** Introduce varied environments, handler positions, and dog sizes/breeds. Maintain high reinforcement rate during new contexts. Data: environment type, dog size/energy level, success rate.

Go/no-go decisions: advance to the next phase only if the dog achieves the target success rate (80%+ calm trials) in two consecutive sessions at the current level. A reactive episode resets to the previous distance/criterion for that session.

**Answer 6**
**What went wrong:**

*Plan-design level:* The proofing progression skipped intermediate distraction steps. A structured proofing plan should raise only one variable at a time (distraction intensity, environment novelty, distance from dog, handler's position). Jumping to high distraction is equivalent to failing to break the task into manageable sub-steps — the dog was never given the opportunity to succeed at intermediate difficulty, so generalization never occurred.

*Execution level:* The owner's repeated cueing after failure is a critical error with two consequences. First, it puts the recall cue on an extinction schedule in high-distraction contexts (the cue is presented but rarely reinforced because the dog rarely complies), which degrades the cue's value. Second, the dog learns that the cue can be ignored — repeated non-responses go without consequence, and the behavior weakens. This is exactly how extinction works: the previously reinforced response is no longer reliably followed by reinforcement, so it decreases.

**Redesigned proofing progression:**

1. Return to the last environment where recall was reliable (low-distraction). Re-establish a 90%+ success rate with high-value reinforcers.
2. Introduce mild distraction (handler drops a toy nearby, mild outdoor sounds) while maintaining high reinforcement rate.
3. Introduce a second person at a distance. Reinforce every successful recall.
4. Practice near low-energy dogs at a distance where the dog can still respond. Reinforce heavily.
5. Gradually reduce distance to dogs across sessions, using the 80% criterion as a gate.

Owner instruction must include: call the dog only once. If no response, move closer, reduce distraction, or end the session. Never repeat the cue — doing so teaches that the first cue is optional. This directly applies extinction principles: protect the cue's reinforcement history by only using it when the dog is likely to succeed, so the cue retains its conditioned value.
