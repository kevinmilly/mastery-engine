## Exercises

**Exercise 1**
You are teaching a dog to sit. You give the treat two seconds after the dog's rear touches the ground. Explain what problem this creates and what you should do instead.

**Exercise 2**
A trainer is teaching a recall command using hot dogs as the reward. The dog performs perfectly in the kitchen but ignores the command at the dog park. What is the most likely cause of this failure, and what specific adjustment to the reward should the trainer consider?

**Exercise 3**
A dog has learned to shake paws reliably and gets a treat every single time. Over the following weeks, the trainer notices the dog becomes disengaged and slow to respond unless the treat bag is visible. Identify what reinforcement schedule is being used and explain why it is producing this outcome. What schedule would create a more durable behavior?

**Exercise 4**
A trainer is using a piece of cheese to lure a dog into a "down" position. After two weeks, the dog only performs the down when the trainer's hand moves toward the floor with food in it. What training error occurred, and describe the step-by-step process the trainer should use to fix it.

**Exercise 5**
A dog has a strong fear response to strangers (barking and backing away). A new trainer decides to use positive reinforcement — giving the dog treats whenever a stranger is present — to fix this. A colleague argues this approach is misapplied here and that a different framework is more appropriate. Drawing on both classical conditioning (from prior topics) and your understanding of positive reinforcement, evaluate both positions. Which framework better addresses the root problem, and why?

**Exercise 6**
You are working with a rescue dog that has learned to jump on guests. You want to use positive reinforcement to teach "four paws on the floor" as an incompatible behavior. Design a complete training progression: starting reward schedule, how you would fade the lure if used, how you would transition the reinforcement schedule for long-term reliability, and one mistake a novice trainer would likely make at each stage.

---

## Answer Key

**Answer 1**
The two-second delay breaks the association between the behavior and the reward. Dogs learn through immediate feedback; a two-second gap means the dog is more likely to associate the treat with whatever it was doing at the moment of delivery — perhaps shifting weight, sniffing, or glancing away — rather than the sit itself. The trainer should mark the exact moment the rear touches the ground (with a clicker or verbal marker like "yes") and then deliver the treat. The marker bridges the gap between behavior and reward, preserving precise timing even if the physical treat takes a moment to reach the dog.

**Answer 2**
The dog park is a high-distraction environment with competing stimuli (other dogs, smells, movement) that have higher intrinsic value to the dog than the kitchen setting. The reward value must compete with the environment. Hot dogs are moderately high-value, but the trainer should test whether the dog will take food at all in that environment — if not, the dog is over threshold and training location needs to be adjusted first. If the dog will take food, the trainer should use a higher-value reward (e.g., real meat, cheese) specifically for recall in challenging contexts, reserving lower-value treats for easy settings. Reward value must scale with environmental difficulty.

**Answer 3**
The trainer is using a continuous reinforcement (CRF) schedule — every correct response is rewarded. CRF is optimal for initial learning but produces low resistance to extinction: the dog learns that no treat likely means no point in responding, and the treat bag becomes a discriminative stimulus (a cue that reinforcement is available). Switching to a variable ratio (VR) schedule — rewarding after an unpredictable number of correct responses — would produce higher response rates and greater resistance to extinction, because the dog cannot predict when a reward is coming and therefore keeps responding. VR schedules mirror the psychology of slot machines: the unpredictability maintains engagement.

**Answer 4**
The trainer failed to fade the lure, so the food in the hand became part of the cue itself rather than a consequence of the behavior. The dog learned "move toward hand with food" not "respond to the down signal." To fix it: (1) Put the food in the other hand or pocket. Use an empty hand to perform the same lure motion — when the dog downs, immediately reward from the other hand. (2) Gradually reduce the hand motion over several sessions until only a subtle finger point or verbal cue is needed. (3) Practice with the treat completely out of sight before the cue is given. The principle is that lures must be faded early — before the dog has had many repetitions with food present — to prevent the lure from becoming the cue.

**Answer 5**
The colleague is correct that positive reinforcement alone is not the primary tool here. The dog's barking and retreat are fear responses — emotional reactions rooted in a conditioned association between strangers and threat. Positive reinforcement addresses voluntary operant behavior, but fear is a reflexive, classically conditioned emotional state. Flooding the dog with treats in the presence of strangers may suppress the visible behavior without changing the underlying emotional response, or may not work at all if the dog is too aroused to eat.

Classical counter-conditioning is the more appropriate framework: pair the presence of a stranger (conditioned stimulus) with something highly positive (food, play) at a distance where the dog is below threshold, gradually decreasing distance. This changes the emotional association — strangers predict good things — rather than just reinforcing a behavior. Once the emotional state shifts, positive reinforcement can then build specific calm behaviors. The two frameworks are complementary but are not interchangeable: classical conditioning targets emotion; operant conditioning targets voluntary action.

**Answer 6**
**Starting reward schedule:** Use continuous reinforcement (CRF) initially. Every time the dog keeps four paws on the floor when a guest enters, mark and reward immediately. This builds the association quickly.

**Fading the lure:** If a treat is used to lure the dog into a standing position rather than jumping, fade it within 3–5 sessions: first hold the lure but deliver the reward from the other hand, then use an empty hand with the same gesture, then reduce the gesture to a natural posture. The dog should be responding to a cue, not chasing food.

**Transitioning the reinforcement schedule:** Once the dog is performing reliably on CRF, shift to a variable ratio schedule — reward after 1, then 3, then 2, then 4 correct responses unpredictably. This builds durability. Eventually, social praise and occasional jackpots can maintain the behavior without constant food rewards.

**Novice mistakes at each stage:**
- *Early stage:* Waiting too long to reward, allowing the dog to jump and then rewarding four paws — inadvertently rewarding the wrong behavior sequence.
- *Lure fading:* Keeping the lure too long, so the dog learns "don't jump when food is visible" rather than a generalizable rule.
- *Schedule transition:* Dropping to variable ratio too quickly before the behavior is fluent, causing the dog to stop responding reliably before the habit is established.
