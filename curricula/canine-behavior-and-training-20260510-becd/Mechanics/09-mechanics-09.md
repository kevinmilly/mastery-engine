## The Hook

After this lesson, you'll be able to look at a trained dog's behavior and predict whether it will hold up under pressure or collapse the moment you stop rewarding it — and you'll know exactly which reinforcement pattern is responsible for the difference.

Think of intermittent reinforcement like a slot machine versus a vending machine. The vending machine gives you a reward every time you press the button. Reliable, predictable — and the moment it stops working, you walk away after one or two tries. The slot machine pays out unpredictably. Sometimes you win on the third pull, sometimes the thirtieth. That unpredictability doesn't make you quit — it keeps you playing. The same mechanism operates in your dog's nervous system, and understanding it determines whether the behaviors you've trained will last.

---

## Why It Matters

Most trainers know that positive reinforcement builds behavior. Fewer understand that *how you deliver that reinforcement over time* determines whether the behavior becomes robust or fragile.

Here's the wall trainers hit: you've built a reliable sit using consistent food rewards. The dog performs it beautifully in every session. Then you start fading the treats — rewarding every third sit, then every fifth — and the behavior degrades. The dog starts sitting slowly, reluctantly, or stops offering it at all. You conclude the dog is "being stubborn" or "only works for food," and you're stuck.

What actually happened is that you moved from one reinforcement schedule to another without understanding the effect each has on behavior. Intermittent reinforcement schedules aren't just a trick for weaning dogs off treats — they're the architecture that determines a behavior's long-term strength and persistence. Skipping this understanding means you'll accidentally train fragile behaviors and misread their collapse as a character flaw in your dog.

## The Ladder

### Start with continuous reinforcement

When you first teach a new behavior, you reward every single correct response. This is called **continuous reinforcement (CRF)**. It's the fastest way to build a new association — the dog gets clear, consistent feedback that a specific action produces a specific outcome. You learned about this directly in operant conditioning: the consequence follows the behavior reliably, and the behavior strengthens.

CRF is ideal for *acquisition* — the phase where the dog is learning what the behavior is. But it creates a fragile behavior. If the reinforcer disappears entirely, the dog's nervous system detects the change almost immediately. From prior context: this is exactly what drives extinction. A behavior maintained only on CRF extinguishes quickly once rewards stop, because the contrast between "always rewarded" and "suddenly never rewarded" is sharp.

### What intermittent reinforcement is

**Intermittent reinforcement** means rewarding the behavior on some occasions but not others — not every correct response earns a reward. This might sound like a worse deal for the dog, but it produces something important: **resistance to extinction**. The behavior persists longer without rewards because the dog's experience has included unrewarded trials before, so the absence of a reward doesn't immediately signal "this behavior no longer works."

Intermittent schedules come in four main types, organized along two axes:

- **Ratio vs. Interval**: Is the reward delivered based on *how many responses* the dog makes, or *how much time has passed*?
- **Fixed vs. Variable**: Is the requirement *predictable* (always the same number or interval) or *unpredictable* (changes each time)?

### Fixed Ratio (FR)

A **fixed ratio schedule** delivers a reward after a set number of responses. FR5 means: reward every fifth correct sit. The requirement never changes.

The behavioral signature of FR schedules is a *post-reinforcement pause* — the dog performs well as the reward approaches, gets the reward, then briefly slows or stops before ramping up again. You can think of it like a factory worker who knows they get a break every 50 parts assembled: there's often a pause right after the break before production picks back up.

FR schedules produce high, consistent response rates, but the behavior is moderately predictable in its weaknesses: if you stop delivering the reward at the expected interval, the dog notices relatively quickly.

### Variable Ratio (VR)

A **variable ratio schedule** delivers a reward after an unpredictable number of responses — sometimes after 2, sometimes after 8, sometimes after 15 — but averaging out to some number over time. VR5 means the reward comes after an average of 5 responses, but the exact timing is never predictable.

This is the slot machine. Variable ratio schedules produce the **highest and most persistent response rates** of any schedule. Because the dog can never predict when the next reward is coming, there is no clear signal that rewards have stopped. Each unrewarded trial might be followed by the next rewarded one. The dog keeps responding.

This is why variable ratio is the target schedule for most trained behaviors once they're well-established. It produces behavior that is fast, frequent, and remarkably resistant to extinction.

### Fixed Interval (FI)

A **fixed interval schedule** delivers a reward for the first correct response after a set amount of time has elapsed — say, 30 seconds. The dog doesn't need to respond 10 times; it needs to respond once, but only after the clock has run.

The behavioral signature here is a *scallop pattern*: slow responding right after a reward (when the next interval has just started and there's no point hurrying), then accelerating as the interval end approaches. Fixed interval schedules produce lower overall response rates and moderate resistance to extinction.

In practical dog training, pure FI schedules are rare, but interval-based thinking appears in duration work — holding a stay, maintaining focus — where the reward arrives after time has passed, not after a set number of responses.

### Variable Interval (VI)

A **variable interval schedule** delivers a reward for the first correct response after an unpredictable amount of time — sometimes 10 seconds, sometimes 45 seconds, sometimes 2 minutes. The dog can't predict the timer.

Variable interval schedules produce **steady, moderate response rates** and **very high resistance to extinction** — second only to variable ratio. Because the dog never knows when the interval will end, unrewarded responses don't signal failure. The behavior persists.

VI schedules are particularly useful for behaviors that need to be sustained over time in unpredictable environments: a dog maintaining a loose leash walk, holding a relaxed position near a distraction, or checking in with the handler during off-leash exercise.

### The resistance hierarchy

Putting it together: not all intermittent schedules create equal durability.

| Schedule | Response Rate | Extinction Resistance |
|---|---|---|
| Continuous (CRF) | High initially | Very low |
| Fixed Ratio | High | Moderate |
| Fixed Interval | Moderate (scallop) | Moderate |
| Variable Interval | Steady/moderate | High |
| Variable Ratio | Highest | Highest |

The move from CRF to variable schedules is not just about using fewer treats — it's about deliberately shaping the dog's experience so that unrewarded trials become a normal part of what the behavior "feels like," making the behavior immune to the kind of extinction described in prior lessons.

## Worked Reality

A handler has spent three weeks teaching a solid recall — the dog comes running when called, rewarded with high-value treats every single time. It works reliably in low-distraction settings.

Now the handler starts taking the dog to a park. The dog comes when called a few times, gets treats each time (still on CRF). Then the handler decides to "test" the behavior by calling the dog and withholding the treat to see if it works without food. The dog comes, gets nothing, gets called again, gets nothing again. By the fourth or fifth call, the dog is slowing down, veering off, or ignoring the cue entirely.

What went wrong is not that the dog "knows" treats are absent. It's that the handler inadvertently shifted to extinction from CRF — a sharp, immediate contrast. The dog's history contains no experience of coming when called and not receiving a reward, so the absence of reward reads as strong information: *this behavior no longer works*.

Compare this to a handler who transitioned the recall onto a variable ratio schedule over several weeks. Some recalls earned jackpots. Some earned a single treat. Some earned only praise. A few earned nothing — the dog got called, ran over, received enthusiastic praise and a game of tug, then the session continued. The reward type and frequency varied unpredictably.

When this handler takes the dog to the park and the dog comes when called without receiving food, the dog's nervous system has no way to distinguish this from the many other times a recall was followed by something other than food, or by a brief delay. The behavior persists because unrewarded trials are not new information — they're already part of the dog's history with that behavior.

The difference between these two dogs is not talent or drive. It's the reinforcement history behind the behavior.

## Friction Point

**The wrong mental model:** Intermittent reinforcement is just a strategy for using fewer treats. The goal is to gradually reduce how often you reward until eventually you barely reward at all.

This is tempting because it sounds like a reasonable progression: reward a lot at first, then taper off as the behavior becomes more reliable. And there's truth embedded in it — you do reduce reward frequency over time. But the reasoning is wrong, and the wrong reasoning leads to the wrong execution.

**Why it breaks:** If you think of intermittent reinforcement as "rewarding less," you'll reduce rewards in a regular, predictable pattern — every third time, then every fifth time, then every tenth time. This keeps the schedule on a fixed ratio, which produces only moderate extinction resistance. When you eventually stop rewarding consistently, the behavior degrades because the dog's experience still contains a clear pattern that has now broken.

**The correct mental model:** Intermittent reinforcement works because it makes the *absence of a reward uninformative*. The goal is not to reward less — it's to reward *unpredictably*, so that any given unrewarded trial is indistinguishable from the trials that came before a reward. The dog can't detect that rewards have stopped, because the gap between rewards has always varied.

The practical implication: it matters not just *how often* you reward, but *which pattern* you use. Randomly varying the schedule — sometimes rewarding the second response, sometimes the seventh, sometimes the first — is categorically different from predictably thinning the schedule. One builds resistance. The other builds a behavior waiting to collapse.

## Mastery Question

You are working with a service dog program. Two dogs have been trained to perform alert behaviors (notifying their handler of a medical event). Dog A was trained to alertness on continuous reinforcement throughout — every correct alert in training earned an immediate food reward. Dog B was trained identically in the acquisition phase but then transitioned to a variable ratio schedule across six months of advanced training.

Both dogs have passed their program assessments. Both perform the behavior reliably in controlled testing.

Six months into field placement, the program receives reports that Dog A's alert rate has begun declining — alerts are slower, sometimes absent, and the handler has started rewarding them more frequently to compensate. Dog B's alert rate remains consistent.

Using what you know about reinforcement schedules, explain specifically why Dog A's behavior is declining and Dog B's is not. Then identify what the handler's compensation strategy (rewarding more frequently) is likely doing to Dog A's reinforcement history going forward, and whether this is solving or compounding the problem.
