## Exercises

**Exercise 1**
A trainer is teaching a dog to sit. During initial learning, she rewards every correct sit. Once the behavior is reliable, she switches to rewarding every third sit. A few weeks later, she notices the dog sits quickly and repeatedly, almost frantically, especially right after an unrewarded sit. Which reinforcement schedule is now in effect, and what specific feature of that schedule best explains the dog's behavior pattern?

**Exercise 2**
Two dogs have each learned to press a lever. Dog A was trained on a variable ratio schedule; Dog B on a fixed interval schedule. The trainer stops all reinforcement for both dogs. Predict which dog's lever-pressing behavior will extinguish faster, and explain the mechanism behind the difference.

**Exercise 3**
A search-and-rescue trainer notices that her dog works intensely and persistently during actual searches — which yield finds only unpredictably — but loses motivation quickly during repetitive drills where finds are predictably available after every 10 minutes of searching. Identify which schedule each situation approximates and explain why the dog's motivational response differs between them.

**Exercise 4**
A pet owner successfully taught her dog to come when called using continuous reinforcement. She wants to maintain the behavior long-term without always having treats available. She decides to switch abruptly to rewarding only every tenth recall. Shortly after, the dog's recall reliability drops sharply. Describe what went wrong and outline a more effective transition strategy, referencing relevant schedule properties.

**Exercise 5**
A dog has developed a strong fear response to strangers and is undergoing counter-conditioning and desensitization. The trainer also wants to build a reliable "look at me" cue to use as an incompatible behavior in the presence of strangers. She asks: should she put "look at me" on an intermittent schedule before using it in the fear context, and if so, which type? Construct a reasoned recommendation that integrates what you know about both reinforcement schedules and counter-conditioning principles.

**Exercise 6**
A trainer is proofing a competition-level heel behavior across multiple environments. The dog heels perfectly in the training hall but breaks position inconsistently in novel locations. The trainer considers two options: (A) return to continuous reinforcement in new environments until the behavior stabilizes, then thin to a variable ratio; or (B) immediately apply a variable ratio in new environments to build persistence. Evaluate both options by analyzing how reinforcement history, generalization, and schedule effects interact, then recommend the stronger approach with conditions under which the other might be appropriate.

---

## Answer Key

**Answer 1**
The schedule in effect is a fixed ratio (FR) schedule — specifically FR-3, rewarding every third correct response. The dog's frantic, rapid sitting after an unrewarded trial reflects the **post-reinforcement pause** pattern reversed: on fixed ratio schedules, animals learn that a set number of responses is required, so they often respond rapidly to "complete the ratio" and earn the next reward. The clustering of sits immediately after a miss is consistent with the dog attempting to complete the ratio count and reach reinforcement. This high, steady response rate with minimal pausing is a hallmark of FR schedules and distinguishes them from fixed interval schedules, which produce a scallop pattern (slow start, fast finish within each interval).

**Answer 2**
Dog A (variable ratio) will extinguish more slowly. The mechanism is the **partial reinforcement extinction effect (PREE)**: behaviors reinforced intermittently are more resistant to extinction than those reinforced continuously or on predictable schedules. On a variable ratio schedule, the dog cannot distinguish "this is extinction" from "reinforcement is just a few more responses away," because the number of responses required has always varied unpredictably. Dog B on a fixed interval schedule develops a clearer temporal expectation; when the expected interval passes repeatedly without reward, the signal that conditions have changed is detectable sooner, accelerating extinction.

**Answer 3**
Actual searches approximate a **variable ratio** schedule — the dog finds the subject after an unpredictable number of search behaviors (sniffing, ranging, alerting). Drills approximate a **fixed interval** schedule — reinforcement (a find) becomes available after a predictable 10-minute block regardless of the dog's specific actions within that window. Variable ratio schedules generate the highest and most persistent response rates because each response could be the one that pays off. Fixed interval schedules produce a scallop pattern: low effort early in the interval (since responding cannot accelerate the reward) and a burst near the expected time. The dog's low drill motivation reflects this temporal pattern — the schedule inadvertently teaches the dog that early effort is unnecessary.

**Answer 4**
The problem is an abrupt shift from continuous reinforcement (CRF) directly to a very lean fixed ratio (FR-10), without an intermediate thinning process. Behaviors trained on CRF have low resistance to extinction precisely because each unrewarded trial is a clear signal that conditions have changed. Jumping to FR-10 immediately creates many unrewarded trials before the dog has built the reinforcement history needed to persist through them, leading to rapid deterioration.

A more effective strategy: thin the schedule gradually. After CRF, move to FR-2, then FR-3, then introduce variability (VR-3, VR-5) over multiple sessions. Variable ratio schedules should be the end goal because they produce higher resistance to extinction than fixed ratio. Throughout thinning, quality of the behavior should remain the criterion — reward the best recalls, not just any recall. This builds a reinforcement history that supports persistence when treats are occasionally unavailable.

**Answer 5**
Yes, "look at me" should be on an intermittent schedule — specifically a **variable ratio** — before it is deployed in the fear context, but only after the behavior is fluent and reliable.

Reasoning: Counter-conditioning requires the dog to maintain a conditioned emotional response (CER) associating the stranger with good things. If "look at me" is on CRF, it may extinguish quickly under the stress of the fear context when the handler is managing distance and timing. A variable ratio schedule builds persistence — the dog continues offering the behavior despite occasional non-reinforcement — which is precisely what's needed when the handler's attention is divided.

However, the behavior must be fully proofed and generalized *before* thinning the schedule. Introducing intermittent reinforcement on a behavior that isn't yet fluent in low-stress settings will produce frustration and may compromise the counter-conditioning process. The sequencing matters: CRF to build the behavior → gradual thinning to VR → generalization to mild stressors → deployment in the fear context alongside desensitization protocols.

**Answer 6**
Option A is the stronger choice as the default approach, but Option B can be appropriate under specific conditions.

**Why A is stronger:** Generalization is itself a training challenge — a behavior that is fluent in one context is not automatically fluent in another. Introducing a lean variable ratio in a novel environment before the behavior is re-stabilized there compounds two demands simultaneously: the dog must both generalize the behavior and persist through non-reinforcement. If the dog fails repeatedly, the behavior degrades and the dog may begin avoiding or anticipating failure. Returning to CRF (or near-CRF) in the new environment treats generalization as a new learning phase — which it effectively is — and rebuilds the reinforcement history the dog needs before the schedule is thinned.

**Why B might be appropriate:** If the dog's reinforcement history on VR is already extensive across many environments (i.e., the dog has experienced thinned schedules in various contexts and has demonstrated persistence as a trained trait), jumping directly to VR in a new location may be reasonable. The dog's history predicts it will persist through initial variability. B is also worth considering if the dog is breaking heel due to distraction rather than a true fluency gap — in that case, the issue may be proofing against specific stimuli rather than schedule effects, and the reinforcement schedule is a secondary concern.

**Recommendation:** Default to A. Reset to denser reinforcement in each novel environment, then thin systematically. Reserve B for dogs with deep, varied VR reinforcement histories where the trainer has evidence the dog treats "no reward yet" as a feature rather than a signal to disengage.
