## The Hook

After this lesson, you will be able to look at any behavior you want to teach and construct a session-by-session plan that specifies what to work on, in what sequence, under what conditions, and how to tell whether you're making progress.

Think of a pilot filing a flight plan before takeoff. The pilot knows the destination, but the plan exists for a different reason: when conditions shift mid-flight, the plan tells you where you are relative to where you need to be, what to adjust, and whether you're still on course. Without it, every deviation just becomes confusion. A structured training plan does the same thing—it converts a fuzzy goal ("I want my dog to come when called") into a navigable route with checkpoints, so you can distinguish "we're on track, keep going" from "something broke, let's diagnose it."

---

## Why It Matters

The previous topics in this sequence built your diagnostic vocabulary: classical conditioning, the four quadrants, timing, reinforcement schedules, extinction, proofing. You now understand *why* training mechanisms work. The gap that kills progress for most trainers isn't understanding—it's structure. They know the tools but apply them haphazardly, session by session, based on how they feel that day.

Here's the specific wall they hit: after a few weeks of inconsistent sessions, the dog knows something but not reliably. The trainer can't tell if the problem is insufficient repetitions, premature environmental difficulty, weak reinforcers, or a missed proofing step—because there's no record of what actually happened. Without a plan, "it's not working" becomes a dead end instead of a diagnostic.

A structured plan turns training from an ongoing guess into a system that generates interpretable data. When something breaks, you can trace exactly where it broke and why.

## The Ladder

**Start with what you're actually building.**

A training plan is a written document—not a mental intention—that answers four questions before you begin any session:

1. What is the target behavior, precisely defined?
2. What is the current approximation the dog can perform reliably?
3. What environmental conditions are present?
4. How will you measure progress?

Each of these deserves unpacking, because each one is a place where plans quietly fail.

---

**Question 1: Precisely defining the terminal behavior**

A "terminal behavior" is the final, complete version of what you want the dog to do—the endpoint of training. "Sit" sounds simple, but is it: sit until released, or sit until you give another cue? Sit in any position the dog starts from, or only from a standing position? Sit at a distance, or only in front of you?

Vague goals produce vague training. If you haven't written down the terminal behavior in enough detail to demonstrate it to a stranger, you haven't defined it yet.

---

**Question 2: Establishing your baseline**

A baseline is where the dog is right now, without any new training. Before designing a plan, you need to know what the dog can already do reliably—and "reliably" has a threshold. A common working standard is 8 out of 10 correct responses in a controlled environment before increasing difficulty. This isn't arbitrary: it means the behavior has been reinforced enough to be stable, not just performed occasionally when conditions are perfect.

The gap between the baseline and the terminal behavior is what your plan needs to fill. That gap gets broken into a task hierarchy—a sequence of smaller, achievable steps, each of which builds on the last. You've already seen this in shaping: this is the same principle applied to the whole arc of training, not just a single session.

---

**Question 3: Managing environmental variables**

An environmental variable is anything in the context that affects whether the dog can perform the behavior: location, distractions, handler position, time of day, other animals nearby, whether the dog has already eaten, noise levels. You've seen from proofing that dogs don't generalize automatically—they encode the full context of a trained behavior.

This means you don't randomly vary environments. You systematically control them. Early in training, reduce variables to make the behavior easy—a quiet room, no distractions, close proximity. Then introduce variables deliberately, one at a time, so you know which change caused any breakdown in performance.

The key phrase is "one variable at a time." If you increase distance, add a distraction, and change locations simultaneously and the dog fails, you've learned nothing. If you change only location and the dog fails, you've learned something precise: the behavior hasn't generalized to new locations yet.

---

**Question 4: Tracking progress**

A training log doesn't need to be elaborate. What it needs to capture is: the date, the behavior worked on, the current criteria (what counts as correct), the success rate, and any variables present. That's it.

This record does three things. First, it prevents you from accidentally stalling at one level—many trainers drill the same step far longer than necessary because they're going on feel rather than data. Second, it lets you identify patterns: maybe your dog performs well at 10am but poorly at 5pm, and you've never noticed because you weren't tracking. Third, it gives you something to look back at when progress seems to plateau—you can often see exactly when and why momentum stopped.

---

**Putting the pieces together: session structure**

A single training session should have three elements:

- **A warm-up**: one to two minutes reinforcing a known, easy behavior. This primes the dog to offer responses and establishes the reinforcement context.
- **A working phase**: the actual new learning, typically no longer than five to fifteen minutes for most dogs. Shorter, focused sessions beat long unfocused ones.
- **A finish**: always end on a success. If the dog fails three times in a row, drop back to an easier approximation they can succeed at, get several clean reps, then end the session. Never end on failure—not for sentimental reasons, but because the last behavior practiced is the one with the most recent reinforcement history.

## Worked Reality

Suppose you want to train a 14-month-old Labrador mix, currently untrained, to perform a reliable "down-stay" for 60 seconds in a busy park—useful for vet waiting rooms, outdoor restaurants, or anywhere you need the dog to settle.

**Step 1: Define the terminal behavior precisely.**
Down, held until a verbal release cue, from any starting position, for 60 seconds, with pedestrian traffic passing within 10 feet, regardless of handler position up to 6 feet away.

**Step 2: Establish the baseline.**
The dog knows "sit" but not "down." The first task hierarchy step isn't "down-stay in a park"—it's teaching the lure-to-down position in a quiet room, then fading the lure, then adding the verbal cue.

**Step 3: Build the task hierarchy.**
You might outline it like this:
- Week 1: "Down" from a stand in quiet kitchen, lure, no duration.
- Week 2: "Down" with verbal cue only, 8/10 reliability, still no duration.
- Week 3: Add duration—one second, then three, then five. Same quiet kitchen.
- Week 4: Begin duration at 10 seconds; introduce mild distraction (television on).
- Week 5: Change location to backyard; reset duration to five seconds, rebuild.
- Week 6–8: Increase duration; introduce leashed pedestrians.
- Week 9–10: Transition to public spaces with low traffic; rebuild as needed.

Notice that every time a new variable is introduced (location, distraction), duration resets to a level the dog can succeed at. This is the core logic of the plan: you don't advance difficulty on multiple axes simultaneously.

**Step 4: Track.**
After each session, you note: "Week 3, Day 2—down with verbal cue, kitchen, 6/10 on 3-second duration. Dog broke early twice, repositioned both times. Will stay at 3 seconds tomorrow."

This note tells you everything you need to design the next session. Without it, tomorrow's session is just guesswork.

## Friction Point

The most common mistake is treating the training plan as a schedule rather than a decision framework.

This looks like: "Week 1 is down, Week 2 is stay, Week 3 is in new locations." The trainer moves to Week 2 on schedule—not because the dog hit the reliability threshold, but because seven days passed.

This is tempting because it feels organized. You have dates, you have topics, you feel like you're making progress. But a schedule-based plan measures time elapsed, not learning achieved. When you advance before the behavior is stable, you build on an unstable foundation, and the structure above it will eventually collapse.

The correct mental model is that your plan is a set of *criteria-gated stages*, not a calendar. You move to the next step when the dog meets the success threshold—not when enough time has passed. This means some stages take longer than planned, and that's information, not failure. It tells you the previous step needed more repetitions, the variable you introduced was too large a jump, or the reinforcer you're using may not be motivating enough at that level of difficulty.

A training plan isn't a timeline. It's a map with locked gates: you only open the next gate when you've earned it.

You decide to advance the plan by introducing off-leash heeling in your fenced backyard. In the first three sessions, performance drops to 2/10. A friend suggests the dog is "just not ready for off-leash work." Using the structured planning framework from this lesson, explain how you would diagnose whether this is true—and what evidence would distinguish "the dog lacks the skill" from "the plan introduced too many variables simultaneously."
