## Exercises

**Exercise 1**
A dog starts barking and spinning excitedly the moment its owner picks up the leash — before the walk has even begun. Using the language of classical conditioning, identify the unconditioned stimulus, the conditioned stimulus, and the conditioned response in this scenario.

**Exercise 2**
A puppy receives a vaccination at the vet. The needle causes pain (and a yelp). Three weeks later, the puppy trembles and tries to hide as soon as the car pulls into the vet's parking lot. Explain the step-by-step process by which this fear response was acquired.

**Exercise 3**
A rescue dog is terrified of men wearing hats. A trainer decides to have hat-wearing men toss high-value treats toward the dog repeatedly over several sessions. After six weeks, the dog wags its tail when it sees a man in a hat. What conditioning process is at work, and why does the order of the hat appearance and treat delivery matter?

**Exercise 4**
Two trainers use different approaches with a dog that fears thunderstorms. Trainer A plays recordings of thunder at very low volume while feeding the dog its meals, gradually increasing the volume over weeks. Trainer B plays recordings at full volume while feeding the dog, hoping to "overwhelm" the fear quickly. Predict the likely outcome for each dog and explain why the approaches would differ in effectiveness.

**Exercise 5**
A dog owner notices their dog becomes anxious every time they put on their work shoes — an association formed because the owner always leaves shortly after. The owner tries to break the association by putting on their shoes and then sitting on the couch for 30 minutes without leaving, repeated daily. After two weeks, the dog seems indifferent to the shoes. However, the owner then goes on a two-week vacation and leaves the dog with a sitter. When they return and put on their shoes again, the anxiety briefly returns. Identify and explain the phenomena that account for both the reduction of anxiety and its brief return.

**Exercise 6**
A certified trainer is working with a dog-reactive Labrador. The dog lunges and barks at other dogs on leash. The trainer recommends a counter-conditioning protocol. A well-meaning neighbor suggests that the owner should instead correct the dog firmly with a leash pop every time it reacts, arguing "the dog needs to know that behavior isn't allowed." Construct an argument — grounded in classical conditioning — for why the counter-conditioning approach targets the problem at a more fundamental level than the correction-based approach, and identify one realistic risk of the correction approach.

---

## Answer Key

**Answer 1**
The unconditioned stimulus (US) is the walk itself — the actual outdoor experience that naturally produces excitement. The conditioned stimulus (CS) is the leash, which previously had no special meaning but has been repeatedly paired with the walk. The conditioned response (CR) is the barking and spinning that occurs in response to the leash alone. This is involuntary — the dog is not "deciding" to get excited; the emotional arousal fires automatically because the leash has become a reliable predictor of the walk.

**Answer 2**
Step 1: The needle (unconditioned stimulus) causes pain (unconditioned response — yelp, distress). Step 2: The vet clinic environment — smells, sounds, the parking lot — were all present at the same time as the painful event. Through temporal pairing, these neutral stimuli became associated with the pain. Step 3: On the next visit, the parking lot alone (now a conditioned stimulus) is sufficient to trigger trembling and hiding (conditioned response). The dog's nervous system has learned that "this place predicts pain," and the fear response fires before any painful event actually occurs. No deliberate thought is required — this is automatic associative learning.

**Answer 3**
The process is counter-conditioning: the trainer is working to replace a negative emotional association (man-in-hat = threat) with a positive one (man-in-hat = food/good things). The order of stimuli is critical. The hat must appear *before* the treat, so the hat becomes a reliable predictor of the treat. If the treat appeared first or simultaneously without clear temporal ordering, the dog's nervous system cannot use the hat as a signal. The hat must function as the conditioned stimulus that predicts the unconditioned stimulus (food). Reversing the order — treat first, hat second — would not produce the same association and could even make the dog anxious about when the treat ends and the hat appears.

**Answer 4**
Trainer A's approach (low volume, gradual increase) is likely to succeed. By keeping the thunder sounds below the threshold that triggers fear, the dog can eat calmly and form a positive association — meals predict a manageable sound. The emotion attached to thunder gradually shifts from fear to neutral or positive anticipation. This is systematic desensitization combined with counter-conditioning.

Trainer B's approach is likely to backfire. Presenting the full-volume thunder while feeding may exceed the dog's stress threshold, making it too anxious to eat or to form any positive association. Worse, the food itself could become contaminated — associated with the frightening experience — making the dog less food-motivated in future sessions. Flooding (overwhelming exposure) without control or escape can entrench fear rather than reduce it, because the emotional state during the pairing is negative, not neutral or positive.

**Answer 5**
The reduction of anxiety over two weeks is **extinction** — the conditioned stimulus (shoes) is repeatedly presented without the unconditioned stimulus (owner's departure), so the predictive relationship between them is weakened. The dog's conditioned fear response fades because the shoes no longer reliably signal anything.

The brief return of anxiety after the vacation is **spontaneous recovery** — a well-documented phenomenon in classical conditioning where an extinguished conditioned response temporarily reappears after a rest period. Extinction does not erase the original learning; it suppresses it. After a gap in time (the vacation), the old association reasserts itself briefly before re-extinction occurs. This is important practically: owners should not assume that extinction is permanent after a two-week protocol. Maintenance and re-exposure may be needed, especially after breaks.

**Answer 6**
The counter-conditioning argument: A dog-reactive dog is not misbehaving out of defiance — it is experiencing a genuine conditioned fear or arousal response triggered by the sight of other dogs. The lunging and barking are the conditioned response to a conditioned stimulus. A leash correction addresses only the outward behavior (the response) without changing the underlying emotional association. The dog may suppress the lunge to avoid the correction, but the internal state — anxiety or arousal at the sight of other dogs — remains or worsens. Counter-conditioning targets the association itself: pairing other dogs with something positive (high-value food, play) so the conditioned stimulus begins to predict good things rather than threat, changing the emotional response at its root.

A realistic risk of the correction approach: if the leash pop is administered every time another dog appears, the other dog becomes a reliable predictor of an unpleasant event. This can deepen the negative association through classical conditioning — the dog now links "other dog = pain" more strongly, potentially increasing rather than decreasing reactivity over time.
