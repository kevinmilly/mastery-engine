## Exercises

**Exercise 1**
A dog reliably sits on cue in your kitchen but ignores the cue at the park. What does this tell you about the dog's current understanding of the "sit" cue, and what is the appropriate next training step?

**Exercise 2**
You are proofing a "down-stay" behavior. You currently practice it in your living room with no distractions. List three specific variables you would change — one at a time — to begin generalizing the behavior, and explain why you must change them one at a time rather than all at once.

**Exercise 3**
A trainer teaches a dog to retrieve a dumbbell reliably in a quiet training hall. When the dog is tested at a dog show with spectators, loud announcements, and unfamiliar smells, performance collapses entirely. The trainer concludes the dog "forgot" the behavior. Evaluate this conclusion. What is a more accurate explanation, and what should the trainer have done differently during training?

**Exercise 4**
A family dog performs "come" perfectly for the owner but ignores the cue when the teenage son calls. The son uses the same word and hand signal. Identify what proofing dimension was neglected and design a two-week plan to address it.

**Exercise 5**
A dog learned "leave it" through shaping: the trainer reinforced successive approximations of nose-away from a treat on the floor. The behavior is now solid in the training room. However, during a walk, the dog lunges at a dropped piece of food. Using your understanding of both shaping and generalization, explain why the trained behavior failed in this context and outline how you would rebuild it to be reliable outdoors, without starting entirely from scratch.

**Exercise 6**
You are working with a rescue dog that was trained by shelter staff using positive reinforcement to sit, stay, and walk on a loose leash. In its new home, the adopter reports that none of these behaviors work. The adopter has tried using the same cues and the same treat the shelter used. Drawing on proofing, generalization, and your knowledge of classical conditioning and extinction, construct a complete explanation of what may be happening and a structured plan to restore reliable behavior in the new environment.

## Answer Key

**Answer 1**
The dog has learned a context-specific behavior, not a generalized cue. The dog associates "sit" with the specific combination of stimuli present in the kitchen — the floor surface, ambient sounds, the owner's position, household smells — not with the abstract verbal cue itself. The behavior has not been proofed across environments. The appropriate next step is to begin systematic generalization: take the dog to a low-distraction outdoor area (not the park yet) and re-teach "sit" there, using high-value reinforcement. The trainer should temporarily lower the criterion — accepting a slower or less crisp sit — because the dog is effectively learning in a new context. Only after success in several low-distraction locations should the trainer introduce higher-distraction environments like the park.

**Answer 2**
Three variables to change, one at a time:
1. **Location** — move practice to a different room, then outside in the yard. Changing location alone tests whether the behavior generalizes beyond the original room.
2. **Distance** — once the dog is solid in a new location, increase handler distance from the dog during the stay. This changes the spatial relationship without adding other demands.
3. **Duration** — extend the length of the stay incrementally. Duration is a distinct pressure from location or distance.

Changing one variable at a time is essential because proofing works by exposing the dog to one new challenge at a time. If multiple variables change simultaneously and the dog fails, you cannot identify which variable caused the breakdown. More importantly, compounding challenges too quickly overwhelms the dog's ability to succeed, which erodes the behavior and can trigger extinction if reinforcement stops arriving.

**Answer 3**
The conclusion that the dog "forgot" is inaccurate. Forgetting implies the neural representation of the behavior degraded, which does not happen this quickly in a well-trained dog. The accurate explanation is that the behavior was never generalized to environments with that level of distraction and novelty. The dog learned "retrieve dumbbell" as a behavior tied to the specific context of a quiet training hall. At the show, the stimuli were so different and so intense that the dog was effectively in an entirely new environment where the trained behavior had no established history of reinforcement.

What the trainer should have done: progressively introduce distraction and novelty during training — practicing in multiple locations, gradually adding spectators, novel sounds, and unfamiliar smells, building up the dog's ability to perform under increasing environmental pressure before the show. The show environment should have been approximated in practice, not encountered cold.

**Answer 4**
The neglected proofing dimension is **handler generalization** — ensuring the cue works across different people, not just the primary trainer. The dog has associated "come" with a specific person: the owner's particular voice tone, body language, scent, and history of reinforcement. Even if the son uses the same words and signal, these are different stimuli coming from a different source.

Two-week plan:
- **Week 1, Days 1–3:** The owner calls the dog and, as the dog begins moving toward them, the son steps in to deliver the treat. The dog starts associating the son with reinforcement delivery.
- **Days 4–7:** The son gives the cue while the owner stands nearby as a visible "anchor." Success is reinforced heavily by the son. The owner's presence provides mild support.
- **Week 2, Days 1–4:** The son gives the cue independently in low-distraction settings indoors, reinforcing every correct response with high-value rewards.
- **Days 5–7:** Fade owner's presence entirely. Practice in multiple locations with the son as sole handler. Use a variable reinforcement schedule once the behavior is solid.

**Answer 5**
The behavior failed because shaping built the behavior in a specific context (training room, stationary treat on floor, calm environment) and generalization to the outdoors was never systematically trained. Two mechanisms are at play:

First, the cue "leave it" became a contextual stimulus — it predicted reinforcement in the training room but has no reinforcement history on walks. Second, the arousal level outdoors (movement, novel smells, the surprise of dropped food) likely exceeds the threshold at which the dog can execute the behavior, even if it weakly recognizes the cue.

To rebuild without starting from scratch:
1. **Begin in a low-arousal outdoor setting** (e.g., a quiet corner of the yard) with the same floor-treat setup used in training. Reinforce "leave it" heavily there to begin building an outdoor reinforcement history.
2. **Increase environmental complexity gradually** — move to a sidewalk with low foot traffic, then busier areas.
3. **Proof the specific trigger** — dropped food while in motion. Practice by deliberately dropping food during leash walks in low-distraction areas, cueing "leave it" before the dog reacts, and reinforcing success.
4. **Build a reinforcement history at the moment of temptation**, not just during formal practice. The goal is for the cue to predict reinforcement in exactly the conditions where the dog previously failed.

**Answer 6**
Several overlapping mechanisms likely explain the breakdown:

**Classical conditioning:** The shelter was a distinct environment with specific stimuli (smells, staff, kennel sounds) that became part of the conditioned context for each behavior. In the new home, those context cues are absent. The dog may not recognize the training situation as one in which these cues apply, even with identical verbal cues and treats.

**Generalization failure:** The behaviors were trained and reinforced exclusively at the shelter. The new home represents a novel environment that was never part of the proofing process. The dog has no reinforcement history for these behaviors in this context.

**Extinction risk:** If the adopter has been repeatedly cueing behaviors that the dog does not perform, and giving no reinforcement (or giving it inconsistently), this constitutes an extinction procedure — the cue is being presented without reinforcement, which weakens the cue-behavior association further.

**Handler generalization:** The adopter is a new person with a different voice, body language, scent, and reinforcement style. The dog has not generalized behaviors to this handler.

**Structured plan:**
1. **Stop cueing behaviors the dog is not performing.** Each failed, unreinforced trial advances extinction. Pause formal cue use for 48–72 hours.
2. **Build a positive association with the new environment and handler** using classical conditioning: deliver high-value treats freely, with no behavioral requirements, to establish the adopter as a reliable predictor of good things.
3. **Restart each behavior from near-scratch in the new home**, using luring or shaping to get the behavior, then re-attach the cue. Treat this as if proofing a new environment, because it is.
4. **Generalize systematically** — once each behavior is solid in one room, move to other rooms, then outdoors, adding distractions gradually.
5. **Communicate with shelter staff** to learn what cues, reward schedules, and reinforcers were used — match these initially before transitioning to the adopter's own routine.
