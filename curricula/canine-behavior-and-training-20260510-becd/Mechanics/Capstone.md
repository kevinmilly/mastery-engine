## Capstone: Mechanics — Canine Behavior and Training

### The Scenario

You are a certified dog trainer brought in to work with a 3-year-old intact male Border Collie named Drift and his owner, Marisol. Drift was purchased as a potential agility competitor but has spent most of his life in a suburban home with limited socialization. Marisol reports that Drift is brilliant in the backyard — he knows sit, down, stay, and several tricks — but falls apart completely in new environments. At the local park, he ignores cues entirely, pulls relentlessly on leash, and fixates on bicycles with an intensity that Marisol describes as "like he goes somewhere else." Last week, a passing cyclist triggered a lunge that nearly pulled Marisol off her feet.

Marisol has tried correcting the bicycle fixation by jerking the leash and saying "no" firmly. She reports that Drift "knows he did something wrong" because he slinks away afterward, but the behavior continues — and seems to be getting worse. She also mentions that she has been giving Drift a treat whenever he looks at her at home to "practice focus," but she does it randomly, "whenever I remember." The agility club Marisol wants to join requires dogs to perform a reliable recall and a 30-second down-stay in a busy environment before they can participate in group classes.

Your intake assessment reveals that Drift's response to bicycles is not simply excitement — his body language shows tight muscles, a fixed forward stare, shallow breathing, and he refuses food when a bike is within 50 feet. When no bikes are present in a neutral environment, his food motivation is excellent and he engages readily. You have eight weeks before Marisol's application deadline for the agility club. Your job is to produce a written training plan and rationale that Marisol can execute with your weekly guidance.

---

### Your Tasks

**Task 1: Diagnose the Bicycle Response**
Analyze what behavioral and emotional process is driving Drift's reaction to bicycles. Explain why Marisol's leash-correction approach has not only failed to resolve the problem but may be actively worsening it. Use precise terminology and identify the mechanism responsible for the escalation.

**Task 2: Design a Bicycle Desensitization Protocol**
Write a step-by-step protocol to address Drift's bicycle response over the eight-week timeline. Your protocol must specify starting threshold distance, criteria for progressing to the next step, what to do if Drift goes over threshold, and how you will know the emotional response — not just the surface behavior — has changed.

**Task 3: Repair and Rebuild Marisol's Reinforcement Approach**
Evaluate Marisol's current "random treat for focus" practice at home. Identify what reinforcement schedule she is accidentally using, explain its behavioral effects on Drift's focus behavior specifically, and prescribe a corrected reinforcement approach — including the schedule, timing, and a plan for transitioning to the conditions the agility club requires.

**Task 4: Address the Generalization Gap**
Drift performs known cues reliably at home but not in the park. Design a structured three-phase proofing plan for the recall and the down-stay that will take him from backyard reliability to performance in a busy environment by the application deadline. Specify the variables you will manipulate in each phase and why you are sequencing them in that order.

**Task 5: Write the Full 8-Week Training Plan**
Integrate your work from Tasks 1–4 into a coherent weekly training plan. For each of the eight weeks, specify the primary focus, session structure (duration, location, what is practiced), and the measurable criterion that must be met before the following week's work begins. Explain how the concurrent tracks (bicycle work, focus/recall, down-stay proofing) are sequenced to support each other rather than conflict.

---

### What Good Work Looks Like

- **Mechanistic precision**: Strong responses name the specific conditioning processes at work — classical versus operant, the relevant quadrant, the schedule type — and explain *why* those mechanisms produce the observed outcomes, not just what the outcomes are.
- **Threshold awareness**: The desensitization protocol reflects genuine understanding of the relationship between arousal, learning, and sub-threshold work; a response that jumps straight to exposure without addressing emotional state will not meet the standard.
- **Internal consistency**: The full training plan in Task 5 is a synthesis, not a list. A strong response shows that the sequencing decisions across all tracks are deliberate — that bicycle work, focus training, and proofing are paced to reinforce each other and that the plan accounts for Drift's current behavioral state, not an idealized dog.
- **Practical specificity**: Criteria are measurable (distance, duration, number of repetitions, rate of reinforcement) rather than vague ("when he seems comfortable"). The plan could be handed to Marisol and followed without guessing.
- **Behavioral honesty**: Strong responses acknowledge what could go wrong — extinction bursts, regression under distraction, over-threshold moments — and prescribe contingency responses rather than assuming linear progress.
