## Exercises

**Exercise 1**
A trainer uses a food lure to guide a dog into a "down" position. After 30 repetitions, the dog follows the hand motion reliably — but only when the trainer's hand actually contains food. Identify what went wrong in the training process and describe what the trainer should have done differently.

**Exercise 2**
A dog is bored with standard treat rewards mid-session and starts offering behaviors half-heartedly. The trainer decides to try jackpotting. Describe specifically when and how the trainer should deliver the jackpot to make it most effective, and explain what outcome they should expect.

**Exercise 3**
A dog loves to sniff the ground but is distracted during heel work whenever interesting smells are present. A trainer wants to use the Premack principle to improve heeling. Describe a concrete training plan using this principle, including what the "high-value" behavior is and how it becomes contingent on the "low-value" behavior.

**Exercise 4**
Two trainers are working on recall with the same dog. Trainer A always calls the dog and rewards with a treat. Trainer B uses the environment — sometimes releasing the dog to greet another dog, sometimes opening the car door (which the dog loves), and sometimes giving a treat. After six weeks, which dog is likely to have a more reliable recall and why?

**Exercise 5**
A trainer has successfully lure-trained a dog to perform a tight "spin" behavior. They now want a competition-level spin: fast, tight, and responsive to a hand signal without food present. Design a lure-fading plan in stages, explaining what is happening cognitively for the dog at each stage and what criteria must be met before advancing.

**Exercise 6**
A professional trainer is working with a rescue dog that has low food motivation and is easily overwhelmed in new environments. Standard reinforcement schedules are failing — the dog shuts down. Using jackpotting, the Premack principle, and environmental reinforcers together, propose an integrated approach for this specific dog. Justify each choice in terms of why it addresses the dog's particular challenges.

---

## Answer Key

**Answer 1**
The trainer failed to fade the lure. A lure is a training tool, not a permanent cue — it should be removed as soon as the dog understands the movement. The correct process involves: (1) using the food lure for the first few repetitions to establish the physical pattern, (2) switching to an empty hand in the same motion (a "ghost lure") so the gesture becomes the cue, and (3) delivering the reward from the other hand or a pocket after the behavior is complete. By keeping food in the luring hand throughout 30 reps, the trainer conditioned the dog to respond to the scent/presence of food rather than the hand signal itself. The behavior became dependent on the lure rather than the cue.

**Answer 2**
Jackpotting is most effective when delivered immediately after an especially crisp or correct repetition — not randomly, and not as a consolation for poor effort. The trainer should wait for one clean, enthusiastic response, then immediately deliver a much larger-than-usual quantity or a higher-value reward (e.g., ten small treats in rapid succession, or a piece of real chicken). The timing must be precise so the dog associates the jackpot with that specific behavior quality. The expected outcome is a brief pause followed by renewed enthusiasm and effort — the dog is essentially reset motivationally and tends to offer the behavior with more energy in subsequent reps.

**Answer 3**
In the Premack principle, access to a high-probability (preferred) behavior reinforces a low-probability (less preferred) behavior. Here, sniffing is the high-value behavior; heeling attentively is the lower-value behavior. A concrete plan: (1) ask for a short heel segment of 5–10 steps in a low-distraction area, (2) the moment the dog completes it with attention, say "go sniff" and release them to sniff freely for 15–30 seconds, (3) gradually increase heel duration before releasing to sniff. The dog learns that heeling *produces* sniffing — the preferred activity becomes the reward. Over time, the dog may begin offering better heel attention because sniffing is now contingent on it.

**Answer 4**
Trainer B's dog is likely to have more reliable recall, especially in real-world conditions. This is because Trainer B is using environmental reinforcers — varied, contextually meaningful outcomes — which creates a dog that doesn't predict a specific reward and therefore doesn't discount the recall based on context. Trainer A's dog has learned a narrow rule: "come → treat." In situations where a treat seems unlikely (e.g., at the dog park), the behavior weakens. Trainer B's dog has learned "come → something great, and I don't know what" — which mirrors a variable reinforcement schedule that produces more durable, extinction-resistant behavior. Additionally, the rewards Trainer B uses (greeting a dog, car ride) are often intrinsically more motivating than food in high-distraction environments.

**Answer 5**
A staged lure-fading plan:

**Stage 1 — Full lure:** Food in hand guides the full spin. Dog is learning the physical movement. Cognitive load is low; the dog follows the food, not a cue.

**Stage 2 — Ghost lure:** Empty hand makes the same circular motion. Reward comes from the other hand after completion. Criterion: dog completes the spin 8/10 times. The dog is beginning to respond to the gesture as a cue rather than the food itself.

**Stage 3 — Abbreviated signal:** The circular gesture shrinks — a smaller arc, then a flick of the wrist. Reward rate stays high. The dog is generalizing from the lure motion to a more abstract signal.

**Stage 4 — Formal cue + speed criteria:** Introduce a consistent, minimal hand signal. Now add speed as a criterion — only reinforce spins that are tight and fast. This raises the behavioral standard and begins to produce competition quality.

**Stage 5 — Variable reinforcement:** Reward the best spins intermittently. The dog must now sustain effort without guaranteed reward, which consolidates the behavior's reliability.

Before advancing each stage, the trainer must see 80%+ success at the current stage across multiple sessions and environments.

**Answer 6**
This dog requires an approach that lowers arousal threshold while building reinforcement history. Here's an integrated plan:

**Environmental reinforcers first:** In a new environment, food motivation may be suppressed by stress. Instead of food, identify what the dog naturally seeks — sniffing, retreating to a safe corner, or calm petting. Use access to these as rewards for tiny offered behaviors (e.g., eye contact, orienting toward the trainer). This builds a reinforcement history without requiring the dog to overcome its food disinterest.

**Premack for engagement:** Once the dog shows any voluntary movement toward the trainer, use Premack — brief exposure to whatever the dog finds calming or appealing (sniffing a specific object, returning to its mat) contingent on small approach or engagement behaviors. This leverages what the dog already wants rather than what the trainer wants to give.

**Jackpotting for breakthrough moments:** If and when the dog offers an unexpected engagement — eye contact, a relaxed body orientation toward the trainer — jackpot it immediately with whatever functions as high-value for this dog (e.g., a pause in pressure, a scatter of food on the ground, a quiet verbal release). This creates a strong associative spike around that behavior and makes it more likely to recur.

The integration rationale: environmental reinforcers meet the dog where it is motivationally; Premack turns the dog's own preferences into a training lever; jackpotting marks the rare moments of genuine engagement with disproportionate value, accelerating the building of a working relationship.
