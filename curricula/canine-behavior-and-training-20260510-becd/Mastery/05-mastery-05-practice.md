## Exercises

**Exercise 1**
A dog owner reports that their dog barks continuously for the first 20 minutes after they leave, then settles down and sleeps. The owner assumes this is separation anxiety. What diagnosis does this pattern most likely suggest, and what single piece of additional information would most help you confirm it?

**Exercise 2**
You are beginning a desensitization protocol for a dog with confirmed separation anxiety. On day one, the dog shows no distress when the owner picks up their keys. On day two, the owner skips the session and leaves for 4 hours for work. What is the likely consequence of this skip, and why does it matter for the overall treatment timeline?

**Exercise 3**
Two dogs are presented with similar profiles: both destroy furniture when left alone and both have owners who work 9-hour days. Dog A's destruction is concentrated near exits and is accompanied by salivation and self-injury; Dog B's destruction is scattered, the dog appears calm on return, and the behavior started when a neighborhood construction project began. Outline how you would differentiate between these two cases diagnostically and what intervention path each would require.

**Exercise 4**
A trainer suggests that because a leash-reactive dog has learned to tolerate strangers at threshold distances through a desensitization protocol, the same graduated exposure approach should work identically for separation anxiety — just substitute "distance from owner" as the variable. Evaluate this reasoning. Where does the analogy hold, and where does it break down?

**Exercise 5**
A dog with separation anxiety has made good progress through a desensitization protocol over 8 weeks: tolerating absences of up to 45 minutes. The owner now wants to use a long-line recall game in the backyard as part of daily enrichment, having read that exercise reduces anxiety. A second trainer suggests introducing a "go to mat" behavior chain so the dog has a structured settling behavior before departures. Assess both suggestions in terms of their compatibility with the current treatment plan, noting any risks or sequencing considerations.

**Exercise 6**
A rescue dog has a documented history of resource guarding around food and has recently been diagnosed with moderate separation anxiety. The owner, following advice from an online forum, has begun hand-feeding all meals to build trust and using a stuffed Kong as the sole departure cue — meaning the dog only receives the Kong when the owner leaves. Three weeks in, the dog's separation distress has worsened and it has begun growling at the owner during Kong preparation. Using your understanding of both resource guarding and separation anxiety intervention, explain what has likely gone wrong and propose a revised approach.

---

## Answer Key

**Answer 1**
This pattern — intense vocalization that resolves within 20–30 minutes — is more consistent with **isolation distress** or a **frustration/boredom response** than true separation anxiety. Separation anxiety typically persists or escalates and is not time-limited in this way; it is driven by attachment to a specific person, not merely the absence of stimulation.

The single most useful additional piece of information would be **video footage of the dog's behavior when left with another person or dog**. If distress disappears entirely when the dog has any company, isolation distress is the more likely diagnosis. If distress persists even with company (because the specific attachment figure is absent), separation anxiety becomes more probable. This distinction drives the entire intervention pathway.

**Answer 2**
The 4-hour absence likely constitutes a **significant setback** to the desensitization protocol. Systematic desensitization works by keeping the dog consistently sub-threshold — exposures are increased in tiny, controlled increments. A full-day absence when the dog has only been conditioned to tolerate very short durations floods the dog far beyond threshold, re-sensitizing the fear response and potentially undoing weeks of progress.

The consequence is not just one bad day; the dog's nervous system has been conditioned to associate absences with high distress again. The treatment timeline must effectively reset to the last reliably tolerated duration, and the owner must understand that **the protocol is not something to resume between real-life departures** — real-life and training must be reconciled, often requiring the owner to arrange alternate care on workdays throughout treatment.

**Answer 3**
**Diagnosis:**
Dog A's profile — exit-focused destruction, hypersalivation, self-injury — points strongly to **separation anxiety**. The behaviors reflect panic and desperate escape attempts, not boredom or environmental reaction. Video review would likely show pre-departure anxiety and continuous distress.

Dog B's scattered destruction, calm demeanor on return, and correlation with a new external stressor (construction noise) suggests **noise phobia or a situational fear response** rather than attachment-based anxiety. The dog may be attempting to cope with an aversive auditory environment, not distress at the owner's absence.

**Intervention paths:**
- Dog A requires a systematic desensitization protocol targeting the owner's departure cues and absence duration, with no full absences during treatment. Veterinary consultation for anti-anxiety medication should be considered given the severity.
- Dog B requires noise desensitization and management (soundproofing, white noise, possible anxiolytic support during construction). The treatment does not need to address the owner-attachment dynamic.

**Answer 4**
The analogy **holds** in its core mechanism: both leash reactivity and separation anxiety respond to graduated exposure that keeps the dog sub-threshold, pairing the feared stimulus with neutral or positive associations. In both cases, too-fast progression causes sensitization rather than desensitization.

The analogy **breaks down** in several important ways:
- In leash reactivity, the trainer controls the trigger's distance precisely and in real time. With separation anxiety, "distance from owner" is a binary — the owner is either present or absent — making fine-grained control much harder to achieve. Departure cues, sounds, and context collapse that gradient.
- Leash reactivity sessions can be discrete and frequent without contaminating the rest of the dog's day. Separation anxiety treatment requires that **every real absence be managed**, not just formal training sessions. A single unplanned over-threshold event resets progress.
- The emotional valence differs: leash reactivity is often fear or frustration toward an external threat; separation anxiety involves panic at the loss of a primary attachment figure. The neurobiological profile is different, which is why separation anxiety is more commonly treated with pharmacological support.

**Answer 5**
**Exercise enrichment (long-line recall game):** Physical exercise is a legitimate adjunct to anxiety treatment — a dog with appropriate arousal levels may be better able to settle. However, it carries a risk if the exercise session immediately precedes a departure, as it could elevate arousal rather than reduce it. The session should be scheduled with sufficient buffer time before any planned absence. Given the dog is mid-protocol, it is compatible as long as it does not disrupt the structured departure routine.

**"Go to mat" behavior chain before departures:** This is a higher-risk suggestion at this stage. Introducing a new pre-departure ritual could either help (if the dog finds the familiar chain calming) or become an additional departure cue that triggers anticipatory anxiety. The dog has made 8 weeks of progress on an existing cue structure; layering in a new behavior chain risks creating a new conditioned anxiety trigger. If introduced at all, it should be trained to a reliable, calm baseline **completely outside the departure context first**, then very slowly integrated. The safer path is to continue the established protocol until longer durations are solid, then consider it.

**Answer 6**
**What went wrong:**
Two problems have compounded. First, the Kong has been made a **reliable predictor of the owner's departure** — a conditioned cue for an aversive event. Rather than functioning as a positive conditioned emotional response (CER), it has become a signal that triggers anticipatory distress. This explains worsening separation anxiety.

Second, the hand-feeding has inadvertently created a **resource guarding context around the Kong**. The dog may have learned that the Kong represents a high-value, scarce resource (only available at departures), and the owner's approach during preparation now predicts both departure and access to that resource. Growling during Kong preparation is the dog communicating conflict: the Kong is wanted but also associated with fear and loss.

**Revised approach:**
- **Decouple the Kong from departures entirely.** For several weeks, give the Kong at random times throughout the day when the owner is home and staying home. This breaks the predictive relationship.
- **Rebuild the departure protocol from scratch** using a different high-value item (or nothing) as the departure marker, ensuring it does not become a conditioned fear cue.
- **Address the resource guarding separately** using a systematic trade-up protocol. Do not use hand-feeding as a guarding intervention without a structured plan — proximity during resource possession can worsen guarding if not handled deliberately.
- Consult a veterinary behaviorist, as the dog now has two interacting behavioral challenges that require careful sequencing. Treating both simultaneously with an owner who has already introduced confounding variables warrants professional oversight.
