## Exercises

**Exercise 1**
You want to teach your dog a retrieve-to-hand chain: find object → pick it up → carry it → deliver to hand. You start by reinforcing the dog for picking up the object, but when you add the delivery step, the dog drops the object before reaching you. What does this breakdown tell you about how the chain was built, and what would you do to fix it?

**Exercise 2**
A trainer builds a "go to mat and lie down" chain by teaching the down first, then the mat approach, then linking them. Another trainer teaches the mat approach first, then adds the down. Both dogs learn the chain, but one dog is more likely to anticipate the down before fully reaching the mat. Which training sequence produced this problem, and why?

**Exercise 3**
You are teaching a dog a three-behavior chain: sit → shake → spin. The dog performs each behavior perfectly in isolation, but when chained together, the middle behavior (shake) becomes sloppy and rushed. Using your understanding of how reinforcement flows through a chain, explain why the middle position is particularly vulnerable to degradation and what you would do to strengthen it.

**Exercise 4**
A service dog must perform: pick up dropped item → bring it to owner → place it in owner's hand → hold until released. During real-world practice (outside the training room), the dog completes the first three steps but consistently releases the item too early in the final step. The dog performs the full chain flawlessly in training sessions. What factors specific to behavior chains could explain this real-world failure, and how would you address it?

**Exercise 5**
You have a dog that has learned both a behavior chain (fetch → retrieve → drop-it) and a solid understanding of a differential reinforcement strategy that rewards only the best responses. During chain maintenance, you notice the fetch portion is deteriorating — the dog approaches the toy slowly and sniffs it before picking it up. A fellow trainer suggests applying differential reinforcement to the fetch step to sharpen it back up. Evaluate this suggestion: what are the risks and benefits of applying differential reinforcement within an established behavior chain, and what would you recommend instead or in addition?

**Exercise 6**
Design a training plan for a six-step agility-style chain for a dog who already knows each individual behavior: tunnel → jump → weave poles → pause table → jump → contact obstacle. The dog has no prior experience having these behaviors linked. Using principles of behavior chains, reinforcement scheduling, and strategies from prior topics in this tier, outline the sequence of steps you would take to build a fluent, reliable chain. Justify each major decision.

## Answer Key

**Answer 1**
The breakdown reveals that the two behaviors — pickup and delivery — were not yet properly linked as a chain; the reinforcement history stopped at the pickup, making that the terminal behavior (the point where the dog expects the reward). When delivery was added, the pickup remained the strongest conditioned endpoint, so the dog releases the object there.

Fix: Use backchaining. Reinforce the delivery step first (dog already holding object, trainer's hand nearby), then extend backward — add the carry, then the pickup. This means every repetition ends at the strongest link (delivery), and earlier behaviors lead toward that known payoff rather than away from it.

**Answer 2**
The trainer who taught the down first, then the mat approach, created the problem. When down was trained first, it accumulated the most reinforcement history and became the dog's dominant expectation. When the mat approach was added later, the dog learned "movement toward mat predicts the down," which triggers anticipation of the down before the mat is fully reached.

The trainer who started with the mat approach established a cleaner positional criterion before introducing the down, reducing the likelihood of anticipatory responding. Sequence matters because whichever behavior has the strongest prior reinforcement history tends to intrude into new contexts.

**Answer 3**
In a behavior chain, reinforcement flows from the end backward — the final behavior is directly reinforced, and each preceding behavior derives its value from predicting the next step toward that reinforcement. The middle position is vulnerable because it is neither the first behavior (which has the novelty and trainer attention at chain onset) nor the last (which is directly reinforced). It functions purely as an intermediate conditioned reinforcer, with weaker independent reinforcement history.

To strengthen it: temporarily "break" the chain at the shake step, reinforce it directly and independently several times, then re-embed it in partial chains (shake → spin, then sit → shake → spin). You may also use a bridging signal specifically at the shake to mark it clearly as a valued step.

**Answer 4**
Two chain-specific factors are likely at work. First, generalization failure: the chain was rehearsed primarily in one environment, so its discriminative stimuli (cues, context, body position) are narrowly conditioned. Outside the training room, subtle contextual differences weaken stimulus control, and the least-rehearsed or most recently added link (the hold/release) is the first to break down.

Second, the final link is where duration criteria apply, and duration is the hardest criterion to maintain under distraction or novel environments. The dog's threshold for releasing drops when arousal or distraction increases.

Address it by systematically proofing the chain in progressively varied environments before adding distractions. Use a strong, consistent release cue to clarify the hold endpoint. Also rehearse the hold step in isolation in new environments before re-embedding it in the full chain.

**Answer 5**
The suggestion carries real risk. Applying differential reinforcement within an established chain can disrupt chain fluency because it introduces variable reinforcement at an intermediate step rather than at the terminal behavior. If the dog is now sometimes reinforced for the fetch and sometimes not, the predictive value of fetch (as a conditioned reinforcer signaling the rest of the chain) is weakened. The dog may pause, check in, or become uncertain rather than flowing into the retrieve.

Benefits: differential reinforcement could sharpen the fetch criterion if applied carefully in isolation.

Recommendation: first remove the fetch step from the chain and work it in isolation using differential reinforcement to restore quality. Once the behavior is sharp again in isolation, reintegrate it into the chain gradually, ensuring the chain itself flows uninterrupted to terminal reinforcement. Avoid using differential reinforcement mid-chain during maintenance; instead, use it in isolation and keep the chain itself on a consistent reinforcement schedule to preserve fluency.

**Answer 6**
**Step 1 — Confirm individual behaviors.** Verify each of the six behaviors is fluent and on a reliable cue in the training environment. Any weak link will degrade faster under chain pressure.

**Step 2 — Backchain from the end.** Start with the contact obstacle alone, reinforce it heavily. Then add jump → contact obstacle; rehearse until smooth. Continue adding links backward (pause table → jump → contact; weave poles → pause table → jump → contact; etc.) until the full chain is linked. Justification: backchaining ensures each new step leads toward the portion of the chain with the strongest reinforcement history, maintaining motivation throughout.

**Step 3 — Use a high rate of reinforcement during chain construction.** At each stage, reinforce the partial chain on a continuous or near-continuous schedule. Justification: the chain is still fragile; withholding reinforcement too early risks extinction of intermediate links.

**Step 4 — Thin reinforcement gradually once the full chain is fluent.** Shift to a variable ratio schedule for the complete chain once the dog runs it smoothly. Justification: variable reinforcement increases resistance to extinction and maintains engagement, a key principle from advanced reinforcement strategies.

**Step 5 — Proof in novel environments.** Run the chain in progressively different settings, starting with minor changes (different time of day, different tunnel color) and building toward fully novel locations. Address any breakdowns by temporarily re-inserting reinforcement at the failing link.

**Step 6 — Maintain with periodic partial reinforcement and individual behavior "tune-ups."** Monitor for degradation at middle links (particularly weave poles and pause table) and reinforce those in isolation when needed before re-embedding them in the full chain.
