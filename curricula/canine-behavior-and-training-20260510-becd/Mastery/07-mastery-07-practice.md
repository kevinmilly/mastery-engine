## Exercises

**Exercise 1**
A family has two dogs: a 4-year-old resident dog and a newly adopted 1-year-old. Every time the owner brings out a single food bowl, the older dog stiffens, stares at the younger one, and eats rapidly. The younger dog retreats to another room. What does this behavioral pattern tell you about the current feeding arrangement, and what specific change would you recommend first?

**Exercise 2**
Two dogs in the same household greet each other enthusiastically every time the owner returns home — jumping, barking, and spinning in tight circles near the door. Within 30 seconds, this escalates to snapping between them. The owner assumes they are "just excited." What is actually happening, and why is the owner's interpretation a problem for management?

**Exercise 3**
You are consulting on a household with three dogs. The owners report that conflict only happens "out of nowhere" — usually in the hallway or at the top of the stairs. There has been no food or toy present during any of the incidents. What environmental hypothesis would you form, and what would you look for to confirm or rule it out?

**Exercise 4**
A trainer is working with two dogs who have a history of fighting over the owner's attention. The trainer begins a protocol where both dogs are rewarded simultaneously when they sit calmly side by side near the owner. After two weeks, the dogs perform the sit reliably in training sessions but still compete for the owner's lap on the couch each evening. Why might the behavior chain be failing to transfer, and what would you adjust?

**Exercise 5**
A household has a dog with a documented history of resource guarding (assessed and partially mitigated through a prior behavior modification program) and a newly introduced dog. The resident dog has been desensitized to humans approaching its bowl but has had no exposure to another dog near its resources. Design the first three stages of an introduction and management protocol, integrating what you know about resource guarding mitigation and multi-dog dynamics. Justify the sequencing.

**Exercise 6**
An owner describes the following situation: their two dogs lived peacefully for three years, but after one dog returned from a five-day veterinary hospitalization, the other dog began attacking it on sight. The owner has tried scolding the aggressor and separating them for a day, then reintroducing them — with no improvement. Drawing on behavioral principles from this topic and prior topics in the tier, explain what is most likely driving the aggression, why the owner's interventions are insufficient, and outline a more appropriate intervention approach.

---

## Answer Key

**Answer 1**
The pattern indicates active resource guarding by the older dog and appeasement or avoidance by the younger dog — the younger dog has already learned that proximity to the food area is unsafe. The rapid eating and stiff posture are stress signals preceding potential conflict. The first recommended change is to feed the dogs in completely separate locations (different rooms with closed doors or physical barriers), removing the competitive context entirely before attempting any behavior modification. This eliminates the trigger rather than managing the reaction, which is the appropriate starting point before more complex protocols are introduced.

**Answer 2**
The escalation is a classic case of redirected arousal or frustration-based aggression. The dogs are not fighting *at each other* — they are in a highly aroused state triggered by the owner's arrival, and that arousal has no outlet, so it gets redirected onto the nearest moving target (the other dog). The owner's interpretation is problematic because labeling it "excitement" frames the behavior as benign and reduces the perceived urgency of intervention. It also encourages the owner to allow the greeting ritual to continue, which rehearses the arousal-escalation-snap sequence, making it more likely to occur and more entrenched over time.

**Answer 3**
The hypothesis is spatial competition over a bottleneck resource — narrow, high-traffic areas (hallways, stairwells) create forced proximity with no escape route, which can trigger conflict even without obvious resources present. The location is itself the trigger because it limits a dog's ability to create distance. To confirm: map all incidents by location and note whether they share spatial characteristics (narrow, enclosed, one exit). Rule out other causes by checking whether conflicts also occur in open spaces, and whether any dog shows anxiety-related behaviors (panting, lip-licking) when approaching those specific areas independently.

**Answer 4**
The behavior chain was trained in a controlled, low-distraction context where both dogs were cued and rewarded simultaneously — a structured setup that doesn't generalize to the couch scenario. On the couch, the owner has not set up the same antecedent conditions: there is no cue, no simultaneous reinforcement delivery, and the couch itself is a higher-value resource than the open floor used in training. The dogs default to their previously reinforced competing behavior because the new behavior chain hasn't been proofed in that context. Adjustments should include: (1) applying the same structured cue before the dogs approach the couch, (2) reinforcing calm proximity specifically in the couch context using graduated approximations, and (3) managing access to the couch until the behavior chain is reliable there.

**Answer 5**
**Stage 1 — Parallel separate feeding and activity, no shared space.** Before any introduction, ensure the newly introduced dog has no access to the resident dog's resource zones. Both dogs are fed, given toys, and receive owner attention fully separated. This establishes a baseline of safety and prevents the newly introduced dog from immediately triggering the resident dog's guarding threshold. Jumping straight to proximity would skip the desensitization hierarchy established in the prior guarding mitigation work.

**Stage 2 — Visual exposure at distance with positive reinforcement.** Introduce the dogs to each other's presence (through a baby gate or across a room) while each is engaged with their own low-value resource. Reinforce calm, non-reactive behavior in both dogs. This extends the resident dog's existing desensitization (previously only to humans) into the inter-dog context, starting well below threshold.

**Stage 3 — Parallel feeding at a safe distance in the same room.** Place both food bowls far apart in the same room, allowing both dogs to eat simultaneously without proximity. Gradually decrease distance across sessions only when both dogs remain relaxed. This mirrors the systematic desensitization approach used in resource guarding mitigation, now applied to the inter-dog context specifically. Sequencing matters because skipping Stage 1 or 2 would expose the resident dog to the new dog before a new safe association is built, likely reinstating guarding behaviors at full intensity.

**Answer 6**
The most likely mechanism is **inter-dog aggression driven by non-recognition or olfactory change** — the returning dog smells different (veterinary odors, stress hormones, medications) and the resident dog responds as if encountering a stranger or a threat signal. This is sometimes called "vet smell" aggression. The owner's interventions fail for two reasons: (1) **Scolding the aggressor** applies positive punishment without addressing the underlying trigger — the resident dog's threat response to the altered smell — and may add conditioned fear or conflict around the returning dog, increasing rather than decreasing aggression. (2) **Separating and re-introducing after one day** does not allow the returning dog's scent to normalize, so the trigger is still present at each reintroduction. A more appropriate approach: keep the dogs fully separated while allowing gradual scent exchange (swapping bedding, feeding on opposite sides of a door) to rebuild positive or neutral associations with each other's odor before any visual or physical contact. Reintroduce using the same structured neutral-ground protocol used for initial introductions — parallel walks, no forced face-to-face contact — and reinforce calm responses. The prior work on leash reactivity and reinforcement strategies is directly applicable here: treat each reintroduction session as a counter-conditioning opportunity, building a positive association with the other dog's presence below the threshold for reactive behavior.
