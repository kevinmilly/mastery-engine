## Exercises

**Exercise 1**
A dog begins barking and lunging at other dogs when they appear within 15 feet, but remains calm at 40 feet. You are beginning a desensitization program. At what distance should you start your training sessions, and why does this specific threshold matter for the protocol to work?

**Exercise 2**
During a leash reactivity assessment, you observe that a dog reacts to some dogs but not others — specifically, intact males trigger a reaction while spayed females do not. How does this information change your management and training approach compared to treating all dogs as equivalent triggers?

**Exercise 3**
A trainer is working with a reactive dog using counter-conditioning. After six weeks, the dog reliably looks at the owner for treats when a dog appears at threshold distance. The owner reports that last Tuesday the dog suddenly "exploded" after a dog appeared from around a corner at close range. The owner concludes the training has failed. Evaluate this conclusion. What is actually happening, and what should the trainer do next?

**Exercise 4**
You are designing a behavior modification plan for a dog with leash reactivity. You decide to use a "look at that" (LAT) protocol rather than simply redirecting the dog away from the trigger every time it appears. What is the functional difference between these two approaches, and under what circumstances would each be appropriate?

**Exercise 5**
A client has been building a reliable "watch me" cue using high-value reinforcement chains (treat → praise → play sequence) as covered in prior training. They want to use this cue as a primary tool for managing their dog's leash reactivity. Explain the strengths and limitations of this approach, and describe how you would integrate it into a complete reactivity protocol rather than relying on it alone.

**Exercise 6**
You are working with a dog whose leash reactivity has worsened over the past year despite the owner's attempts to redirect and comfort the dog. The owner walks the dog daily in a busy neighborhood, and reactive incidents happen almost every walk. Design a realistic short-term management and medium-term modification plan. Your answer must address: why the current approach is likely making things worse, what immediate changes are needed before behavior modification can begin, and how prior-learned concepts (reinforcement strategies, behavior chains) will be applied in the modification phase.

## Answer Key

**Answer 1**
Training should begin below threshold — the distance at which the dog notices the trigger but does not react (in this case, beyond 15 feet, ideally closer to 30–40 feet to build a reliable history of success). The threshold distance matters because desensitization requires the dog to remain in a calm, learnable state. If you begin at or inside the reactive threshold, you are exposing the dog to a stimulus intensity it cannot cope with, which does not produce learning — it produces rehearsal of the reactive response. Each reactive episode also risks sensitization, raising the threshold further. Starting at sub-threshold distance allows the dog to form new associations (trigger predicts good things) while the emotional system remains regulated.

**Answer 2**
This information narrows the trigger profile significantly, which changes both management and training. For management: the owner can make better real-time decisions about which dogs to avoid or create distance from, rather than treating every dog encounter as equally risky. For training: the modification work should be built around the specific trigger class (intact males) rather than generic dog stimuli. Using neutral or female dogs as training helpers early in desensitization is appropriate to build confidence and correct associations, but the protocol must eventually be generalized to intact males — the true trigger — at controlled sub-threshold distances. Ignoring trigger specificity would mean the dog never gets the targeted exposure it needs and the owner never develops accurate predictive judgment.

**Answer 3**
The conclusion that training has failed is incorrect. What happened is a threshold breach caused by an unexpected close-proximity exposure — a management failure, not a training failure. The six weeks of progress reflect genuine counter-conditioning at a distance the dog can handle. The corner incident bypassed the dog's learned coping capacity because it presented the trigger inside the reactive threshold with no warning. This is sometimes called "trigger stacking" if combined with prior arousal, or simply flooding if the intensity was too high to process. The trainer should: (1) reinforce management protocols to prevent close encounters while the dog's emotional response is still being remodeled; (2) not interpret the incident as evidence the counter-conditioning has been undone — one breach does not erase weeks of sub-threshold work; (3) continue systematic desensitization, perhaps slightly increasing threshold distance temporarily to rebuild confidence.

**Answer 4**
Redirecting the dog away from the trigger suppresses the visible reaction but does not change the dog's underlying emotional response to the trigger — the dog learns "look away" as a behavior, not "that dog predicts something good." LAT, by contrast, teaches the dog to orient toward the trigger as a conditioned cue to look back at the handler for reinforcement. The functional difference is that LAT transforms the emotional valence of the trigger (classical counter-conditioning) while redirect-only approaches manage behavior without addressing emotion. LAT is appropriate when the dog is sub-threshold and capable of voluntary orientation, and the goal is long-term emotional change. Redirect is appropriate as an emergency management tool when a dog is already at or near threshold and you need to interrupt escalation — it preserves safety but should not be the primary modification tool, as repeated use without counter-conditioning leaves the underlying reactivity unchanged.

**Answer 5**
Strengths: A well-trained "watch me" backed by a reinforcement chain (as covered in behavior chain work) gives the dog a clear alternative behavior and a strong history of reinforcement for orienting toward the owner. It can interrupt arousal early if cued before the dog reaches threshold, and the chained reward sequence maintains high motivation. Limitations: "Watch me" is an operant behavior — it requires the dog to make a choice and execute a response. Under high arousal or when the dog is at or past threshold, operant responding degrades because the emotional system overrides the learned behavior. The cue will fail precisely when it's most needed if the dog has not been counter-conditioned to have a neutral or positive emotional response to the trigger itself. Integration into a complete protocol: use "watch me" as a bridging and engagement tool at sub-threshold distances while simultaneously running LAT and counter-conditioning to change the trigger's emotional meaning. The behavior chain can be used to mark and reward calm noticing of a trigger, not just eye contact. As threshold distance decreases through systematic desensitization, "watch me" becomes increasingly reliable because the emotional response to the trigger is no longer competing with it.

**Answer 6**
**Why the current approach is worsening the problem:** Daily walks in a high-trigger environment with near-certain reactive incidents mean the dog is rehearsing the reactive response repeatedly. Each rehearsal strengthens the behavior pattern and may sensitize the dog further. Redirecting and comforting during or after reactions can inadvertently reinforce the emotional state or the behavior, depending on timing. The dog is never in a state calm enough for learning to occur.

**Immediate management changes:** (1) Temporarily modify the walk route or schedule to dramatically reduce trigger encounters — early morning walks, low-traffic areas, or driving to quieter locations. (2) Increase distance from triggers at all costs; use physical barriers (cars, buildings) as visual blocks when needed. (3) Introduce a structured "exit strategy" — a practiced U-turn or emergency retreat cue — so the owner has a rehearsed response before reactive incidents occur, not during. The goal of this phase is to stop the rehearsal cycle and give the nervous system a period of lower arousal.

**Medium-term modification plan:** Once the dog is having fewer reactive incidents, begin formal desensitization and counter-conditioning at established sub-threshold distance using high-value, novel reinforcers. Apply LAT protocol to begin shifting the trigger's emotional valence. Introduce the reinforcement chain (from prior tier work) to reward calm engagement sequences — not just single treats — so the dog builds a robust history of sustained calm behavior in the presence of distant triggers. Gradually decrease distance only when the dog is offering relaxed, voluntary orientation without prompting, not on a fixed schedule. "Watch me" and other operant cues learned in prior training are integrated as supplemental engagement tools once the emotional baseline improves, not as the primary intervention. Progress is measured by threshold distance decrease and spontaneous calm behavior — not by absence of incidents alone.
