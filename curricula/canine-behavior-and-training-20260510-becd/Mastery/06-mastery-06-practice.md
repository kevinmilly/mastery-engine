## Exercises

**Exercise 1**
A trainer uses a prong collar to stop a dog from jumping on guests. The jumping stops immediately and does not return. A colleague argues this is ethical because it "worked." What is missing from this analysis, and why does short-term suppression of a behavior not constitute ethical justification?

**Exercise 2**
A client asks you to use an e-collar to stop their dog from barking at other dogs on walks. Before deciding on any approach, you conduct a welfare assessment. List the specific domains of welfare you would evaluate and what you would look for in each to determine whether aversive intervention is contraindicated.

**Exercise 3**
A shelter dog is displaying resource guarding over food bowls. A staff member proposes alpha rolls and leash corrections to "establish dominance" and stop the guarding. Using what you know about both resource guarding behavior and ethical training frameworks, explain why this approach is likely to produce a worse outcome than a reinforcement-based protocol — and what the worse outcome might look like.

**Exercise 4**
A dog undergoing desensitization for leash reactivity suddenly begins showing whale eyes, lip licking, and attempts to flee when the trainer approaches with the leash. The trainer interprets this as "stubbornness" and proceeds with the session. Identify the ethical failure here, explain what the signals actually indicate, and describe what a humane practitioner should do instead.

**Exercise 5**
A trainer experienced in behavior chains is designing an intervention for a dog with separation-related distress. The owner has seen videos of "flooding" used to treat separation anxiety — simply locking the dog alone for increasing durations — and is asking the trainer to use this approach because "it worked for a neighbor's dog." Construct a response that addresses the ethical problems with flooding, explains the mechanism of harm using learning theory, and proposes an evidence-based alternative that integrates what you know about separation behavior and humane modification.

**Exercise 6**
You are supervising a junior trainer who is working with a dog that has a history of aversive training and now shows learned helplessness — the dog performs behaviors but shows flat affect, no anticipatory behavior, and no engagement with the trainer. The junior trainer says: "At least he's compliant. Isn't that the goal?" Write a critique of this position that draws on welfare ethics, the distinction between behavioral compliance and psychological wellbeing, and what a genuine mastery-level outcome should look like. Then outline the first three steps of a rehabilitation plan.

## Answer Key

**Answer 1**
The analysis fails because behavioral outcome (suppression) is not the same as ethical justification. Several critical factors are missing: (1) **Mechanism of suppression** — did the behavior stop because the dog learned jumping leads to pain/fear, rather than learning an incompatible behavior? Suppression through aversion does not teach the dog what to do instead. (2) **Side effects** — aversive tools can produce fallout: fear of the trainer, of guests, or of the context in which punishment occurred, even when the target behavior stops. (3) **Emotional state** — the dog's internal experience is not captured by behavior alone. A dog that has stopped jumping but is now anxious around guests has not been helped. (4) **Long-term welfare trajectory** — short-term suppression can mask unresolved arousal or fear that re-emerges in different forms. Ethical evaluation requires asking not just "did it stop?" but "at what cost to the animal's welfare, and was a less aversive alternative attempted first?"

**Answer 2**
A welfare assessment before any aversive intervention should examine the Five Domains (or comparable framework): (1) **Nutrition** — is the dog in a healthy physical state that supports learning? Hunger or illness affects baseline behavior. (2) **Physical environment** — is the dog exposed to chronic stressors (noise, confinement) that elevate baseline arousal and could interact with any aversive stimulus? (3) **Health** — pain (e.g., neck, spine) would make collar-based tools especially harmful and could itself be driving the reactivity. (4) **Behavioral expression** — has the dog's ability to engage in normal behavior been restricted, elevating frustration? (5) **Mental state** — what is the dog's baseline anxiety level? Dogs with high baseline fear or arousal are at greater risk of sensitization rather than desensitization from aversives. Contraindications for e-collar use would include: existing fear-based reactivity (aversion risks increasing arousal), pain history, unknown trigger threshold, or no prior positive reinforcement foundation.

**Answer 3**
Resource guarding is maintained by a contingency: the dog guards because guarding has historically kept valued resources safe. Alpha rolls and corrections do not address this contingency — they introduce a competing threat (the human) which, from the dog's perspective, *confirms* that the approach of humans near food is dangerous. This is likely to: (a) suppress overt guarding signals (growling, stiffening) while leaving the underlying motivation intact — creating a dog that bites without warning because warning signals were punished away; (b) increase the dog's overall arousal and threat-sensitivity around resources; (c) damage trust in the handler, which undermines any future behavior modification work. The ethical and effective alternative is a counter-conditioning protocol that changes the dog's emotional association with human approach near food from "threat" to "predicts good things," gradually rebuilding the contingency in the dog's favor. This addresses the root cause rather than suppressing the symptom.

**Answer 4**
The ethical failure is ignoring clear stress signals and proceeding with a protocol the dog is not ready for. Whale eyes, lip licking, and escape attempts are displacement and appeasement behaviors indicating the dog is over threshold — the current stimulus intensity exceeds what the dog can process without distress. Proceeding at this point is not desensitization; it is sensitization or flooding, which can worsen the conditioned fear response and erode trust in the handler. A humane practitioner should: (1) immediately end the session and remove the stressor; (2) reassess the desensitization hierarchy — the current step is clearly too steep; (3) identify what sub-threshold presentation (e.g., leash visible at a distance, or trainer holding leash without moving toward dog) the dog can tolerate without stress signals; (4) resume only at that sub-threshold point in a future session; (5) document the signals and adjust the protocol to ensure progression is genuinely gradual.

**Answer 5**
**Ethical problems with flooding:** Flooding (response prevention at full intensity) violates the principle of least intrusive, minimally aversive intervention. It exposes the dog to sustained high-level distress without an escape route, which, if extinction does not occur quickly, produces learned helplessness or sensitization rather than habituation. The dog cannot consent to or escape the stimulus, which is a direct welfare harm regardless of outcome. Even in cases where flooding "works," the emotional cost — sustained fear, panic, cortisol elevation — is ethically unjustifiable when graduated alternatives exist.

**Learning theory mechanism of harm:** Flooding works, when it does, via extinction of the fear response through prolonged non-reinforced exposure. But if the dog's arousal escalates faster than extinction occurs, the experience is simply aversive conditioning at high intensity — associating separation with extreme distress. This can strengthen, not weaken, the conditioned emotional response to owner departure.

**Evidence-based alternative:** Systematic desensitization combined with counter-conditioning for separation-related behavior: (1) Establish a reliable pre-departure cue baseline — identify what triggers anticipatory anxiety; (2) Begin with sub-threshold absences (seconds, not minutes) at an intensity where the dog remains below anxious arousal; (3) Build a reinforcement history for calm behavior during very brief separations using food-stuffed enrichment, never progressing faster than the dog's threshold allows; (4) Address the departure cue sequence through desensitization to individual cues (picking up keys, putting on shoes) before full absences; (5) Use owner-absence recording to monitor behavior between sessions.

**Answer 6**
**Critique:** "Compliance" as a goal confuses behavioral output with welfare. A dog exhibiting learned helplessness has not been trained — it has been conditioned to stop attempting any behavior because its actions have had no reliable positive consequence. The flat affect and absence of engagement are diagnostic of chronic aversive exposure that has suppressed not just unwanted behavior but all motivated behavior. This is not a training success; it is a welfare failure. Mastery-level outcomes require not just that a dog performs behaviors but that it does so with positive emotional engagement — approach behavior, anticipatory arousal, voluntary participation. A dog that works for food with enthusiasm, offers behaviors, and shows recovery behavior after errors is behaviorally healthy. A compliant but affectless dog is not.

**Rehabilitation plan — first three steps:**
1. **Remove all aversive stimuli and pressure** — no corrections, no demands. Create a predictable, low-stress environment where the dog can decompress. Duration: at minimum several days to weeks depending on history.
2. **Rebuild a reinforcement history using free operant shaping** — present a clicker or marker and reward any voluntary movement or offered behavior, no matter how small. The goal is to restore the dog's belief that its actions produce good outcomes (re-establishing operant contingency awareness). Start with low-stakes, low-arousal settings.
3. **Introduce choice and agency** — use consent-based handling, allow the dog to approach or disengage from interactions freely, and reinforce approach. This rebuilds trust and begins to restore positive emotional association with the trainer and the training context.
