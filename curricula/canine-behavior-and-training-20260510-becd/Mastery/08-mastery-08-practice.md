## Exercises

**Exercise 1**
A trainer receives a referral for a dog displaying "aggression." Before designing any intervention, what are the four categories of information the trainer must gather during the initial assessment, and why is each category necessary for accurate case formulation?

**Exercise 2**
A dog has been assessed and found to lunge and bark at other dogs only when on leash, never when off leash at the dog park. The owner reports the behavior started six months ago after a dog attacked their dog during a walk. Walk through the steps of a structured problem-solving framework to move from this presenting complaint to a working hypothesis about root cause.

**Exercise 3**
A trainer implements a desensitization and counter-conditioning protocol for a dog with leash reactivity. After four weeks, the dog shows no improvement and appears to be getting worse. The owner reports they have been "practicing" by walking the dog past the local dog park every morning. Using a diagnostic framework, identify the most likely reasons the protocol is failing and explain what assessment questions you would ask before modifying the plan.

**Exercise 4**
You are presented with a dog in a multi-dog household where Dog A (a 4-year-old male) has begun resource guarding food bowls and resting spots from Dog B (a 2-year-old female), a behavior that emerged three months ago with no clear triggering event the owner can identify. Using a structured case analysis approach, identify at least three hypotheses for why the behavior emerged at this time and describe how you would test each hypothesis before designing an intervention.

**Exercise 5**
A trainer has been working with a dog exhibiting separation-related behavior. The initial plan (graduated departures, independence training, and enrichment) showed early promise but stalled. The owner now reports the dog is also showing resource guarding toward the owner's new partner, who moved in two months ago — roughly when the separation anxiety progress plateaued. Synthesize what you know about separation-related behavior, resource guarding, and multi-dog/multi-person household dynamics to develop a revised, integrated intervention plan. Justify why addressing these as interconnected rather than separate cases matters.

**Exercise 6**
A behaviorist colleague presents you with an ethically complex case: a dog with a documented bite history (two on-leash bites to strangers) is being managed with a prong collar by the owner, who refuses to remove it. The dog has not bitten in eight months under this management. The owner is asking for help "fixing" the behavior so the dog can eventually be off-leash at public parks. Using your problem-solving framework and the ethical principles governing behavior modification, describe how you would approach this consultation — including what you would and would not agree to, and why.

## Answer Key

**Answer 1**
The four categories are:

1. **Medical/physical history** — Pain, neurological conditions, hormonal imbalances (e.g., hypothyroidism), or medication effects can directly produce or exacerbate behavioral signs. Skipping this can lead to behavioral interventions that fail because an underlying physical cause is driving the behavior.

2. **Learning history** — What has the dog been exposed to, trained with, and reinforced for? What aversive experiences has it had? This determines what behavioral patterns have been conditioned and what associations exist, shaping the design of any modification protocol.

3. **Environmental and contextual factors** — Where, when, and under what conditions does the behavior occur? Triggers, antecedents, and setting events (like sleep deprivation, prior exercise, or household stressors) determine function and inform management strategies.

4. **Owner/handler variables** — Owner consistency, emotional responses to the behavior, current management practices, and training history affect both the dog's behavior and the realistic scope of any intervention. A technically sound plan that the owner cannot execute is not a useful plan.

Each category is necessary because behavior is multiply determined. Omitting any one can produce a misdiagnosis — for example, attributing to anxiety what is actually pain-related aggression, or designing a protocol that assumes owner consistency that doesn't exist.

**Answer 2**
A structured walkthrough:

1. **Define the behavior precisely** — On-leash lunging and barking directed at other dogs; absent off leash. This specificity rules out generalized dog-dog aggression and points toward a leash-specific variable.

2. **Identify the antecedent** — Other dogs in proximity while on leash. The onset correlates with a dog attack six months ago, suggesting a classical conditioning event (attack = aversive US → other dogs on leash = CS predicting danger).

3. **Analyze the function** — The behavior is most consistent with fear-based reactivity. The leash removes the dog's ability to flee (escape behavior is blocked), so the dog escalates to agonistic display. Off-leash, the dog retains flight as an option, which is why behavior differs.

4. **Consider maintaining factors** — Each time the dog lunges and the other dog passes, the threat goes away. This negatively reinforces lunging. The behavior is self-sustaining regardless of its origin.

5. **Working hypothesis** — The dog developed a conditioned fear response to on-leash encounters with dogs following a traumatic attack. The leash creates a constraint that removes flight, triggering a fight-or-flight display that is maintained by negative reinforcement.

This hypothesis directly predicts what a successful intervention must target: the conditioned emotional response to dogs while on leash, plus restoration of a coping strategy (distance) the dog cannot currently access.

**Answer 3**
Diagnostic questions and likely failure modes:

**Most likely reason the protocol is failing:** The owner is conducting "practice" sessions at a dog park, which almost certainly means the dog is being exposed above threshold repeatedly. Desensitization requires sub-threshold exposure; if the dog is reacting during sessions, counter-conditioning is not occurring — the CS (other dogs) continues to predict the original emotional state, or is now also predicting the aversive experience of being flooded.

**Assessment questions to ask:**
- What does "practicing" look like? How close to the park does the dog get before reacting?
- Is the dog eating treats during these sessions, or too aroused to take food?
- What is the dog's body language at the start of the walk versus near the park?
- Has anything else changed — new stressors at home, changes in exercise, medication?

**Diagnostic conclusion:** The protocol isn't failing because it's wrong; it's likely failing because the stimulus gradient was never established correctly, and the owner's "practice" is actually repeated over-threshold exposure. Before modifying the plan, the trainer must re-establish the dog's threshold distance, redesign the owner's practice structure, and assess whether the dog's overall stress load (cumulative stress) has increased due to repeated flooding.

**Answer 4**
Three testable hypotheses:

1. **Age/social maturity** — Dogs often reach social maturity between 18 months and 3 years. Dog A at age 4 may have recently completed social maturation, leading to increased competition for status and resources. **Test:** Review whether any other competitive behaviors (posturing, space-taking) have emerged in the same timeframe, not just guarding.

2. **Underlying medical change in Dog A** — Pain or illness can increase irritability and resource-guarding behavior. If Dog A had a physical change three months ago (even subtle), this could explain the new behavior. **Test:** Recommend a full veterinary workup including pain assessment before behavioral intervention.

3. **Environmental change three months ago** — Even if the owner cannot identify it, something changed. This could include a change in feeding routine, furniture arrangement, addition of new items (beds, bowls), reduced exercise, or increased time home alone. **Test:** Walk the owner through a detailed timeline — "What was different in February versus now?" Ask specifically about feeding location, access to sleeping spots, and daily schedule changes.

**Why this matters:** If the hypothesis is social maturity, management focuses on structure and resource control. If it's medical, the primary intervention is veterinary. If it's environmental, the fix may be as simple as changing feeding logistics. Designing an intervention without testing hypotheses risks treating the wrong cause.

**Answer 5**
These cases are interconnected, not parallel, because both involve the dog's sense of security and predictability in the attachment environment. The new partner's arrival changed the household's social structure — the dog's primary attachment figure now shares attention, space, and possibly routine in ways the dog cannot predict or control. This likely increased the dog's overall anxiety load, which explains both the stalled separation progress (higher baseline arousal makes graduated departures harder) and the emergence of resource guarding toward the new person (threat to valued resources, including the owner).

**Revised integrated plan:**

1. **Pause graduated departures temporarily** — The dog's threshold for tolerable absence has likely dropped due to elevated baseline stress. Restarting at a lower intensity is more efficient than pushing through.

2. **Structured relationship-building with the new partner** — The partner should become a reliable, predictable source of good things: feeding, training sessions, play. This reduces the "threat" valence assigned to them and addresses the resource guarding at its emotional root rather than just managing the guarding behavior.

3. **Resource guarding management** — Trade-up protocols and spatial management during high-risk moments (meals, valued resting spots), run by both household members consistently. Do not use confrontational management (removing items without trade), which will increase stress.

4. **Independence training continues in parallel** — Short, daily exercises where the dog earns reinforcement for calm, settled behavior independently remain important and should not be abandoned.

5. **Cumulative stress tracking** — Treating these as one case means monitoring both symptom sets together. Progress in relationship with the partner should correlate with renewed progress in separation tolerance.

**Why integration matters:** Treating separation anxiety in isolation while the resource guarding escalates will undermine progress — both stem from a dog whose sense of security has been destabilized. Fixing one while ignoring the other means working against yourself.

**Answer 6**
This case requires separating what you can assess, what you can ethically assist with, and what you must decline.

**What to assess:**
Using the problem-solving framework: the dog has a documented bite history, is currently managed with a suppression tool (prong collar), is behaviorally stable under that management, and the owner is seeking a pathway to off-leash public access. The critical diagnostic question is whether the bites were fear-based, predatory, redirected, or pain-related — because the intervention and prognosis differ substantially, and the risk profile for off-leash access depends on this.

**What you would agree to:**
- Conducting a thorough behavioral assessment to establish the function of the aggression and current bite risk level.
- Providing honest, evidence-based information about prognosis for the specific goal (off-leash public access) given the bite history.
- Offering an alternative management and modification plan that does not rely on the prong collar if the owner is willing to discuss it, framed as a recommendation not a mandate.

**What you would not agree to:**
- Designing a protocol aimed at off-leash public access for a dog with two documented stranger bites without a realistic safety analysis. The goal as stated carries a foreseeable risk of harm to the public. This is an ethical boundary, not just a technical one.
- Continuing work if the owner refuses to consider any change in management and the prong collar is being used to suppress behavioral signals rather than support learning — suppression without modification increases unpredictability.

**Why this framing matters:**
Ethical practice requires being honest about what behavior modification can and cannot guarantee. A dog with a bite history can often be helped to have a better quality of life and reduced reactivity — but promising a path to unsupervised off-leash public access may be beyond what responsible practice can endorse. The trainer's role is to present that clearly, offer what is genuinely achievable, and let the owner make an informed decision.
