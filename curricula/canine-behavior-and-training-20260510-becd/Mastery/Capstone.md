## Capstone: Mastery — Canine Behavior and Training

### The Scenario

You have been hired as a behavior consultant by the Harmon family: two adults and a 10-year-old child. They own two dogs — **Biscuit**, a 4-year-old neutered male Labrador Retriever, and **Nova**, a 2-year-old intact female Border Collie mix recently adopted from a rescue eight weeks ago. Biscuit has lived with the family since puppyhood and was described as "easy" until Nova arrived. Nova came with a history of being surrendered twice, with notes indicating she was "too much energy" and "didn't get along with other dogs."

Since Nova's arrival, the household has deteriorated significantly. Biscuit has begun guarding his food bowl and a particular spot on the couch, growling when Nova approaches — behavior he never exhibited before. Nova displays intense leash reactivity toward other dogs on walks, lunging and barking to the point that the family's 10-year-old can no longer walk her safely. The family reports that Nova also destroys furniture and vocalizes for 2–3 hours after everyone leaves for work and school, though they are uncertain whether she is distressed or "just bored." The adults disagree on how to handle things: one wants to use a prong collar on Nova during walks because "it's the only thing that works," and the other wants to follow your guidance entirely. Both are exhausted and have mentioned they are considering rehoming Nova if things don't improve within 6–8 weeks.

You have completed an in-home consultation lasting 90 minutes. You observed Biscuit guard his bowl twice (stiffening and a low growl, no snap), saw Nova pull hard on leash and redirect toward Biscuit when frustrated, and noted that Nova showed no signs of separation distress during your visit but the family has shared a video of her behavior when alone. The video shows Nova pacing, whining, and scratching at the door continuously for the first 90 minutes after departure — with no settling — before falling asleep from exhaustion. Your job now is to produce a comprehensive written **Behavior Intervention Plan (BIP)** for the Harmon family.

---

### Your Tasks

1. **Triage and Prioritization.** Before writing the full plan, you must determine which behavioral issues to address first, second, and third — and justify that sequence. Explain what criteria you are using to prioritize (safety, severity, family capacity, interdependence of issues), and identify whether any issue requires an immediate management protocol before any behavior modification begins.

2. **Differential Diagnosis for Nova's Alone-Time Behavior.** Based on the video description provided, evaluate whether Nova's behavior is most consistent with separation anxiety, isolation distress, confinement distress, or boredom/under-stimulation. Specify which behavioral indicators from the description support or argue against each diagnosis, and describe what additional information or assessment steps you would use to confirm your working diagnosis before designing an intervention.

3. **Leash Reactivity Modification Plan.** Design a phased behavior modification protocol for Nova's leash reactivity. Your plan must specify the training technique(s) you are using and why, how you will set up the environment to manage threshold during early phases, how progression between phases is determined, and how you will adapt the plan given that a 10-year-old is one of the handlers. Address the prong collar proposal directly and provide a professional, evidence-based response to the family.

4. **Resource Guarding Management and Modification.** Design a two-stage approach for Biscuit's resource guarding: first, an immediate management protocol to prevent escalation in the multi-dog household, and second, a behavior modification plan to reduce the underlying motivation for guarding. Explain what reinforcement strategies from the Mastery tier are appropriate here and how you would implement them. Identify any conditions under which you would escalate this case to a veterinary behaviorist.

5. **Client Education and Realistic Expectation-Setting.** Write the core of what you would communicate to the Harmon family at the close of your consultation — specifically addressing the 6–8 week rehoming ultimatum, the disagreement between the two adults on training methodology, and how you will structure their role in the behavior modification process. Explain how you would handle the human behavior change component without alienating the adult who proposed the prong collar.

---

### What Good Work Looks Like

- **Prioritization reasoning is principled, not just intuitive** — strong responses name explicit criteria (e.g., safety risk, case interdependence, handler capacity) and show awareness that addressing issues in the wrong order can undermine progress or create new problems.
- **Differential diagnosis demonstrates clinical precision** — strong responses distinguish between conditions that look similar on the surface using specific behavioral indicators, avoid over-diagnosing, and identify what evidence would change the working diagnosis rather than treating one observation as definitive.
- **Behavior modification plans are operationally specific** — strong responses go beyond naming a technique (e.g., "counter-conditioning") to describe how it is set up, what the dog needs to demonstrate before advancing, and how the plan accounts for real-world constraints like handler skill and household chaos.
- **Ethical reasoning is integrated throughout, not siloed** — strong responses treat the prong collar question, the rehoming ultimatum, and the inter-adult disagreement as interconnected professional challenges that require both technical knowledge and communication skill, not just a policy statement.
- **The human system is treated as part of the case** — strong responses recognize that the family's consistency, emotional state, and divided methodology are behavioral variables just as much as the dogs' responses, and the plan reflects that.
