## Exercises

**Exercise 1**
A new client brings in their 3-year-old Labrador for pulling on leash. During the intake, you demonstrate loose-leash walking and the dog responds well immediately. The client says, "Great! So I just do that and he'll stop pulling?" What is the most important thing to clarify with this client at this moment, and why does failing to do so commonly undermine training outcomes?

**Exercise 2**
You are working with a family on reducing their dog's jumping on guests. The parents are applying the protocol consistently, but the teenage son thinks it's funny when the dog jumps and occasionally encourages it. The dog's jumping is not improving. Identify the core behavioral principle being violated and describe what you would say to the family to address it.

**Exercise 3**
A client reports that their dog "was doing great" with the sit-stay protocol for two weeks, but has "completely regressed" in the past few days. When you ask about changes, the client mentions they had houseguests who "let the dog do whatever." Explain what likely happened in behavioral terms, and describe how you would use this situation as a teaching moment with the client rather than a setback to simply fix.

**Exercise 4**
You are consulting with a client whose dog has been resource guarding food bowls. You design a counter-conditioning protocol and explain it in detail. At the follow-up session, the client admits they didn't do the exercises because they felt "cruel" approaching the dog during meals. How do you interpret this as a human behavior change problem rather than a compliance problem, and what would you adjust in your approach?

**Exercise 5**
A trainer has been working with a client on their dog's leash reactivity for six weeks using a combination of BAT and counter-conditioning. Progress has plateaued. In reviewing the case, the trainer suspects the client is inadvertently reinforcing anticipatory tension before walks — tightening the leash and talking nervously to the dog as soon as the harness goes on. The client is a high-anxiety person who is deeply attached to the dog and tends to catastrophize the dog's reactions. Drawing on both behavior chain analysis and what you know about client psychology, design a conversation strategy that addresses the human behavior pattern without alienating the client.

**Exercise 6**
You work with two clients in the same week who present nearly identical cases: both have adolescent dogs with impulse control problems, both have been given the same basic protocol, and both have shown slow progress. Client A lives alone, works from home, and engages enthusiastically with training but tends to over-train in long marathon sessions. Client B is a couple with young children, practices inconsistently due to schedule chaos, and has different household members using conflicting cues. Construct a differentiated intervention plan for each client that targets the specific human behavior change needed in each case. Explain why a one-size-fits-all approach would fail here, integrating your reasoning with what you know about reinforcement schedules and behavior chains.

## Answer Key

**Answer 1**
The most important thing to clarify is that the dog's behavior in session is not predictive of how quickly or reliably that behavior will transfer to the client's handling, environment, and daily routine. The client is conflating a demonstration with a trained and generalized behavior. What they witnessed was the dog responding to an experienced handler in a controlled setting — essentially a single unreinforced trial in a novel environment.

Failing to set accurate expectations here creates a specific problem: when the dog pulls on walk two days later (which is virtually certain), the client interprets it as failure — of the dog, the method, or themselves — rather than as a normal part of the learning process. This expectation mismatch is one of the most common reasons clients abandon protocols prematurely. The trainer should explain stimulus control, generalization, and the need for repetition across contexts, framed in plain language: "What you saw today is the starting point, not the finish line."

**Answer 2**
The core principle being violated is consistency of reinforcement contingencies. Specifically, the jumping behavior is on a variable reinforcement schedule because the son intermittently reinforces it. Variable ratio schedules produce highly resistant, persistent behavior — meaning the son's occasional reward for jumping is likely making the jumping more durable than if no one were intervening at all.

When addressing this with the family, the key is to make the behavioral principle concrete without blaming the son. A useful framing: "Right now the dog is getting mixed signals — sometimes jumping leads to nothing, and sometimes it leads to fun attention. That actually makes dogs try harder and longer, not less. For this to work, everyone in the house needs to respond the same way, every time." Involving the son in an alternative behavior (e.g., asking the dog to sit for attention) gives him a positive role rather than positioning him as the problem.

**Answer 3**
In behavioral terms, the houseguests likely extinguished the sit/stay or attention behaviors the dog had been reinforced for, while simultaneously reinforcing incompatible behaviors (jumping, demand barking, door-rushing, etc.) through attention and permissiveness. What the client is calling "regression" is more precisely a combination of extinction of newly learned behaviors (which are fragile without sufficient reinforcement history) and resurgence or reinforcement of older competing behaviors.

The teaching moment here is twofold. First, use this to explain why building a strong reinforcement history matters — behaviors with deep histories are more resistant to disruption. Second, reframe the setback: "This isn't the dog forgetting — it's the dog doing exactly what behavior science predicts when rules change temporarily. The good news is that what you taught him is still in there, and re-establishing it will go faster than the first time." This converts a demoralizing event into evidence that the client's earlier work was real and meaningful, and motivates them to rebuild with more deliberate robustness planning for future disruptions.

**Answer 4**
The client's non-compliance is not defiance or laziness — it reflects an emotional and conceptual barrier. The client has a mental model in which approaching a guarding dog is inherently threatening or unkind. Until that model changes, the protocol cannot be implemented regardless of how clearly it is explained. This is a human behavior change problem: the target behavior (approaching the bowl during meals) conflicts with the client's existing values and emotional associations.

Adjustments to the approach should include: (1) psychoeducation framed around the dog's experience, not the procedure — explain that counter-conditioning is designed to change the dog's emotional state, so the approach becomes a predictor of good things rather than a threat. Use the client's care for the dog as a motivational lever, not an obstacle. (2) Reduce the initial ask to something that doesn't feel confrontational — perhaps starting with simply dropping a high-value treat near the bowl from a distance, which allows the client to feel they are giving rather than taking. (3) Check for emotional readiness before assigning homework. Compliance requires not just understanding but willingness.

**Answer 5**
The conversation strategy must accomplish two things simultaneously: surface the specific human behavior pattern and invite the client into problem-solving without triggering defensiveness or shame. A direct accusation ("you're making him nervous") will likely cause the client to shut down or over-explain.

A productive approach begins with validation and curiosity: "You've put real work into this and I want to understand what's happening before walks, because that's often where the chain starts." From there, the trainer can introduce the concept of the behavior chain — that reactivity on walk doesn't begin when the dog sees another dog; it begins at harness-on or earlier. This is grounded in the prior topic of behavior chains and is presented as clinical information, not judgment.

Next, the trainer can use a collaborative observation frame: "I'd love to watch what the pre-walk routine looks like — sometimes there are very subtle things that set the emotional tone." This shifts the client from defendant to co-investigator. Once the pattern is visible to the client, the trainer can work on a replacement: a calm, structured pre-walk ritual where the client practices their own regulated state (slow breathing, deliberate movements) before the harness goes on. The trainer should name the human-dog emotional feedback loop explicitly but gently — "dogs are incredibly sensitive to our nervous system states, and anxious humans often have anxious dogs through no fault of anyone."

For this particular client, avoid language that implies blame. Reframe the client's anxiety not as a problem but as evidence of how much they care, and position behavior change as something they are doing *for* the dog.

**Answer 6**
**Client A** (solo, over-training): The human behavior change target is session length and structure. Long marathon sessions undermine impulse control work because they lead to fatigue, frustration, and degraded stimulus control — the dog begins responding to satiation and boredom rather than cues. The behavior chain the client is building is one where training becomes aversive. The intervention should be prescriptive: cap sessions at 3–5 minutes, 3–4 times daily, with clear start/end rituals. Frame this not as "doing less" but as working with the dog's attention window. Client A's engagement is an asset — redirect it toward logging sessions, varying environments, and proofing behaviors rather than drilling duration.

**Client B** (couple with children, inconsistent cues): The human behavior change targets are consistency of cues and scheduling. Inconsistent cues mean the dog's behavior is on a variable schedule for the wrong reasons — not intentional intermittent reinforcement to build resilience, but accidental randomness that prevents stable stimulus control from forming. The behavior chain never reaches reliable fluency because its constituent links are reinforced differently by different people. The intervention must address the household as a unit: create a shared cue vocabulary (written on the fridge if needed), assign one primary training adult per session, and build training into existing routines (meals, walks, greetings) rather than requiring separate dedicated time.

**Why one-size-fits-all fails**: A standard "practice 10 minutes twice daily" prescription over-trains Client A and is unachievable for Client B. The dog's behavior is a downstream variable — the upstream variable is the human behavior pattern, which differs completely between cases. Effective client education means diagnosing the human training environment with the same rigor applied to diagnosing the dog.
