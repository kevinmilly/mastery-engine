## Exercises

**Exercise 1**
A dog trainer is working with a client whose dog has begun growling and snapping at family members during mealtimes — behavior that started suddenly three months ago. The dog had no history of food guarding as a puppy. What is the most professionally appropriate next step, and why does the sudden onset matter to your decision?

**Exercise 2**
A client asks their certified dog trainer to prescribe a short course of anti-anxiety medication for their dog, who panics during thunderstorms. The trainer has read extensively about veterinary psychopharmacology and believes they know which drug would help. What should the trainer do, and what boundary does this situation illustrate?

**Exercise 3**
You are a behavior consultant working with a 4-year-old neutered male Labrador who has begun compulsively chasing his tail for 30–60 minutes at a time, unresponsive to interruption. A trainer colleague suggests this is a habit the owner inadvertently reinforced with attention. Evaluate that hypothesis and explain what professional pathway you would recommend and why.

**Exercise 4**
A dog trainer has been successfully using a desensitization protocol for a dog with leash reactivity. After six weeks of steady progress, the dog suddenly regresses — becoming more reactive than at baseline — despite no obvious changes in the training environment or the owner's technique. Analyze what this pattern suggests and describe the referral decision the trainer should make.

**Exercise 5**
You are consulting on a multi-dog household where a previously subordinate dog (Dog B) has begun unprovoked attacks on the other dog (Dog A) over the past month. The owner also reports Dog B seems "off" — less playful, sleeping more, and occasionally disoriented. Using your knowledge of scope of practice, behavior chain disruption, and multi-dog dynamics, construct the full professional response: who should be involved, in what order, and what you would and would not attempt on your own.

**Exercise 6**
A client comes to you frustrated after a veterinary behaviorist prescribed fluoxetine for their dog's severe separation anxiety. The client wants you to take over the case entirely and skip the medication, arguing that "training should be enough." You have worked successfully with separation-related behaviors before and believe in the least-intrusive approach. How do you navigate this situation — balancing client autonomy, your ethical obligations, evidence-based practice, and the collaborative care model — without abandoning the client or overstepping your scope?

---

## Answer Key

**Answer 1**
The sudden onset is the critical signal. Resource guarding that develops in an adult dog with no prior history should raise immediate suspicion of a medical cause — pain (e.g., dental disease, orthopedic injury making the dog guard its space during feeding), neurological changes, or hormonal shifts. A trainer's scope covers behavior modification, but diagnosing or ruling out physical causes is outside that scope. The appropriate next step is to refer the client to a veterinarian before initiating any behavior modification protocol. Beginning a guarding modification program while an undiagnosed pain condition is present could suppress warning signals without addressing the cause, potentially increasing bite risk. Sudden behavioral change in an adult dog is a red flag that warrants medical clearance first.

**Answer 2**
The trainer should decline and refer the client to a veterinarian or veterinary behaviorist. Prescribing, recommending, or dispensing medication — even informally — is practicing veterinary medicine without a license. This boundary exists regardless of the trainer's personal knowledge level; competence does not confer legal or ethical authority to practice outside one's professional scope. The trainer can acknowledge the client's distress, explain the referral rationale, and offer to coordinate with the veterinarian on a complementary behavior modification plan (e.g., desensitization to storm cues). The appropriate role is collaborative, not substitute.

**Answer 3**
The colleague's reinforcement hypothesis is plausible but insufficient given the presentation. Tail chasing that is prolonged (30–60 minutes), intense, and unresponsive to interruption fits the profile of a compulsive disorder — stereotypy driven by neurological dysregulation rather than a simple operant habit. Compulsive behaviors in dogs have documented neurobiological underpinnings and are distinct from attention-seeking repetitions, which are typically interruptible and context-dependent. The professional pathway should be referral to a veterinary behaviorist, who can assess for compulsive disorder, rule out neurological pathology (e.g., partial seizure activity), and consider whether pharmacological intervention is warranted alongside behavior therapy. A trainer attempting to extinguish this with standard operant techniques risks making the behavior worse or missing a treatable medical condition.

**Answer 4**
Sudden regression to baseline or below — especially after a period of genuine progress — is a pattern that warrants medical investigation, not just protocol adjustment. In leash reactivity cases, regression can be explained by training drift or environmental changes, but when those are ruled out, physical factors must be considered: pain (e.g., a new joint issue making the dog more defensive), vision or hearing changes affecting startle threshold, or the onset of an illness affecting stress tolerance. The trainer should pause the behavior modification work, communicate the regression pattern clearly to the client, and recommend a full veterinary examination before resuming. Continuing to push desensitization on a dog whose threshold has dropped for a medical reason is unlikely to succeed and may erode the training relationship built over six weeks.

**Answer 5**
The combination of sudden inter-dog aggression and physical/behavioral changes in Dog B (lethargy, disorientation, reduced play) strongly suggests a medical etiology driving the aggression — not a social dominance shift or a reinforcement history problem. The professional response has a clear order:

1. **Immediate safety management**: Separate the dogs to prevent further injury while the cause is investigated. Do not attempt inter-dog reintroduction protocols yet.
2. **Urgent veterinary referral for Dog B**: The physical symptoms are the first priority. Conditions like hypothyroidism, brain lesions, pain, or metabolic dysfunction can produce both behavioral change and aggression. This is outside trainer scope to assess.
3. **Hold behavior modification**: No multi-dog reintegration work should begin until Dog B has medical clearance. Designing a behavior plan around a medically compromised dog risks ineffectiveness and may mask important clinical signals.
4. **Coordinate with the veterinarian**: Once a diagnosis is established, collaborate with the vet (or veterinary behaviorist if warranted) on a reintegration plan that accounts for any ongoing condition or medication.

What the trainer should not do: attempt to reintroduce the dogs using counterconditioning, interpret the attacks as a behavior chain problem solvable with management alone, or reassure the client that this is "normal dominance reshuffling."

**Answer 6**
This scenario requires holding multiple obligations simultaneously without collapsing any of them.

**Client autonomy is real but not absolute**: The client has the right to make decisions about their dog's care, including declining medication. However, autonomy doesn't mean the trainer's role is to validate every preference — it means ensuring the client can make an informed choice.

**Ethical obligation and evidence**: Severe separation anxiety is among the behavioral conditions with the strongest evidence base for pharmacological support alongside behavior therapy. Fluoxetine in this context isn't a shortcut — it reduces the dog's arousal ceiling so behavior modification can work. A trainer who proceeds with behavior-only intervention for a severe case, knowing the evidence, is operating at the edge of ethical practice. The least-intrusive principle applies within cases where the intervention is appropriate; it doesn't mean withholding effective treatment.

**The collaborative model**: The trainer should have an honest conversation with the client — acknowledging their concern, explaining what the medication is intended to do (not replace training, but make the dog accessible to it), and strongly recommending they discuss discontinuation concerns with the veterinary behaviorist rather than simply stopping. The trainer can continue working on the behavior modification components while encouraging the client to stay engaged with the prescribing clinician.

**What not to do**: Do not take over the case in a way that functionally removes the veterinary behaviorist from the picture. Do not tell the client medication is unnecessary if you believe otherwise. Do not abandon the client because they're non-compliant — that removes the last professional with appropriate scope still present. The goal is to keep the collaborative care model intact even under client pressure.
