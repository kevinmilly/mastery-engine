## Exercises

**Exercise 1**
A coffee shop app wants to predict your favorite drink. The app looks at your past 100 orders and then suggests a "Caramel Macchiato" on the home screen. In this scenario, identify the data, the model, and the prediction.

**Exercise 2**
An autonomous car's camera system is designed to identify pedestrians. It was trained by being shown millions of images, some labeled "pedestrian" and others "not pedestrian." When driving, it processes a live video feed. What is the specific input it uses to make a *single* prediction, and what is the output of that prediction?

**Exercise 3**
A startup builds a model to predict whether a user will click on a web advertisement. They train it using data exclusively from users who are 18-25 years old. What is the most likely problem they will encounter when the ad is shown to a 60-year-old user? Explain your reasoning in terms of the relationship between data and the model.

**Exercise 4**
An agricultural company uses an AI system to predict crop yield. They collect sensor data from a field (e.g., moisture, nitrogen levels) and the model predicts a yield of "4.5 tons per acre." A farmer argues, "The model isn't the prediction. The model is the software I downloaded." Is the farmer's statement correct? Clarify the distinction between the model and the prediction in this context.

**Exercise 5**
Before the AI/ML paradigm, a bank might approve a loan based on a programmer writing explicit rules, such as: `IF credit_score > 700 AND income > $50,000 THEN approve_loan`. How does the ML approach to this same problem fundamentally differ? Describe the process using the concepts of data, model, and prediction.

**Exercise 6**
A company develops a model to predict employee turnover. It is trained on company data from 2018-2020. The model works well for a year, but after the company implements a new permanent "work-from-anywhere" policy in 2022, the model's predictions become unreliable. Propose a hypothesis for why the model is failing. Your explanation must connect the training data, the model, and its current predictions.

---

## Answer Key

**Answer 1**
-   **Data:** The record of your past 100 coffee orders. This is the historical information the system learns from.
-   **Model:** The set of learned patterns or rules, derived from your order history, that associates your past behavior with a likely future preference. We don't see the model itself, only its output.
-   **Prediction:** The suggestion of "Caramel Macchiato." This is the output generated by the model when applied to your user profile.

**Answer 2**
-   **Input for a single prediction:** A single frame (or a small sequence of frames) from the live video feed. This is the new, unseen data the model must evaluate.
-   **Output of the prediction:** A classification label for objects in the frame, such as "pedestrian" or "not pedestrian," often with a bounding box around the identified object.

**Answer 3**
The most likely problem is that the model will make an inaccurate prediction.

**Reasoning:** The model has learned patterns exclusively from the data of 18-25 year olds. The web browsing habits, interests, and responsiveness to ads of a 60-year-old are likely very different. Because the model was never trained on data representing this demographic, it has no reliable patterns to apply. The model's internal logic is a reflection of its training data, and when it encounters new data that is fundamentally different, its predictions are unreliable.

**Answer 4**
The farmer's statement is incorrect.

**Reasoning:**
-   The **model** is the complex mathematical representation—the "brain"—that has learned the relationship between sensor readings and crop yield from historical data. The software the farmer downloaded *contains* this model.
-   The **prediction** is the specific output generated by the model when it is given a new set of sensor data. In this case, "4.5 tons per acre" is the prediction. The model is the persistent system that *generates* the prediction; the prediction is the temporary output for a given input.

**Answer 5**
The ML approach differs by learning the rules from data rather than having them explicitly programmed.

**Process:**
1.  **Data:** Instead of starting with rules, the bank would gather a large historical dataset of past loan applications. Each application would include the applicant's features (credit score, income, etc.) and the final outcome (whether they defaulted or paid it back successfully).
2.  **Model:** A machine learning algorithm would process this data to "learn" the complex patterns and relationships between the applicant features and the likelihood of a successful loan. The result is a trained model that internally represents these learned patterns, which are often far more nuanced than simple `IF/THEN` rules.
3.  **Prediction:** When a new application arrives, its data is fed into the model. The model then outputs a prediction, such as "approve," "deny," or a probability of successful repayment (e.g., "92% chance of success").

**Answer 6**
**Hypothesis:** The model is failing because the factors that predicted employee turnover before the policy change are no longer the same as the factors that predict it now. This is a *data representation problem*.

**Explanation:**
-   **Training Data:** The model was trained on data from 2018-2020. During this period, predictors of turnover might have included commute time, office location, or in-office team dynamics.
-   **Model:** The model learned to weigh those factors heavily. For example, it might have learned that a long commute time strongly predicts that an employee will leave.
-   **Current Predictions:** After the "work-from-anywhere" policy, commute time is irrelevant for most employees. New factors, such as feelings of isolation, home office setup, or timezone coordination challenges, may now be the key drivers of turnover. The model continues to apply its outdated logic (based on the old data) to the new reality, leading to inaccurate predictions because the underlying patterns in the real world have changed. The model itself hasn't changed, but the data it was trained on no longer represents the current situation.
