## Exercises

**Exercise 1**
An ancient Roman general writes in his personal diary about a recent battle: "Our disciplined legions easily crushed the rebellion of the savage tribes. Their chaotic charge was no match for our superior tactics and the righteousness of our cause. We have brought Roman order to a land that knew only barbarism."

Based on his role and background, identify two potential sources of bias in the general's account.

**Exercise 2**
A history book published in the Soviet Union in 1970 describes the American moon landing as a desperate, expensive stunt designed to distract from social inequality and poverty within the United States.

How might the author's nationality and the time period (the Cold War) influence this interpretation of the event?

**Exercise 3**
You are studying the Boston Massacre and find two different engravings of the event, both from 1770.
-   **Engraving A (by Paul Revere, a colonist activist):** Shows organized British soldiers firing on a peaceful, unarmed crowd of colonists. The soldiers look menacing, and the colonists appear as innocent victims.
-   **Engraving B (by a British artist):** Shows a chaotic mob of colonists brandishing clubs and throwing rocks at a small group of British soldiers who appear to be defending themselves.

Explain how the different creators' intentions likely shaped these two conflicting visual accounts of the same event.

**Exercise 4**
A historian is researching the life of a famous medieval queen. She finds two primary sources:
-   **Source 1:** A series of official royal chronicles written by a monk who was paid by the queen's court to document her reign for posterity.
-   **Source 2:** A collection of private letters from a foreign ambassador who was a political rival of the queen and frequently competed with her for influence in the region.

What different types of bias should the historian anticipate from each source, and how might these biases affect the portrayal of the queen's accomplishments?

**Exercise 5**
A student is writing a report on the construction of the Panama Canal. They use a secondary source: a book written in 2015. The author of this book relies heavily on one primary source: the published memoir of the lead American engineer on the project, written in 1920. This memoir rarely mentions the contributions of the thousands of West Indian laborers or the high death toll from disease.

How might the bias in the primary source (the engineer's memoir) affect the historical narrative presented in the secondary source (the 2015 book)? What is the modern author's responsibility when using such a source?

**Exercise 6**
Following a major civil war, two histories are written.
-   **History A (written 5 years after the war):** Written by a former general from the winning side. His account explains the cause of the war as a necessary struggle against the losing side's treachery and backward ideas. He presents the victory as inevitable and just.
-   **History B (written 100 years after the war):** Written by an academic historian. Her account explains the cause of the war as a complex result of decades of economic disparity, regional political tensions, and a prolonged drought that created resource scarcity.

How does the time passed since the event (chronology) influence the authors' different explanations of cause and effect? Which account is more likely to be shaped by personal involvement and which by a desire for a comprehensive explanation?

## Answer Key

**Answer 1**
The general's account is likely influenced by at least two sources of bias:
1.  **Professional/Nationalistic Bias:** As a Roman general, his career and loyalty are tied to Rome's success. He is inclined to portray his army (the "legions") as superior and his nation's actions as righteous and civilizing ("bringing order").
2.  **Cultural Bias:** He refers to the enemy as "savage tribes" and "barbarians." This language reflects a Roman worldview that considered non-Roman peoples to be culturally inferior. This bias leads him to interpret their military tactics as "chaotic" rather than analyzing them from a neutral standpoint.

**Answer 2**
The author's perspective is heavily influenced by the context of the Cold War, a period of intense ideological, political, and economic rivalry between the Soviet Union and the United States.
-   **Nationality/Ideology:** As a writer in the Soviet Union, the author would be working within a communist framework that was critical of capitalism. Therefore, they interpret a major American achievement not as a triumph of science, but as a symptom of capitalism's flaws—a way to mask domestic problems like inequality.
-   **Time Period:** Writing in 1970, at the height of the Cold War "Space Race," the author's purpose is likely not just to record history, but to frame the event as a propaganda victory for the Soviet Union by diminishing the achievement of its primary rival.

**Answer 3**
The creators' intentions are to persuade viewers to adopt their political viewpoint.
-   **Paul Revere's Intention:** As a colonial activist, his goal was to rally support for the cause of independence. His engraving is a piece of propaganda designed to provoke outrage against the British. By showing disciplined soldiers firing on innocent civilians, he intentionally frames the event as an unprovoked "massacre" to create martyrs and fuel revolutionary sentiment.
-   **The British Artist's Intention:** Their goal was likely to defend the actions of the British soldiers and portray the colonists as a lawless, violent mob. By showing the colonists with weapons and in the act of attacking, they create a narrative of self-defense, justifying the soldiers' use of lethal force and supporting the British government's position.

**Answer 4**
The historian should anticipate conflicting biases that will create very different portraits of the queen.
-   **Source 1 (The Court Monk):** This source will likely have a **positive bias**. The monk was employed by the court, so his job was to present the queen in the best possible light. He would likely emphasize her wisdom, piety, and successes while downplaying or omitting her failures, mistakes, or controversial decisions. This source is useful for understanding how the queen wanted to be seen.
-   **Source 2 (The Rival Ambassador):** This source will likely have a **negative bias**. As a political rival, the ambassador would have an interest in portraying the queen as incompetent, weak, or immoral to his own government. He might exaggerate her difficulties, misinterpret her actions in the worst possible way, or focus only on court scandals. This source is useful for understanding the political challenges and opposition the queen faced.

**Answer 5**
The bias in the primary source could significantly skew the narrative of the secondary source.
-   **Effect on the Narrative:** If the 2015 author relies too heavily on the engineer's memoir, their book might present a "Great Man" history, focusing solely on the achievements of the American engineers. It would likely erase the critical contributions and immense sacrifices of the West Indian labor force, creating an incomplete and biased historical account that glorifies the project's leaders while ignoring its human cost.
-   **Author's Responsibility:** A responsible modern historian should not take the engineer's memoir at face value. Their job is to **contextualize and corroborate** the primary source. They should acknowledge the engineer's perspective and its inherent biases (e.g., his focus on engineering challenges, his potential nationalistic pride, and his social position). To create a balanced history, the author must seek out other primary sources (like labor records, hospital logs, or newspapers from the Caribbean) to include the perspectives that the engineer's memoir leaves out.

**Answer 6**
The amount of time that has passed dramatically changes the authors' perspectives and their approach to explaining cause and effect.
-   **History A (written 5 years after):** This account is heavily shaped by **personal experience and lack of distance**. The general's bias is that of a participant. His "cause" is a justification for his side's actions (a "necessary struggle") and a condemnation of the enemy ("treachery"). His goal is to create a simple, morally clear narrative that validates the war's outcome. His proximity to the event makes it difficult to see complex, long-term causes.
-   **History B (written 100 years after):** This account benefits from **historical distance and access to more diverse sources**. The historian is not personally invested in justifying the war. Her "cause" is a multi-faceted analysis based on long-term trends (economics, politics, environment) that may not have been obvious to those living through the events. Her goal is not to assign blame but to provide a complex, comprehensive explanation. This demonstrates how perspective changes as more time allows for more evidence to emerge and for personal passions to fade.
