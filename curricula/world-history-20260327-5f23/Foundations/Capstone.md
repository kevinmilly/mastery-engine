## Capstone: Foundations — World History

### The Scenario
You are a junior research associate at the Meridian Museum of World History. You've been assigned to help a senior curator, Dr. Anya Sharma, develop a new exhibit titled "Echoes of the River: Life in Ancient Sumer." The museum has recently acquired a small collection of artifacts and textual sources from the region, and Dr. Sharma needs your help making sense of them.

Your task is to produce a formal "Preliminary Curatorial Report" that analyzes these materials. This report will help Dr. Sharma decide on the exhibit's central narrative and which artifacts to feature most prominently. A clear, well-reasoned report is crucial for ensuring the final exhibit is both engaging and historically accurate.

Here is the inventory of the materials you must analyze:

*   **Item A:** A set of baked clay farming tools, including a sickle with a serrated edge and a flat stone for grinding grain. The museum's acquisition notes state they were found near the remains of an ancient, human-made irrigation canal.
*   **Item B:** Two clay tablets inscribed with cuneiform script. The first tablet is an administrative record, appearing to be a list of grain rations distributed to individuals designated as "temple builders." The second tablet contains a fragment of an epic poem praising a king for "raising a mighty wall around Uruk to shield the people from the savage highlanders."
*   **Item C:** A small, carved alabaster statue of a robed, bearded figure with large, oversized eyes fixed in a wide stare. It was unearthed in what archaeologists believe was the central temple complex of a large city.
*   **Source D:** An excerpt from a world history textbook, *A Patriot's History of Mankind*, published in 1955: "The Sumerian state was a rigid theocracy, a society of god-kings and peasant-slaves. The rulers, through fear and superstition, commanded the masses to construct grand monuments for their own glory. This oppressive system was necessary to defend their isolated civilization against constant threats from barbaric nomadic peoples."

### Your Tasks

Your report should be structured in five sections, corresponding to the tasks below.

1.  **Source Classification:** For each of the four items (A, B, C, and D), classify it as either a primary or secondary source. For each classification, write a one-sentence justification explaining *why* it fits that category.
2.  **Geographical Analysis:** Using Items A and B as evidence, explain how the geography of Mesopotamia likely influenced the development of Sumerian society. Focus on the relationship between the environment and their agricultural practices and political organization.
3.  **Evaluating 'Civilization':** Based *only* on the evidence provided in Items A, B, and C, construct an argument for whether the society that produced these artifacts meets the definition of a "civilization." You must reference specific characteristics of a civilization and connect them directly to evidence from the items.
4.  **Assessing Historical Perspective:** Compare the story told by the primary sources (Items A, B, and C) with the interpretation offered by the secondary source (Source D). Identify one significant point of difference and analyze how the author's perspective or potential bias in Source D might account for that difference.
5.  **Exhibit Narrative Proposal:** Propose a central theme for one wing of the "Echoes of the River" exhibit. Your theme should be a single, clear statement (e.g., "Innovation Born from Scarcity"). Justify your choice by explaining how it connects the insights from your analysis in the previous tasks into a compelling story for the public.

### What Good Work Looks Like
*   **Evidence-Based Reasoning:** Consistently uses specific details from the provided sources to support every claim and interpretation, avoiding generalizations.
*   **Conceptual Fluency:** Accurately applies key historical concepts (like 'civilization', 'geographical influence', and 'bias') as analytical tools, not just as vocabulary words.
*   **Critical Assessment:** Moves beyond simple descriptions of the sources to analyze their limitations, purposes, and potential perspectives, especially when comparing primary evidence to a secondary narrative.
*   **Logical Synthesis:** Integrates insights from multiple tasks to build a coherent and well-supported final proposal, showing how different threads of analysis connect to tell a larger story.
