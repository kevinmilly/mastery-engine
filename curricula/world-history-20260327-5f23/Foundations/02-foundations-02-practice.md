## Exercises

**Exercise 1**
A historian is researching the experiences of immigrants passing through Ellis Island in the early 1900s. For each of the following items, identify whether it would be a primary or a secondary source for this specific research project.
a) A ship's passenger manifest from 1912.
b) A 2018 documentary about the history of U.S. immigration.
c) An oral history interview recorded in 1980 with an elderly woman who came through Ellis Island as a child.
d) A history textbook chapter discussing immigration patterns in the 20th century.

**Exercise 2**
You are studying the history of science and find a personal letter written by Marie Curie in 1905. In the letter, she describes her frustration with the lack of funding for her radioactivity research. Is this letter a primary or a secondary source? Explain your reasoning.

**Exercise 3**
A history book about the Roman Empire, written in 2022, quotes extensively from *The Gallic Wars*, a book written by Julius Caesar himself in approximately 50 BCE. For a historian studying the military tactics of the Roman legions, which book is the primary source and which is the secondary source? Explain the relationship between the two sources in this context.

**Exercise 4**
Two students are researching the Great Depression in the United States. Student A uses a collection of photographs taken by Dorothea Lange in the 1930s showing migrant farmworkers. Student B uses a biography of President Franklin D. Roosevelt published last year. What different kinds of information is each student likely to learn from their chosen source?

**Exercise 5**
You are assigned to write a paper about the political causes of World War I. You begin by reading a chapter in your history textbook (published in 2021) that provides an overview of the conflict's origins. The textbook mentions the "July Crisis" of 1914, which followed the assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand. The author cites a series of diplomatic telegrams exchanged between European leaders during that month. How does using these two types of sources (the textbook and the telegrams) in this order demonstrate an effective historical research process?

**Exercise 6**
Imagine you are a historian examining a medieval European map created by a monk in 800 CE. The map shows Jerusalem at the exact center of the world. A modern travel website dismisses the map as "useless and inaccurate" because its geography is completely wrong by modern standards. As a historian, how would you argue that this "inaccurate" map is, in fact, an extremely valuable primary source? What can it teach you that a modern, geographically accurate map cannot?

---

## Answer Key

**Answer 1**
a) **Primary Source.** A passenger manifest is a record created at the time of the event, providing direct evidence of who was on the ship.
b) **Secondary Source.** A modern documentary is an interpretation of past events, created long after they occurred, using existing evidence like old photographs and records.
c) **Primary Source.** Even though recorded in 1980, this is a firsthand account from someone who directly experienced the event. It is a memory, but it is still direct testimony.
d) **Secondary Source.** A textbook synthesizes and interprets information from many sources to provide a broad explanation of events. It was not created by a participant or witness.

**Answer 2**
The letter is a **primary source**.
**Reasoning:** The letter was written by Marie Curie herself at the time she was conducting her research. It is a direct piece of evidence from the historical period being studied, capturing her personal thoughts and feelings as they were happening. It offers a direct, uninterpreted window into her specific challenges and perspective in 1905.

**Answer 3**
Julius Caesar's book, *The Gallic Wars*, is the **primary source**, and the 2022 history book is the **secondary source**.
**Reasoning:** The primary source is Caesar's firsthand account of the events. The secondary source is the modern history book, which analyzes, interprets, and quotes the primary source to construct an argument about Roman military tactics. The modern historian uses Caesar's direct evidence to support their own interpretation of the past.

**Answer 4**
The two students will learn very different types of information because they are using different types of sources.
**Reasoning:**
- Student A, using Dorothea Lange's **primary source** photographs, will learn about the human, emotional, and on-the-ground reality of the Great Depression. They will see direct evidence of the poverty, living conditions, and daily struggles of migrant workers.
- Student B, using the **secondary source** biography, will learn about the high-level political responses to the Depression. They will gain an analyzed and synthesized understanding of President Roosevelt's policies, motivations, and the broader economic and political context, as interpreted by the biographer.

**Answer 5**
This order represents an effective research process because it moves from a broad overview to specific, direct evidence.
**Reasoning:**
1.  Starting with the **secondary source** (the textbook) is efficient. It provides context, summarizes the key events, and introduces the main historical arguments about the causes of the war. It gives you a roadmap for your research.
2.  Moving next to the **primary source** (the diplomatic telegrams) allows you to analyze the direct evidence yourself. After getting the context from the textbook, you can critically read the telegrams to see how leaders were communicating, what their immediate concerns were, and form your own conclusions about their intentions, rather than just relying on the textbook's interpretation.

**Answer 6**
The map is an extremely valuable primary source precisely *because* it is not geographically accurate.
**Reasoning:** A historian's goal is not just to know what the world looked like, but to understand how people in the past saw the world. The map is a primary source for the medieval worldview and religious beliefs.
- By placing Jerusalem at the center, it provides direct evidence of the central role of Christianity in the medieval European mindset.
- It shows what the mapmaker (and the culture he belonged to) considered important, not just what was geographically true. It's a map of belief and theology, not of coastlines.
- A modern, "accurate" map could not provide this insight into the past's perspectives; it can only show the physical layout of the Earth. The medieval map offers a window into the mind of someone from that period.
