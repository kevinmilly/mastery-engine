## Exercises

**Exercise 1**
Johannes Gutenberg's invention of the printing press in the 1440s allowed for the mass production of written materials. A few decades later, Martin Luther's Ninety-five Theses were printed and distributed widely across Europe, fueling the Protestant Reformation. In this scenario, identify the primary cause and the primary effect, and explain the connection between them.

**Exercise 2**
Consider the following sequence of events in medieval Europe: (1) Unusually cold and rainy weather ruins grain harvests for several years in a row. (2) The price of bread skyrockets, leading to widespread food shortages. (3) Weakened by malnutrition, the population becomes more susceptible to disease. In this sequence, identify the initial cause, an intermediate effect, and the final effect. Explain how the events are linked in a causal chain.

**Exercise 3**
The fall of the Western Roman Empire in the 5th century CE was a complex process. A common explanation points to the "barbarian invasions" by various Germanic tribes. Using the concept of multiple causality, explain why this is likely an oversimplified explanation. Propose one other *type* of cause (e.g., economic, political, or social) that could have contributed to the empire's collapse and briefly explain how it might have done so.

**Exercise 4**
The domestication of the horse by nomads on the Eurasian steppe thousands of years ago was a major technological development. The most obvious effect was a change in transportation. Beyond this, propose one plausible long-term effect this development could have had in *either* warfare or trade, and explain your reasoning.

**Exercise 5**
You are researching the causes of the French Revolution. You find a personal diary entry from a Parisian shopkeeper in 1788 (a primary source) describing the high price of bread and his family's hunger. You also read a 2015 textbook (a secondary source) that argues the revolution was caused by a combination of economic hardship, the spread of Enlightenment ideas, and a weak monarchy. How would a historian use both the diary entry and the textbook to construct a stronger cause-and-effect argument?

**Exercise 6**
A student observes two facts: (A) In the 14th century, the Black Death killed a massive portion of Europe's population. (B) In the 15th century, the Renaissance—a period of great artistic and cultural flourishing—began in Italy. The student concludes, "The Black Death caused the Renaissance." Based on your understanding of chronology and causality, what is the primary flaw in this direct conclusion? Suggest a more nuanced way to describe the potential relationship between these two events.

## Answer Key

**Answer 1**
- **Cause:** The invention of the printing press.
- **Effect:** The rapid spread of the Protestant Reformation.
- **Reasoning:** The printing press is the cause because it created the *condition* that allowed for the effect. Without the ability to mass-produce written material, Martin Luther's ideas would have spread slowly, likely through hand-copied manuscripts or word of mouth. The printing press enabled his Ninety-five Theses and other writings to be copied cheaply and quickly, reaching a vast audience across Europe and turning a local religious dispute into a continent-wide movement.

**Answer 2**
- **Initial Cause:** Poor weather ruining harvests.
- **Intermediate Effect:** Food shortages and high bread prices.
- **Final Effect:** Increased population susceptibility to disease.
- **Reasoning:** This is a causal chain where each event triggers the next. The poor weather directly *caused* the harvest failure. The harvest failure, in turn, directly *caused* a food shortage and price increases. Finally, the resulting widespread malnutrition (an effect of the food shortage) *caused* the population's immune systems to weaken, making them more vulnerable to disease. The intermediate effect acts as both an effect of the first event and a cause of the last.

**Answer 3**
- **Oversimplification:** Blaming the "barbarian invasions" alone is an oversimplification because historical events of this magnitude rarely have a single cause. The invasions were a critical factor, but they happened to an empire that was already weakened by other long-term problems. The invasions were perhaps the final blow, not the sole reason for the entire collapse.
- **Proposed Alternative Cause (Example):** An economic cause, such as over-reliance on slave labor and a failure to develop new technologies, led to a stagnant economy. This economic weakness meant the empire could not afford to pay its soldiers or maintain its infrastructure (like roads and aqueducts), making it much harder to defend its borders from the very invasions that are often cited as the sole cause.

**Answer 4**
- **Plausible Effect (Warfare):** The domestication of the horse revolutionized warfare by leading to the development of cavalry. Armies with horse-mounted soldiers were faster, more mobile, and could strike with greater force than infantry-only armies. This created a major military advantage and changed the scale and tactics of battles for millennia.
- **Plausible Effect (Trade):** Domestication of the horse allowed for the creation of long-distance trade routes across land, like the Silk Road. Horses could carry heavier loads and travel much faster than humans or oxen, making it possible to transport goods over vast, difficult terrains. This connected distant civilizations, allowing for the exchange of not just goods, but also ideas and technologies.

**Answer 5**
A historian would use both sources to build a multi-layered cause-and-effect argument.
- The **primary source** (the diary) provides specific, ground-level evidence of the "economic hardship" mentioned by the textbook. It shows that this large-scale cause was a real, lived experience for ordinary people, adding a powerful, human dimension to the argument. It helps establish the *what* and *how* of the suffering.
- The **secondary source** (the textbook) provides a broader analytical framework. It synthesizes information from many primary sources to argue that economic hardship was one of several contributing causes. It connects the shopkeeper's personal experience to wider political and intellectual trends (Enlightenment ideas, a weak monarchy) that the shopkeeper himself might not have been aware of.
- **Synthesis:** By combining them, the historian can make the argument that the economic grievances expressed in the diary were a key cause that, when combined with the intellectual and political factors identified in the textbook, created the conditions for revolution. The primary source validates the secondary source's claim, and the secondary source gives context to the primary source.

**Answer 6**
- **Flaw:** The student's conclusion commits a logical fallacy known as *post hoc ergo propter hoc* ("after this, therefore because of this"). Just because the Renaissance happened *after* the Black Death does not automatically mean the plague *caused* it. Chronological order is necessary for a causal relationship, but it is not sufficient proof on its own.
- **Nuanced Relationship:** A more nuanced explanation would be that the Black Death was a major disruptive event that created new social and economic conditions which may have *contributed to* or *accelerated* the trends that led to the Renaissance. For example, the massive population loss led to a labor shortage, which increased wages for survivors and disrupted the old feudal social structure. This newfound social mobility and concentration of wealth among survivors could have created a new class of patrons who could fund the arts and sciences, thereby helping to fuel the cultural flourishing of the Renaissance. The plague was not a direct cause, but a powerful catalyst for change.
