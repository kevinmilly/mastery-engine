## Exercises

**Exercise 1**
The Democratic Republic of Congo (DRC) is immensely rich in natural resources like cobalt, copper, and diamonds, yet it has consistently ranked among the world's poorest and most unstable countries since its independence. In contrast, Botswana, also rich in diamonds, has experienced decades of political stability and is considered an upper-middle-income country. Based on your understanding of economic colonial legacies, explain one critical difference between Belgian rule in the Congo and British rule in Bechuanaland (modern Botswana) that helps account for this divergence in post-independence outcomes.

**Exercise 2**
The borders of modern Nigeria, drawn by British colonial administrators in the early 20th century, combined the predominantly Muslim Hausa-Fulani kingdoms of the north, the Yoruba kingdoms of the southwest, and the Igbo societies of the southeast into a single colony. The British implemented a policy of "indirect rule," often reinforcing the power of local emirs in the north while imposing new "warrant chiefs" in the more decentralized south. How did this combination of border creation and administrative strategy create a structural legacy of political tension that persists in Nigeria today?

**Exercise 3**
Thailand (formerly Siam) was never formally colonized, unlike its neighbors Burma (Myanmar), Laos, Cambodia, and Malaysia. However, in the late 19th and early 20th centuries, it was pressured to cede territory to both the British and French empires and sign unequal trade treaties that limited its economic sovereignty. Analyze how Thailand's post-colonial development path might differ from its formerly colonized neighbors. In your analysis, identify one advantage and one disadvantage of its unique historical position as an uncolonized but constrained "buffer state."

**Exercise 4**
Imagine you are an analyst for a development agency reviewing a proposal to fund a large-scale agricultural project in a post-colonial West African nation. The project aims to boost the nation's GDP by massively expanding its cocoa production for export to European and American markets. The proposal argues this will create jobs and bring in foreign currency. Using your knowledge of colonial economic legacies, formulate a critical question you would ask the project's developers, and explain what potential long-term risk your question is designed to uncover.

**Exercise 5**
A historian from a former colonial power states: "To attribute all of the challenges in post-colonial nations to a colonial legacy that ended over 60 years ago is a form of historical determinism. It strips post-independence leaders and peoples of their agency and ignores the contingent choices—both good and bad—they have made."
Using concepts from this lesson and your prior knowledge of historical determinism vs. contingency, construct a counter-argument. Acknowledge the role of agency, but explain how colonial legacies can create structural constraints that profoundly shape and limit the choices available to post-independence societies.

**Exercise 6**
Compare the long-term legacy of the Roman Empire in Western Europe with the legacy of 19th-century British colonialism in South Asia. Identify one significant similarity in their lasting impact (e.g., in law, language, or infrastructure). Then, analyze two fundamental differences that explain why modern European colonialism, unlike pre-modern empires, created such deep and enduring *global* economic inequalities.

## Answer Key

**Answer 1**
The divergence between the DRC and Botswana can be substantially explained by the nature of the colonial economic systems imposed upon them. Belgian rule in the Congo Free State (and later the Belgian Congo) was a purely extractive and brutal system. Its infrastructure, like railways, was built for the sole purpose of moving resources from the interior to the ports for export, with no investment in creating an integrated internal economy. The system was designed to maximize profit for Belgium with no regard for local development, creating a classic "extractive state" model. This legacy of an economy built for external exploitation, not internal growth, combined with the extreme violence of the regime, left the DRC with virtually no functioning national institutions or diversified economic base at independence.

In contrast, while the British in Bechuanaland also sought economic benefit, their administration was far less extractive and interventionist. Because the territory was seen as less economically vital than other colonies, traditional institutions were left more intact, and the economy was not as violently reconfigured around a single export. This less disruptive colonial footprint, while still a system of foreign domination, inadvertently left Botswana with stronger pre-colonial social structures and a less distorted economic foundation upon which to build a stable, resource-managing state after independence.

**Answer 2**
This dual strategy created a legacy of regional imbalance and political mistrust.
1.  **Imbalanced Power Structures:** By using and strengthening the hierarchical emirate system in the north, the British gave northern elites experience in large-scale administration and solidified a regional power base. In the south, the imposition of "warrant chiefs" often disrupted existing social structures and was seen as illegitimate, preventing the development of a similarly unified regional leadership. This disparity created a political imbalance at independence, with the north possessing a more cohesive and experienced administrative structure, leading to fears of northern domination.
2.  **Fusing Incompatible Polities:** The artificial borders forced distinct ethno-religious groups with different political traditions into a single state. The administrative separation between north and south during the colonial era meant there was little opportunity to forge a common national identity. Instead, political competition after independence immediately fell along these pre-existing regional and ethnic lines, as each group sought to secure power within the new federal structure. This colonial construction is a root cause of Nigeria's ongoing challenges with national unity, federalism, and sectarian conflict.

**Answer 3**
Thailand's unique position created a distinct development path with both advantages and disadvantages.
*   **Advantage:** The primary advantage was the continuity of its own institutions. Unlike its neighbors, Thailand retained its monarchy, bureaucracy, and legal-social systems. This institutional continuity provided a foundation of stability and national identity that was shattered in colonized states. It allowed Thai leaders to selectively modernize and adapt on their own terms (to a degree), avoiding the complete societal rupture and the challenge of building a nation from scratch with foreign-imposed institutions at independence.
*   **Disadvantage:** A key disadvantage was that Thailand's economy was still forced into a subordinate role within the global system. The "unequal treaties" compelled Thailand to adopt low tariffs and open its markets, effectively integrating it into the colonial world economy as a supplier of raw materials (like rice and teak) without it being a formal colony. This limited its ability to protect nascent industries and pursue independent economic policies, mirroring the economic dependency experienced by its colonized neighbors. It experienced many of the economic effects of colonialism (e.g., monoculture, dependency) without the direct administrative control.

**Answer 4**
A critical question would be: **"Beyond GDP growth, what specific measures are included in this project to promote economic diversification and build local processing capacity, ensuring that the value-added steps of production are not all exported along with the raw cocoa?"**

This question is designed to uncover the risk of reinforcing neo-colonial economic patterns. The colonial economic model was based on extracting raw materials from the colonies, which were then processed into finished goods in the metropole, capturing the most profitable part of the value chain. A project that *only* expands raw cocoa exports, without investing in local chocolate manufacturing, refining, or other related industries, risks perpetuating this dynamic. It would make the nation's economy more, not less, dependent on a single commodity whose price is set on volatile world markets and keep it at the bottom of the global supply chain, a classic legacy of colonial economic structures.

**Answer 5**
While acknowledging that post-independence leaders have agency and that their choices matter, the "historical determinism" critique misunderstands the nature of structural constraints. The argument isn't that colonial legacies make certain outcomes *inevitable*, but that they fundamentally alter the landscape of possibilities.

A more nuanced counter-argument is that colonial legacies create a "path dependency." For example:
1.  **Political Structures:** A newly independent nation does not start with a blank slate. It inherits the borders, legal systems, and administrative institutions built by the colonizer. If these borders group together rival ethnic groups and the institutions are designed for authoritarian control rather than democratic consensus-building, the "choices" available to new leaders are severely constrained. Their agency is exercised within a framework that is inherently unstable and prone to conflict.
2.  **Economic Structures:** Similarly, a nation inheriting an economy based entirely on exporting one or two raw materials cannot simply choose to industrialize overnight. It lacks the capital, infrastructure, and skilled workforce, all of which were intentionally underdeveloped by the colonial power. The "choice" to diversify is constrained by dependency on foreign markets, investment, and technology controlled by the very powers that established the initial unequal relationship.

Therefore, agency is not eliminated, but it is powerfully shaped. Post-colonial leaders make contingent choices, but these choices are made on a playing field that was steeply tilted by the colonial experience. Their struggles are often not a result of poor choices from an open set of options, but of navigating a set of poor options left behind by history.

**Answer 6**
**Similarity:** One significant similarity is the legacy of infrastructure and legal frameworks. The Romans built roads, aqueducts, and cities that formed the backbone of medieval and even modern Europe, and Roman law became the foundation for many Western European legal systems. Similarly, the British built railways, ports, and irrigation systems in South Asia and implemented a system of common law that continues to be the basis of the legal systems in India, Pakistan, and Bangladesh. In both cases, the imperial power left a lasting physical and institutional imprint.

**Differences:**
1.  **Integration into a Global Capitalist System:** Roman imperial economics was primarily about tribute, taxation, and resource extraction for the benefit of the core (Rome). However, it was a pre-capitalist system. 19th-century British colonialism was different because it systematically restructured the colony's economy to serve the needs of an *industrial capitalist* metropole. India was de-industrialized (e.g., its textile industry was dismantled) and remade into a supplier of raw cotton for British mills and a captive market for British manufactured goods. This created a lasting dependency and a core-periphery relationship within a single, integrated global economy that the Roman world lacked.
2.  **The Ideology of Race and the Nation-State:** While the Romans certainly had a concept of "civilized" vs. "barbarian," their empire was relatively inclusive and fluid, allowing provincials to become citizens and even emperors. Modern European colonialism was built on a new, more rigid ideology of scientific racism that posited a permanent, biological hierarchy between the colonizer and the colonized. This ideology justified perpetual subjugation in a way that pre-modern imperial ideologies did not. Furthermore, European colonialism ended by breaking the world into a system of theoretically "sovereign" nation-states, but these new states were born into a global hierarchy of power and wealth already defined by their former colonial status, perpetuating inequality on a global scale. The fall of Rome led to fragmentation and new power centers, not a lasting global system of structured inequality between the former core and its periphery.
