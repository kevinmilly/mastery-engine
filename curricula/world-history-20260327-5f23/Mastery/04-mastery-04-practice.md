## Exercises

**Exercise 1**
You are a historian studying the decline of the Western Roman Empire. You uncover two new documents:
A) A sermon by a Christian bishop in Gaul from 450 CE, lamenting the moral decay and lingering paganism in the Roman aristocracy, which he claims has invited God's wrath in the form of barbarian invasions.
B) A set of shipping ledgers from an Alexandrian merchant dated 440-460 CE, showing a catastrophic drop in grain and pottery exports to Italian ports following the Vandal conquest of North Africa.

Which major historiographical interpretation of Rome's fall does each document primarily support? Explain your reasoning.

**Exercise 2**
A student writes the following in an essay: "The primary reason for the rapid success of the early Islamic Caliphates' expansion was that their leaders, unlike the old Roman and Persian emperors, understood the modern principles of religious tolerance and self-determination for conquered peoples, offering them a clearly superior and more enlightened form of governance."

Identify the main historiographical flaw in this argument and explain why it is problematic for a historian.

**Exercise 3**
Imagine a historical debate about the origins of the 1911 Xinhai Revolution, which overthrew the Qing Dynasty in China.
- Historian A argues that the revolution was the direct result of Western ideas of nationalism and republicanism, introduced by figures like Sun Yat-sen, which inspired a new generation to overthrow an outdated imperial system.
- Historian B argues that the revolution was caused by long-term internal decay within the Qing state, such as widespread corruption, devastating famines, and the monarchy's inability to implement effective reforms, which eroded its popular legitimacy over decades.

Based on the principles of historiographical debate, what kind of primary evidence would each historian need to find to strengthen their respective cases?

**Exercise 4**
Two historians are debating the collapse of the Classic Maya civilization around 900 CE.
- The first historian focuses on evidence of prolonged, severe droughts in the region, arguing that this environmental catastrophe led to crop failures, famine, and the breakdown of the political and social order.
- The second historian focuses on inscriptions detailing escalating warfare between city-states, the lavish spending of rulers on monuments, and the rise of a demanding noble class, arguing that these political decisions created an unsustainable and fragile society.

Classify these two interpretations using the historiographical concept of 'agency vs. structure'. Which interpretation emphasizes agency, and which emphasizes structure? Justify your classification.

**Exercise 5**
Karl Jaspers' concept of the "Axial Age" (c. 800-200 BCE) proposes a period of profound philosophical and religious transformation that occurred roughly simultaneously and independently in China, India, Iran, Israel, and Greece. Drawing on your understanding of historiographical debates and the prior topic of "Global Historical Synchronicity and Divergence," construct a critique of the "Axial Age" thesis. What potential problems or biases might this framework introduce when interpreting world history?

**Exercise 6**
You are a historian proposing a new study on the decline of the Mongol Empire. The "Great Man" historiographical tradition largely attributes its fragmentation to the death of singular, powerful leaders like Genghis Khan and his immediate successors, after which the empire's unity was lost. A revisionist interpretation, drawing from the study of "Comparative Empires," argues that the Mongol Empire's decline was primarily caused by its failure to transition from a nomadic-extractive state to a stable, bureaucratic-agrarian state capable of administering such vast and diverse territories.

Propose a specific type of primary evidence you would search for that is *not* a chronicle of a great leader's life. Explain how this evidence, depending on what it showed, could either support or challenge the revisionist interpretation.

## Answer Key

**Answer 1**
- **Document A (The Sermon):** This document supports the "moral and religious decline" interpretation, most famously associated with historians like Edward Gibbon. The bishop's focus on moral decay, paganism, and divine punishment as the cause for military defeats is a primary source reflection of the exact viewpoint that this school of thought emphasizes. The reasoning is that the evidence directly points to internal, values-based decay as the root cause of the empire's problems.

- **Document B (The Ledgers):** This document supports an "economic and logistical collapse" interpretation, such as the Pirenne Thesis, which emphasizes the disruption of Mediterranean trade routes. The ledgers provide quantitative evidence of a severe economic shock directly linked to a key geopolitical event (the loss of North Africa). The reasoning is that this evidence suggests the empire's fall was driven by the fracturing of its economic systems, which made it impossible to support the state and its army, rather than by a decline in morals or virtue.

**Answer 2**
The main historiographical flaw is **presentism**. This is the error of interpreting past events and actors using modern-day values, knowledge, and concepts.

The argument is problematic because it imposes 20th and 21st-century concepts like "religious tolerance" and "self-determination" onto 7th-century societies. While the early Caliphates did have policies regarding non-Muslim subjects (the *dhimmi* system), framing this as "modern tolerance" is anachronistic and misleading. It ignores the historical context, motivations, and worldview of the people at the time, such as the theological and pragmatic reasons for their policies. A historian's job is to understand the past on its own terms, and this argument evaluates it based on the political and ethical standards of the present.

**Answer 3**
- **Historian A (Ideas-driven):** This historian would need evidence demonstrating the *causal link* between Western ideas and the actions of revolutionaries. This could include:
    - The private letters and diaries of revolutionary leaders explicitly discussing translations of Western political texts.
    - Records of student groups and secret societies showing their curriculum was based on republican ideals.
    - Content analysis of revolutionary pamphlets and newspapers to show the prevalence and influence of terms like "nationalism," "liberty," and "republic."
    - Evidence that these ideas were spreading beyond a small elite to influence merchants, soldiers, and others who participated in the uprisings.

- **Historian B (Internal decay-driven):** This historian would need evidence demonstrating the systemic failure of the Qing state. This could include:
    - Provincial tax records showing declining revenues or an inability to collect taxes.
    - Local gazettes and official reports detailing the increasing frequency and scale of peasant uprisings or riots related to famine.
    - Court records or memorials to the throne from officials, complaining about corruption, bureaucratic paralysis, and the failure of reform efforts (like the Self-Strengthening Movement).
    - Population and land-use data suggesting unsustainable pressure on resources that the state was unable to manage.

**Answer 4**
- **The first historian (environmental catastrophe) emphasizes structure.** This interpretation posits that the Maya collapse was caused by massive, impersonal forces largely outside of human control. The climate and environment acted as a fundamental *structure* that constrained the possibilities for Maya society. The drought is not a decision made by an individual but a large-scale condition to which the society failed to adapt.

- **The second historian (warfare and elite spending) emphasizes agency.** This interpretation focuses on the specific choices and actions of human actors (*agents*), such as kings, priests, and nobles. Decisions to go to war, to build lavish temples instead of investing in agriculture, or to increase demands on the populace are all examples of human agency. This view suggests that the collapse was not inevitable but resulted from the cumulative effect of poor leadership and political choices.

**Answer 5**
A critique of the "Axial Age" thesis, drawing on principles of historiography, could raise the following problems:
1.  **Eurocentrism/Confirmation Bias:** The concept was formulated by a European scholar, and the "axial" civilizations chosen (especially Greece and Israel) are central to the Western historical narrative. This framework risks projecting a Western model of philosophical/religious development onto the rest of the world and then finding what it's looking for.
2.  **Exclusion and Marginalization:** By focusing on a few specific regions in Eurasia, the "Axial Age" framework implicitly defines other parts of the world (the Americas, Sub-Saharan Africa, Oceania) as non-axial, or peripheral to major historical developments. This can sideline or devalue their unique intellectual and cultural histories during that same period.
3.  **Illusory Synchronicity:** As covered in "Global Historical Synchronicity and Divergence," the framework can create a false sense of alignment. It groups developments that occurred over six centuries and across vast distances, potentially minimizing crucial differences in their causes, content, and long-term impacts. It risks overstating the "convergence" and understating the "divergence."
4.  **Oversimplification of Causality:** The thesis suggests a mysterious global spirit or trigger for these changes but struggles to provide a concrete, universal cause. This can obscure the distinct local, material, and political conditions that led to Confucianism in China versus monotheism in Israel, for example.

**Answer 6**
A type of evidence to search for would be **provincial tax records and administrative correspondence from a specific region of the empire (e.g., Persia or China under Mongol rule) from the mid-to-late 13th century.**

This evidence could support or challenge the revisionist interpretation in the following ways:

- **To support the revisionist view:** If the records showed inconsistent or falling tax revenues over several decades, a shift from collecting currency/grain to less stable tribute, and frequent letters from local administrators complaining of their inability to enforce decrees from the central Khan, it would support the argument of systemic administrative failure. This would demonstrate that the empire's bureaucratic and economic structures were breaking down, regardless of who the "Great Khan" was at the time.

- **To challenge the revisionist view (and indirectly support the traditional one):** If the records showed relatively stable tax collection and effective local administration that only became disrupted *immediately following* the news of a succession crisis or the death of a specific ruler (e.g., Möngke Khan in 1259), it would weaken the "systemic failure" argument. This would suggest that the administrative structures were functional but were entirely dependent on the personal authority of a powerful individual leader, lending more weight to the traditional, agency-focused interpretation.
