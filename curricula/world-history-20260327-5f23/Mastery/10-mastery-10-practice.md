## Answer Key

**Answer 1**
The critique is overly simplistic because it assumes all change is decay. A more refined understanding distinguishes between corruption and adaptation.

*   **Corruption** refers to the unintentional degradation of a narrative due to memory lapses, misunderstandings, or errors in transmission over time. This leads to a loss of historical detail and coherence.
*   **Adaptation** is the intentional, meaningful alteration of a tradition to reflect the current social, political, or cultural needs of the community. The core historical event may remain, but its interpretation, emphasis, or moral is updated to reinforce social structures, legitimize a ruling lineage, or explain a current reality. This change is itself a historical source.

A historian could distinguish between them by analyzing the nature of the change. For example, if multiple, independent versions of a story from different families all omit the same detail, it could suggest corruption (a forgotten element). However, if one version of a founding myth is altered to include the ancestor of a newly powerful family, this is likely a purposeful adaptation to legitimize that family's rise to prominence. The change is strategic, not random.

**Answer 2**
To corroborate the oral tradition about the founding of Dahomey, a historian could seek the following types of evidence:

1.  **Archaeological Evidence:** Excavations at sites associated with the kingdom's founding could reveal settlement patterns, dates of occupation (via carbon dating of organic materials), and the scale of the initial community. If the tradition claims the founder brought a new type of pottery or metalworking, the presence or absence of these artifacts in the earliest strata would be powerful corroborating or conflicting evidence. This helps establish a timeline and material culture independent of the narrative.
2.  **Linguistic Analysis:** A historical linguist could analyze regional dialects and naming conventions. If the founding lineage is said to have come from a specific neighboring region, linguistic evidence might show traces of that region's language in the royal court's vocabulary, place names associated with the founding, or loanwords that entered the local language around the estimated time of the kingdom's establishment.
3.  **Comparative Oral Traditions:** The historian could collect oral traditions from neighboring peoples or from rival lineages within Dahomey. Neighboring groups might have their own accounts of the kingdom's formation, which could confirm, contradict, or provide a different perspective on the event. Rival lineages might have counter-narratives that challenge the legitimacy of the official story. Comparing these traditions helps to identify shared elements (which may point to a historical core) and points of political contention.

**Answer 3**
A historian should not assume one tradition is "true" and the other "false." The existence of two conflicting accounts is itself a rich historical source.

*   **Analysis of Purpose:** Tradition A serves a clear political purpose: to legitimize the rule of the chiefly lineage by linking their power directly to a divine, heroic, and singular origin. It creates a hierarchical social structure with them at the top by right of ancestry. Tradition B reflects a different social reality, possibly one that is more egalitarian or consensus-based. It emphasizes community and shared experience over the authority of a single leader.
*   **Social Structure:** The conflict between the traditions suggests a society with internal social and political tensions. There may be a stratified class system where the ruling class and the common people maintain different historical memories to explain and justify their respective positions. The chiefly narrative is a tool of power, while the popular narrative could be a form of subtle resistance or an assertion of the importance of kinship groups beyond the chief.
*   **Historical Interpretation:** The historian might hypothesize that the historical reality was complex, perhaps involving an initial voyage by a powerful leader (the kernel of Tradition A) who was later joined by other groups (the basis of Tradition B). Over time, these different memories evolved into distinct traditions, each shaped by the social function it served. The analysis thus shifts from "what really happened?" to "why are there two different stories about what happened, and what does that reveal about the society?"

**Answer 4**
In this context, the oral traditions have several strengths that allow them to function as a powerful tool for historical reinterpretation:

1.  **Reveals Internal Logic and Agency:** The British officer's diary reflects an outsider's perspective, likely influenced by colonial prejudices and a lack of understanding of Asante political culture. He frames the events using his own cultural lens ("chaotic," "tribal"). The oral traditions, in contrast, provide an insider's view, revealing the internal logic, established procedures, and sophisticated political maneuvering that characterized the succession. They restore agency to the Asante actors, showing them not as chaotic subjects but as strategic participants in a well-understood political process.
2.  **Exposes Colonial Bias:** By directly contradicting the colonial narrative's central claim (that the dispute was unstructured and required British intervention), the oral traditions expose the self-serving nature of the written record. The officer's account may have been written to justify British encroachment and portray the colonial presence as a stabilizing force. The oral sources challenge this "civilizing mission" trope, suggesting the British may have misunderstood, exaggerated, or even instigated the conflict to create a pretext for intervention.
3.  **Provides Different Categories of Information:** The officer's diary likely focuses on events he witnessed and people he spoke to, prioritizing what was relevant to British interests. The oral traditions would focus on aspects central to Asante culture: the role of the Queen Mother, the significance of various stools (symbols of office), the invocation of proverbs in debate, and the genealogical basis of claims. This provides a richer, more culturally-grounded understanding of the event that is entirely absent from the colonial document.

**Answer 5**
**Recommendation for the Historical Commission:**

The commission must move beyond a simplistic "written vs. oral" debate and adopt an approach that respects both historiographical rigor and the ethical demands of public memory.

**1. Historiographical Approach:**
The commission should treat both the state-sponsored written texts and the indigenous oral traditions as primary sources requiring critical evaluation. The 19th-century texts are not objective truth; they are products of their time, likely designed to build a nationalistic narrative and legitimize the state's authority, as learned in "Historiographical Debates." The oral traditions are also not unmediated truth; they are a community's living memory, shaped to preserve identity and transmit values. The commission's task is not to declare one "correct" but to investigate *why* these sources diverge so sharply. Corroborating evidence (archaeology, land deeds, records from other groups) should be sought for both narratives.

**2. Ethical Obligations (from "Ethics of Historical Commemoration"):**
Public history and commemoration are not just about facts; they are about whose story gets told and whose is erased. To build a monument based solely on the state's narrative would be to perpetuate the historical violence of indigenous displacement and cultural erasure. It would violate the ethical principle of multivocality—the need to represent multiple, even conflicting, perspectives on the past. An ethical commemoration acknowledges historical injustices and recognizes that different communities have different, and equally valid, relationships to national figures.

**3. Final Recommendation:**
Instead of the proposed monument, the commission should facilitate a new form of public commemoration. This could be:
*   A "monument of dialogue" that explicitly presents both the state narrative and the indigenous oral history, using text and imagery to show the contested nature of this figure's legacy.
*   An interpretive center or museum exhibit on the site that explores the period from both perspectives, allowing visitors to engage with the conflicting sources directly.
*   A "living memorial" project developed in direct partnership with the indigenous community, which might focus on cultural revitalization or land restoration rather than a statue.

This approach acknowledges that the "problem" is not the unreliability of one source type, but the historical power imbalance that has privileged one narrative over another for centuries.

**Answer 6**
Systematically integrating African oral traditions into the history of the "Scramble for Africa" would fundamentally reframe the historiographical debate by shifting the analytical focus from European diplomatic chambers to the African societies being colonized. This addresses a core issue from "The Long-Term Impact of Colonial Legacies": the erasure of African agency and perspective from historical narratives.

**Chosen Aspect: Treaty-Making**

**Traditional Historiographical Debate (Eurocentric):**
The debate has often centered on questions like: Were these treaties legally binding under European law? Were they a sincere diplomatic effort or cynical manipulation? How did they factor into inter-European rivalries (e.g., Britain vs. France vs. Germany)? The focus is on European intentionality, legal interpretation, and diplomacy. African signatories are often portrayed as passive, ignorant of the terms, or as simple dupes.

**Reframed Debate (Integrating Oral Traditions):**
Oral traditions would reframe the debate by centering African motivations, political context, and understanding of the agreements. The key questions would shift:

1.  **From "Was the treaty legal?" to "How was the treaty understood?"** Oral histories can reveal what African leaders and their councils thought they were agreeing to. Many "treaties of protection" were likely interpreted through the lens of local diplomacy as temporary alliances, trade agreements, or grants of use-rights for land, not the permanent surrender of sovereignty that Europeans claimed. The traditions would highlight the profound conceptual gap between European and African ideas of law, land, and power.
2.  **From "Were Africans duped?" to "What was the African political strategy?"** Oral sources can illuminate the complex internal and regional politics that shaped African leaders' decisions. A leader might sign a treaty with a European power not out of naivete, but as a strategic move to gain an advantage against a powerful local rival, secure access to firearms, or resolve a succession crisis. The "Scramble" then becomes not just something done *to* Africans, but a process in which Africans participated strategically to navigate a rapidly changing and dangerous political landscape.
3.  **From "Who signed the treaty?" to "Did the signatory have authority?"** European records simply list a "chief" or "king." Oral traditions can clarify the signatory's actual political position. Was he a legitimate ruler with the consent of his council of elders, or a minor, illegitimate figure propped up by the Europeans to get a signature? This fundamentally challenges the validity and interpretation of the documents that form the "legal" basis of colonial rule, exposing its often-fraudulent foundations.

In conclusion, oral traditions transform the study of treaty-making from a history of European diplomatic maneuvers into a complex history of cross-cultural encounter, misunderstanding, and strategic political action by all parties involved. This directly counters the colonial legacy of rendering Africans as passive objects of European history.
