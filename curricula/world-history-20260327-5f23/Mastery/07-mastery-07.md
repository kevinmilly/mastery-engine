## The Hook
This lesson will equip you to understand why debates over historical statues are not just about the past, but are fierce battles over a society's present-day values and future direction.

Imagine your family's photo album. It’s not a complete, minute-by-minute recording of your family’s life. It’s a carefully selected collection of images from birthdays, vacations, and graduations. Photos of arguments, illnesses, or failures are usually left out. The album’s purpose is to tell a particular story: "We are a happy, successful family." Who chooses the photos, what moments are included, and what is omitted all serve to build this narrative.

Public historical commemoration—monuments, street names, national holidays—is a nation's photo album. It is a curated narrative, not a neutral record.

## Why It Matters
Understanding this concept moves you beyond the frustratingly simple "Leave it up!" vs. "Tear it down!" arguments that dominate public discourse. Without this framework, you get stuck. You might see protesters toppling a statue and think, "They're trying to erase history," or see defenders of that same statue and think, "They're just defending racism." Both views miss the point.

The real conflict is not about remembering versus forgetting. It is a struggle over a society’s core identity. The critical question is not *if* we remember, but *what* we choose to honor in our shared public spaces and *why*. Someone who fails to grasp this can only see the debate as an attack on the past, instead of what it truly is: a negotiation over the values that will define our present and future.

## The Ladder
Historical commemoration is an active process of constructing a public memory. This process involves three key decisions: selection, framing, and purpose.

**1. Selection: Deciding *What* to Remember**

Out of the infinite number of people, events, and movements from the past, only a tiny fraction are ever chosen for public commemoration. This act of selection is the first and most powerful editorial choice.

Think of a major war. A society could choose to build a monument to the brilliant general who masterminded the final victory. Or it could build one to the unknown soldier. Or it could build one to the diplomat who negotiated the peace, the factory workers who built the weapons, or the medics who saved lives.

Each choice creates a different story about what matters. Honoring the general tells a story of heroic leadership and military might. Honoring the unknown soldier tells a story of common sacrifice and patriotism. These choices are not neutral; they reflect the values a society wants to project. The people and events we leave out are just as telling as the ones we put on a pedestal.

**2. Framing: Deciding *How* to Remember**

Once a subject is selected, it must be framed. Framing is the way the story is told—the artistic choices, the words on the plaque, the location of the monument.

Let’s stay with the general.
-   **A heroic frame:** A massive bronze statue of him on a rearing horse, sword raised, placed in the center of the capital's most important square. The plaque lists his victories. The message is one of triumph, power, and national glory.
-   **A tragic frame:** A simple, human-scale statue of the same general, looking weary and grieving, placed in a quiet memorial garden. The plaque lists the number of soldiers who died under his command. The message is one of sober reflection on the human cost of conflict.

Same person, two radically different stories. The frame dictates the public's emotional and intellectual response. It tells us whether we should feel pride, sorrow, inspiration, or caution.

**3. Purpose: Deciding *Why* We Remember**

Finally, and most importantly, every act of commemoration has a purpose. It is a tool used in the present to shape the present.

Monuments are expensive and are built for a reason. Often, the purpose is to unify a nation. For example, many Confederate monuments in the American South were not built right after the Civil War (1861-1865) but decades later, during the era of Jim Crow segregation. Their **purpose** was not simply to "remember" Confederate soldiers, but to assert a narrative of white supremacy and intimidate African Americans. They were a political statement about who held power *at that time*.

As a society's values evolve, the original purpose of a monument can become disconnected from, or even opposed to, current values. This is where conflict arises. The monument hasn't changed, but the society has. The debate becomes about whether the story that monument was built to tell is still a story we want to endorse today.

## Worked Reality
Let's examine the statues of King Leopold II in Belgium.

For decades, statues of Leopold II stood largely unquestioned in Belgian cities. In the public narrative, he was the "Builder King" who commissioned great public works in Belgium and brought "civilization" to Africa through his personal colony, the Congo Free State.

Let's apply our framework to the original context:
*   **Selection:** King Leopold II. This choice highlights the monarchy and the nation's colonial project as central to Belgian identity.
*   **Framing:** The statues typically depict him as a stern, visionary patriarch, a regal and benevolent figure. The plaques would often praise his "civilizing mission" in the Congo. This framing presents colonialism as a noble, modernizing enterprise.
*   **Purpose:** The statues were erected in the early 20th century to legitimize Belgian colonialism. They served to build national pride and reinforce the idea that exploiting the Congo's resources (rubber, ivory) was a righteous and heroic endeavor for the benefit of both Belgium and the Congolese people.

Now, consider the present day. As you know from our lesson on *Historiographical Debates*, new evidence and perspectives have dramatically changed the historical understanding of Leopold's reign in the Congo. We now know it was a brutal regime of forced labor, terror, and mass death, resulting in millions of Congolese deaths. This wasn't a "civilizing mission"; it was a profit-driven atrocity.

This new understanding creates a clash:
*   The **framing** of Leopold as a great humanitarian now seems like a grotesque lie.
*   The **purpose** of celebrating colonialism is now seen as morally repugnant, especially in light of the ongoing *Colonial Legacies* that still affect the region.

For a modern Belgian citizen, especially one of Congolese descent, walking past a heroic statue of Leopold II is not a neutral history lesson. It is a public endorsement of a man responsible for the suffering of their ancestors. The debate over these statues is not about "erasing Leopold from history books." It is about asking: "Should our public spaces continue to *honor* a figure responsible for a humanitarian disaster? What does it say about our values *today* if we do?"

## Friction Point
The most common friction point is the belief that **"removing a monument is erasing history."**

**The tempting but wrong mental model:** This view treats history as a fixed collection of objects. A statue is seen as a factual artifact, like a fossil in a museum. Removing it feels like destroying a piece of the past, leaving a blank space where history used to be. It's a simple, clean idea: keep things as they are to "preserve" the full story.

**The correct mental model:** History is the full, complex, and often brutal story of what happened, a story we are always learning more about through research and debate. A public monument, by contrast, is not history itself. **A monument is a public honorific.** It is a statement of veneration. It singles out one person or event from the infinite complexity of the past and declares it worthy of celebration and emulation *in the present*.

The debate is not about "erasing" a person from textbooks or archives. The debate is about **revoking a public honor**.

Tearing down a statue of a slave trader doesn't make the history of the slave trade disappear. On the contrary, the act of tearing it down—and the debate surrounding it—often brings that history to public attention more powerfully than the statue ever did. The real choice isn't between remembering and erasing. It's between continuing to honor something and deciding it is no longer honorable.
