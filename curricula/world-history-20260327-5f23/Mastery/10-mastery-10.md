# The Hook

After this lesson, you will be able to analyze an oral account—like a family origin story or an epic poem—not as a simple anecdote, but as a complex historical source with its own rules for determining truth.

Think of an oral tradition as witness testimony in a courtroom. A good lawyer doesn’t just accept or reject the testimony outright. They cross-examine it. They ask: What is the witness's relationship to the events? Is their story internally consistent? Does physical evidence—like security footage or DNA—support or contradict their account? Do other independent witnesses tell a similar story? Oral tradition is this testimony. It’s a primary source that requires rigorous cross-examination, not blind acceptance or dismissal.

## Why It Matters

If we only accept written records as valid history, we effectively erase the past of most of humanity for most of its existence. Writing is a relatively recent invention, and for millennia, societies without it still had complex laws, governments, and histories.

A historian who dismisses oral tradition hits a wall when studying the pre-colonial history of many West African empires, Indigenous peoples of the Americas, or early Polynesian navigators. Their entire past becomes "pre-history," a silent darkness before the "light" of European written accounts. This isn’t just a gap in the record; it’s a profound distortion. It often means accepting a colonizer's version of events as the only version, simply because it was written down. As we learned when studying colonial legacies, this creates an "invisible architecture" that continues to shape the present. Understanding how to work with oral traditions is a necessary tool to hear these silenced voices and construct a more accurate, global picture of the past.

## The Ladder

Our intuitive model for oral stories is often the "game of telephone," where a message gets hopelessly garbled through informal repetition. This is precisely the wrong model for formal oral traditions, which are structured systems for preserving information.

Let's build the correct model by looking at how a historian analyzes these sources.

**1. Start with the Internal Evidence: Analyzing the Tradition Itself**

Before looking for outside proof, the historian first examines the tradition on its own terms, much like a literary critic analyzes a text.

*   **Function Determines Form:** The first question is: What is this tradition *for*? Its purpose reveals what parts were most crucial to preserve accurately.
    *   A **genealogy** used to decide land rights or succession has immense legal and political weight. The names and lines of descent are likely to be preserved with meticulous care.
    *   A **king list** recited at a coronation ceremony legitimizes the new ruler. The sequence of rulers is paramount.
    *   A **creation myth**, however, is primarily about conveying cultural values and a society's worldview. While it may contain historical echoes, its primary purpose is not factual chronology.

*   **Look for Mnemonic Devices:** These are memory aids embedded in the structure of the tradition. They act as error-correction codes, making the information "sticky" and hard to change.
    *   **Poetry, song, and rhythm:** It’s much harder to alter a line in a song with a strict meter and rhyme scheme than it is to change a sentence in a prose story.
    *   **Formulas:** Repetitive phrases like "Son of X, who conquered Y" create a predictable structure that aids memory and makes omissions or insertions obvious.

By understanding the function and form, a historian can make an educated guess about which parts of the tradition are most likely to be stable and which are more likely to be symbolic or flexible.

**2. Seek External Corroboration: Cross-Examining with Other Sources**

No single source, written or oral, should be taken at face value. The real work begins when we test the oral tradition against independent lines of evidence. This is how we move from "story" to "verifiable history."

*   **Archaeology:** Does the tradition speak of a city founded by a great lake? Archaeology can confirm if a settlement of the right age exists in that location. A story of a great fire that destroyed a dynasty might be confirmed by an ash layer in the archaeological record dated to the same period.
*   **Linguistics:** The study of language history can trace migration patterns. If a tradition describes a people migrating from the east, linguists can test this by seeing if their language is related to languages spoken to the east.
*   **Rival Traditions:** Sometimes the most powerful confirmation comes from an enemy. If two rival kingdoms both have oral traditions that agree on the outcome of a specific battle—even if they each claim their own side was more heroic—it strongly suggests the battle actually happened.
*   **Written Records:** In some cases, later written sources (often from outside observers, like traders or travelers) can corroborate elements of an earlier oral tradition.

**3. The Implication: From "True/False" to Historical Data**

The goal is not to label an entire oral tradition as "true" or "false." The goal is to extract reliable historical data from a complex source. A historian might conclude: "The epic's chronology of the first five kings is corroborated by the king lists of two neighboring peoples and aligns with the founding dates of three major cities we've excavated. However, the deeds attributed to each king follow a standardized heroic formula, so the descriptions of their individual personalities are likely unreliable praise-poetry."

This is how oral traditions are used: not as infallible scriptures, but as data-rich sources that, when critically examined and corroborated, can recover the history of the non-literate world.

## Worked Reality

**Case: The Sunjata Epic of the Mali Empire**

The Mali Empire (c. 1235–1670 CE) was one of the largest and wealthiest in world history. Our primary source for its founding and the story of its first ruler, Sunjata Keita, is not a book but an oral epic, performed by highly trained specialists known as *jeliw* (or *griots*). If we dismiss this source, the empire's origin is a blank.

Here's how a historian applies the toolkit:

1.  **Internal Analysis:**
    *   **Function:** The Sunjata Epic is not just entertainment; it's a political charter. It establishes the legitimacy of the Keita dynasty, explains the foundational relationships between the different clans that make up the empire, and codifies social values. The *jeli* who performs it is a professional historian, and their reputation depends on accuracy.
    *   **Mnemonic Devices:** The epic is a poem, often sung with musical accompaniment. The poetic structure, rhythm, and formulaic language make it stable and resistant to casual change over centuries.

2.  **External Corroboration:**
    *   **Written Sources:** We have no written accounts of Sunjata's life *from his time*. But the famed Arab traveler Ibn Battuta visited Mali in the 1350s, about a century after Sunjata's reign. His written account describes the political structure, the court, and the power of the emperor. His description of the society Sunjata *created* matches the political and social relationships outlined in the epic. He confirms the *result* of the story.
    *   **Archaeology:** Excavations at sites identified as Niani, the traditional capital of Mali, confirm a major urban center existed at the right time. While we haven't found a sign saying "Sunjata slept here," the physical evidence confirms the existence of the powerful kingdom the epic describes.
    *   **Neighboring Traditions:** Other oral traditions in the region refer to the power of the Malian emperors, confirming their regional dominance as described in the epic.

**Conclusion:** A historian doesn't treat the epic's magical elements as fact. But they can confidently conclude that the epic reliably preserves core historical data: a real leader named Sunjata united several Mande clans, overthrew a rival power, and established the political and social framework for the Mali Empire. The epic is a valid historical source, providing crucial information unavailable anywhere else.

## Friction Point

**The Wrong Mental Model:** "Oral traditions are just like the game of 'telephone'—a story that gets distorted a little more with each telling until it’s completely unreliable."

**Why It's Tempting:** This matches our everyday experience with informal gossip and rumors. We see how easily stories get twisted when passed casually from person to person. It feels intuitive to project this modern, informal experience onto all spoken stories from the past.

**The Correct Mental Model:** "Formal oral traditions are more like a constitution or a sacred text passed down by trained, professional specialists. They have built-in checks—like poetic structure, public performance, and social consequences for error—that are designed to *ensure accuracy and stability* over many generations."

**The Distinction:** The crucial difference is between **formal transmission** and **informal transmission**. The "telephone" game is a perfect model of informal transmission—it has no rules, no specialists, and no stakes. The recital of a genealogy by a *jeli* whose family has been tasked with the role for 500 years, in front of a king whose legitimacy rests on that genealogy, is a high-stakes act of formal transmission. Confusing the two is the single biggest error in assessing the value of oral history.
