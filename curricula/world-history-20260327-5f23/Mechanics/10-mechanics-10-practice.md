## Exercises

**Exercise 1**
A powerful duke has vast tracts of land but faces constant threats from neighboring rivals. Instead of hiring a professional, salaried army, he grants large parcels of his land (fiefs) to several knights. In exchange, these knights pledge to fight for him for 40 days a year. Why is this arrangement a more logical and stable solution for the duke within a feudal system?

**Exercise 2**
A serf family lives and works on a manor. This year, a severe drought has caused their harvest to fail, leaving them with barely enough food for themselves. Despite this, the lord of the manor still demands his share of the crop and their labor on his personal fields. Based on the principles of manorialism, what is the serf's primary obligation in this situation, and what is the lord's reciprocal obligation that might prevent the serf family from starving?

**Exercise 3**
Imagine a fictional kingdom where a new king, wanting to consolidate his power, declares that all legal disputes, even minor ones between peasants on a manor, must now be settled in a royal court by a royally appointed judge, bypassing the local lord. Why would a powerful baron in this kingdom view this decree not just as an administrative change, but as a fundamental threat to his power and authority?

**Exercise 4**
A provincial governor in the Roman Empire was a salaried official appointed by the central government in Rome. His job was to collect taxes, administer justice, and maintain order on behalf of the emperor. Identify two fundamental differences between this Roman governor's source of authority and that of a feudal count who governs a county.

**Exercise 5**
A devastating plague sweeps through a region, killing nearly half of the peasant population. A few years later, the lord of a large manor finds that the surviving serfs are demanding lower rents and more personal freedoms. Using your understanding of manorialism and the lesson on "Disease as a Historical Agent," explain the economic leverage the serfs now possess that they did not have before the plague.

**Exercise 6**
A 14th-century king wishes to fund a large, permanent navy to protect his kingdom's growing sea trade, a project requiring massive, consistent funding and centralized coordination of resources. Drawing on your knowledge of feudal mechanics and the dynamics of trade networks, explain why the traditional feudal system of obligation is ill-suited for this goal and what alternative system of revenue a king would need to develop.

---

## Answer Key

**Answer 1**
In a feudal system, power is based on land, and cash economies are often weak. The duke's arrangement is more logical and stable for two main reasons:
1.  **Resource Management:** The duke may not have enough cash (coinage) to pay a standing army, but he has abundant land. By granting land, he is using his primary resource to secure military service.
2.  **Delegated Administration:** The knights (now his vassals) are not just soldiers; they are also responsible for managing their fiefs, including the peasants and production. This decentralizes the burden of local governance, which the duke cannot manage himself across a vast territory. This creates a stable, self-funding military and administrative structure based on reciprocal obligation rather than a fragile cash payroll.

**Answer 2**
The serf's primary obligation is to provide the lord with his share of the harvest and their required labor days (corvée labor), regardless of their personal hardship. This is because their right to live on and work the land is conditional on fulfilling these duties. However, the system's reciprocal nature means the lord has a key obligation: protection. This includes military defense from outside raiders and, crucially, providing a basic measure of security and subsistence. In a famine, a lord who let all his serfs starve would lose his entire labor force and the source of his wealth. Therefore, he would be expected to open his own stores to prevent mass starvation, ensuring the manor's survival.

**Answer 3**
A baron would see this as a fundamental threat because power in feudalism is localized and justice is a key component of that power.
1.  **Erosion of Authority:** The lord's authority on his manor is near-absolute. He is the law. By redirecting all legal disputes to a royal court, the king is stripping the baron of one of his most important functions and symbols of power. The peasants would begin to see the distant king, not their immediate lord, as the ultimate arbiter of justice.
2.  **Loss of Revenue:** Manor courts were often a source of income for lords through fees and fines. The king's decree would divert this revenue stream from the baron to the royal treasury, further weakening the baron's economic independence and strengthening the king's.

**Answer 4**
The two systems demonstrate a core difference between centralized and decentralized authority:
1.  **Source of Authority:** The Roman governor's authority comes directly from a higher power (the emperor) and is temporary; he is an employee of the state. A feudal count's authority comes from the personal oath of fealty and the hereditary control of his land (his fief). His power is personal and proprietary, not bureaucratic.
2.  **Basis of Power:** The governor's power is based on his official position within the imperial administration. A feudal count's power is based on his direct control of land and the military service owed to him by his own vassals. The governor commands soldiers paid by the state; the count commands knights who fight for him in exchange for land he granted them.

**Answer 5**
The plague dramatically altered the supply of labor, which was the foundation of the manorial economy.
*   **Before the plague:** There was a surplus of peasants, meaning labor was cheap and serfs were easily replaceable. A serf had no leverage; if they complained, the lord could easily find another family to work the land.
*   **After the plague:** There was a severe labor shortage. Land was now abundant, but workers were scarce. This gave the surviving serfs unprecedented economic leverage. A lord could not afford to lose his remaining workers. The serfs could now negotiate for better terms (lower rent, freedom from certain obligations) or even threaten to leave for a neighboring manor where another lord might offer them a better deal. This illustrates how an external event like a disease can fundamentally shift the power dynamics within a rigid socio-economic structure.

**Answer 6**
The traditional feudal system is poorly suited for this goal because it is decentralized, non-monetized, and based on short-term, specified obligations.
1.  **Decentralized Resources:** The king does not have direct access to the wealth of the entire kingdom. That wealth is locked in the fiefs of his vassals (dukes, barons, etc.). To get resources, he has to request "aid" from his vassals, who can refuse or delay. A navy requires a centralized treasury, not a collection of promises.
2.  **Nature of Obligation:** Feudal military service was typically a limited, in-kind obligation (e.g., 40 days of knightly service per year). This is useless for building and crewing a permanent navy, which requires specialized, paid, full-time sailors and shipwrights.
3.  **Lack of Cash Economy:** Manorialism is largely a subsistence economy. To fund a navy, the king needs cash (money) to buy timber, pay wages, and purchase supplies on a large scale.

To achieve his goal, the king would need to bypass the feudal structure and develop a system of direct, centralized taxation paid in cash. This would involve creating a royal bureaucracy to assess and collect taxes on trade, property, or individuals, shifting the basis of state power from land-based feudal obligation to a monetized, centrally controlled economy.
