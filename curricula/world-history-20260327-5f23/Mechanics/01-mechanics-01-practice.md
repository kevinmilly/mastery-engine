## Exercises

**Exercise 1**
A merchant in Samarkand buys a bolt of silk from a trader who has just arrived from the east. He then sells that same bolt of silk to a different trader who is heading west toward Persia. The Samarkand merchant never travels more than 50 miles from his home city, yet the silk he briefly owned started in China and will end up in Rome. Explain the mechanism of trade that makes this possible.

**Exercise 2**
Imagine you are an official in the Han Dynasty government in China. A group of merchants requests permission to export a large quantity of silk westward. Another official argues against it, stating, "Why should we sell our most precious textile to distant barbarians? We should keep it for ourselves." Based on the economic motivations of trade, what is the strongest counter-argument you could make in favor of allowing the export?

**Exercise 3**
For centuries, the city of Petra thrived by controlling overland caravan routes that brought spices from the Arabian Peninsula to the Mediterranean. A historian discovers that around 100 CE, Roman mariners perfected a sea route using the monsoon winds to trade directly with ports in India and Arabia via the Red Sea. What would be the most likely long-term economic consequence for Petra, and why?

**Exercise 4**
An archaeological dig in a port city on the Swahili Coast of East Africa (modern-day Tanzania) uncovers the following items in a single, well-preserved layer dated to the 13th century:
- A large quantity of local ivory and tortoise shells.
- Shards of Chinese porcelain pottery.
- A small pile of Indian peppercorns.
Based on these findings, what can you infer about this city's role in the wider world of its time? Explain how these specific items support your conclusion.

**Exercise 5**
You are the ruler of the early medieval Kingdom of Srivijaya, which is centered on the island of Sumatra. Your kingdom produces few valuable goods of its own, but it strategically controls the Strait of Malacca, the narrow waterway connecting the trade networks of the Indian Ocean and the South China Sea. What specific policies could you implement to transform your kingdom from a simple tollgate into a powerful and wealthy commercial center?

**Exercise 6**
A common view is that historical trade networks like the Silk Road were overwhelmingly positive forces, fostering wealth and cultural exchange. A critic, however, argues they were primarily engines of inequality and instability, draining resources from some areas and transmitting devastating diseases. Construct an argument that supports the critic's negative view, using at least two distinct points based on the dynamics of inter-societal trade.

## Answer Key

**Answer 1**
This is possible due to a mechanism called **relay trade**. In this system, goods are passed from one merchant to another in a chain, with each merchant covering only one segment of the total route. The Samarkand merchant is a single link in a long chain. The silk itself travels thousands of miles, but the individual people transporting it do not. This system allowed goods to cross vast distances and connect societies even when no single person or group made the entire journey.

**Answer 2**
The strongest counter-argument is based on **economic motivation and resource distribution**. You could argue that while silk is precious, China has a relative abundance of it (a monopoly on its production). The "distant barbarians" in the west, particularly the Roman Empire, have a very high demand for silk and a low supply, making them willing to pay for it in gold and silver, which are scarce in China. By exporting silk, China can trade a product it has in surplus for precious metals it lacks, thereby increasing the overall wealth of the Han state, which can be used to pay armies, fund infrastructure, and secure the frontiers.

**Answer 3**
The most likely long-term consequence for Petra would be significant **economic decline**. Its prosperity was based on its geographic position as a necessary middleman on the overland route. The new, all-sea route would be faster, cheaper, and allow for carrying more goods than camel caravans. Merchants, driven by the motivation to maximize profit, would overwhelmingly choose the more efficient sea route. This would divert the flow of trade—and the associated taxes, tolls, and business for local merchants—away from Petra, causing its primary source of revenue to dry up.

**Answer 4**
One can infer that this city was a **thriving and well-connected commercial hub** within the Indian Ocean trade network.
- The **local ivory and tortoise shells** represent the valuable goods the region exported, providing the economic basis for its participation in trade.
- The **Chinese porcelain** demonstrates that the city was a destination for high-value manufactured goods from as far away as East Asia, indicating its connection to the eastern part of the network.
- The **Indian peppercorns** show its connection to the Indian subcontinent, a major source of spices.
The presence of goods from three distinct regions (Africa, India, and China) in one place proves the city was not an isolated outpost but an active participant and likely a key transit point (an entrepôt) in a complex system of international exchange.

**Answer 5**
To become a powerful commercial center, I would implement policies that add value and create dependency on my kingdom.
1.  **Establish a Safe and Reliable Port:** I would invest in building a secure harbor (an entrepôt) protected from pirates and political instability. I'd offer fair, predictable taxes, warehouses for storing goods, and access to fresh water and food. This security and reliability would attract merchants over less predictable routes.
2.  **Foster a Marketplace:** Instead of just taxing goods that pass through, I would create a vibrant market where merchants from the east (China) and west (India, Arabia) could meet and trade directly with each other under my protection. This makes my kingdom the center of exchange itself, not just a waypoint.
3.  **Encourage Support Industries:** I would support local artisans and shipbuilders. Ships often need repairs after long voyages. By becoming the best place to get a ship refitted or to hire skilled local pilots to navigate the tricky strait, I make my kingdom indispensable to the traders who rely on the route.

**Answer 6**
While trade networks did create wealth, the critic's negative view is justifiable.
1.  **Economic Disruption and Exploitation:** Trade networks often created a massive outflow of wealth from certain regions. For example, the Roman Empire's immense demand for silk and spices led to a severe and continuous drain of its gold and silver eastward. This created a trade imbalance that weakened the Roman economy over time. Furthermore, the focus on producing goods for export (like spices or ivory) could disrupt local subsistence economies, making regions dependent on and vulnerable to the whims of distant markets.
2.  **Biological Dangers:** The same networks that carried goods and ideas also acted as perfect conduits for infectious diseases. The most famous example is the Black Death (bubonic plague) in the 14th century, which traveled from Asia to Europe along established trade routes, carried by rats and fleas on merchant ships and caravans. This pandemic killed a massive portion of the European population, causing catastrophic social, economic, and political instability that far outweighed the economic benefits of the goods it traveled with.
