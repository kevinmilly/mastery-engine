## Exercises

**Exercise 1**
The Sumerians in ancient Mesopotamia developed one of the world's first large-scale irrigation systems to water their crops in an arid environment. Explain the negative environmental consequence that this system predictably created over several centuries, and how that consequence ultimately affected their society's agricultural foundation.

**Exercise 2**
The society on Rapa Nui (Easter Island) famously cleared the island's dense palm forests. Beyond the loss of wood as a building material, identify two distinct ways this widespread deforestation would have directly undermined the society's ability to feed its population.

**Exercise 3**
Imagine a society in a mountainous region that has long practiced sustainable terrace farming. A neighboring empire introduces a new, highly effective metal plow. While this technology initially boosts food production and leads to a population increase, describe a plausible sequence of events through which this "improvement" could lead to an environmental crisis and societal decline within three generations.

**Exercise 4**
Consider two large, pre-modern agricultural societies. Society A is based in a vast, temperate grassland with deep, rich topsoil. Society B is based in a tropical rainforest, relying on slash-and-burn agriculture. Both societies experience significant population growth, forcing them to intensify their farming practices. Which society's environment is more vulnerable to rapid and potentially irreversible degradation? Justify your choice.

**Exercise 5**
An expanding empire conquers a new, heavily forested territory. The imperial core demands large timber shipments for its navy and tributes of grain to feed its cities. Using concepts from this lesson and the prior topic on *The Mechanics of Imperial Expansion and Control*, explain how the empire's political and economic demands would likely transform the environment of the new territory and create long-term instability there.

**Exercise 6**
A prolonged drought strikes a region inhabited by several competing city-states, severely depleting their shared water sources and crippling agricultural output. Drawing on concepts from this lesson and the prior topics of *Disease as a Historical Agent* and *Patterns of Migration*, construct a chain of events showing how this initial environmental crisis could escalate into a widespread regional collapse involving more than just famine.

## Answer Key

**Answer 1**
The predictable negative consequence of Mesopotamian irrigation was soil salinization. In an arid climate, the irrigation water brought to the fields evaporated quickly, leaving behind dissolved mineral salts in the soil. Over centuries, these salts accumulated in the topsoil to toxic levels. This process gradually poisoned the land, making it less fertile for wheat, their primary crop. The societal effect was a long-term decline in agricultural productivity. Historical records show a shift from growing wheat to the more salt-tolerant barley, and eventually, even barley yields declined, undermining the economic foundation of the Sumerian city-states and contributing to their eventual decline.

**Answer 2**
1.  **Loss of Soil and Nutrients:** The tree roots held the island's volcanic soil in place and contributed to its fertility through decomposition. Once the forests were cleared, the soil was exposed to heavy rains and wind, leading to massive soil erosion. The thin topsoil washed into the sea, drastically reducing the land's capacity to grow crops like sweet potatoes, which were a staple food.
2.  **Elimination of Ocean Food Sources:** The palm trees were the only resource strong and large enough to build sturdy, ocean-going canoes. Without these canoes, the islanders could no longer venture into deep water to hunt large fish, porpoises, and other marine life. This effectively cut them off from a critical source of protein, forcing them to rely more heavily on their rapidly degrading agricultural land and leading to widespread famine.

**Answer 3**
A plausible sequence of events would be:
1.  **Initial Boom:** The new, more efficient plow allows for the clearing and cultivation of more land with less labor. Food surpluses lead to a significant population boom in the first generation.
2.  **Increased Pressure:** The larger population requires even more food. To meet this demand, farmers use the plows to expand terrace farming onto steeper, more marginal hillsides that were previously (and wisely) left forested.
3.  **Deforestation and Erosion:** This expansion causes widespread deforestation. Without the tree cover and root systems to hold the soil and absorb rainfall, heavy rains begin to cause severe soil erosion and landslides on the steeper slopes during the second generation.
4.  **Systemic Collapse:** By the third generation, the topsoil from the higher terraces has washed away, destroying their productivity. The runoff buries the older, more stable terraces below in silt and debris. The society now has a larger population than ever before but its agricultural base has been critically damaged, leading to famine, social conflict, and potential collapse.

**Answer 4**
Society B (tropical rainforest) is far more vulnerable. The apparent lushness of a rainforest is deceptive. Most of the system's nutrients are locked up in the living biomass (trees, plants), not in the soil itself. Rainforest soils are often thin and nutrient-poor. Slash-and-burn agriculture works on a small scale by releasing a temporary burst of nutrients from the burned biomass into the soil, but this fertility is quickly exhausted. Intensifying this practice by clearing larger areas or shortening fallow periods rapidly depletes the soil's few nutrients and leads to the growth of non-arable grasses or hardened, brick-like earth (laterization), making recovery nearly impossible. In contrast, the grassland soil of Society A is deep and inherently fertile, giving it much greater resilience to intensified farming before becoming exhausted.

**Answer 5**
The integration of imperial demands and environmental impact would create a feedback loop of degradation.
1.  **Imperial Policy:** The empire's need for resources (timber, grain) would override any local, sustainable practices. Imperial governors and tax collectors would be incentivized to maximize short-term extraction, not ensure long-term stability.
2.  **Resource Extraction:** The demand for naval timber would lead to rapid deforestation of old-growth forests, especially near rivers for easy transport. The demand for grain would force the conversion of this newly cleared land into vast monoculture plantations.
3.  **Environmental Consequences:** This large-scale deforestation would lead to soil erosion and disrupt local water cycles. Monoculture farming of grain would exhaust soil nutrients far more quickly than traditional, diverse agriculture.
4.  **Long-Term Instability:** Over time, the territory's resource base would be depleted. Crop yields would fall, and the timber would be gone. This would make the territory less valuable to the empire and more prone to famine and unrest. When the empire's control eventually weakened, the local population would be left with a degraded, unproductive environment, making it difficult to re-establish a stable, self-sufficient society.

**Answer 6**
A plausible chain of events demonstrating this cascading collapse would be:
1.  **Initial Shock (Environment):** The prolonged drought causes widespread crop failure and water shortages. This leads to famine and malnutrition across the region's city-states.
2.  **Interaction with Disease:** The widespread malnutrition weakens the population's immune systems. This makes them highly susceptible to endemic diseases, leading to epidemics with high mortality rates. What might have been a minor illness becomes a major killer.
3.  **Interaction with Migration:** As food and water disappear in the worst-hit areas, desperate groups begin to migrate. They move towards city-states that are perceived to have more resources (e.g., better wells, stored grain).
4.  **Cascading Effects:** This migration has two major effects. First, the migrants introduce and spread diseases between previously separated population centers. Second, their arrival puts immense strain on the resources of the "host" city-states, leading to conflict between locals and newcomers. This internal and external conflict over scarce resources destabilizes political structures, leading to warfare, the breakdown of trade, and the collapse of social order. The initial environmental problem has thus triggered a multi-faceted crisis of disease, conflict, and social disintegration.
