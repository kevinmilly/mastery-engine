## Capstone: Mastery — software engineering systems design

### The Scenario
You are a senior systems architect at "PaySphere," a rapidly growing online payment processing company. For the past five years, PaySphere has found significant success in the North American market. Its current architecture consists of a set of core services running in a single AWS region (us-east-1), all communicating with a large, vertically-scaled PostgreSQL database. This monolithic database handles everything from user account data and merchant information to individual transaction records.

The company is now embarking on its most ambitious project yet: a major expansion into the European Union. This expansion comes with two non-negotiable requirements. First, the platform must comply with the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR), which mandates that the personal data of EU citizens must be stored on servers physically located within the EU. Second, the system must provide a low-latency experience for both North American and European users.

Initial performance analysis shows that the current database is already a bottleneck, experiencing significant write contention and query latency during peak North American business hours. Simply replicating this architecture in a European region will not solve the underlying scaling issues and will introduce major data consistency challenges. The executive board has also mandated a platform-wide Recovery Time Objective (RTO) of 15 minutes and a Recovery Point Objective (RPO) of 5 minutes, citing the catastrophic business impact of any extended downtime or data loss. Your task is to design the next generation of PaySphere's architecture to meet these demanding new requirements.

### Your Tasks
1.  **Geo-Distributed Data Architecture:** Propose a comprehensive data partitioning (sharding) and geo-distribution strategy for the primary PostgreSQL database. Justify your choice of sharding key (e.g., user ID, merchant ID, region) and sharding strategy (e.g., hash-based, range-based). Explain precisely how your design will satisfy GDPR's data residency requirements while aiming to minimize transaction latency for both user bases.

2.  **Disaster Recovery and High Availability:** Based on your proposed data architecture, design a disaster recovery plan. Specify whether you would recommend an active-active or active-passive multi-region setup and defend your choice. Describe the specific mechanisms and replication strategies you would use to achieve the company's RTO of 15 minutes and RPO of 5 minutes in the event of a complete regional outage.

3.  **Resilient Transaction Processing:** The core payment workflow involves multiple steps: authorizing the payment with a bank, running a fraud detection check, capturing the funds, and initiating settlement. Propose an event-driven architecture to manage this complex workflow across distributed services. Argue for either a choreography-based or orchestration-based pattern, explaining why it is better suited for this specific use case. Detail how your design will handle transient failures at any stage to ensure the eventual consistency of a transaction's state.

4.  **Cross-Region Security Model:** Outline a security strategy for the new distributed system. Specifically, address how services in one region (e.g., us-east-1) can securely and efficiently communicate with services in another (e.g., eu-west-1). Detail your recommendations for service-to-service authentication and authorization, as well as the encryption standards for data both in-transit across regions and at-rest in each regional database.

### What Good Work Looks Like
*   **Demonstrates Tradeoff Analysis:** A strong response doesn't present a single "correct" answer, but rather justifies its chosen design by explicitly weighing the pros and cons of viable alternatives (e.g., explaining why hash-based sharding was chosen over range-based sharding despite its known drawbacks for certain query types).
*   **Integrates Solutions Holistically:** The proposed solutions are not designed in isolation. The disaster recovery plan is compatible with the chosen sharding strategy, and the security model is designed to support the proposed event-driven communication patterns.
*   **Provides Specific, Justified Reasoning:** Recommendations are grounded in the specific constraints of the scenario. For instance, the RPO of 5 minutes is directly used to justify the choice of a specific data replication technology (e.g., synchronous vs. asynchronous).
*   **Acknowledges Operational Complexity:** The response shows an understanding that design decisions have real-world operational consequences. It may mention challenges related to database rebalancing, monitoring a distributed system, or the performance overhead of cross-region communication.
