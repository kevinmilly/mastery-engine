## Exercises

**Exercise 1**
You've made a simple vinaigrette with olive oil, red wine vinegar, salt, and pepper. You whisk it vigorously and it looks uniform. You leave it on the counter, and when you return 15 minutes later, there is a clear layer of oil sitting on top of the vinegar. Why did this separation occur, even after vigorous whisking?

**Exercise 2**
A recipe for a creamy Caesar dressing calls for olive oil, lemon juice, garlic, and a raw egg yolk. A food blogger suggests that to make the recipe safer and more stable, you should use 1 tablespoon of store-bought mayonnaise instead of the raw egg yolk. From an emulsification standpoint, why is this a chemically sound substitution?

**Exercise 3**
A chef is making a hollandaise sauce (an emulsion of egg yolk, lemon juice, and melted butter). They add the hot melted butter too quickly while whisking. The resulting sauce is thin, greasy, and looks curdled. What has happened to the emulsion, and why did the speed of adding the butter cause this failure?

**Exercise 4**
A cook is developing a recipe for a "broken" vinaigrette, where small beads of oil are meant to be visible in the vinegar for texture and visual appeal. To achieve this, they are considering adding a very small amount of a weak emulsifier, like ground mustard powder. This seems counterintuitive. Explain the rationale: how could adding an emulsifier help *create* a "broken" (but still partially stable) emulsion rather than a fully uniform one?

**Exercise 5**
You are tasked with creating a thick, creamy, and stable vegan soup. The base is puréed roasted cauliflower and vegetable stock. To add richness, you want to blend in a significant amount of olive oil. Drawing on your knowledge of **Starch Gelatinization**, explain why adding a spoonful of cornstarch slurry and simmering the soup for a few minutes *before* blending in the oil would help prevent the final product from tasting greasy or separating.

**Exercise 6**
A chef is making two emulsions. The first is a classic mayonnaise (oil emulsified in lemon juice/egg yolk). The second is browned butter solids emulsified into a dashi broth (a water-based stock). The chef notices the mayonnaise breaks if it gets too warm, but the dashi emulsion is most stable when it is kept hot. Based on your knowledge of **Protein Denaturation and Coagulation**, explain the likely difference between the primary emulsifying agents in each mixture that accounts for this opposite temperature stability.

---

## Answer Key

**Answer 1**
This separation occurred because the vinaigrette is an unstable temporary emulsion. Oil and vinegar are immiscible, meaning their molecules are not attracted to each other. Whisking applies mechanical force to break the oil into tiny droplets and disperse them throughout the vinegar (the continuous phase). However, without an emulsifier to hold the droplets apart, the natural surface tension of the water-based vinegar forces the small oil droplets to merge back into larger ones, eventually reforming a separate layer.

**Answer 2**
This is a sound substitution because mayonnaise is itself a stable emulsion of oil, acid, and egg yolk. The egg yolk in the mayonnaise has already been used to emulsify the oil it contains. By adding mayonnaise, you are not just adding an emulsifier (the lecithin from the yolk); you are adding a pre-stabilized emulsion. This base provides a large number of already-formed, emulsifier-coated fat droplets that can be easily incorporated, which helps to stabilize the new oil and lemon juice being added to the dressing.

**Answer 3**
The emulsion has broken. A stable emulsion requires the dispersed phase (butter) to be broken down into droplets that are small enough to be completely surrounded and stabilized by the emulsifier (lecithin in the egg yolk). By adding the butter too quickly, the chef overwhelmed the system. There was too much fat introduced at once for the whisking action to break down and for the limited amount of emulsifier to coat. The fat droplets quickly coalesced back into larger pools, leading to a separation of fat and water, which presents as a greasy, curdled texture.

**Answer 4**
The goal is not to prevent separation entirely, but to control it. Without any emulsifier, the oil would coalesce into a single large slick very quickly. A weak emulsifier like mustard powder will surround *some* of the oil droplets, but not all of them, and not perfectly. This prevents total separation but isn't strong enough to create a uniform, opaque mixture. The result is a suspension where some oil droplets merge into visible, stable beads, while others remain small, creating the desired "broken" but stable texture for a specific period. It's about finding the middle ground between a fully stable and a fully unstable emulsion.

**Answer 5**
This answer requires integrating knowledge of starch. Simmering the soup with the cornstarch slurry triggers starch gelatinization. The starch granules absorb water, swell, and burst, releasing long starch molecules (amylose and amylopectin) into the soup. This dramatically increases the viscosity of the continuous phase (the stock). When the olive oil is blended in, this thickened liquid physically traps the oil droplets, preventing them from moving freely and coalescing. This is a method of physical stabilization, using viscosity to create a stable emulsion rather than relying solely on a chemical emulsifier.

**Answer 6**
This answer requires synthesizing knowledge of proteins and emulsions. The difference lies in the nature of the protein emulsifiers.
1.  **Mayonnaise:** The emulsifier is the lecithin and raw proteins in the egg yolk. These proteins are in their natural state. Like most raw proteins, heat will cause them to **denature and coagulate**. This changes their shape and functional properties, destroying their ability to stabilize the oil-water interface and causing the emulsion to break.
2.  **Dashi Emulsion:** The browned butter solids (*beurre noisette*) contain milk proteins (casein and whey) that have already been heated and denatured during the browning process (a Maillard reaction). These heat-altered proteins are effective emulsifiers. Because they are already denatured, they are stable at high temperatures and will not coagulate further in the hot dashi. In fact, keeping the mixture hot keeps the butterfat melted and less viscous, making it *easier* for the denatured milk proteins to stabilize the small fat globules within the hot broth.
