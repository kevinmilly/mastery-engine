## Exercises

**Exercise 1**
You are preparing green beans for freezing. A recipe instructs you to blanch them in boiling water for two minutes, then immediately plunge them into an ice bath before bagging and freezing. What is the primary purpose of the brief boiling step in this process?

**Exercise 2**
A food safety guide states that for chicken breast, a temperature of 165°F (74°C) ensures it is safe to eat. However, an advanced guide for sous-vide cooking lists a pasteurization time of 11 minutes at 150°F (65.5°C). Explain why both of these can result in a safe-to-eat product, despite the different temperatures.

**Exercise 3**
A chef is preparing two batches of guacamole. For Batch A, they mash the avocados and leave the mixture on the counter. For Batch B, they briefly heat the mashed avocado in a pan to 175°F (80°C) and then cool it down. An hour later, Batch A has turned significantly brown, while Batch B has remained bright green. What has happened on a molecular level to the enzyme responsible for browning in Batch B?

**Exercise 4**
You are making a creamy potato soup. Your recipe calls for simmering the potatoes until tender, then adding milk and cream before blending. You decide to blend the raw potatoes with the milk and then cook the entire mixture. The resulting soup has a strangely gluey and thick texture. Based on the principles of thermal denaturation, explain how the timing of the heating step likely contributed to this undesirable outcome.

**Exercise 5**
A cheesemaker is producing a raw milk cheese, which is aged for 90 days. During this time, native enzymes in the milk, like lipase, are crucial for developing complex flavors. However, the cheesemaker is concerned about harmful microbes like *Listeria*. Why can't the cheesemaker simply pasteurize the milk at a high temperature (e.g., 161°F / 72°C for 15 seconds) to eliminate microbial risk and still produce the same cheese? Connect this to the effects of heat on both enzymes and microbes.

**Exercise 6**
A cook is developing a recipe for teriyaki-glazed chicken thighs. They test two methods.
*   **Method 1:** The chicken is marinated in a raw teriyaki sauce (soy sauce, sugar, raw garlic, raw ginger) and then grilled to an internal temperature of 175°F (79°C).
*   **Method 2:** The chicken is grilled plain to 175°F (79°C). Separately, the teriyaki sauce is simmered in a pot for 5 minutes and then brushed onto the cooked chicken.

Both methods result in safe, fully cooked chicken. However, the chef notices that the glaze in Method 2 is significantly thicker and glossier. Integrating your knowledge of thermal denaturation of enzymes and starch gelatinization, explain the difference in the final glaze consistency.

---

## Answer Key

**Answer 1**
The primary purpose of blanching is to thermally denature enzymes present in the green beans, such as lipoxygenase and polyphenol oxidase. If not inactivated, these enzymes would remain active even in the freezer (albeit slowed down) and would cause the beans to develop off-flavors, poor textures, and dull colors over time. The brief boiling provides enough heat to change the enzymes' molecular structure, rendering them non-functional.

**Answer 2**
Both methods produce a safe product because microbial destruction is a function of both temperature and time. The 165°F (74°C) guideline is based on achieving a near-instantaneous "kill" of common pathogens like *Salmonella*. At this high temperature, the microbes are destroyed very quickly. The lower temperature of 150°F (65.5°C) also denatures the vital proteins and enzymes within the microbes, but it does so more slowly. By holding the chicken at this temperature for a longer period (11 minutes), the same level of microbial reduction is achieved, resulting in a product that is just as safe.

**Answer 3**
In Batch B, the heat from the pan caused the thermal denaturation of the enzyme polyphenol oxidase (PPO). PPO is responsible for the browning reaction that occurs when the avocado flesh is exposed to oxygen. Heating to 175°F (80°C) permanently alters the three-dimensional shape of the enzyme, specifically its active site. This change is irreversible, meaning that even after the avocado cools down, the enzyme cannot function. Without a functional enzyme to catalyze the reaction, the browning process is prevented.

**Answer 4**
This undesirable outcome is due to the activity of the enzyme amylase, which is naturally present in raw potatoes. When you blended the raw potatoes with the liquid, you ruptured the potato cells, releasing their starch granules and activating the amylase. Amylase breaks down starch molecules into smaller sugars. By cooking the mixture *after* blending, you gave the amylase time to act on the starch before the heat was high enough to denature it. This enzymatic breakdown of starch resulted in a gluey, sticky texture instead of the clean, creamy body that comes from properly gelatinized, intact starch molecules. Simmering the potatoes first would have denatured the amylase before the cells were broken open during blending.

**Answer 5**
The cheesemaker cannot use standard high-temperature pasteurization because heat is non-selective; it will denature the desirable flavor-producing enzymes (like lipase) at the same time it destroys the harmful microbes. The complex, unique flavors of this raw milk cheese depend on the activity of those native enzymes during the aging process. Pasteurization would inactivate them, resulting in a cheese with a much blander, less complex flavor profile, essentially defeating the purpose of using raw milk. The cheesemaker must rely on other food safety controls, like the 90-day aging period, during which factors like salt content, acidity, and competition from starter cultures create an environment inhospitable to pathogens.

**Answer 6**
The difference in glaze consistency is due to the enzymatic activity of amylase found in the raw garlic and ginger in Method 1's marinade.
*   **In Method 1:** The raw marinade is applied to the chicken. The soy sauce and other ingredients contain starches (often used as thickeners in soy sauce). The amylase from the raw garlic and ginger breaks down these starches into simpler sugars while the chicken grills. This prevents the starches from fully gelatinizing and forming a thick, viscous sauce. The resulting glaze is thinner.
*   **In Method 2:** By simmering the sauce separately, the cook applies heat that accomplishes two things simultaneously. First, it gelatinizes the starches in the soy sauce, causing them to swell and thicken the liquid. Second, it thermally denatures the amylase enzymes from the garlic and ginger, preventing them from breaking down the starches. This results in a much thicker, glossier glaze that clings to the cooked chicken.
