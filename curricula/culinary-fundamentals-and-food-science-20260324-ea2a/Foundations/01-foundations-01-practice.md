## Exercises

**Exercise 1**
A cook uses a knife and cutting board to cube raw chicken. They then rinse the knife and board with hot water and are about to use them to dice an onion for a fresh salsa. Identify the primary food safety risk in this process and describe the correct procedure.

**Exercise 2**
You are pan-frying on a stove. The handle of the hot pan is sticking out over the edge of the stove. You need to grab a spice from a cabinet directly above the stove. What are the two most immediate physical safety hazards you face?

**Exercise 3**
At a food station, a chef uses the same single-use gloves to handle raw fish fillets, then to portion out cooked rice, and finally to garnish plates with fresh parsley. The chef believes this is efficient and sanitary because their hands never touched the food. Analyze this situation and explain the flaw in the chef's reasoning.

**Exercise 4**
A large pot of soup is finished cooking at 200°F (93°C). The cook turns off the heat and leaves the pot, covered, on the stovetop to cool down before refrigerating it. After two hours, the soup has cooled to 120°F (49°C). Is this cooling method safe? Explain why or why not, referencing the relevant food safety principle.

**Exercise 5**
You enter a kitchen and observe three situations simultaneously:
1. A small puddle of water is on the tile floor in the main walkway.
2. A chef is using a knife with a dull blade, forcing it hard to cut through a butternut squash.
3. A container of raw ground beef is stored on the top shelf of the refrigerator, directly above a tray of uncovered cupcakes.

Prioritize these three hazards from most urgent to least urgent to fix. Justify your ranking based on the potential severity and immediacy of the risk each one poses.

**Exercise 6**
You are tasked with preparing a meal that involves breading and frying raw chicken cutlets and making a fresh garden salad. You have only one cutting board and one knife available. Describe a safe and logical workflow for preparing these two items, explaining how you would manage your tools and workspace to prevent cross-contamination.

---

## Answer Key

**Answer 1**
The primary food safety risk is cross-contamination. Rinsing with hot water is not sufficient to kill harmful bacteria like Salmonella that may be present on the raw chicken. These pathogens can be transferred from the board and knife to the onion, which will be served raw in the salsa.

The correct procedure is to wash, rinse, and sanitize the cutting board and knife after they have been in contact with raw poultry and before using them for ready-to-eat food like an onion. This involves scrubbing them with soap and hot water, rinsing them thoroughly, and then applying a sanitizing solution.

**Answer 2**
The two most immediate physical safety hazards are:
1.  **Burns:** Reaching over the hot pan exposes your arm and body to heat and steam, which could cause a burn. The pan handle itself is also a burn risk.
2.  **Spills/Accidents:** The pan handle sticking out creates a risk of being bumped. This could cause the hot pan and its contents to spill onto you or the floor, leading to severe burns and creating a slip hazard.

**Answer 3**
The flaw in the chef's reasoning is treating the gloves as a permanent barrier that stays clean. The principle of cross-contamination applies to surfaces, including gloves.

When the chef handled the raw fish, the gloves became contaminated. Without changing gloves and washing their hands, they transferred bacteria from the raw fish to the cooked rice and then to the fresh parsley. Since the rice and parsley are ready-to-eat foods that won't be cooked further, any bacteria transferred to them can multiply and cause foodborne illness. The correct procedure would be to dispose of the gloves and wash hands between handling raw and ready-to-eat foods.

**Answer 4**
This cooling method is unsafe. The relevant principle is the Temperature Danger Zone (TDZ), which is typically between 41°F and 135°F (5°C and 57°C). Bacteria multiply most rapidly in this range.

Leaving the soup to cool at room temperature allows it to spend a prolonged time in the TDZ. In two hours, it has only cooled to 120°F, meaning it has been in the most dangerous part of the zone for a significant period. The correct procedure for large quantities of hot food is to cool it rapidly by using methods like an ice bath, an ice paddle, or by dividing it into smaller, shallow containers before refrigeration. The goal is to move the food through the TDZ as quickly as possible.

**Answer 5**
The hazards should be prioritized as follows:

1.  **Most Urgent: Puddle of water on the floor.** This is a slip-and-fall hazard that poses an immediate and unpredictable risk of serious injury to anyone walking through the kitchen. It is the quickest to fix and prevents an imminent accident.
2.  **Second Most Urgent: Raw ground beef above cupcakes.** This is a severe cross-contamination risk. Juices from the raw meat could drip onto the ready-to-eat cupcakes, potentially causing serious foodborne illness. While the illness isn't as immediate as a fall, the potential for harm is very high. It needs to be corrected immediately by reorganizing the refrigerator (raw meats always on the bottom).
3.  **Least Urgent: Dull knife.** While a dull knife is a cut hazard because it can slip more easily than a sharp one, the risk is currently confined to the person using it and is an active, ongoing risk rather than a passive, imminent one like the puddle or the cross-contamination. The chef is aware of the situation, and while it should be addressed soon by sharpening the knife, the other two hazards pose a more immediate or widespread danger.

**Answer 6**
A safe workflow would separate the preparation of the ready-to-eat food (salad) from the raw food (chicken) in time.

1.  **Prepare the Salad First:** Begin by washing and prepping all the salad ingredients (lettuce, tomatoes, cucumbers, etc.) using the clean knife and cutting board. Once finished, place the finished salad in a clean bowl and store it away from the prep area, ideally in the refrigerator.
2.  **Sanitize the Workspace:** Thoroughly wash, rinse, and sanitize the cutting board and knife. Also, wipe down and sanitize the countertop where you were working.
3.  **Prepare the Raw Chicken:** Now, use the same newly cleaned and sanitized board and knife to handle the raw chicken, breading it for frying.
4.  **Final Cleanup:** After the chicken is in the frying pan, clean and sanitize the board, knife, and work surface one last time.

**Reasoning:** This workflow prevents cross-contamination by ensuring that the tools and surfaces are clean and sanitized before they touch ready-to-eat ingredients. By preparing the salad first, you eliminate the risk of transferring pathogens from the raw chicken to the fresh vegetables. The intermediate sanitizing step is crucial to creating a safe surface for the subsequent raw chicken preparation.
