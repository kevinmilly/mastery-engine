## Capstone: Foundations — Culinary Fundamentals and Food Science

### The Scenario
Your friend, Alex, is excited to participate in a competitive charity potluck next month. They want to bring a dish with personal meaning: "Grandma’s Hearty Chicken and Vegetable Bake." The original recipe is a family treasure, but Alex admits it has some significant flaws. They’ve tried making it twice, following the old, handwritten recipe card exactly, and the feedback from family has been consistent.

Alex has asked for your help. They don't want a new recipe; they want to understand *why* the original goes wrong and how to adapt it to create a version that is truly delicious and impressive. Your task is to create a detailed Recipe Adaptation Plan based on the original recipe and the feedback.

**The Original Recipe:**
*   **Ingredients:** 4 bone-in, skin-on chicken thighs, 1 large yellow onion, 2 large carrots, 2 russet potatoes, 1 head of broccoli, "a good glug" of olive oil, salt, pepper, dried rosemary.
*   **Method:** Preheat oven to 375°F (190°C). Roughly chop all vegetables and place them in a deep, ceramic casserole dish. Place the raw chicken thighs directly on top of the vegetables. Drizzle everything with olive oil and sprinkle generously with salt, pepper, and rosemary. Bake uncovered for one hour, or until the chicken is cooked through.

**The Feedback from Alex's Family:**
*   "The chicken skin was pale and rubbery, not brown and crispy."
*   "My portion of potato was still hard in the center."
*   "The broccoli was very mushy and had turned a dull, olive-green color."
*   "The whole dish tasted a bit greasy and the flavors felt flat, like something was missing."

### Your Tasks
Your deliverable is a written "Recipe Adaptation Plan" that addresses the four tasks below in separate sections.

1.  **Problem Analysis:** Based on the family's feedback, analyze the four specific problems (rubbery skin, undercooked potato, mushy broccoli, greasy/flat flavor). For each problem, explain the most likely cause by referencing concepts from this tier, such as ingredient properties, heat transfer methods, or flavor balancing.

2.  **Ingredient and Measurement Revision:** Revise the ingredient list. First, convert the vague quantities into precise measurements (using either weight or volume where appropriate). Second, propose the addition or substitution of **one** ingredient that would specifically address the "flat flavor" complaint. Justify your choice by explaining how it helps to balance the dish's overall flavor profile.

3.  **Revised Cooking Method:** Write a new, step-by-step procedure for preparing and cooking the dish. Your revised method must be designed to solve the textural problems with the chicken, potatoes, and broccoli. In your description, explicitly state how your new method alters the use of heat transfer (conduction, convection, or radiation) to produce a better result for at least two of the ingredients.

4.  **Mise en Place and Safety Plan:** Create a detailed *mise en place* plan for your revised recipe. This should be a checklist of all preparation tasks to be completed before cooking begins, including specific knife cuts for the vegetables and the tools required for each step. As part of this plan, identify two potential food safety risks (e.g., cross-contamination) specific to this recipe and describe the sanitation steps you would take to prevent them.

### What Good Work Looks Like
*   Demonstrates a clear link between the chosen cooking method, heat transfer principles, and the desired textural outcome for the ingredients.
*   Justifies all proposed changes with specific reasoning grounded in foundational culinary concepts, explaining *why* a new temperature, ingredient, or preparation step will solve the identified problems.
*   Presents a logical, efficient, and safe workflow in the *mise en place* plan that a real cook could follow.
*   Uses precise vocabulary from the tier to describe sensory attributes, ingredient properties, and cooking processes.
*   Shows a practical understanding of food safety by identifying plausible risks in the context of the recipe and outlining specific, effective preventative actions.
